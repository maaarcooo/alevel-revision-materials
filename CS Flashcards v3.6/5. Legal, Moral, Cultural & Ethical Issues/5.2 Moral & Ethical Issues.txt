What are ethics? | The values held by a community and how these impact different groups of people in society
The values held by a community and how these impact different groups of people in society — what term is this? | Ethics
What are morals? | A personal code of conduct encompassing how we choose to behave, including decisions we make at the expense of others
A personal code of conduct encompassing how we choose to behave, including decisions we make at the expense of others — what term is this? | Morals
How do ethics differ from morals? | Ethics are community values about the impact on groups in society. Morals are an individual's personal code of conduct
Give two advantages of computers in the workforce | They improve efficiency (e.g. reducing delivery times and unit labour costs) and reduce strain on workers by removing repetitive, tedious tasks
What type of unemployment results from computerisation replacing jobs? | Structural unemployment
Which income level of jobs has computerisation hit hardest? | Middle-income manufacturing jobs, where entire production lines have been replaced by robots
What shift in job types has resulted from the automation of manufacturing? | A shift from middle-income manufacturing jobs toward low-income service jobs
How can the effects of job displacement from computerisation be mitigated? | Governments invest in retraining programmes so workers can change occupation
Give two examples of new job roles created by increased computerisation | Software developers (for embedded systems in smart devices) and network engineers (to maintain networks)
How has the Internet led to a rise in exclusively online services? | Services like online shopping and banking reduce the cost of renting physical space and cut labour costs, while creating demand for delivery drivers and web developers
What is automated decision making? | The process of using algorithms and computer systems to make decisions without direct human intervention, based on data analysis, pattern recognition, and predefined rules
The process of using algorithms and computer systems to make decisions without direct human intervention — what term is this? | Automated decision making
What is the danger of social media feeds being determined solely by algorithms based on users' interests? | It creates a filter bubble where users' beliefs are never challenged, leading to a close-minded society
Give two examples of automated decision making used in application processes | Assessing a person's suitability for mortgages, loans, and jobs by screening candidates for desired qualities
Why could relying entirely on algorithms for decisions treat people unfairly? | Algorithms do not consider extenuating circumstances and cannot process information with the same consideration of contextual factors that humans can
Why must algorithms not remain a "black box"? | Decisions must be justified and some level of human input is essential to ensure fairness, since algorithms designed by humans cannot be assumed to be free of bias
What ethical dilemma does algorithmic decision making raise for driverless cars? | How the car should decide who is harmed if an unavoidable accident occurs (e.g. a pedestrian or the driver), and who is responsible for the consequences
How is automated decision making used in stock market trading? | Algorithms analyse past trends to model future changes, enabling rapid trading decisions faster than humans can react
How is automated decision making used in plant automation and power distribution? | Various inputs are collected and analysed to respond instantly to changes, such as increasing power supply when demand is forecast to rise
What is artificial intelligence (AI)? | A field of computer science aiming to create intelligent machines that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence, such as learning, problem-solving, reasoning, perception, and decision-making
What is an expert system? | A system (also called a knowledge-based system) that replicates the knowledge and experience of a human expert in a particular subject
What three components make up an expert system? | A knowledge base (set of facts and rules), an inference engine (interrogates the knowledge base), and a user interface
What is the knowledge base in an expert system? | A set of facts and rules about a particular domain, used to build an inference engine for finding solutions such as diagnoses
What are neural networks in AI? | Systems that replicate biological neural networks by learning from a given data set and applying that knowledge to new data sets
Give two applications of neural networks | Pattern detection and identifying financial fraud
Why do AI-powered voice recognition systems (e.g. Alexa, Google Home) raise privacy concerns? | They must be constantly switched on to function, meaning they are always listening
What accountability question does AI raise? | Who is responsible when an AI system makes an error or causes harm — the AI, its developers, or the user
What is censorship? | The control or suppression of information, ideas, or content that people are able to view, publish, and access
The control or suppression of information, ideas, or content that people are able to view, publish, and access — what term is this? | Censorship
What type of content do UK ISPs block? | Content associated with terrorism and extremist political beliefs
What is the concern about censorship being misused? | It could be used to block alternative political beliefs, pushing a certain ideology rather than protecting the country
What is the concept of a "Free Internet"? | The idea that nothing online should be filtered at all, in line with the principle of freedom of speech
Why do most people agree some censorship of the Internet is necessary? | For national security purposes and to filter offensive comments and extremist propaganda
Give two examples of censorship on a smaller scale | Schools blocking unsuitable material from pupils, and workplaces using censorship to maintain productivity and prevent distractions
Give two reasons governments may censor Internet content | Protecting national security by preventing the spread of sensitive information, and restricting harmful content such as hate speech or incitement to violence
How can censorship exacerbate the digital divide? | By further widening the gap in access to information and online resources for marginalised communities with restricted Internet access
How are computers used to monitor behaviour in the workplace? | Employers track the websites and applications workers access and the time spent on them, to monitor productivity
How are CCTV cameras used to monitor behaviour? | They are used for security purposes and to detect crime (from speeding to violence), helping to trace and punish criminal activity
What are ankle monitors used for? | Tracking the location of people under house arrest as a surveillance device
What is the argument for surveillance measures like ankle monitors? | They are a necessary measure to deter people from committing crime
What is the argument against surveillance measures like ankle monitors? | They are unethical and contravene basic human rights
What is big data? | Large amounts of data collected from a number of sources that can be analysed to reveal insights about people and their behaviours
Large amounts of data collected from a number of sources — what term is this? | Big data
What is data mining? | The process of analysing big data to make inferences about people's behaviour, likes, and dislikes, and to identify previously unknown connections between variables
The process of analysing big data to make inferences about behaviour and identify unknown connections between variables — what term is this? | Data mining
Give two uses of data mining | Informing personalised political propaganda and creating targeted advertising
What ethical concern arises from data mining in insurance? | An insurer could discover a person is at high risk of a disease and deny them insurance or charge a higher premium
Why was the Data Protection Act 1998 considered insufficient for modern data protection? | It could no longer adequately protect public welfare given the scale of modern data collection, which is why the GDPR was enforced from May 2018
What is piracy in computing? | The unauthorised copying, distribution, or reproduction of copyrighted content such as software, music, or films
The unauthorised copying, distribution, or reproduction of copyrighted content — what term is this? | Piracy
Give three types of piracy | Software piracy (unauthorised copying or use of copyrighted software), music and film piracy (via peer-to-peer networks or illegal download sites), and eBook piracy
What is offensive communication? | Any sort of online harassment, including cyber-bullying or stalking, facilitated by the seemingly anonymous front the Internet provides
What does the Malicious Communications Act 1988 make illegal? | Sending indecent or offensive messages to anyone online, making it a criminal offence that can result in a criminal record
What does the Equality Act 2010 require regarding websites? | It makes it illegal to discriminate against providing a service to a certain group of people, so websites must be designed to accommodate all users
Why might a website display menus on the right-hand side of the page? | To accommodate languages read from right to left (e.g. Arabic), where placing menus on the right matches natural reading direction
Give three accessibility features a website should provide for users with disabilities | Alternative text (alt text) for images, a screen magnifier option for users with visual impairments, and transcripts of audio files for users with hearing impairments
Why must web developers consider colour paradigms when designing for a global audience? | Different colours have different meanings across cultures, so a colour considered positive in one culture may have negative connotations in another
Give an example of a colour having different cultural meanings | White is associated with mourning in the Middle East but with purity in Western cultures
Why is Unicode preferred over ASCII for international websites? | ASCII uses only 7 bits and cannot represent all characters in languages like Chinese, whereas Unicode can represent over a million characters
What is e-waste? | Discarded electronic or electrical devices whose improper disposal can cause serious environmental and health problems due to toxic elements they contain
Discarded electronic or electrical devices that can cause environmental harm if improperly disposed of — what term is this? | E-waste
What toxic substances can be found in computer components? | Mercury, radioactive isotopes, lead, and cadmium
Why is shipping e-waste to developing countries considered immoral? | These countries often have lower environmental standards, and the toxic waste contaminates their water supplies and environment
How does increased computerisation contribute to global warming? | More devices require more electricity, which is generated by burning non-renewable fossil fuels that emit greenhouse gases
Give two examples of environmentally-friendly technologies that offset the environmental impact of computing | Smart home systems (using temperature and motion sensors to reduce energy use) and sleep or stand-by features on computers and laptops
What is the digital divide? | The disparity between individuals who have access to technology and those who do not, creating barriers to information, education, and economic opportunities
The disparity between individuals who have access to technology and those who do not — what term is this? | Digital divide
What happened with Amazon's AI recruitment tool? | It was abandoned because it was biased against women, having been trained on 10 years of male-dominated application data, causing it to penalise CVs containing the word "women"
What is the COMPAS program? | An AI program used in the US judicial system to predict a defendant's potential for future criminal behaviour using undisclosed factors to generate a risk score
Why is the COMPAS program criticised? | It uses undisclosed factors (lack of transparency), AI cannot fully understand nuances of human behaviour, and it may reflect human biases present in its training data
Give four steps for proper e-waste disposal | Reduce and reuse devices, recycle to recover valuable materials, use manufacturer take-back programs, and dispose of at designated facilities equipped for safe handling
Why must personal data be erased before disposing of electronic devices? | To protect privacy and prevent sensitive information from being recovered by others
What moral concern does AI raise about intellectual property and creative works? | Artists' work may be used without consent to train AI systems, raising questions about copyright infringement and whether creators should be paid when their work is used for AI training
How do algorithms need to be improved to ensure fair automated decisions? | They must be thoroughly tested, provided with factually correct and unbiased data, made transparent (not a black box), and include human oversight
