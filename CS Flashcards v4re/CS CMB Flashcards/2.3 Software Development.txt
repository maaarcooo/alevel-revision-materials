What is the waterfall model? | A sequential software development process divided into distinct phases, where each phase must be completed before the next one begins
A sequential software development methodology where each phase must finish before the next begins — what model is this? | The waterfall model
What are the six stages of the waterfall lifecycle in order? | Requirements, Design, Implementation (coding), Testing, Deployment, Maintenance
What happens during the Requirement Gathering and Analysis stage of the waterfall model? | All possible system requirements are captured and documented clearly
What happens during the System Design stage of the waterfall model? | Requirements are translated into a design; architects define the overall architecture and identify the main components
What happens during the Implementation stage of the waterfall model? | The actual code is written based on the design documents, turning the system design into a functional program
What happens during the Integration and Testing stage of the waterfall model? | All components and modules are integrated and tested to ensure the entire system works as expected
What happens during the Deployment stage of the waterfall model? | The product is released to the market or handed to the client, which may involve installation, customisation, and training
What happens during the Maintenance stage of the waterfall model? | The system receives regular updates to fix bugs, improve performance, or add new features
What is the TELOS feasibility study used for in the waterfall model? | To evaluate the feasibility of a project during the analysis stage across five dimensions: Technical, Economic, Legal, Operational, and Scheduling
What does the T in TELOS stand for? | Technical — is the project possible given the technology available and accessible?
What does the E in TELOS stand for? | Economic — can the project be financed in the short-term and long-term?
What does the L in TELOS stand for? | Legal — can the project be completed within the law?
What does the O in TELOS stand for? | Operational — can the project be successfully implemented and maintained?
What does the S in TELOS stand for? | Scheduling — can the project be completed given the time available?
Give one benefit of the waterfall model related to simplicity | It is simple and linear, making it easy to understand and follow, with each stage progressing linearly to the next
Give one benefit of the waterfall model related to tracking progress | Each phase has specific deliverables and milestones, making progress easy to measure
Why is the waterfall model considered well-documented? | Because each phase must be completed with clear deliverables before the next begins, encouraging thorough documentation at each stage
Give one drawback of the waterfall model related to flexibility | It is inflexible — changes are difficult to implement once the project has started, as the model does not easily allow revisiting previous stages
Explain why fixing problems late in the waterfall model is expensive | If a problem appears in a later stage, programmers must revisit all stages between the current one and the stage where the change is needed, making it costly and time-consuming
Why do users have limited input in the waterfall model? | Users are only involved at the very beginning (analysis) and end (evaluation) of the lifecycle, with no involvement during intermediate stages
The waterfall model has no built-in [...] stage | risk analysis
What type of projects is the waterfall model most suitable for? | Projects where requirements are well understood and unlikely to change, and where high quality and compliance are essential
What is extreme programming (XP)? | A type of Agile software development methodology that promotes adaptability and high customer involvement
An Agile methodology that promotes adaptability and high customer involvement — what is this called? | Extreme programming (XP)
What is Agile development? | A collection of methodologies that aim to improve the flexibility of software development and adapt to changes in user requirements faster
A collection of methodologies aimed at improving flexibility and adapting to changing user requirements — what term describes this? | Agile development
What are user stories in Agile development? | System requirements specified by the end-user, typically written in the format "As a user, I want to..." and used when designing the program
Requirements written as "As a user, I want to..." — what are these called? | User stories
What is a sprint in Agile development? | A short time-boxed development period, usually lasting 1-4 weeks, during which a set of tasks or features is completed
A short time-boxed development period of 1-4 weeks in Agile — what is this called? | A sprint
What happens during Sprint Planning? | Requirements are broken down into tasks, a set of features is chosen for the current sprint, and the sprint goal is defined
What type of design approach does Agile/XP favour? | Simple and adaptable design, rather than heavy upfront documentation
What is pair programming in XP? | A development practice where code is written by one programmer and critiqued by the other simultaneously, aiming to produce higher quality code
Explain why pair programming produces high-quality code | One person writes the code while the other reviews and critiques it in real time, so errors are caught and improvements made as the code is written
What types of testing are performed during an Agile sprint? | Unit testing, integration testing, and acceptance testing — all performed continuously during the sprint, not saved for the end
What happens during a Sprint Review? | Working software is demonstrated to stakeholders, and feedback is collected to identify changes or improvements
What happens during a Sprint Retrospective? | The team reflects on what went well and what needs improving, focusing on team performance and process optimisation
In XP, programmers work no longer than [...] hours a week | forty (to ensure quality is not compromised)
What is a 'working version' in XP? | Each iteration through the development cycle generates a version of the program that could function as the final product
In Agile, what is prioritised over documentation? | User satisfaction
Give one benefit of Agile/XP related to adaptability | It can quickly respond to changes in requirements, even late in development
Give one benefit of Agile/XP related to quality | It emphasises technical excellence and good design, with continuous testing throughout the development cycle
Give one drawback of Agile related to team requirements | It requires experienced and skilled team members and can be challenging to implement without knowledgeable developers
Give one drawback of Agile related to documentation | The focus on adaptability and immediate coding may result in insufficient documentation
What is scope creep? | Uncontrolled changes in requirements that can occur due to the flexible nature of methodologies like Agile and RAD
Uncontrolled expansion of project requirements beyond the original plan — what term describes this? | Scope creep
What type of projects is XP most suitable for? | Small to medium-sized projects where requirements can change and customer involvement is high
What is the spiral model? | A software development methodology that combines aspects of both iterative (Agile) and sequential (waterfall) processes, with a focus on risk management
A software development methodology that combines iterative and sequential approaches with a focus on risk management — what model is this? | The spiral model
What are the four stages of the spiral model? | Planning, Risk Analysis, Engineering (development/testing/implementation), and Evaluation and feedback
What happens during the Planning stage of the spiral model? | The objectives, alternatives, and constraints for the current phase of the project are defined
What happens during the Risk Analysis stage of the spiral model? | Potential risks are identified and assessed, and mitigation strategies are planned
What happens during the Engineering stage of the spiral model? | The next version of the product is developed, including design, coding, testing, and integration
What happens during the Evaluation and Feedback stage of the spiral model? | Progress is reviewed with stakeholders, and the next iteration is planned
How does the spiral model progress through its stages? | The process repeats (spirals) through Planning, Risk Analysis, Engineering, and Evaluation, with each loop representing a development phase until the final product is ready
What happens if the project is found to be too risky at any point in the spiral model? | The project is terminated
Give one benefit of the spiral model related to risk | It emphasises risk assessment and mitigation, helping to identify and address issues early in development
Give one benefit of the spiral model related to customer involvement | It encourages feedback and input from clients throughout the development process
Give one benefit of the spiral model related to deliverables | It provides early partial working solutions (prototypes), enabling early usage and feedback
Give one drawback of the spiral model related to complexity | It can be more complex and harder to manage compared to other methodologies
Give one drawback of the spiral model related to cost | It is expensive because it often requires hiring specialist risk assessors and involves constant prototyping
Why is the spiral model not suitable for small projects? | The extensive planning and risk management processes are overkill for simple or small-scale projects, adding unnecessary cost and complexity
What type of projects is the spiral model most suitable for? | Large, complex, risk-intensive projects with a high budget where requirements may change
What is Rapid Application Development (RAD)? | A software development methodology that emphasises fast and iterative development using partially functioning prototypes
A methodology that emphasises fast, iterative development with prototypes continually built upon — what is this called? | Rapid Application Development (RAD)
What are the five stages of RAD? | Requirement Planning, User Design and Prototyping, Construction (iterative development), Cutover (deployment), and Maintenance and Updates
What happens during the User Design and Prototyping stage of RAD? | Developers collaborate with users to develop prototypes, ensuring alignment with user needs
How are user requirements initially gathered in RAD? | Using focus groups to gather requirements, which are then used to develop an initial incomplete prototype for the user to trial
What happens during the Construction stage of RAD? | The system is built incrementally with continuous user feedback and adaptation
What happens during the Cutover stage of RAD? | The product is transitioned into the live environment, including user training, support, and documentation
Give one benefit of RAD related to speed | It enables rapid development and delivery of a high-quality system at a relatively low investment cost
Give one benefit of RAD related to user involvement | Clients are involved throughout the development process, ensuring the system aligns with user needs and expectations
Give one drawback of RAD related to quality | The focus on speed might lead to skipping rigorous testing or documentation, impacting overall quality
Give one drawback of RAD related to code efficiency | As requirements change over the course of the project, additions and changes made to the code may be inefficient
What type of projects is RAD most suitable for? | Small to medium, low-budget projects where rapid delivery is required and requirements can be developed and refined on the go
How does Agile differ from the waterfall model in terms of structure? | Agile is iterative with repeated cycles of planning, building, testing, and reviewing; waterfall is sequential with each phase completed once before the next begins
How does the spiral model differ from Agile in terms of primary focus? | The spiral model focuses primarily on risk analysis and mitigation at each iteration; Agile focuses on adaptability and customer collaboration
How does RAD differ from the spiral model in terms of cost and scale? | RAD is suited to small-to-medium, low-budget projects with short timeframes; the spiral model is suited to large, high-budget, risk-intensive projects
How does documentation compare between the waterfall model and Agile? | Waterfall produces thorough documentation due to its structured sequential phases; Agile produces poorer documentation because priority is given to user satisfaction and adaptability over documentation
How does user involvement compare between the waterfall model and Agile/XP? | In waterfall, users are only involved at the beginning (analysis) and end (evaluation); in Agile/XP, users are involved continuously throughout development
What is alpha testing? | Testing carried out in-house by the software development teams within the company to find and fix bugs
Testing carried out internally by the development team to find and fix bugs — what type of testing is this? | Alpha testing
What is beta testing? | Testing carried out by end-users after alpha testing has been completed; feedback from users informs the next stage of development
Testing carried out by end-users after alpha testing, with feedback used to inform development — what type of testing is this? | Beta testing
What is white box testing? | A form of testing carried out by development teams where the test plan is based on the internal structure of the program, and all possible routes through the program are tested
Testing based on the internal structure of the program where all possible routes are tested — what type of testing is this? | White box testing
What is black box testing? | A form of testing where the software is tested without knowledge of its internal structure, tracing inputs and outputs; can be done internally or by end-users
Testing without knowledge of the internal structure, focusing on inputs and outputs — what type of testing is this? | Black box testing
How does white box testing differ from black box testing? | White box testing is based on the program's internal structure and tests all possible routes; black box testing is done without knowledge of internal structure and traces inputs/outputs only
What is the purpose of the Analysis stage in a software development lifecycle? | Stakeholders state their requirements, and this information is used to clearly define the problem and the system requirements
What is the purpose of the Evaluation stage in the waterfall lifecycle? | The effectiveness of the software is evaluated against the system requirements defined at analysis, considering criteria such as robustness, reliability, portability, and maintainability
What four criteria are used to evaluate software after implementation? | Robustness, reliability, portability, and maintainability
What is an algorithm? | A set of instructions used to solve a problem
What are the key qualities of a good algorithm? | Clearly defined inputs, always produces valid output for defined input, handles invalid inputs, always reaches a stopping condition, is well-documented, and is well-commented
In the waterfall model, a [...] study evaluates whether the project should proceed | feasibility
How does XP differ from general Agile in terms of team structure? | In XP, the development team specifically consists of a pair of programmers working alongside a representative end-user; general Agile does not mandate this specific structure
Which software development methodology has no built-in risk analysis? | Waterfall
Which software development methodology is built around managing risk-heavy projects? | The spiral model
Both Agile and RAD can suffer from [...] due to their flexible nature | scope creep
In the Development stage of a generic SDLC, how is the project structured? | The design is used to split the project into individual, self-contained modules, which are allocated to teams for programming
