What is an input device? | A hardware component that allows users to input data or commands into a computer system for processing
A hardware component used to put data and information into a computer — what is this called? | An input device
What is an output device? | A hardware component that receives information from a computer and presents it to the user in a comprehensible form
A hardware component that presents processed data from a computer to the user — what is this called? | An output device
What is a keyboard used for? | Entering text and commands into a computer by pressing keys
What does a mouse allow a user to do? | Navigate the computer screen, click on items, and perform other functions
What does a touchscreen allow a user to do? | Interact with a device by touching the screen directly
What does a microphone capture? | Audio input, used for voice commands, recording audio, or video conferencing
What is a scanner used for? | Digitising physical documents or images into a format a computer can process
What does a barcode reader do? | Scans barcodes, typically used in retail and inventory management
What is a graphics tablet used for? | Allows artists and designers to draw or sketch directly onto a computer, useful for graphic design and 3D modelling
What are biometric devices used for? | Verifying a user's identity for security purposes (e.g. fingerprint scanners, facial recognition systems)
What does a magnetic stripe reader do? | Reads data from magnetic stripes on cards like credit and debit cards
Give an example of a device that is both an input and output device | A touchscreen — it outputs visual information like a monitor while also accepting touch input
What does a monitor do? | Displays visual output from the computer, including text, images, and videos
What does a printer do? | Produces a hard copy of digital documents or images
What does a projector do? | Projects the computer's display onto a large screen or wall
What is a Braille display used for? | Outputting information in Braille, allowing visually impaired users to read text from the computer
What is a plotter used for? | Printing large, high-quality diagrams and designs, often used in engineering or architecture
What does a VR headset provide? | An immersive visual and audio output, primarily used for gaming and virtual simulations
Name four factors to consider when choosing the right input or output device | User needs, user skills, environment, and cost
Why might a wireless mouse be preferred in an office but not a public computer lab? | A wireless mouse suits a clutter-free office, but a wired mouse is better for a public lab to prevent theft
What are the three types of secondary storage? | Magnetic, optical, and flash
A type of storage that magnetises particles on a disk or tape — what is this? | Magnetic storage
A type of storage that uses a laser to burn pits into the surface of a disc — what is this? | Optical storage
A type of storage that stores data in flash memory cells using silicon semiconductors — what is this? | Flash storage
How do magnetic storage devices store data? | By magnetising particles on a disk or tape, using two magnetic states (polarised and unpolarised) to represent binary 1 and 0
Name two benefits of magnetic storage | High storage capacity and relatively low cost per gigabyte
Name two drawbacks of magnetic storage | Slower read/write speeds compared to flash, and susceptible to physical damage due to moving parts
How do optical storage devices store data? | A laser burns pits into the surface of the disc; pits scatter light (representing 0) and lands reflect light (representing 1), written in spiral tracks
Name two benefits of optical storage | Durable and relatively immune to environmental conditions, and easy to transport
Name two drawbacks of optical storage | Lower storage capacity compared to other types, and can be easily scratched or damaged
How does flash storage store data? | Using silicon semiconductors forming NAND and NOR logic gates to store electrical charge in one of two states (high or low) representing binary values
Name two benefits of flash storage | Fast read/write speeds and no moving parts (less likely to fail from physical shock)
Name two drawbacks of flash storage | Higher cost per gigabyte and flash memory cells can wear out after a certain number of write cycles
What is the typical consumer-grade storage capacity of a hard disk drive (HDD)? | 500 GB to 5 TB
How does a hard disk drive (HDD) work? | Rotating magnetic platters spin at high speed under a read/write head on an actuating arm, with multiple platters stacked to maximise capacity
Why are HDDs susceptible to physical damage? | They contain brittle platters and moving parts, making them delicate and vulnerable to shocks or falls
What is the typical consumer-grade storage capacity of a solid-state drive (SSD)? | 120 GB to 4 TB
Why are SSDs more durable than HDDs? | SSDs have no moving parts, making them much more resistant to damage from physical shock
What is the main disadvantage of SSDs? | Higher cost per gigabyte compared to magnetic or optical storage
Explain why SSDs have a limited lifespan | Every time a page is written to, the voltage required to write to it increases; over time this required voltage becomes too high for the page to be written to
What are typical storage capacities for USB flash drives? | 8 GB to 256 GB (common sizes)
What is the typical storage capacity of a CD? | 700 MB
What is the typical storage capacity of a DVD? | 4.7 GB to 9 GB
What is the typical storage capacity of a Blu-ray disc? | 25 GB to 50 GB
What does CD stand for? | Compact disc
What does DVD stand for? | Digital versatile disc (or digital video disc)
What is the difference between CD-R, CD-RW, and CD-ROM? | CD-R can only be written to once; CD-RW can be written to more than once; CD-ROM is written during production and can then only be read from
How does a Blu-ray disc compare to a DVD in storage capacity? | A Blu-ray disc has more than five times the storage capacity of a traditional DVD
Which logic gate type is preferred for storing small quantities of data in flash memory (e.g. code)? | NOR
Which logic gate type is preferred for storing larger files in flash memory (e.g. photos and videos)? | NAND
How does flash storage differ from magnetic and optical in terms of cost? | Flash memory is generally more expensive per gigabyte than magnetic and optical storage
How does flash memory differ from RAM? | Both use electronic storage, but RAM is volatile (loses data when power is lost) while flash memory is non-volatile (retains data without power)
Name four factors to consider when choosing a storage device | Storage needs (capacity), performance needs (speed), portability, and cost
What is virtual storage (cloud storage)? | A model where digital data is stored across multiple servers and locations, typically hosted by third parties, and accessed over the internet
Data stored remotely on third-party servers and accessed via the internet — what is this called? | Virtual storage (cloud storage)
Name two examples of virtual storage services | Google Drive and Microsoft OneDrive
How does virtual storage work? | Data is transmitted over the internet to a data centre where servers store, secure, and back up the data, making it accessible from anywhere with an internet connection
Virtual storage is often an abstraction of what? | Multiple drives (hundreds of HDDs or SSDs) formatted to act as a single piece of storage
Name two benefits of virtual storage | Scalability (easy to increase capacity) and accessibility (data can be accessed from anywhere with an internet connection)
Name two drawbacks of virtual storage | Dependence on internet connection (access can be slow or impossible) and security concerns (storing sensitive data off-site poses a risk)
Explain why virtual storage provides better data protection than local storage alone | Virtual storage uses data redundancy across multiple servers, protecting against data loss from hardware failure at any single location
How does virtual storage differ from virtual memory? | Virtual storage is data stored remotely on the cloud; virtual memory uses secondary storage as an extension of RAM when RAM is full
What is RAM (Random Access Memory)? | A type of fast primary memory used to store data and programs the computer is currently using
A type of volatile primary memory that stores data currently being used by the computer — what is this? | RAM (Random Access Memory)
What does volatile mean in the context of memory? | The memory loses its contents when the computer is turned off or loses power
Why is RAM called "Random Access" Memory? | The processor can access any part of the RAM equally fast, regardless of location
Why does more RAM improve a system's performance? | More RAM allows the system to process more data simultaneously, reducing the need to swap data to slower secondary storage
Why is RAM more expensive per gigabyte than secondary storage? | RAM provides much higher access speeds than secondary storage, and this performance comes at a greater cost per unit of storage
What is ROM (Read-Only Memory)? | A type of non-volatile primary memory that stores fixed data (such as the BIOS/bootstrap routine) which can be read but not modified
A non-volatile memory that stores the BIOS or firmware and cannot be written to after manufacture — what is this? | ROM (Read-Only Memory)
What does non-volatile mean in the context of memory? | The memory retains its contents even when the computer is turned off or loses power
What does ROM typically store? | The BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) or firmware used to start up the computer and initialise hardware components
What is the bootstrap routine? | A fixed sequence of startup instructions stored in ROM that initialises the computer's hardware and loads the operating system
How does RAM differ from ROM in terms of volatility? | RAM is volatile (loses data when power is lost); ROM is non-volatile (retains data without power)
How does RAM differ from ROM in terms of read/write ability? | RAM can be both read from and written to; ROM can only be read from (not modified)
How does RAM differ from ROM in terms of purpose? | RAM stores data and programs currently being used by the computer; ROM stores fixed startup instructions (BIOS/firmware) needed to boot the computer
Why is ROM essential for a computer system? | It stores the bootstrap routine needed to start up the computer and initialise hardware, and this data must be preserved even when power is off
What are RAM and ROM both examples of? | Primary storage (main memory), as opposed to secondary storage devices like HDDs and SSDs
Why is RAM used as temporary storage rather than for personal files? | Because RAM is volatile, it loses all data when power is lost, so it cannot reliably store files long-term
