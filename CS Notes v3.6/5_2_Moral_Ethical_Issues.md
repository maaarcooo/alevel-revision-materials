# 5.2 Moral and Ethical Issues

## Morals and Ethics

**Ethics** concern our values as a community and how decisions impact different groups in society. **Morals** relate to a personal code of conduct, encompassing how individuals choose to behave, including decisions made at the expense of others.

With computers integrated into almost every aspect of daily life, it is essential to consider the **moral, ethical, environmental, social and cultural** implications of digital technology. The specification requires discussion of these across nine topic areas.

## Computers in the Workforce

### Advantages of Computerisation

Computers improve **efficiency** in the workplace. Amazon uses warehouse robots to package and transfer goods between stations, and Effidence's PostBot navigates neighbourhoods to deliver parcels door-to-door. These technologies reduce delivery times, speed up manufacturing, improve customer satisfaction, and lower unit labour costs, which feeds through as lower consumer prices. Computerised roles also **reduce strain** on workers and remove the need to work in repetitive, tedious environments.

With the internet now widely accessible, many services are offered **exclusively online**, such as online shopping and banking. This reduces the cost of renting physical premises and cuts labour costs because fewer front-facing retail staff and bank clerks are needed. Meanwhile, demand for **delivery drivers** and **web developers** has grown.

### Disadvantages and Risks

Computerisation has caused **structural unemployment**, where workers' existing skills no longer match available jobs. Middle-income manufacturing jobs have been hit hardest, with entire production lines replaced by robots, producing a shift toward low-income service jobs. More recently, automation has extended beyond manufacturing to roles such as library assistants and taxi drivers, suggesting a broader range of jobs is at risk than the repetitive roles commonly associated with automation.

Governments must invest in **retraining programmes** so displaced workers can change occupation. The question remains whether retraining is sufficient to counteract the scale of computerisation.

A major downside of a highly computerised workplace is **high dependence** on technology. If something goes wrong, there can be a significant loss of output.

### New Roles and Skills

Increased computerisation has raised demand for people in **computing-related occupations** such as software developers (for embedded systems in smart devices) and network engineers (to maintain networks in schools and businesses). Employers increasingly expect workers to use computers confidently and productively.

### Broader Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Implications

**Moral:** The use of computers raises concerns about **data privacy** and surveillance. Personal data can be used in ways individuals did not anticipate or consent to, and data breaches can cause identity theft and financial loss. **Cybersecurity** measures are essential but may themselves infringe on privacy through network monitoring.

**Social:** Increasing reliance on computers exacerbates the **digital divide**, creating inequality between those with and without access to technology. This can hinder opportunities for education, employment, and economic advancement. Computer-mediated communication may reduce face-to-face interaction and interpersonal skills. Remote work can blur boundaries between **work and personal life**.

**Ethical:** Automation raises concerns about **job displacement** and loss of livelihood. **Algorithmic bias** in hiring, lending, and criminal justice can perpetuate discrimination. The ease of digital copying raises questions about **intellectual property** rights and fair compensation for creators, with digital piracy causing significant revenue loss.

**Cultural:** The digital divide can result in unequal access to **cultural resources** and online information, affecting cultural preservation and identity. Online content can unintentionally reinforce **cultural stereotypes**. Content disseminated globally should be **localised** to the cultural context of the audience, including language translation and culturally appropriate symbols.

## Automated Decision Making

**Automated decision making** is the process of using algorithms and computer systems to make decisions without direct human intervention, based on data analysis, pattern recognition, and predefined rules.

### Applications

| Domain | How automated decision making is used |
|---|---|
| Social media | Algorithms determine what users see on their feeds based on interactions, interests, and connections |
| Criminal justice | The COMPAS system in the US assesses a defendant's likelihood of reoffending using undisclosed factors to generate a risk score |
| Loan/mortgage/job applications | Algorithms assess suitability based on financial history, qualifications, and other data |
| Driverless cars | Algorithmic decisions are made faster than human reaction times, potentially saving lives |
| Stock market trading | Algorithms analyse past trends to model future changes and execute trades at high frequency |
| Plant automation and power distribution | Inputs are collected and analysed to respond instantly to changes, e.g. increasing power supply when demand is forecast to rise |
| Credit scoring | Algorithms analyse credit history, income, and other data to assess repayment risk |
| Fraud detection | Transaction patterns and user behaviour are analysed to flag suspicious activities in real time |
| Recommendation systems | E-commerce and streaming platforms analyse user behaviour and preferences to suggest tailored content |
| Traffic management | Smart systems optimise traffic flow, detect congestion, and adjust signal timings using real-time data |
| Manufacturing and supply chain | Production schedules, inventory, and resource allocation are adjusted based on demand forecasts |
| Recruitment | Applicants are screened and ranked based on qualifications and compatibility with job requirements |
| Predictive maintenance | Sensor data from machines predicts potential failures and schedules proactive maintenance |

### Advantages

Automated decision making improves **productivity** and makes application processes more convenient. Companies can screen candidates faster for desired qualities before the interview stage. In driverless cars, decisions can be made faster than humans can react. In stock trading, plant automation, and power distribution, rapid analysis of inputs enables instant responses to changing conditions.

### Risks and Ethical Concerns

**Bias and fairness:** Algorithms can maintain biases present in their training data, leading to **discriminatory outcomes**. Amazon abandoned an AI recruitment tool because it was biased against women: trained on 10 years of predominantly male applicant data, the system taught itself that male candidates were preferable and began penalising CVs containing the word "women."

**Lack of contextual understanding:** Relying entirely on algorithms can result in people being treated unfairly because algorithms do not consider **extenuating circumstances** and cannot process information with the same consideration of contextual factors as humans.

**Filter bubbles:** On social media, solely reinforcing users' interests creates a **dangerous bubble** in which their beliefs are never challenged, leading to a close-minded society.

**Accountability:** When AI makes errors, determining responsibility is challenging, especially if the decision-making process is opaque. The **"trolley problem"** dilemma arises with driverless cars: how should the algorithm decide who is harmed when an accident is unavoidable, and who bears responsibility for that decision?

**Transparency:** Algorithms must not remain a **"black box."** Decisions must be justified, and some level of human input is essential to ensure fairness. Algorithms must be thoroughly tested, provided with factually correct data, and recognised as designed by human developers who may themselves carry biases.

**Informed consent:** Automated decisions can significantly impact lives. Ethical considerations arise regarding obtaining consent from affected individuals, especially if they are unaware a decision about them has been made algorithmically.

**Cultural sensitivity:** Automated decisions may not adequately account for cultural nuances and preferences, potentially clashing with cultural values. AI algorithms can perpetuate cultural biases and fail to represent certain cultural perspectives.

## Artificial Intelligence

**Artificial intelligence (AI)** is a field of computer science that aims to create intelligent machines that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence. AI systems simulate human cognitive functions such as **learning, problem-solving, reasoning, perception, and decision-making**, enabling them to analyse complex data, adapt to new situations, and improve performance over time.

### Expert Systems

**Expert systems** (also called **knowledge-based systems**) replicate the knowledge and experience of a human expert in a particular subject. They consist of:

- A **knowledge base**: a set of facts and rules about the domain.
- An **inference engine**: uses the knowledge base to derive conclusions, such as medical diagnoses.

Expert systems are used in medicine to draw connections between illnesses and form diagnoses.

### Neural Networks

**Neural networks** replicate biological neural networks. They "learn" from a training data set and apply that knowledge to new data, in the same way a human generalises from experience. Applications include **pattern detection** and identifying **financial fraud**.

### Voice Recognition and Smart Home

AI powers **voice recognition systems** in smart home devices such as Google Home and Amazon's Alexa. These increase convenience but raise **privacy concerns** because they must be constantly switched on to function, continuously listening for activation commands.

### Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Impacts

**Moral:** AI developers face dilemmas about the potential consequences of their creations, including accountability, privacy, job displacement, and social inequality. They must ensure AI systems are designed to **prioritise ethical values, uphold human rights, and avoid biases**. End users encounter concerns about personal data privacy, security, and informed consent. Users must be provided with clear information about how their data is used, who accesses it, and the associated risks, though the technical complexity of AI often makes this difficult.

**Social:** AI adoption may change employment structures, with some jobs automated, leading to unemployment or role shifts. **Reskilling and upskilling** initiatives are necessary. The accessibility of AI technologies may create a **digital divide**, favouring those with more resources. In healthcare, AI can improve diagnostics and treatment, but incorrect diagnoses or recommendations in critical situations can have severe consequences. Determining the extent of **human oversight** and final decision-making authority is a key concern. Liability in the event of adverse AI-driven outcomes is complex: is the AI system, its developers, or the treating physician responsible?

**Ethical:** AI algorithms may show **bias if trained on biased datasets**, leading to discrimination. Autonomous systems like self-driving cars raise ethical dilemmas about prioritising safety and choosing who is harmed in unavoidable accidents. The collection and use of vast amounts of data by AI systems raises concerns about data privacy, consent, and potential misuse.

**Cultural:** Language models and translation tools can unintentionally uphold **cultural biases** or inaccurately represent specific languages and dialects. The use of AI in art and creative work sparks debate about the **authenticity and originality** of AI-generated works. Artists have raised concerns about their work being used without consent to train AI systems, leading to lawsuits against firms like Stability AI. AI can help preserve cultural heritage through digitisation and restoration, but **cultural sensitivity** and respect for indigenous knowledge must be considered. UNESCO produced the first global standard on AI ethics in November 2021, adopted by all 193 member states.

### Accountability and Sentience

AI raises fundamental questions about **accountability**: who is responsible when things go wrong? If AI ever reaches a stage at which it is considered sentient, what rights should it have?

## Environmental Effects

### E-Waste

With devices produced cheaply and widely, the pressure of keeping up with trends and their affordability means people throw away more devices than before. Some computer components contain **mercury, radioactive isotopes**, and other toxic substances (lead, cadmium) that can **contaminate water supplies**. This **e-waste** is often shipped to developing countries with lower environmental standards to be disposed of, which is widely considered immoral and unacceptable.

**Proper e-waste disposal** involves: reducing consumption and reusing devices where possible, recycling to recover valuable materials, using manufacturer take-back programmes, disposing at designated facilities equipped to handle toxic materials safely, following local regulations, and erasing personal data before disposal.

### Energy Consumption

More devices and increasingly computerised processes require **more electricity**. This consumes non-renewable **fossil fuels**, which emit **greenhouse gases** contributing to **global warming**. The effects on the planet will impact future generations and biodiversity.

### Environmentally-Friendly Technologies

There has been a push in the UK for **renewable energy** which counteracts increased electricity consumption to an extent. Examples of eco-friendly technology include: **smart home systems** using temperature sensors to control heating and motion sensors to switch off lights in empty rooms, **Sleep and Stand-by** features on computers and laptops, and car engines designed to prevent idling, reducing emissions. Technology has the potential to offset some of its own environmental impact.

### Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Implications

**Moral:** Proper e-waste disposal and energy-efficient usage is a moral duty to protect the planet for future generations. The impact of environmental effects is **not evenly distributed**: lower-income communities may bear a disproportionate burden of e-waste pollution (**social justice**).

**Social:** **Environmental equity** is the fair distribution of environmental benefits and burdens among different social groups. Unequal access to energy-efficient technologies and recycling facilities can worsen social disparities, contributing to the **digital divide**.

**Ethical:** Responsible stewardship requires adopting sustainable practices, including reducing e-waste and minimising energy consumption. Organisations should be held **accountable** for their environmental practices with transparency about their environmental impact. **Environmental justice** concerns arise when specific communities suffer disproportionately from negative environmental effects.

**Cultural:** Cultures prioritising environmental preservation and sustainability may be at odds with practices contributing to e-waste and energy-intensive computing. The increasing **digitalisation of cultural artefacts** raises questions about preserving cultural identity and heritage.

## Censorship and the Internet

**Censorship** is the act of suppressing or controlling the content that people are able to view, publish, and access. It involves methods used by governments, organisations, or ISPs to restrict access to specific websites, content, or online platforms.

### Reasons for Censorship

**Protecting national security:** Governments censor content to prevent the spread of sensitive information threatening national security. In the UK, ISPs block websites with content associated with **terrorism and extremist political beliefs**.

**Restricting harmful content:** Censorship restricts hate speech, encouragement of violence, or exploitation of children.

**Preserving cultural norms:** Some countries censor content contradicting their cultural or religious beliefs to preserve traditional values.

**Combating disinformation:** Censorship can counter the spread of false or misleading information.

### The Free Internet Debate

Many people believe in a **"Free Internet"** where nothing is filtered, in line with the principle of freedom of speech. Tim Berners-Lee created the internet to be a space where people could share and mutually benefit from resources. However, most agree that some extent of censorship is necessary for national security and to filter offensive comments and extremist propaganda.

There is fear that censorship may be used to **block alternative political beliefs**, at which point it would not be acting to protect the country but rather to push a certain ideology, which many consider unethical.

### Censorship at Different Levels

Censorship operates at different scales: within **schools** to prevent pupils accessing unsuitable material, and within **workplaces** to maintain productivity and prevent distractions.

### Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Implications

**Moral:** Freedom of expression is a fundamental human right at the core of democratic societies. Safeguarding this freedom must be balanced against protecting **vulnerable communities** from harmful content that may incite violence, promote discrimination, or perpetuate oppression. Those responsible for censorship must exercise power **transparently and accountably** to prevent abuse.

**Social:** When information is controlled or suppressed, it can hinder the growth of an informed and engaged society. Censorship measures can exacerbate the **digital divide**, widening the gap in access to information for marginalised communities. Balancing the regulation of harmful content with fostering **social cohesion** and diverse perspectives is essential.

**Ethical:** Ethical concerns arise when censorship relies on invasive **surveillance and monitoring** of online activities, challenging the right to privacy. Transparency and accountability in content removal decisions are essential to prevent abuses of power. Individuals have a right to access **accurate and diverse information** for informed decision-making.

**Cultural:** Censorship can restrict the free flow of diverse cultural perspectives online, impacting **cultural expression**. It can also hinder **cross-cultural dialogue** and intercultural understanding. Balancing content moderation with cultural preservation is essential to respect diverse communities.

### Case Studies

A study identified the UAE as the world's most censored country on the internet, surpassing North Korea, Iran, China, and Russia, with nearly half of its internet users employing VPNs to access restricted content. In China, over 66,000 rules govern content on search engines, with all major online platforms subject to extensive legal restrictions.

## Monitoring Behaviour

Computers are used to **monitor people's behaviour** in various environments. Monitoring includes CCTV surveillance, tracking phone calls, GPS monitoring, and email monitoring, involving the systematic observation and recording of individuals' activities, interactions, and communications.

### Reasons for Monitoring

**Security and crime prevention:** CCTV cameras are widely used for security purposes and to detect crime, from speeding to violence. This is useful for tracing and punishing criminal activity.

**Public safety and deterrence:** Visible surveillance measures like CCTV can act as a **deterrent** to criminal activities in public spaces.

**Investigative purposes:** Monitoring phone calls and emails may be used by law enforcement to gather evidence and intelligence about criminal activities.

**Emergency response:** GPS monitoring is invaluable in emergency situations, enabling swift location of individuals needing assistance, such as in missing person cases or during disasters.

**Employee monitoring:** Employers monitor productivity by tracking the websites and applications workers access and the time spent on them. Email monitoring and phone tracking can assess adherence to company policies.

**Ankle monitors** are used on people under house arrest to track their location. Some argue this contravenes basic human rights, while others argue it is a necessary measure to deter crime.

### Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Implications

**Moral:** Monitoring technologies can infringe on individuals' right to **privacy**, raising questions about the balance between security and personal freedom. Monitoring may challenge **individual autonomy**, leading to feelings of being constantly watched. The moral implications depend on the **intent** behind monitoring and whether clear, justifiable reasons exist.

**Social:** Extensive monitoring can erode public **trust in institutions**. Mass surveillance may lead to social norms that promote **self-censorship** and fear of expressing dissenting opinions. Marginalised communities may be **disproportionately affected**, leading to discrimination and mistrust.

**Ethical:** Ethical practices require robust **data security** measures to protect sensitive information. Monitoring demands **transparency** about purposes and methods of data collection, and **accountability** for its use. Data collection should be **minimised** and limited to intended purposes only.

**Cultural:** Monitoring should respect **cultural values** and traditions, ensuring data collection does not undermine cultural norms. It may also be used to protect cultural heritage from exploitation. Ethical monitoring should be **inclusive**, embracing diverse cultural perspectives and avoiding bias.

### Case Study

An employee was ordered to repay money to her former employer after tracking software (TimeCamp) found she had falsely reported her work hours. Around 20% of companies have admitted to using or planning to use surveillance technology.

## Analysing Personal Information

Companies have recognised the value of data, which can reveal key insights about people and their behaviours.

### Methods of Analysis

**Big data** refers to large amounts of data from multiple sources. **Data mining** is the process of analysing big data to extract patterns, make inferences about people's behaviour, likes and dislikes, and identify previously unknown connections between variables. Data mining can be used to inform **personalised political propaganda**, **targeted advertising**, or to discover new correlations.

**Machine learning** algorithms can predict outcomes, assess risks, and personalise plans (e.g. patient treatment) based on past data. **AI** can process large datasets and make predictions or recommendations, such as analysing medical images to detect diseases.

### Risks and Concerns

Insights from personal data could reveal information that acts **against certain individuals**. For example, data mining might uncover that a person is at high risk of developing a particular disease. An insurance company could use this to deny insurance or charge a higher premium. This raises ethical questions about whether companies have a **responsibility** to feed information back to users.

Although companies are legally required to state what kinds of data they collect, this may not always be obvious to users. The **Data Protection Act (1998)** is no longer sufficient for protecting public welfare, which is why the **GDPR** has been enforced since May 2018.

### Moral, Social, Ethical and Cultural Implications

**Moral:** Obtaining **informed consent** before gathering, storing, and analysing personal information is crucial. Consent respects individuals' autonomy. The benefits of analysis, such as improved healthcare outcomes, must be distributed **equitably** and not disproportionately favour specific groups.

**Social:** Personal information, particularly health data, is sensitive. Increased computer management of this data raises the risk of **privacy breaches**. The **digital divide** means those without access to digital technology may be inadvertently excluded from benefits such as improved healthcare.

**Ethical:** There is an ethical obligation to protect data from breaches, theft, or misuse through robust **data security**. Organisations must be **transparent** about how personal information is used and held **accountable** for any misuse, requiring robust auditing mechanisms.

**Cultural:** The use of personal information should respect **cultural norms** and practices. Some cultures have specific beliefs about health and privacy. There is a risk that certain cultural groups may be **underrepresented** in datasets, leading to biased outcomes.

### Case Study

A Swedish study (Lund University) found that AI could read breast cancer screening images and detect cancer at a rate similar to two radiologists. Across over 80,000 women, AI-supported screening identified cancer in 244 women compared to 203 through standard screening, without increasing the rate of false positives.

## Piracy and Offensive Communications

### Piracy

**Piracy** is the unauthorised copying, distribution, or reproduction of copyrighted material, such as software or media including music, films, and eBooks. It is a form of theft and is illegal. The internet has made it easier than ever to source content illegally, but the decision whether to pirate is an individual moral choice.

**Types of piracy:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Software piracy | Unauthorised copying, distribution, or use of copyrighted software (counterfeiting, internet piracy, end-user piracy) |
| Music and film piracy | Unauthorised copying and distribution via peer-to-peer networks or illegal download websites |
| eBook piracy | Unauthorised distribution of digital books or creation of digital books from physically copied ones |

**Moral:** Piracy directly infringes intellectual property rights by using creative output without permission. However, piracy can also be seen as **democratising access** to content in regions where economic or geographic constraints make materials otherwise inaccessible.

**Social:** Piracy inflicts **financial losses** on creators and industries, potentially leading to job losses and slower economic development. It may also be viewed as encouraging knowledge sharing and contributing to open-source culture.

**Ethical:** The existence of piracy can highlight gaps in legal access to media, prompting discussion about more **affordable distribution models**. However, it fosters a culture of dishonesty and unfair practices, undermining ethical norms.

**Cultural:** Pirated content can increase **global exposure** for certain cultural products. However, creators from marginalised cultures who rely on sales for survival are disproportionately affected, endangering cultural preservation.

**Case studies:** In the late 1990s, Napster became a platform for sharing MP3 files. Metallica filed a lawsuit against Napster in 2000 for copyright infringement, marking a turning point in the fight against online piracy. Napster settled and shut down its free file-sharing system in 2001. Steve Jobs argued piracy was a **service issue**, not a pricing issue, leading to the creation of iTunes as a legal, user-friendly alternative. Sky has won High Court orders requiring ISPs to block services that illegally stream its football games and TV shows.

### Offensive Communications

**Offensive communication** refers to any sort of online harassment, including **cyber-bullying** or **stalking**. The internet provides a seemingly anonymous front for perpetrators.

The **Malicious Communications Act (1988)** makes it a criminal offence to send indecent or offensive messages to anyone online. Such behaviour can be traced by law enforcement and result in a criminal record.

## Layout, Colour Paradigms and Character Sets

These topics relate to how digital products (particularly websites) should be designed to be accessible and appropriate for a global, diverse audience.

### Layout

**User Experience (UX) design** is the process of creating products that provide meaningful and relevant experiences to users. Layout refers to the arrangement of elements (text, images, buttons, interactive elements) on a page or screen.

When designing websites, developers must consider **who will be viewing** the site and ensure it accommodates all users. The **Equality Act (2010)** makes it illegal to discriminate against providing a service to a certain group of people.

Websites must be laid out for easy **navigation**. In English-speaking countries, menus are displayed on the **left-hand side**. In countries where Arabic is the primary language (e.g. Egypt, UAE), menus may be on the **right-hand side** because Arabic is read right to left. Programs must support **bi-directional text** to accommodate both reading directions. For online stores, a well laid-out, easy-to-navigate website attracts more customers.

### Accessibility

Websites must be accessible to people with **visual impairments** or other disabilities:

- Users may need to **enlarge text** or **alter contrast** to view content comfortably.
- **Alternative text (alt text)** should be provided for images.
- A **screen magnifier** option should be available.
- **Transcripts** of audio files should be provided for those with hearing impairments.

### Colour Paradigms

Colour paradigms refer to the systematic use of colours to elicit specific responses from users. Developers must consider how **different colours are interpreted** across cultures:

| Colour | Western interpretation | Other cultural interpretations |
|---|---|---|
| White | Purity | Mourning (Middle East) |
| Red | Danger / urgency | Luck (Chinese culture) |
| Green | Luck, nature | Widely positive connotations globally |

A neutral colour scheme with widely positive connotations is typically chosen. Contrasting colours help highlight essential elements and improve **accessibility** for users with visual impairments. Consistent colour schemes enhance visual cohesion and align with brand identity.

### Character Sets

To make websites accessible to a global audience, content must be translated into multiple languages. Some **character sets** are too small to represent all characters of a language. **ASCII** uses only 7 bits and cannot represent all characters in languages like Chinese. **Unicode** is the preferred character set because it can represent over a million characters, supporting most of the world's writing systems.

Developers must consider language differences during program creation to ensure applications can be **localised** (adapted to different languages and regions without engineering changes).
