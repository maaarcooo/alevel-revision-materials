# 5.1 Computing Related Legislation

## The Data Protection Act 1998

The **Data Protection Act (DPA)** is a law that protects **personal data** from being misused. It applies to information stored both on computers and in organised paper filing systems.

**Personal data** is any data that can be used to identify a living person. Data that can be used in combination with other data to identify a living person also counts as personal data. Examples include name, address, date of birth, race, and religion.

An individual who can be identified by personal data is called the **data subject**.

### Exemptions

Not all data handling is covered by the DPA. Key exemptions include:

- **Domestic purposes** -- using personal data for private activities such as writing to friends and family or taking photographs for personal enjoyment.
- **Law enforcement** -- the police investigating a crime are not subject to the DPA. A suspect cannot request to see the evidence held about them.
- **Intelligence services processing** -- personal data processed by agencies such as MI5 is not covered by the DPA.

### The Eight Data Protection Principles

| Principle | Effect on a Company | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Personal data must be processed fairly and lawfully | Must be clear about what data is collected and what it will be used for | A school requests personal data to contact guardians in emergencies |
| 2. Personal data must be obtained for specified and lawful purposes | Cannot use data for purposes beyond what was originally stated, and cannot pass it on without permission | A company collects a phone number for delivery updates but then uses it for marketing |
| 3. Personal data must be adequate, relevant and not excessive | Cannot request data that is not needed for the stated purpose | A bank cannot ask about previous holidays when opening an account |
| 4. Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date | If data is wrong or outdated, the data subject has the right to have it corrected or deleted | A bank holding an old address cannot send current statements |
| 5. Personal data must not be kept longer than necessary | Must delete data once there is no longer a need for it | When a customer closes an account, the company must delete their data |
| 6. Personal data must be processed in line with data subjects' rights | Must provide all personal data held on a subject if requested | A hospital must provide a patient's full records if the patient requests them |
| 7. Personal data must be held securely | Must implement security measures (usernames, passwords) and backups to prevent accidental loss | Using encrypted cloud backups for external storage |
| 8. Personal data must not be transferred outside the European Economic Area unless that country ensures adequate data protection | Cannot send data to countries that have not been deemed by the European Commission to provide adequate protection | A company cannot transfer data to a country without equivalent data protection laws |

### Actions Companies Must Take

Companies face extremely large fines if found in breach of the DPA. They must:

- Appoint a **Data Controller** responsible for ensuring the principles are upheld and for maintaining communication with the **Information Commissioner**.
- Implement physical or digital security measures to prevent data being accessed without consent.
- Train staff to follow the Data Protection principles.
- Respond to a **Subject Access Request (SAR)** by sending a copy of the subject's data securely, after verifying the identity of the requester.

### Rights of Data Subjects

Under Data Protection legislation, data subjects have the right to:

- Be informed about the collection and use of their personal data
- Access their personal data and supplementary information
- Have inaccurate data rectified or incomplete data completed
- Be forgotten (erasure) in certain circumstances
- Restrict processing in certain circumstances
- **Data portability** -- obtain and reuse their personal data across different services
- Object to processing in certain circumstances
- Challenge automated decision making and profiling
- Withdraw consent at any time (where relevant)
- Complain to the Information Commissioner

### GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018

As of 2018, the Data Protection Act 1998 has been replaced by the **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** and the **Data Protection Act 2018**. OCR are aware that the law changes and will accept answers that reference the DPA 1998 or GDPR/DPA 2018.

### Case Study: British Airways

British Airways was fined £20 million by the UK's data protection authority after data security failings allowed unauthorised access to personal and payment card information for over 400,000 customers. Preventive measures BA could have taken include multi-factor authentication on employee logins, role-based access controls, effective security monitoring, and regular penetration testing.

### Examiner Tips

When asked to explain the principles, do not just state them. Explain what each principle means in practice. For example: "Personal data must be fairly and lawfully processed, which means companies must have a lawful reason for collecting personal information."

## The Computer Misuse Act 1990

The **Computer Misuse Act (CMA)** concerns the **malicious use of computers**. It was originally created to ensure that computer **hacking** was covered by law, and has been updated regularly to remain relevant.

Countermeasures that can be deployed to minimise threats from unauthorised access include **digital signatures** or certificates. **Firewalls** can prevent external access to systems and are key in preventing **DoS** (Denial of Service) or **DDoS** (Distributed Denial of Service) attacks.

### Three Primary Offences

| Offence | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Unauthorised access to computer materials | Accessing a computer system without permission | A student finds a teacher's password and opens their files |
| 2. Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences | Accessing a system without permission with the intention of committing another crime | A student finds a teacher's password and accesses their files intending to change their marks |
| 3. Unauthorised modification of computer material | Actually changing data or programs on a system without permission | A student finds a teacher's password, accesses their files, and increases their mark |

The consequences escalate from offence 1 to offence 3, with each offence punishable by imprisonment.

### Case Study: ICO Prosecution for Data Theft

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) successfully prosecuted two individuals for violating the CMA by stealing personal information to make scam calls. Kim Doyle, a former RAC employee, was found guilty of transferring personal data (road traffic accident data, names, phone numbers, registration numbers) to an accident claims firm without permission. She pleaded guilty to conspiracy to secure unauthorised access to computer data and selling unlawfully obtained personal data. Doyle and William Shaw received 8-month prison sentences suspended for 2 years.

### Worked Example: 9-Mark Discussion

**Question:** "The Computer Misuse Act means that computer users are criminalised for simply trying to learn how systems work." Discuss whether or not you agree with this statement.

**Approach:** Describe the CMA and its three offences. Apply to a scenario. Keep the argument balanced (argue both sides). Write a conclusion with your own opinion.

**Key arguments for the statement:** The CMA could be broken by, for example, accessing someone's email without permission, even if the motivation was curiosity rather than malice.

**Key arguments against:** Users can still investigate how computer systems work by obtaining authorisation first, or by creating virtual systems to experiment with. There are many online platforms and programs freely accessible that allow legal exploration without breaching the CMA. Users can easily educate themselves about the law and should understand the consequences of accessing systems without authorisation.

### Examiner Tips

When answering CMA questions, do not just describe the law in isolation. Relate it to the scenario given: How does the CMA affect the company? What are the consequences of the actions described? If no scenario is provided, construct your own.

## The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

This act protects the **intellectual property** of individuals and companies. It makes it illegal to copy, modify, or distribute software or other intellectual property without the relevant permission. The act covers everything from logos to music recordings, software, and videos.

If a work is **original**, **copyright automatically applies** from the moment of creation and does not expire until **25-70 years after the death of the creator**, depending on the type of work.

In 1992, the Act was extended to include **computer programs** as a type of literary work, meaning copyright automatically applies to code.

If an individual believes their work has been copied, it is **their responsibility** to take action under the Act.

Many sites online offer free downloads of copyrighted software and videos, which prevents the intellectual copyright holder from earning income on their work. The act also covers **peer-to-peer streaming** where it prevents a copyright owner from receiving income.

### Primary Breaches

These involve direct infringement of copyright:

- Copying an original work
- Issuing copies of the original work to the public
- Renting or lending copies of the original work to the public
- Performing, showing, or playing the original work in public
- Making an adaptation of the original work

### Secondary Breaches

These involve facilitating or enabling infringement:

- Importing a copy of original work
- Possessing or dealing with a copy of the original work
- Providing the means to make copies of the original work
- Permitting the use of premises for making copies of the original work
- Providing props or equipment for a performance of a copy of the original work

### Examiner Tips

Always specify that the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act covers **original work** and is **automatically applied** from the moment of creation, then explain the implications for distribution. For example: "The work was protected by the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act when it was created. Therefore if anyone wishes to distribute it they must gain the owner's permission; posting it online without permission would breach the act."

Use the act's full name (The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act). It is not called "the Copyright Act." Using a shortened version may cost marks.

## The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000

The **Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act (RIPA)** legislates how organisations can monitor **electronic communications**. It covers **investigation**, **surveillance**, and **interception** of communication by **public bodies**.

For example, MI6 can legally wiretap online conversations in the interests of national security provided that a **warrant** has been issued. Certain actions require government approval, and different organisations have different limits on what they can do (GCHQ has the most freedom).

### Obligations on ISPs and Companies

The Act enforces **internet service providers (ISPs)** and **mobile phone companies** to:

- Give up information upon request from an authorised authority
- Ensure their networks have sufficient hardware installed to facilitate surveillance
- Implement hardware and software solutions that facilitate the storage of digital communications

### Powers Granted to Public Bodies

The Act allows the police and other public bodies to:

- Demand ISPs provide access to a customer's communications
- Conduct mass surveillance of communications
- Demand access to protected information
- Monitor an individual's internet activities
- Prevent the existence of interception activities from being revealed in court

### Controversies

RIPA is particularly controversial because:

- Its powers extend to small agencies like **local councils**, not just intelligence services
- Some people consider it an **invasion of privacy**
- It has been used improperly for purposes beyond monitoring criminal or terrorist activities, including monitoring cockle fishermen, fly tippers, and even a family to determine whether they lived in a school's catchment area

### Case Study: Councils' Covert Surveillance

In 2016, an investigation revealed that councils had been given permission to carry out more than 55,000 days of covert surveillance over 5 years, covering activities such as dog walking, pigeon feeding, and fly-tipping. A freedom of information request found that 186 local authorities (two-thirds of those that responded) had used RIPA to gather evidence through secret listening devices, cameras, and private detectives.

### Examiner Tips

When describing RIPA, use the specific technical terms **"surveillance"** (the use of technology to gather information on people) and **"communication"** (done electronically). Do not generalise these terms.

## Key Terms Summary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Personal data** | Any data that can identify a living person, either alone or in combination with other data |
| **Data subject** | An individual who can be identified by personal data |
| **Data Controller** | The appointed person in an organisation responsible for ensuring DPA compliance |
| **Information Commissioner** | The authority that enforces data protection law and handles complaints |
| **Subject Access Request (SAR)** | A formal request from a data subject to see all personal data held about them |
| **GDPR** | General Data Protection Regulation, which replaced the DPA 1998 from 2018 |
| **CMA** | Computer Misuse Act 1990 |
| **Intellectual property** | Original creations of the mind protected by the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act |
| **RIPA** | Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 |
| **Surveillance** | The use of technology to gather information on people |
