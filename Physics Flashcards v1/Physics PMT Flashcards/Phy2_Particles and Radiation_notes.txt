What are the three constituents of an atom? | Protons, neutrons, and electrons
What are nucleons? | Protons and neutrons (particles in the nucleus)
What is the charge of a proton in coulombs? | +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the mass of a proton in kg? | 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the relative mass of an electron? | 0.0005
What is specific charge? | The charge-mass ratio of a particle (charge ÷ mass)
What is the specific charge of a proton? | 9.58 × 10⁷ C kg⁻¹
What is the proton number (Z)? | The number of protons in an atom
What is the nucleon number (A)? | The total number of protons and neutrons
What are isotopes? | Atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
What is carbon-14 used for? | Carbon dating to find the approximate age of objects containing organic material
What is the strong nuclear force (SNF)? | The force that keeps nuclei stable by counteracting electrostatic repulsion between protons
What is the range of the strong nuclear force when attractive? | Up to 3 fm (femtometres)
At what separation does the strong nuclear force become repulsive? | Below 0.5 fm
What particles does the strong nuclear force act on? | Nucleons only (protons and neutrons)
What are unstable nuclei? | Nuclei with too many protons, neutrons, or both, causing them to decay
When does alpha decay occur? | In large nuclei with too many of both protons and neutrons
In alpha decay, how does the proton number change? | It decreases by 2
In alpha decay, how does the nucleon number change? | It decreases by 4
When does beta-minus decay occur? | In neutron-rich nuclei (too many neutrons)
In beta-minus decay, how does the proton number change? | It increases by 1
In beta-minus decay, how does the nucleon number change? | It stays the same
Why were neutrinos hypothesised? | To account for energy not appearing to be conserved in beta-minus decay
What is an antiparticle? | A particle with the same rest energy and mass but opposite properties to its corresponding particle
What is the antiparticle of an electron? | A positron (e⁺)
What is the rest energy of an electron in MeV? | 0.511 MeV
What are photons? | Packets of electromagnetic radiation that transfer energy and have no mass
What equation relates photon energy to frequency? | E = hf (where h is Planck's constant)
What is Planck's constant? | 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ Js
What is the alternative equation for photon energy? | E = hc/λ (where λ is wavelength)
What is annihilation? | When a particle and its antiparticle collide, converting their masses into energy
How many photons are produced in annihilation? | 2 photons moving in opposite directions (to conserve momentum)
What is pair production? | When a photon is converted into equal amounts of matter and antimatter
What minimum energy must a photon have for pair production? | Energy greater than the total rest energy of both particles produced
What is a PET scanner? | A medical device using positron annihilation to create 3D images of the body's interior
What are the four fundamental forces? | Gravity, electromagnetic, weak nuclear, and strong nuclear
What are exchange particles? | Particles that carry energy and momentum between particles experiencing a force
What is the exchange particle for the strong force? | Gluon
What is the range of the strong force? | 3 × 10⁻¹⁵ m
What particles does the strong force act on? | Hadrons
What is the exchange particle for the weak force? | W boson (W⁺ or W⁻)
What is the range of the weak force? | 10⁻¹⁸ m
What particles does the weak force act on? | All particles
What is the exchange particle for electromagnetic force? | Virtual photon (γ)
What particles does electromagnetic force act on? | Charged particles
What is responsible for beta decay? | The weak nuclear force
What is the equation for electron capture? | p + e⁻ → n + νₑ
What is the equation for beta-plus decay? | p → n + e⁺ + νₑ
What is the equation for beta-minus decay? | n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ
What are the two main categories of particles? | Hadrons and leptons
What are leptons? | Fundamental particles that do not experience the strong nuclear force
What are hadrons? | Particles formed of quarks that experience the strong nuclear force
What are the two types of hadrons? | Baryons (and antibaryons) and mesons
What are baryons made of? | 3 quarks
What are antibaryons made of? | 3 antiquarks
What are mesons made of? | A quark and an antiquark
What is the baryon number? | A property showing whether a particle is a baryon (+1), antibaryon (-1), or not a baryon (0)
Is baryon number conserved in particle interactions? | Yes, always
What is the only stable baryon? | The proton
What is the lepton number? | A property showing whether a particle is a lepton (+1), antilepton (-1), or not a lepton (0)
What are the two types of lepton number? | Electron lepton number and muon lepton number
Is lepton number conserved in particle interactions? | Yes, always
What is a muon sometimes called? | A "heavy electron"
What do muons decay into? | Electrons
What are strange particles? | Particles produced by strong interaction but decay by weak interaction
What strange particles do you need to know? | Kaons (which decay into pions)
What is strangeness? | A property showing strange particles must be created in pairs
Is strangeness conserved in strong interactions? | Yes
Can strangeness change in weak interactions? | Yes, by 0, +1, or -1
What are quarks? | Fundamental particles that make up hadrons
What is the charge of an up quark? | +2/3 e
What is the charge of a down quark? | -1/3 e
What is the charge of a strange quark? | -1/3 e
What is the baryon number of all quarks? | +1/3
What is the strangeness of an up quark? | 0
What is the strangeness of a down quark? | 0
What is the strangeness of a strange quark? | -1
What quark combination forms a proton? | uud (up, up, down)
What quark combination forms a neutron? | udd (up, down, down)
How do you find the quark combination of an antibaryon? | Change the quarks of the baryon into their respective antiquarks
What is the quark combination for π⁺? | ud̄ (up and anti-down)
What is the quark combination for π⁻? | ūd (anti-up and down)
What is the quark combination for K⁺? | us̄ (up and anti-strange)
What properties must always be conserved in particle interactions? | Energy, momentum, charge, baryon number, electron lepton number, muon lepton number
When must strangeness be conserved? | Only during strong interactions
In beta-minus decay, what quark change occurs? | A down quark changes into an up quark
In beta-plus decay, what quark change occurs? | An up quark changes into a down quark
Why are beta decays caused by the weak interaction? | Because there is a change of quark type
What is the photoelectric effect? | Photoelectrons are emitted from a metal surface when light above a threshold frequency shines on it
What is threshold frequency? | The minimum frequency of light needed to cause photoelectric emission from a specific metal
Why couldn't wave theory explain the threshold frequency? | Wave theory suggested any frequency should cause emission as energy gradually accumulates
What does the photon model say about EM waves? | EM waves travel in discrete packets called photons with energy proportional to frequency
How many photons can each electron absorb? | A single photon
What happens if light intensity increases above threshold frequency? | More photoelectrons are emitted per second
What is the work function (φ)? | The minimum energy required for electrons to be emitted from a metal surface
What is stopping potential? | The potential difference needed to stop photoelectrons with maximum kinetic energy
How do you calculate maximum kinetic energy from stopping potential? | Eₖ(max) = eVₛ (where e is electron charge and Vₛ is stopping potential)
What is the photoelectric equation? | E = hf = φ + Eₖ(max)
What are discrete energy levels? | Specific energy states where electrons can exist in atoms
What is excitation? | When an electron gains energy and moves to a higher energy level
What is ionisation? | When an electron gains enough energy to be completely removed from an atom
When does ionisation occur? | When the free electron energy is greater than the ionisation energy
What happens when an excited electron returns to ground state? | It releases energy in the form of a photon
What is in a fluorescent tube? | Mercury vapour
How does a fluorescent tube produce light? | High voltage accelerates electrons, which collide with mercury atoms causing excitation; de-excitation releases UV photons; phosphorous coating absorbs UV and emits visible light
What is an electron volt (eV)? | The energy gained by one electron passing through a potential difference of 1 volt
What is 1 eV in joules? | 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
How do you convert eV to joules? | Multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
How do you convert joules to eV? | Divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
What is a line spectrum? | A spectrum containing discrete wavelengths of light emitted by excited atoms
What does a line spectrum prove? | Electrons can only transition between discrete energy levels
What is a line absorption spectrum? | A continuous spectrum with black lines at specific wavelengths where photons were absorbed
Why do absorption lines appear at specific wavelengths? | Atoms can only absorb photons with energy exactly equal to the difference between energy levels
What equation relates energy level difference to photon frequency? | hf = E₁ - E₂
What are examples of light acting as a wave? | Diffraction and interference
What is an example of light acting as a particle? | The photoelectric effect
How is the wave nature of electrons demonstrated? | Through electron diffraction
What did de Broglie hypothesise? | If light has particle properties, then particles should have wave-like properties
What is the de Broglie wavelength equation? | λ = h/(mv) where h is Planck's constant, m is mass, v is velocity
What happens to diffraction when particle momentum increases? | Wavelength decreases, diffraction decreases, interference rings become closer
What happens to diffraction when particle momentum decreases? | Wavelength increases, diffraction increases, interference rings move further apart
How does scientific knowledge change over time? | In accordance with experimental evidence gathered by the scientific community
What must happen before experimental evidence is accepted? | It must be published and peer-reviewed by the scientific community
