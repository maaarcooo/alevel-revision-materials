What three particles make up an atom? | Protons, neutrons, and electrons
Where in an atom are protons and neutrons located? | In the nucleus
Where in an atom are electrons located? | Orbiting the nucleus
What is the charge of a proton in SI units? | +1.60 × 10^-19 C
What is the charge of an electron in SI units? | -1.60 × 10^-19 C
What is the charge of a neutron? | 0 (no charge)
What is the mass of a proton in kg? | 1.67(3) × 10^-27 kg
What is the mass of a neutron in kg? | 1.67(5) × 10^-27 kg
What is the mass of an electron in kg? | 9.11 × 10^-31 kg
What is the relative charge of a proton? | +1
What is the relative charge of an electron? | -1
What is the relative mass of an electron compared to a nucleon? | 1/2000 (negligible)
Why is a stable atom electrically neutral? | It has equal numbers of protons and electrons, whose charges cancel
How does an atom differ from a nucleus? | An atom consists of the nucleus plus orbiting electrons; a nucleus contains only protons and neutrons
What is specific charge? | The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass (Q/m), measured in C kg^-1
The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass — what quantity is this? | Specific charge
What is the specific charge of the electron? | 1.76 × 10^11 C kg^-1
What is the specific charge of the proton? | 9.58 × 10^7 C kg^-1
How is the charge of an ion calculated for a specific-charge problem? | Number of electrons gained or lost × 1.60 × 10^-19 C (negative if electrons gained, positive if lost)
How is the charge of a nucleus calculated for a specific-charge problem? | Number of protons × 1.60 × 10^-19 C
How is the mass calculated in a specific-charge problem for an ion or nucleus? | Nucleon number × 1.67 × 10^-27 kg
What does the nucleon number A represent in AZX notation? | The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
What does the proton number Z represent in AZX notation? | The total number of protons in the nucleus
What defines a chemical element? | A fixed number of protons in its atoms
What is an isotope? | Nuclei with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
Nuclei with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons — what are these called? | Isotopes
How does an isotope of an element differ in AZX notation? | Same proton number Z, different nucleon number A
Why are some isotopes unstable? | They have an imbalance of neutrons and protons, causing them to decay and emit radiation to reach a more stable form
What is isotopic data? | The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance
The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance — what term describes this? | Isotopic data
How is the relative atomic mass of an element calculated from isotopic data? | Multiply each isotope's mass number by its fractional abundance, then sum the results
What instrument is used to obtain the percentage abundance of different isotopes in a sample? | A mass spectrometer
What does carbon dating involve? | Comparing the ratio of stable carbon-12 to unstable carbon-14 in dead tissue with the ratio in living tissue
Why must there be a strong nuclear force in the nucleus? | Electrostatic repulsion between protons would push the nucleus apart, and gravitational attraction is far too weak to hold it together
Between which fundamental particles does the strong nuclear force act? | Quarks (which make up protons and neutrons)
What is the behaviour of the strong nuclear force below 0.5 fm separation? | Highly repulsive
Over what range of separations is the strong nuclear force attractive? | 0.5 fm to 3.0 fm
At what nucleon separation does the strong nuclear force reach its maximum attractive value? | About 1.0 fm (typical nucleon separation)
At what separation is the equilibrium position of the strong nuclear force where the resultant force is zero? | About 0.5 fm
What happens to the strong nuclear force beyond a separation of 3.0 fm? | It becomes zero (has no effect)
How does the strength of the strong nuclear force compare between proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron pairs? | Roughly the same for all pairs, because the strong force is not influenced by charge (within its 0.5 to 3.0 fm range)
How does the range of the electrostatic force between protons compare to the strong nuclear force? | The electrostatic force has a much larger range, but only becomes significant when proton separation exceeds about 2.5 fm
At what separation is the equilibrium position for two protons where electrostatic repulsion and strong attraction balance? | About 0.7 fm
What is 1 fm (femtometre) in metres? | 1 × 10^-15 m
What types of nuclei commonly undergo alpha decay? | Large, unstable nuclei with too many nucleons
What is an alpha particle? | 2 protons and 2 neutrons, equivalent to a helium-4 nucleus
How do the nucleon number and proton number change during alpha decay? | Nucleon number decreases by 4; proton number decreases by 2
Write the general decay equation for alpha decay using AZX notation | (A,Z)X → (A-4,Z-2)Y + (4,2)alpha
What happens at the nucleon level during beta-minus decay? | A neutron turns into a proton, emitting an electron and an anti-electron neutrino
How do the nucleon number and proton number change during beta-minus decay? | Nucleon number stays the same; proton number increases by 1
Write the general decay equation for beta-minus decay using AZX notation | (A,Z)X → (A,Z+1)Y + (0,-1)beta + (0,0)anti-nu_e
What happens at the nucleon level during beta-plus decay? | A proton turns into a neutron, emitting a positron and an electron neutrino
How do the nucleon number and proton number change during beta-plus decay? | Nucleon number stays the same; proton number decreases by 1
Write the general decay equation for beta-plus decay using AZX notation | (A,Z)X → (A,Z-1)Y + (0,+1)beta + (0,0)nu_e
Which type of neutrino is produced in beta-minus decay? | Anti-electron neutrino
Which type of neutrino is produced in beta-plus decay? | Electron neutrino
What are the charge and mass of an electron neutrino? | Charge: 0; mass: negligible (approximately zero)
Why was the neutrino hypothesised to exist? | To account for the conservation of energy in beta decay, since beta particles were observed with a continuous range of energies rather than a single value
How does the energy spectrum of alpha particles differ from that of beta particles? | Alpha particles have discrete (fixed) energies; beta particles have a continuous range of energies
Explain why beta particles have a continuous range of energies | The energy released in beta decay is shared between the beta particle and the neutrino (or anti-neutrino) in varying proportions
What properties do a particle and its corresponding antiparticle share, and how do they differ? | Same mass and same rest mass-energy, but opposite charges
What is the antiparticle of the electron? | The positron (relative charge +1)
What is the antiparticle of the proton? | The antiproton (relative charge -1)
How are antiparticles named and symbolised (apart from the positron)? | Named with the prefix "anti-" and symbolised with a bar above the particle symbol
What is meant by the rest mass-energy of a particle? | The energy equivalent to the mass of the particle when it is at rest
What is the rest mass-energy of the proton or antiproton? | 938.257 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of the neutron or antineutron? | 939.551 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of the electron or positron? | 0.511 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of the neutrino or antineutrino? | 0
What is a photon? | A massless packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy
A massless packet or quantum of electromagnetic energy — what is this called? | A photon
What is the equation for photon energy in terms of frequency? | E = hf
What is the equation for photon energy in terms of wavelength? | E = hc/lambda
How does photon energy relate to wavelength? | Photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength
What is annihilation? | The destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair when they collide, converting their mass into two gamma-ray photons emitted in opposite directions
The destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair on collision, converting mass into two gamma-ray photons — what process is this? | Annihilation
What is the minimum energy of one photon produced in annihilation? | The rest mass-energy of one of the particles: E_min = hf_min = E_rest
Why are two photons produced in annihilation rather than one? | To conserve momentum — the two photons travel in opposite directions
What is pair production? | The creation of a particle-antiparticle pair from a high-energy photon that interacts with a nucleus and spontaneously converts its energy into mass
The creation of a particle-antiparticle pair from a high-energy photon interacting with a nucleus — what process is this? | Pair production
What is the minimum photon energy required for pair production? | Twice the rest mass-energy of one of the particles produced: E_min = hf_min = 2E_rest
How do annihilation and pair production compare in terms of energy-mass conversion? | Annihilation converts particle mass into photon energy (minimum energy per photon = E_rest); pair production converts photon energy into particle mass (minimum photon energy = 2E_rest)
What is the conversion factor from MeV to joules? | 1 MeV = 1.60 × 10^-13 J
