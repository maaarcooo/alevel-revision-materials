Define excitation | When an electron absorbs enough energy to move up to a higher energy level
When an electron absorbs enough energy to move to a higher energy level — what term is this? | Excitation
What happens when an electron de-excites? | It moves to a lower energy level and emits a photon
Define ionisation | When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged
When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged — what term is this? | Ionisation
What is the ionisation energy of an atom? | The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of the atom
The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of an atom — what quantity is this? | Ionisation energy
How does excitation differ from ionisation? | In excitation the electron moves to a higher energy level but remains in the atom; in ionisation the electron is completely removed from the atom
What is the ground state of an atom? | The lowest energy level that electrons can occupy, the most stable configuration
In what two ways can an electron in an atom gain energy? | By absorbing a photon or through collision with another electron
What fills a fluorescent tube? | Low-pressure mercury vapour inside a partially evacuated glass tube with a phosphor coating on the inside
What creates the electron beam in a fluorescent tube? | A high voltage applied across the tube causes electrons to flow from the cathode to the anode
In a fluorescent tube, what happens when beam electrons collide with mercury atom electrons? | Kinetic energy is transferred, exciting the mercury atom electrons to higher energy levels
What type of radiation do mercury atoms emit when their excited electrons de-excite? | Ultraviolet (UV) photons
How does the phosphor coating in a fluorescent tube produce visible light? | UV photons excite electrons in the phosphor atoms; these electrons de-excite in stages, emitting lower-energy photons in the visible light range
On an energy level diagram, what does the lowest line with the most negative energy represent? | The ground state
On an energy level diagram, what does the line at 0 eV (or infinity) represent? | The ionisation level
What is the ground state energy of the hydrogen atom? | -13.6 eV
What equation gives the energy of a photon? | E = hf
What equation relates the energy change in an electron transition to the wavelength of the photon? | Delta-E = hc / lambda
What equation relates the wavelength of an emitted photon to two energy levels? | lambda = hc / (E2 - E1)
How does the size of an energy level transition relate to the wavelength of the emitted photon? | Larger energy difference produces shorter wavelength; smaller energy difference produces longer wavelength
How do you convert energy from electronvolts to joules? | Multiply by 1.6 x 10^-19
What are line spectra? | Spectra produced when excited atoms emit or absorb light at specific wavelengths, observed as discrete lines
Why can elements be identified by their line spectra? | Each element has a unique set of energy levels, producing a unique pattern of spectral lines
Name the two types of line spectra | Emission spectra and absorption spectra
What does an emission spectrum look like? | Discrete coloured lines on a black background
What does an absorption spectrum look like? | A continuous spectrum with dark lines at certain wavelengths
How is an absorption spectrum produced? | White light passes through a cool, low-pressure gas; atoms absorb photons at wavelengths that match their energy level differences
Why do some wavelengths appear to be missing in an absorption spectrum? | Absorbed photons are re-emitted in all directions rather than the original direction, reducing intensity at those wavelengths
What is the relationship between the dark lines of an absorption spectrum and the emission spectrum of the same element? | They occur at the same wavelengths
What evidence do line spectra provide about electrons in atoms? | Electrons can only transition between discrete energy levels
Define wave-particle duality | The phenomenon where light and matter can exhibit both wave and particle behaviour
The phenomenon where light and matter can exhibit both wave and particle behaviour — what term is this? | Wave-particle duality
What experimental evidence supports the particle nature of light? | The photoelectric effect
What experimental evidence supports the wave nature of light? | Diffraction and interference in Young's double-slit experiment
In the photoelectric effect, how many photons can each surface electron absorb? | Exactly one
Why does the wave theory of light fail to explain the threshold frequency? | Wave theory predicts energy is absorbed gradually, so any frequency should eventually cause emission; in reality only frequencies above the threshold cause instantaneous emission
In the photoelectric effect, what happens when light intensity (above threshold frequency) is increased? | More photoelectrons are emitted per second, but the maximum kinetic energy is unchanged
Who discovered that matter can behave as a wave? | Louis de Broglie
What is observed when a beam of electrons passes through thin graphite film? | A diffraction pattern of concentric rings on the screen
Why is graphite suitable for demonstrating electron diffraction? | Its crystalline structure has atomic plane spacings similar in size to the de Broglie wavelength of electrons
What pattern would appear on the screen if electrons passing through graphite behaved only as particles? | A uniform distribution with no diffraction pattern
How does increasing the accelerating voltage affect the electron diffraction ring diameter? | The diameter decreases (rings become closer together)
Explain why increasing the accelerating voltage decreases the de Broglie wavelength of electrons | Higher voltage increases electron kinetic energy and speed, giving them greater momentum; since wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum, the wavelength decreases
What equation relates the kinetic energy of an accelerated electron to the potential difference? | Ek = half mv squared = eV
What is the de Broglie equation? | lambda = h / p
What is the de Broglie wavelength in terms of particle speed? | lambda = h / (mv)
What is the de Broglie wavelength in terms of kinetic energy? | lambda = h / sqrt(2mE)
What equation relates momentum to kinetic energy? | p = sqrt(2mE)
How does the de Broglie wavelength change as momentum increases? | It decreases (wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum)
For a proton and electron accelerated through the same potential difference, which has the longer de Broglie wavelength and why? | The electron, because it has smaller mass and therefore smaller momentum at the same kinetic energy
How can electron diffraction patterns be used to study materials? | The pattern reveals the crystal structure and atomic spacing of the material
Why can high-energy electrons probe the size of atomic nuclei? | They have very short de Broglie wavelengths, comparable to nuclear dimensions
How does the de Broglie wavelength affect the radius of an electron diffraction pattern? | Longer wavelength produces a larger radius; shorter wavelength produces a smaller radius
Name three pioneers of quantum theory | Max Planck, Niels Bohr, and Albert Einstein
What is peer review? | The process by which scientific claims are evaluated by other expert scientists in that field before acceptance
What three aspects do scientists check during peer review? | Validity, originality, and significance
What is the difference between repeatable and reproducible results? | Repeatable: same scientists obtain the same results. Reproducible: different teams of scientists obtain the same results
How did the failure of wave theory to explain the photoelectric effect change scientific models? | Scientists developed the photon model and wave-particle duality to account for the new evidence, replacing the purely wave-based model
