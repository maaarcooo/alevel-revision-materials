What is the amplitude of a wave? | The maximum displacement from its equilibrium position
The maximum displacement of a wave from its equilibrium position — what term is this? | Amplitude
What is an antinode? | A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave
A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave — what term is this? | Antinode
What is cladding on an optical fibre? | A protective layer that improves tensile strength, prevents scratching, and prevents signal transfer between adjacent fibres
What does it mean for waves to be coherent? | They have the same wavelength and frequency, with a fixed phase difference between them
Waves with the same wavelength and frequency and a fixed phase difference between them — what property is this? | Coherence
What is a diffraction grating? | A grating with hundreds of slits per millimetre, producing sharper interference patterns than a double slit
What are diffraction gratings used for? | Calculating atomic spacing and analysing elements
What is diffraction? | The spreading of waves as they pass through a gap of a similar size to their wavelength
The spreading of waves as they pass through a gap of a similar size to their wavelength — what term is this? | Diffraction
What are electromagnetic waves? | Waves consisting of perpendicular electric and magnetic oscillations
Waves consisting of perpendicular electric and magnetic oscillations — what type of wave is this? | Electromagnetic waves
What is frequency? | The number of waves passing a point per unit time (f = 1/T)
What is fringe spacing? | The distance between two adjacent bright fringes (or two adjacent dark fringes)
What is interference? | The superposition of waves that occurs when two waves meet
When does constructive interference occur? | When two waves meet in phase
When does destructive interference occur? | When two waves meet out of phase (in antiphase)
What is a laser? | A light source that produces a collimated and coherent beam
A light source that produces a collimated and coherent beam — what is this? | A laser
What is a longitudinal wave? | A wave with oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation (e.g. sound waves)
A wave with oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation — what type of wave is this? | Longitudinal wave
What is material dispersion in optical fibres? | Waves of different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds, arriving at different times and causing pulse broadening
How is material dispersion reduced in optical fibres? | By using monochromatic light, so all light travels at the same speed through the fibre
What is modal dispersion in optical fibres? | Waves enter the fibre at slightly different angles, travelling different distances and arriving at different times, causing pulse broadening
What is a node? | A position of zero displacement in a stationary wave
A position of zero displacement in a stationary wave — what term is this? | Node
How do nodes and antinodes differ in a stationary wave? | Nodes are positions of zero displacement; antinodes are positions of maximum displacement
What is an optical fibre? | A thin glass fibre through which signals are transmitted, usually surrounded by cladding
What is path difference? | A measure of how far ahead one wave is compared to another, usually expressed in terms of wavelength
A measure of how far ahead one wave is compared to another, expressed in wavelengths — what term is this? | Path difference
What is phase difference? | The difference in phase between two points on a wave, usually expressed in radians
What is phase? | A measure of how far through its cycle a given point on a wave is
What is polarisation? | The restriction of a wave's oscillations to a single plane. Only transverse waves can be polarised
The restriction of a wave's oscillations to a single plane — what term is this? | Polarisation
What is pulse broadening? | The elongation of a signal in an optical fibre, commonly caused by modal or material dispersion
The elongation of a signal in an optical fibre — what term is this? | Pulse broadening
What is the refractive index of a material? | The ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in that material (n = c/v)
The ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in a material — what quantity is this? | Refractive index
What is Snell's law? | A law relating angle of incidence to angle of refraction using the refractive indices of the two media (n1 sin theta1 = n2 sin theta2)
What is the wave speed equation? | v = f times lambda (speed = frequency times wavelength)
What is a stationary wave? | A wave that stores energy but does not transfer it
A wave that stores energy but does not transfer it — what type of wave is this? | Stationary (standing) wave
What is total internal reflection? | Complete reflection at the inside boundary of a medium (e.g. an optical fibre), so no radiation passes out
What is a transverse wave? | A wave with oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation (e.g. electromagnetic waves)
A wave with oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation — what type of wave is this? | Transverse wave
How do longitudinal and transverse waves differ? | Longitudinal: oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation. Transverse: oscillations perpendicular to it
What is wavelength? | The distance between two identical positions on adjacent waves (e.g. peak to peak or trough to trough)
The distance between two identical positions on adjacent waves — what term is this? | Wavelength
What is Young's double-slit experiment? | An experiment demonstrating interference of light by passing monochromatic light through two narrow slits, producing a pattern of bright and dark fringes
How do material dispersion and modal dispersion differ? | Material dispersion: different wavelengths travel at different speeds. Modal dispersion: waves enter at different angles and travel different distances. Both cause pulse broadening
