What is a stationary (standing) wave? | A wave pattern formed by the superposition of two waves travelling in opposite directions, in which the positions of nodes and antinodes are fixed
A wave pattern formed by the superposition of two waves travelling in opposite directions, in which the positions of nodes and antinodes are fixed — what type of wave is this? | Stationary (standing) wave
How is a stationary wave usually produced in practice? | By a travelling wave and its reflection
What conditions must two waves satisfy to form a stationary wave? | Same frequency (and hence wavelength), similar amplitude, travelling in opposite directions along the same line
How do stationary waves handle energy compared to progressive waves? | Stationary waves store energy, whereas progressive waves transfer energy
What is a node on a stationary wave? | A point of no vibration (zero displacement at all times)
A point of no vibration on a stationary wave — what is this called? | A node
What is an antinode on a stationary wave? | A point of maximum amplitude
A point of maximum amplitude on a stationary wave — what is this called? | An antinode
How do nodes and antinodes move on a stationary wave? | Nodes are fixed in position; antinodes oscillate vertically but do not travel along the string
What is the phase relationship between two points that lie between adjacent nodes on a stationary wave? | They are in phase (0 degree phase difference)
How can you determine the phase relationship between two points on a stationary wave from the number of nodes between them? | An odd number of nodes between them means they are in anti-phase (180 degrees); an even number means they are in phase (0 degrees)
What is the distance between two adjacent nodes on a stationary wave? | Half a wavelength (lambda / 2)
How does the amplitude of points vary along a stationary wave compared to a progressive wave? | On a stationary wave each point has a different amplitude depending on its position; on a progressive wave all points have the same amplitude (in turn)
How does phase vary along a progressive wave compared to a stationary wave? | Progressive: the phase changes continuously from 0 to 360 degrees within one wavelength. Stationary: points are either in phase or exactly in anti-phase, with no values in between
State the principle of superposition | When two or more waves with the same frequency arrive at a point, the resultant displacement is the sum of the displacements of each wave
When two or more waves with the same frequency arrive at a point, the resultant displacement is the sum of the displacements of each wave — what principle is this? | The principle of superposition
To which types of waves does the principle of superposition apply? | All types: transverse and longitudinal, progressive and stationary
What happens when two waves of equal frequency and amplitude superpose in phase? | Constructive interference: the resultant wave has double the amplitude
What happens when two waves of equal frequency and amplitude superpose in anti-phase? | Destructive interference: the resultant wave has zero amplitude
What type of interference produces nodes on a stationary wave? | Destructive interference (the two waves are in anti-phase at nodes, cancelling out)
What type of interference produces antinodes on a stationary wave? | Constructive interference (the two waves are in phase at antinodes, adding together)
How do stationary waves on a stretched string produce sound? | The string vibrates at resonant frequencies, creating stationary wave patterns that produce sound waves in the surrounding air
What are resonant frequencies in the context of stationary waves on a string? | Specific frequencies at which a whole number of half wavelengths fits exactly on the length of the string, producing stationary waves
A specific frequency at which a whole number of half wavelengths fits on a string, producing a stationary wave — what term describes this? | Resonant frequency
How can stationary microwaves be demonstrated? | Place a microwave source in line with a metal reflecting plate and move a small probe detector between them to locate nodes (minima) and antinodes (maxima)
How can stationary sound waves in an air column be demonstrated? | Place fine powder inside a tube with a loudspeaker at the open end; at certain frequencies the powder collects in evenly spaced heaps at the nodes
In a stationary sound wave in an air column closed at one end, what must be present at each end? | A node at the closed end and an antinode at the open (loudspeaker) end
What is the first harmonic (fundamental frequency) on a string fixed at both ends? | The simplest stationary wave pattern: one loop with two nodes (at each end) and one antinode in the centre
The simplest stationary wave pattern on a string fixed at both ends, consisting of one loop with two nodes and one antinode — what is this called? | The first harmonic (or fundamental frequency)
What is the wavelength of the first harmonic on a string of length L fixed at both ends? | lambda = 2L
What is the frequency of the first harmonic on a string of length L? | f1 = v / (2L)
How many nodes and antinodes does the second harmonic have on a string fixed at both ends? | Three nodes and two antinodes
What is the wavelength of the second harmonic on a string of length L fixed at both ends? | lambda = L
What is the frequency of the second harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f2 = 2f1
How many nodes and antinodes does the third harmonic have on a string fixed at both ends? | Four nodes and three antinodes
What is the wavelength of the third harmonic on a string of length L fixed at both ends? | lambda = 2L / 3
What is the frequency of the third harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f3 = 3f1
For the nth harmonic on a string fixed at both ends, how many antinodes and nodes are there? | n antinodes and n + 1 nodes
What is the frequency of the nth harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | fn = n times f1
What equation gives the speed of a wave on a stretched string? | v = sqrt(T / mu), where T is the tension in newtons and mu is the mass per unit length in kg per metre
What equation gives the frequency of the first harmonic of a stretched string? | f = (1 / 2L) times sqrt(T / mu)
What is the aim of the required practical on stationary waves? | To measure how the frequency of the first harmonic is affected by changing the length, tension, or mass per unit length of the string
In the stationary waves practical, what are the three possible independent variables? | Length of the string, tension in the string, or mass per unit length of the string
In the stationary waves practical (varying length), what is the dependent variable? | The frequency of the first harmonic
When varying length in the stationary waves practical, what must be kept constant? | The tension (same hanging masses) and the mass per unit length (same string)
How is the first harmonic identified in the stationary waves practical? | Increase the frequency of the vibration generator until the string shows a single loop with maximum vibration amplitude
In the stationary waves practical (varying length), what graph is plotted and what does the gradient represent? | Plot f against 1/L; the gradient equals v/2, so wave speed v = 2 times the gradient
How is the tension in the string determined in the stationary waves practical? | T = mg, where m is the total hanging mass and g = 9.81 N/kg
How is mass per unit length (mu) measured in the stationary waves practical? | Measure a known length of string (e.g. 1 m) and weigh it on a top-pan balance; mu = mass / length
How can the wave speed found from the f vs 1/L graph be verified? | Compare it with the value calculated from v = sqrt(T / mu) using the measured tension and mass per unit length
What systematic error should be checked in the stationary waves practical, and how? | The signal generator's frequency readings may drift; verify them with an oscilloscope and allow the generator about 20 minutes to stabilise before taking measurements
What is the main source of random error in the stationary waves practical? | The sharpness of resonance makes it difficult to judge the exact frequency at which the first harmonic occurs
How can the random error from sharpness of resonance be reduced in the stationary waves practical? | Adjust the frequency while watching a node (not the antinode), and take repeat readings by finding resonance from both increasing and decreasing frequency
Why should length measurements span a large range in the stationary waves practical? | Greater resolution is achieved with larger lengths and more half-wavelengths; measurements should span a suitable range such as 20 cm intervals over at least 1.0 m
State two safety precautions for the stationary waves practical | Use a rubber string instead of metal wire in case it snaps under tension (or wear goggles if using metal wire), and place a crash mat under the hanging masses
