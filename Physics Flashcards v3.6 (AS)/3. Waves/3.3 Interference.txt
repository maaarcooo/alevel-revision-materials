What is superposition? | When two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point equals the sum of the displacements of the individual waves
When two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement equals the sum of the individual displacements — what principle is this? | The principle of superposition
What is interference? | The effect of superposition — the combination of two or more waves producing regions of reinforcement and cancellation
What is constructive interference? | When two waves superpose so their amplitudes add together, producing a resultant wave with larger amplitude
What is destructive interference? | When two waves superpose so their amplitudes cancel out, producing a resultant wave with zero amplitude
What phase relationship between two waves causes constructive interference? | The waves are in phase (peaks align with peaks, troughs with troughs)
What phase relationship between two waves causes destructive interference? | The waves are in antiphase (peaks of one wave align with troughs of the other)
When two waves of equal amplitude A interfere constructively, what is the resultant amplitude? | 2A
When two waves of equal amplitude A interfere destructively, what is the resultant amplitude? | Zero
What happens when two overlapping waves are neither fully in phase nor in antiphase? | The resultant amplitude is between the constructive maximum and the destructive minimum
What two conditions must waves satisfy to be coherent? | Same frequency and a constant phase difference
Same frequency and a constant phase difference — what property of waves do these conditions define? | Coherence
Why do non-coherent waves not produce a stable interference pattern? | They have different frequencies or a randomly varying phase difference, so the pattern of constructive and destructive interference changes over time
What is path difference? | The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to the point where they meet
The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to the point where they meet — what quantity is this? | Path difference
How does path difference differ from phase difference? | Path difference compares the distances travelled along two paths; phase difference compares the relative positions in the wave cycle at a given point
What is the path difference condition for constructive interference? | Path difference = n lambda (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
What is the path difference condition for destructive interference? | Path difference = (n + 1/2) lambda (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
A two-source experiment gives a path difference of 3.5 lambda at a point. What type of interference occurs? | Destructive, because 3.5 lambda = (3 + 1/2) lambda, an odd number of half wavelengths
On a wavefront diagram, where does constructive interference occur? | Where a peak meets a peak, or a trough meets a trough (along lines of maximum displacement)
On a wavefront diagram, where does destructive interference occur? | Where a peak meets a trough
Why are lasers ideal for analysing diffraction and interference patterns? | Laser light is coherent (constant phase difference, same frequency) and monochromatic (same wavelength)
What produces bright fringes in a laser diffraction pattern? | Constructive interference
What produces dark fringes in a laser diffraction pattern? | Destructive interference
Why are filament bulbs and sodium lamps unsuitable for producing clear interference patterns? | They are non-coherent, so they do not maintain a constant phase relationship needed for a stable pattern
State three safety precautions when using lasers in an experiment | Never look directly at the beam or its reflection; wear laser safety goggles; place a 'laser on' warning sign outside the room
What class of laser should be used in school experiments and what is its maximum output? | Class 2, maximum output 1 mW
How can two-source interference be demonstrated with sound waves? | Two speakers connected to the same signal generator, emitting coherent sound of the same frequency
What is heard at points of constructive interference between two coherent speakers? | A louder sound (compressions and rarefactions from each wave line up)
What is heard at points of destructive interference between two coherent speakers? | Zero sound (a compression from one wave meets a rarefaction from the other, and they cancel)
What technology uses destructive interference of sound waves? | Noise-cancelling headphones
How can two-source interference be detected for microwaves? | Using a moveable microwave detector that registers maximum signal at constructive interference and zero signal at destructive interference
What is the relationship between intensity and amplitude of a wave? | I is proportional to A squared (intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude)
Describe the interference pattern produced by Young's double-slit experiment with monochromatic light | Equally spaced bright fringes (maxima) of equal width and intensity, separated by dark fringes (minima) of zero intensity
Describe the setup of Young's double-slit experiment | A coherent monochromatic light source (e.g. laser) passes through a double slit; the two diffracted beams overlap and produce an interference pattern of bright and dark fringes on a distant screen
In Young's double-slit experiment, what causes the bright fringes (maxima)? | Constructive interference, where the path difference from the two slits is a whole number of wavelengths (n lambda)
In Young's double-slit experiment, what causes the dark fringes (minima)? | Destructive interference, where the path difference from the two slits is an odd number of half wavelengths ((n + 1/2) lambda)
What does n represent in the double-slit interference pattern? | The order of the maximum; n = 0 is the central maximum, n = 1 is the first maximum on either side, and so on
What is the fringe spacing equation for Young's double-slit experiment? | w = lambda D / s
In the equation w = lambda D / s, what does each variable represent? | w = fringe width (m), lambda = wavelength (m), D = slit-to-screen distance (m), s = slit separation (m)
How does fringe spacing w change if the wavelength lambda increases? | w increases (w is directly proportional to lambda)
How does fringe spacing w change if the slit-to-screen distance D increases? | w increases (w is directly proportional to D)
How does fringe spacing w change if the slit separation s decreases? | w increases (w is inversely proportional to s)
What colour is the central maximum when white light passes through a double slit? | White, because all wavelengths interfere constructively at that point (path difference = 0)
In a white-light double-slit pattern, what do the non-central maxima look like? | Each is a spectrum, with violet/blue nearest the central maximum and red furthest away
Explain why violet/blue light appears nearest the central maximum in a white-light double-slit pattern | Shorter wavelengths are diffracted less, so their fringes form closer to the centre
What happens to the white-light double-slit fringes far from the central maximum? | The spectra from adjacent orders overlap and merge, appearing increasingly blurred
What was Newton's (1672) model of light? | Light is a stream of microscopic particles called corpuscles
What limitation of Newton's corpuscle model led to the wave theory being adopted? | Corpuscles could not explain interference or diffraction effects
What did Huygens (1678) propose about light? | The Wave Theory of Light, which explains diffraction and refraction using Huygens' wavelets
What are Huygens' wavelets? | Secondary waves emitted from every point on a wavefront, all travelling at the same speed as the source wave
What did Young's double-slit experiment (1801) provide? | The first experimental proof that light is a wave, through its ability to undergo constructive and destructive interference
What did Maxwell (1862) demonstrate about light? | That light consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other, obeying the wave equation — electromagnetic waves
What did Einstein (1905) discover about light through the photoelectric effect? | That light also behaves as a particle, consisting of packets of energy called photons
What is wave-particle duality? | The understanding that light behaves both as a wave and as a particle
Light behaves both as a wave and as a particle — what is this called? | Wave-particle duality
In the Young's double-slit required practical, what is the aim? | To investigate the relationship between slit-to-screen distance D and fringe width w
In the Young's double-slit required practical, what is the independent variable? | Distance between the slits and the screen, D
In the Young's double-slit required practical, what is the dependent variable? | Fringe width, w
In the Young's double-slit required practical, what are the control variables? | Wavelength of laser light (lambda) and slit separation (s)
How is fringe width w measured accurately in the Young's double-slit experiment? | Measure the total distance across several fringes using vernier callipers, then divide by the number of fringe widths
In the Young's double-slit required practical, what graph is plotted and how is wavelength determined from it? | w against D; gradient = lambda/s, so lambda = gradient multiplied by s
In the diffraction grating required practical, what is the aim? | To calculate the wavelength of laser light using a diffraction grating
What equation is used to analyse diffraction grating results? | n lambda = d sin theta
In the equation n lambda = d sin theta, what does each variable represent? | n = order of diffraction, lambda = wavelength (m), d = slit spacing (m), theta = angle between the normal and the nth-order maximum
How is slit spacing d calculated from the number of slits per metre N? | d = 1/N
In the diffraction grating practical, how is the angle theta determined from measurements? | theta = arctan(h/D), where h is the distance from the central maximum to the nth-order maximum and D is the grating-to-screen distance
State two methods to reduce random error in the Young's double-slit or diffraction grating experiment | Measure across all visible fringes and divide by the number of fringes; take 3 to 8 repeat readings and calculate the mean
How does increasing the grating-to-screen distance D reduce uncertainty in the diffraction grating experiment? | It increases the fringe separation h, reducing its percentage uncertainty
How does using a diffraction grating with more lines per mm affect measurement precision? | It produces larger fringe separations h, reducing the percentage uncertainty in h
Why should interference experiments be conducted in a darkened room? | So the fringes are clearly visible against the screen
State one systematic error precaution in interference experiments using slits or gratings | Use a set square to ensure the beam passes through the slit or grating at normal incidence, avoiding parallax error in fringe width measurements
