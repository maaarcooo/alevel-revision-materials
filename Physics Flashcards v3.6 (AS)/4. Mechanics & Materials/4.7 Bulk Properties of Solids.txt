Define density | Mass per unit volume of an object
Mass per unit volume of an object — what quantity is this? | Density
What is the equation for density? | ρ = m/V
What is the SI unit of density? | kg m⁻³
What is the volume of a sphere? | V = (4/3)πr³
What is the volume of a cylinder? | V = πr²l
How many m³ is 1 mm³? | 1 × 10⁻⁹ m³ (divide by 1000³)
How many m³ is 1 cm³? | 1 × 10⁻⁶ m³ (divide by 100³)
State Hooke's Law | The extension of a material is directly proportional to the applied force (load) up to the limit of proportionality
What is the equation for Hooke's Law? | F = kΔL
What is the spring constant? | A measure of the stiffness of a material (units: N m⁻¹)
A measure of the stiffness of a material (N m⁻¹) — what quantity is this? | Spring constant (k)
How is extension calculated? | Extension = stretched length − unstretched length
How is the Hooke's law region shown on a force-extension graph? | A straight line through the origin
What does the gradient of a force-extension graph represent in the Hooke's law region? | The spring constant, k
What does the gradient of an extension-force graph represent in the Hooke's law region? | 1/k (the reciprocal of the spring constant)
What is the limit of proportionality? | The point beyond which extension is no longer proportional to the applied force
What is the elastic limit? | The point beyond which the material will no longer return to its original length when the load is removed
How does the elastic limit differ from the limit of proportionality? | Limit of proportionality: extension ceases to be proportional to force. Elastic limit: material no longer returns to its original shape. The limit of proportionality is reached first
Define tensile stress | The force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material
The force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material — what quantity is this? | Tensile stress (σ)
What is the equation for tensile stress? | σ = F/A
What is the unit of tensile stress? | Pascal (Pa), the same unit as pressure
Define tensile strain | The extension per unit length of a material
The extension per unit length of a material — what quantity is this? | Tensile strain
What is the equation for tensile strain? | Strain = ΔL/L
Why is strain a dimensionless quantity? | It is the ratio of two lengths (extension divided by original length), so units cancel
What unit constraint applies when calculating strain from ΔL and L? | Both must be in the same units, but they do not need to be in metres
Define ultimate tensile stress | The maximum force per original cross-sectional area a wire can support until it breaks
The maximum force per original cross-sectional area a wire can support before breaking — what quantity is this? | Ultimate tensile stress (UTS)
What is yield stress? | The force per unit area at which the material extends plastically for no or a small increase in stress
What does the area under the straight-line region of a stress-strain graph represent? | Elastic strain energy stored per unit volume
What is the breaking point on a stress-strain graph? | The point at which the material fractures
What does the area under a force-extension graph represent? | The work done on the material (or elastic strain energy stored)
What is the equation for elastic strain energy when Hooke's law is obeyed? | E = ½FΔL
What is the alternative equation for elastic strain energy using the spring constant? | E = ½k(ΔL)²
What three forms of energy does a vertical mass-spring system convert between? | Elastic potential energy (EPE), kinetic energy (KE), and gravitational potential energy (GPE)
At which position in a vertical mass-spring system is KE at a maximum? | At the equilibrium position, where the mass passes through at maximum speed
At which position in a vertical mass-spring system is EPE at a maximum? | At maximum extension (the lowest point)
At which position in a vertical mass-spring system is GPE at a maximum? | At maximum compression (the highest point)
In a horizontal mass-spring system, what energy conversion occurs? | Only between kinetic energy and elastic potential energy (no GPE)
Define elastic deformation | Deformation where the object returns to its original shape when the load is removed
Deformation where the object returns to its original shape when the load is removed — what type of deformation is this? | Elastic deformation
Define plastic deformation | Permanent deformation where the object does not return to its original shape when the load is removed
Permanent deformation where the material does not return to its original shape when the load is removed — what type of deformation is this? | Plastic deformation
What characterises a brittle material? | It fractures with little elastic deformation and negligible plastic deformation (e.g. glass, concrete)
What characterises a ductile material? | It has a large plastic region and stretches significantly before breaking (e.g. copper)
How does a brittle material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin with no or negligible curved region before fracture
How does a ductile material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin followed by a significant curve towards the x-axis before fracture
Why is copper suitable for making wires? | It is a good electrical conductor and is ductile, so it can be drawn into thin wires without breaking
When a metal wire undergoes plastic deformation and is then unloaded, how does the unloading line relate to the loading line on a force-extension graph? | The unloading line is parallel to the loading line (same gradient) but does not pass through the origin, due to permanent extension
What does the area between the loading and unloading lines on a force-extension graph represent? | The work done in permanently deforming the material
On a rubber band's loading-unloading force-extension graph, what does the area between the two curves represent? | Energy converted to thermal energy (heating the rubber band)
Why does a rubber band become warm when repeatedly stretched and released? | Some elastic strain energy is converted to thermal energy during each loading-unloading cycle
What is the function of shock absorbers in a vehicle suspension system? | They dampen the compression and rebound of suspension springs by converting kinetic energy into thermal energy
