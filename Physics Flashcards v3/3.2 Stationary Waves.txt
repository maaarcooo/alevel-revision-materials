What are stationary waves also called? | Standing waves
How are stationary waves formed? | By the superposition of two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions
The superposition of two waves travelling in opposite directions with the same frequency and amplitude produces what? | A stationary wave
How is a stationary wave usually achieved in practice? | By a travelling wave and its reflection
What happens to the peaks and troughs in a stationary wave pattern? | They do not move
In a progressive wave, what can be said about the amplitude of all points? | All points have the same amplitude (in turn)
In a stationary wave, what can be said about the amplitude of different points? | Each point has a different amplitude depending on the amount of superposition
In a progressive wave, what is the phase relationship between points exactly one wavelength apart? | They are in phase
In a stationary wave, what is the phase relationship between points between adjacent nodes? | They are in phase
In a stationary wave, what is the phase relationship between points on either side of a node? | They are out of phase (antiphase)
How does energy behave in a progressive wave? | Energy is transferred along the wave
How does energy behave in a stationary wave? | Energy is stored, not transferred
Do progressive waves have nodes and antinodes? | No
Do stationary waves have nodes and antinodes? | Yes
In a progressive wave, what does wave speed represent? | The speed at which the wave moves through a medium
In a stationary wave, does the overall wave pattern move? | No, the overall wave does not move
What are nodes on a stationary wave? | Regions where there is no vibration
Regions on a stationary wave where there is no vibration are called what? | Nodes
What are antinodes on a stationary wave? | Regions where the vibrations are at their maximum amplitude
Regions on a stationary wave where vibrations are at maximum amplitude are called what? | Antinodes
Do nodes and antinodes move along the string in a stationary wave? | No, nodes are fixed and antinodes only move in the vertical direction
What are the only two possible phase differences between two points on a stationary wave? | In phase or out of phase (antiphase)
When are two points on a stationary wave in phase with each other? | When they are between nodes (no nodes between them) or have an even number of nodes between them
When are two points on a stationary wave out of phase? | When they have an odd number of nodes between them
What does the principle of superposition state? | The resultant displacement at any point is the sum of the displacements of each wave
The resultant displacement at any point equals the sum of individual wave displacements — what principle is this? | The principle of superposition
What two types of interference can waves in superposition undergo? | Constructive and destructive interference
What conditions are required for two waves to form a stationary wave? | Same frequency, same amplitude, travelling in opposite directions along the same line
What type of interference occurs at nodes? | Destructive interference
What type of interference occurs at antinodes? | Constructive interference
Why does destructive interference occur at nodes? | The waves are in anti-phase, causing them to cancel each other out
Why does constructive interference occur at antinodes? | The waves are in phase
What is the resultant amplitude at an antinode compared to the individual wave amplitudes? | Double the amplitude
In a stationary wave, does each point have the same amplitude? | No, each point has a different amplitude
In a progressive wave, does each point have the same amplitude? | Yes
What produces sound from a stretched string with stationary waves? | Vibrations caused by the stationary waves
At what frequencies do stationary waves form on a stretched string? | At specific frequencies where a whole number of wavelengths fit on the length of the string
How can stationary microwaves be demonstrated? | Using a microwave source, reflecting plate, and small detector between them
How can stationary sound waves in an air column be demonstrated? | By placing fine powder inside an air column with a loudspeaker at the open end
In the air column demonstration, where does the powder settle? | At positions of zero disturbance (nodes)
In the air column demonstration, where is the node located? | At the closed end
In the air column demonstration, where is the antinode located? | At the open end (where the loudspeaker is)
What determines the characteristic wavelengths and frequencies of stationary waves? | The frequency of vibration and the boundary conditions
What is the first harmonic also called? | The fundamental frequency
The fundamental frequency is another name for which harmonic? | The first harmonic
How many nodes does the first harmonic have? | Two nodes
How many antinodes does the first harmonic have? | One antinode
What is the wavelength of the first harmonic on a string of length L? | 2L
What is the formula for the frequency of the first harmonic? | f₁ = v/2L
How many nodes does the second harmonic have? | Three nodes
How many antinodes does the second harmonic have? | Two antinodes
What is the wavelength of the second harmonic on a string of length L? | L
What is the frequency of the second harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f₂ = 2f₁
How many nodes does the third harmonic have? | Four nodes
How many antinodes does the third harmonic have? | Three antinodes
What is the wavelength of the third harmonic on a string of length L? | 2L/3
What is the frequency of the third harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f₃ = 3f₁
For the nth harmonic, how many nodes are there? | n + 1 nodes
For the nth harmonic, how many antinodes are there? | n antinodes
What is the wavelength of the nth harmonic on a string of length L? | 2L/n
What is the frequency of the nth harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | fₙ = nf₁
What is the formula for wave speed on a string? | v = √(T/μ)
In the wave speed equation v = √(T/μ), what does T represent? | Tension in the string (N)
In the wave speed equation v = √(T/μ), what does μ represent? | Mass per unit length (kg m⁻¹)
What is the formula for the first harmonic frequency in terms of tension and mass per unit length? | f = (1/2L)√(T/μ)
How does increasing tension affect wave speed on a string? | Wave speed increases (v ∝ √T)
How does increasing mass per unit length affect wave speed on a string? | Wave speed decreases (v ∝ 1/√μ)
In the required practical for stationary waves, what is the dependent variable? | Frequency of the first harmonic
What three independent variables can be investigated in the stationary waves practical? | Length of string, tension in the string, mass per unit length
What is the purpose of the signal generator in the stationary waves practical? | To operate the vibration generator and measure the frequency of the first harmonic
What is the purpose of the pulley in the stationary waves practical? | To allow masses to hang vertically with less friction than the table edge
What is the purpose of the wooden bridge in the stationary waves practical? | To provide a fixed end and allow variation of string length
How is tension calculated in the stationary waves practical? | T = mg, where m is the mass attached and g is gravitational field strength
When plotting results to find wave speed, what should be plotted on the axes? | f (frequency) against 1/L
What does the gradient represent when plotting f against 1/L? | (1/2)√(T/μ)
How can an oscilloscope improve the stationary waves experiment? | It can verify the signal generator's readings
Why should the signal generator be left for about 20 minutes before measurements? | To allow it to stabilise
Why should the length of string be as large as possible in the experiment? | To achieve greater resolution in measurements
What is the biggest problem in deciding when the first harmonic is achieved? | The sharpness of resonance
What technique helps find the first harmonic more accurately? | Reduce frequency then gradually increase until first harmonic is observed, then increase past it and gradually reduce back, then average the readings
Why is looking at amplitude less reliable for identifying the first harmonic? | Because the wave will be moving
What is the main safety precaution in the stationary waves practical? | Stand well away from the masses in case they fall onto the floor
What stabilises the apparatus in the stationary waves practical? | A G clamp or 2 kg mass placed on the retort stand
What length of string is typically used in the stationary waves practical? | 2.0 m
What is the relationship between frequency and length for the first harmonic? | f ∝ 1/L (frequency is inversely proportional to length)
What is the relationship between frequency and tension for the first harmonic? | f ∝ √T (frequency is proportional to square root of tension)
What is the relationship between frequency and mass per unit length for the first harmonic? | f ∝ 1/√μ (frequency is inversely proportional to square root of mass per unit length)
