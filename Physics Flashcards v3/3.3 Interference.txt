What is the principle of superposition? | When two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point equals the sum of the displacements of the individual waves
When two or more waves overlap, the displacement at that point equals the sum of the individual displacements — what principle is this? | The principle of superposition
What is interference? | The effect of superposition when waves overlap
The effect of superposition when waves overlap is called what? | Interference
What is constructive interference? | When waves meet in phase and their displacements add together, producing a wave with larger amplitude
When waves meet in phase and produce a larger amplitude wave, what type of interference is this? | Constructive interference
What is destructive interference? | When waves meet in antiphase (180° out of phase) and their displacements cancel out, producing zero or reduced amplitude
When waves meet in antiphase and cancel out to produce zero amplitude, what type of interference is this? | Destructive interference
What does "in phase" mean for two waves? | The waves have the same position in their cycle (e.g., both at peaks or both at troughs)
What does "in antiphase" mean for two waves? | The waves are 180° out of phase (one at peak when the other is at trough)
What two conditions are required for coherence? | Same frequency and a constant phase difference
Two waves with the same frequency and constant phase difference are described as what? | Coherent
Give three examples of coherent sources | Laser light; sound waves from two speakers emitting the same frequency; light from a single source passing through two slits (Young's double slit)
What is path difference? | The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to a common point
The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to a common point is called what? | Path difference
What is phase difference? | The comparison of positions in the wave cycle of coherent waves at a point
What determines whether constructive or destructive interference occurs? | The path difference between the two coherent waves
What is the condition for constructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = nλ, where n = 0, 1, 2, 3... (whole number of wavelengths)
What is the condition for destructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = (n + ½)λ, where n = 0, 1, 2, 3... (odd number of half wavelengths)
A path difference of 2λ produces what type of interference? | Constructive interference
A path difference of 1.5λ produces what type of interference? | Destructive interference
A path difference of 0.5λ produces what type of interference? | Destructive interference
A path difference of 3λ produces what type of interference? | Constructive interference
What does a wavefront represent? | A line connecting all points of a wave at the same phase (e.g., all peaks or all troughs)
In a wavefront diagram, what happens where peak meets peak? | Constructive interference (maximum displacement)
In a wavefront diagram, what happens where trough meets trough? | Constructive interference (maximum displacement)
In a wavefront diagram, what happens where peak meets trough? | Destructive interference (zero displacement)
Why are lasers ideal for analysing diffraction patterns? | Laser light is coherent (constant phase difference and frequency) and monochromatic (single wavelength)
What does coherent mean for laser light? | It has a constant phase difference and frequency
What does monochromatic mean for laser light? | It has a single wavelength
What produces the bright fringes in a laser diffraction pattern? | Constructive interference
What produces the dark fringes in a laser diffraction pattern? | Destructive interference
State four laser safety precautions | Never look directly at a laser or its reflection; place a warning sign outside the door; remove reflective surfaces; wear laser safety goggles; turn off when not in use
Why is laser light dangerous? | It produces a very high-energy beam that can cause permanent eye damage or blindness
How can two-source interference be demonstrated with sound waves? | Using two speakers emitting coherent sound (same frequency from the same source)
In sound wave interference, when does constructive interference occur? | When compressions and rarefactions from each wave line up, making the sound louder
In sound wave interference, when does destructive interference occur? | When a compression from one wave lines up with a rarefaction from the other, cancelling out to produce zero sound
What technology uses destructive interference of sound waves? | Noise-cancelling headphones
How can two-source interference be detected for microwaves? | With a moveable microwave detector that picks up maximum amplitude in regions of constructive interference
What is the relationship between intensity and amplitude? | Intensity is proportional to the square of amplitude (I ∝ A²)
If the amplitude of a wave doubles, what happens to the intensity? | The intensity quadruples (increases by a factor of 4)
What is Young's double slit experiment? | An experiment demonstrating interference where a single wave source passes through a double slit, creating two coherent sources that produce an interference pattern
Why is a single slit placed before the double slits in Young's experiment? | To diffract the light, ensuring both double slits act as coherent sources
What pattern is observed on the screen in Young's double slit experiment? | An array of bright and dark fringes
Describe the characteristics of the bright fringes in Young's double slit pattern | Each bright fringe is identical with the same width and intensity; each is a peak of equal maximum intensity
What causes the bright fringes (maxima) in the double slit pattern? | Constructive interference of light
What causes the dark fringes (minima) in the double slit pattern? | Destructive interference of light
In the double slit experiment, what is the path difference at the bright fringes (maxima)? | Path difference = nλ (whole number of wavelengths)
In the double slit experiment, what is the path difference at the dark fringes (minima)? | Path difference = (n + ½)λ (odd number of half wavelengths)
State the double slit equation | w = λD/s
In the double slit equation w = λD/s, what does w represent? | Fringe spacing (distance between adjacent bright or dark fringes) in metres
In the double slit equation w = λD/s, what does λ represent? | Wavelength of light in metres
In the double slit equation w = λD/s, what does D represent? | Distance from the slits to the screen in metres
In the double slit equation w = λD/s, what does s represent? | Separation between the two slits in metres
According to w = λD/s, what happens to fringe spacing if wavelength increases? | Fringe spacing increases
According to w = λD/s, what happens to fringe spacing if the distance to the screen increases? | Fringe spacing increases
According to w = λD/s, what happens to fringe spacing if slit separation decreases? | Fringe spacing increases
Why is the central maximum white in a white light double slit pattern? | Because each wavelength in white light interferes constructively at the centre
In white light interference, which colour appears closest to the central maximum? | Violet/blue (shortest wavelength, diffracted the least)
In white light interference, which colour appears furthest from the central maximum? | Red (longest wavelength, diffracted the most)
What do the maxima look like in white light double slit interference (apart from the central maximum)? | Each maximum is composed of a spectrum of colours
What happens to the fringes as you move further from the centre in white light interference? | The fringes get closer together until the spectra eventually merge
What did Newton propose about the nature of light? | That visible light is a stream of microscopic particles called corpuscles
What did Huygens develop? | The original Wave Theory of Light, describing light as a series of wavefronts where every point is a source of waves
What did Young's double slit experiment prove? | That light is a wave that can undergo constructive and destructive interference
What did Maxwell show about light? | That light is electromagnetic waves made up of electric and magnetic fields travelling through space
What did Einstein describe light in terms of? | Packets of energy called photons, leading to wave-particle duality
What is wave-particle duality? | The understanding that light behaves both like a wave and like a particle

Who developed the Wave Theory of Light? | Huygens
Who devised the double slit experiment? | Young
Who showed light is electromagnetic waves? | Maxwell
Who described light as photons? | Einstein
State the diffraction grating equation | d sin θ = nλ
In the diffraction grating equation d sin θ = nλ, what does d represent? | Distance between adjacent slits in metres
In the diffraction grating equation d sin θ = nλ, what does θ represent? | Angle between the normal and the maxima
In the diffraction grating equation d sin θ = nλ, what does n represent? | Order number
How is the slit spacing d calculated from the number of lines per metre N? | d = 1/N
How is the angle θ calculated in a diffraction grating experiment? | tan θ = h/D, where h is the distance from central maximum to the nth order maximum, and D is the grating-to-screen distance
What is the typical wavelength of a standard school red laser? | 635 nm
In Young's double slit experiment, how should fringe width w be measured accurately? | Measure across many fringes and divide by the number of fringe widths to find the average
Why should a set square be used in interference experiments? | To ensure the beam passes through at normal incidence and to avoid parallax error in measurements
How can the uncertainty in fringe width measurements be reduced? | Measure across all visible fringes and divide by the number of fringes; use a Vernier scale
What is the zero-order maximum in a diffraction pattern? | The central beam/fringe where path difference is zero
How can increasing the grating-to-screen distance improve accuracy? | It increases the fringe separation, reducing percentage uncertainty in measurements
What is a potential disadvantage of increasing the grating-to-screen distance? | The intensity of light reaching the screen may decrease
In Young's experiment, what graph should be plotted to find wavelength? | w against D (fringe width vs distance to screen)
What does the gradient of a w against D graph equal in Young's experiment? | λ/s (wavelength divided by slit separation)
How is wavelength calculated from the gradient of a w against D graph? | Wavelength = gradient × slit separation (λ = gradient × s)
