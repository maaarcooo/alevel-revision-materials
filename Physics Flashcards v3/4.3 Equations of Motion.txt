What are scalar quantities? | Quantities that only have a magnitude (size)
Quantities that only have magnitude but no direction are called what? | Scalar quantities
What is distance? | The total length between two points
What is speed? | The total distance travelled per unit of time
What are vector quantities? | Quantities that have both magnitude and direction
Quantities that have both magnitude and direction are called what? | Vector quantities
What is displacement? | The distance of an object from a fixed point in a specified direction
The distance of an object from a fixed point in a specified direction is called what? | Displacement
What is velocity? | The rate of change of displacement of an object
The rate of change of displacement is called what? | Velocity
What is acceleration? | The rate of change of velocity of an object
The rate of change of velocity is called what? | Acceleration
Is distance a scalar or vector quantity? | Scalar
Is speed a scalar or vector quantity? | Scalar
Is displacement a scalar or vector quantity? | Vector
Is velocity a scalar or vector quantity? | Vector
Is acceleration a scalar or vector quantity? | Vector
What is instantaneous speed? | The speed of an object at any given point in time
The speed of an object at any given point in time is called what? | Instantaneous speed (or velocity)
How is an accelerating object shown on a displacement-time graph? | By a curved line
How do you find instantaneous velocity on a displacement-time graph? | Draw a tangent to the curve at the required point and calculate the gradient of that tangent
What is average speed? | The total distance divided by the total time
What is average velocity? | The total displacement divided by the total time
How is average velocity calculated from a displacement-time graph? | By dividing the total displacement (y-axis) by the total time (x-axis)
On a displacement-time graph, what does the y-intercept represent? | The initial displacement
On a displacement-time graph, what does a diagonal straight line represent? | Constant velocity
On a displacement-time graph, what does a positive slope represent? | Motion in the positive direction
On a displacement-time graph, what does a negative slope represent? | Motion in the negative direction
On a displacement-time graph, what does a horizontal line (zero slope) represent? | A state of rest
What does the gradient of a displacement-time graph give? | Velocity
On a velocity-time graph, what does a straight line represent? | Uniform acceleration
On a velocity-time graph, what does a positive slope represent? | Acceleration in the positive direction
On a velocity-time graph, what does a negative slope represent? | Acceleration in the negative direction
On a velocity-time graph, what does a curved line represent? | Non-uniform acceleration
On a velocity-time graph, what does a horizontal line represent? | Motion with constant velocity
What does the area under a velocity-time graph represent? | Displacement or distance travelled
What does the gradient of a velocity-time graph give? | Acceleration
On an acceleration-time graph, what does the y-intercept represent? | The initial acceleration
On an acceleration-time graph, what does a horizontal line represent? | Constant acceleration
What does the area under an acceleration-time graph represent? | Change in velocity
What do the SUVAT equations describe? | Motion of objects moving with constant acceleration in a straight line
When can SUVAT equations be used? | When an object is moving with constant acceleration in a straight line
In SUVAT equations, what does 's' represent? | Displacement (m)
In SUVAT equations, what does 'u' represent? | Initial velocity (m s⁻¹)
In SUVAT equations, what does 'v' represent? | Final velocity (m s⁻¹)
In SUVAT equations, what does 'a' represent? | Acceleration (m s⁻²)
In SUVAT equations, what does 't' represent? | Time (s)
State the SUVAT equation linking v, u, a, and t | v = u + at
State the SUVAT equation linking s, u, t, and a | s = ut + ½at²
State the SUVAT equation linking s, v, u, and t | s = ½(v + u)t
State the SUVAT equation linking v, u, a, and s | v² = u² + 2as
Why can SUVAT variables be positive or negative? | Because they are vectors, and direction determines sign
What are the steps for solving SUVAT problems? | 1. List known variables, 2. Identify the unknown, 3. Select appropriate equation, 4. Rearrange and solve
What is projectile motion? | The motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone (ignoring air resistance)
The motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone is called what? | Projectile motion
What is the curved path followed by a projectile called? | A trajectory
At what point does a projectile reach maximum height? | When the vertical velocity component equals zero
For a projectile launched and landing at the same level, when does it reach maximum height? | At half of its total flight time
What is the range of a projectile? | The horizontal distance travelled by the projectile
The horizontal distance travelled by a projectile is called what? | Range
How many independent components does projectile motion have? | Two (horizontal and vertical)
What is the horizontal acceleration of a projectile (ignoring air resistance)? | Zero
What is the vertical acceleration of a projectile? | The acceleration due to gravity, g = 9.8 m s⁻²
What happens to horizontal velocity during projectile motion (ignoring air resistance)? | It remains constant
What is the vertical velocity at maximum height of a projectile? | Zero
How do you find the vertical component of a projectile's initial velocity? | u sin θ (where u is initial velocity and θ is angle of projection)
How do you find the horizontal component of a projectile's initial velocity? | u cos θ (where u is initial velocity and θ is angle of projection)
What is air resistance? | A drag force experienced by objects moving through air, caused by friction between air molecules and the object
In which direction does air resistance act? | In the opposite direction to the direction of motion
How does air resistance affect the range of a projectile? | It reduces the horizontal velocity and range
How does air resistance affect the maximum height of a projectile? | It reduces the maximum height
How does air resistance affect a projectile's descent? | It causes a steeper descent gradient
What is a drag force? | A force that opposes the motion of an object moving through a fluid (gas or liquid)
What is a fluid in physics? | A term used to describe both liquids and gases
Name two examples of drag forces | Friction and air resistance
In which direction do drag forces always act? | In the opposite direction to motion
Can drag forces ever speed up an object or start them moving? | No, they can only slow objects down or maintain constant speed
What energy transformation do drag forces cause? | They convert kinetic energy into heat and sound
What happens to drag force as an object's speed increases? | Drag force increases
What two factors mainly affect an object's motion through a fluid? | Mass and surface area
How does surface area affect air resistance? | A larger surface area experiences larger air resistance
Why do aeroplanes travel at high altitudes? | There is less air resistance because the air is less dense
Why is air resistance less than water resistance? | Air is 800 times less dense than water
What is terminal velocity? | The constant velocity reached when drag force equals weight and an object no longer accelerates
The constant velocity reached when drag force equals weight is called what? | Terminal velocity
What is the condition for terminal velocity? | Drag force equals weight (no net force, so no acceleration)
For a falling object, what happens when weight is greater than drag force? | The object accelerates
For a falling object, what happens as speed increases? | Drag force increases
When does a skydiver reach terminal velocity? | When weight equals drag force and acceleration becomes zero
What is the aim of the free fall practical? | To calculate the acceleration due to gravity (g) by measuring the time taken for a ball-bearing to fall through different heights
In the free fall practical, why must the ball-bearing be made of a magnetic material? | So it can be held and released by the electromagnet
What is the purpose of the electromagnet in the free fall practical? | To drop the ball-bearing at a consistent starting point every time
What is the purpose of the light gates in the free fall practical? | To determine the time taken to drop a certain distance
What is the purpose of the cushion in the free fall practical? | To prevent damage to the ball-bearing and the surface when landing
In the free fall practical, what SUVAT equation is used for analysis? | s = ut + ½at² (which simplifies to s = ½at² since u = 0)
In the free fall practical, what is plotted on the y-axis? | 2h/t (where h is height and t is time)
In the free fall practical, what is plotted on the x-axis? | t (time)
In the free fall practical, what does the gradient of the graph represent? | The acceleration due to gravity, g
Why does the initial velocity equal zero in the free fall practical? | Because the ball starts from rest
What is a systematic error in the free fall practical due to residual magnetism? | The time recorded is longer than actual because the ball is momentarily held after the electromagnet switches off
What is a random error in the free fall practical? | The ball may not fall accurately down the centre of each light gate
How are random errors reduced in the free fall practical? | By repeating the experiment at least 3 times for each value of h
What safety precaution relates to the electromagnet in the free fall practical? | Keep water away from it due to high current, and only switch it on when dropping the ball
Why must the clamp stand be secured with a G clamp in the free fall practical? | To keep it rigid and stable
What is the line of worst fit? | The steepest line that still passes through all error bars
How is the error in t calculated in the free fall practical? | Half the range of each set of values
How is the fractional uncertainty in g calculated? | The difference in gradient between lines of best and worst fit, divided by the gradient of the line of best fit
What height increments are used in the free fall practical? | 5 cm increments from around 10 cm to 50 cm
