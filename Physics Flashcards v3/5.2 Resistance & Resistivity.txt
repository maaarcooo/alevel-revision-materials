What causes electrical resistance in a metal wire? | Free electrons collide with ions as they move through the wire
What happens when free electrons collide with ions in a metal wire? | They transfer kinetic energy, causing electrical heating
What is the equation for electrical resistance in terms of resistivity? | R = ρL/A
In the resistance equation R = ρL/A, what does ρ represent? | Resistivity (in Ω m)
In the resistance equation R = ρL/A, what does L represent? | Length of the wire (in m)
In the resistance equation R = ρL/A, what does A represent? | Cross-sectional area (in m²)
How does resistance change when the length of a wire is doubled? | Resistance doubles
How does resistance change when the cross-sectional area of a wire is doubled? | Resistance halves
What three factors does resistance depend on? | Length, cross-sectional area, and resistivity of the material
What is resistivity? | A property that describes the extent to which a material opposes the flow of electric current through it
A property describing how much a material opposes electric current flow — what is this called? | Resistivity
What is the unit of resistivity? | Ω m (ohm metres)
Is resistivity dependent on temperature? | Yes, resistivity is dependent on temperature
Why is copper used for electrical wires? | It has a relatively low resistivity at room temperature, allowing current to flow easily
Why do insulators conduct virtually no current? | They have very high resistivity
Which has higher resistivity: metals or insulators? | Insulators
If the diameter of a wire doubles, what happens to its resistance? | Resistance drops to one quarter (area quadruples, R ∝ 1/A)
What is the relationship between cross-sectional area and diameter for a circular wire? | Area is proportional to diameter squared (A = πd²/4)

What is the typical resistivity of glass at room temperature? | 10¹² Ω m
Are metals, semiconductors, or insulators the best conductors? | Metals
What happens to atoms in a solid when temperature increases? | They vibrate faster
How does increased atomic vibration affect free electrons in a metal? | Electrons collide more with vibrating atoms, impeding their flow
For a metallic conductor, how does resistance change when temperature increases? | Resistance increases
For a metallic conductor, how does resistance change when temperature decreases? | Resistance decreases
What is a thermistor? | A non-ohmic conductor whose resistance varies with temperature
A non-ohmic conductor whose resistance varies with temperature — what is this called? | Thermistor
What does NTC stand for in thermistors? | Negative Temperature Coefficient
For an NTC thermistor, how does resistance change when temperature increases? | Resistance decreases
For an NTC thermistor, how does resistance change when temperature decreases? | Resistance increases
Why is the thermistor called a non-ohmic conductor? | Its resistance changes with temperature, so it doesn't follow Ohm's law
Name three applications of thermistors | Ovens, fire alarms, and digital thermometers
What type of sensor is a thermistor? | A temperature sensor
In a series circuit with a thermistor and resistor, what happens to current when temperature decreases? | Current decreases (thermistor resistance increases, so total resistance increases)
In a series circuit with a thermistor and resistor, what happens to p.d. across the thermistor when temperature decreases? | P.d. increases (thermistor's share of total resistance increases)
What is superconductivity? | The property of having zero electrical resistance below a critical temperature
What is a superconductor? | A material with no resistance below a critical temperature
A material with no resistance below a critical temperature — what is this called? | A superconductor
What is the critical temperature (in superconductivity)? | The temperature at which a material becomes superconducting
The temperature at which a material becomes superconducting — what is this called? | Critical temperature (or transition temperature)
What happens to the resistivity of a superconductor at the critical temperature? | It drops to zero
What is another name for critical temperature in superconductivity? | Transition temperature
What is the critical temperature of mercury? | 4.2 K
Why are superconductors useful? | They allow large electric currents with no energy loss
Name two general uses of superconductors | Production of strong magnetic fields and reduction of energy loss in power transmission
