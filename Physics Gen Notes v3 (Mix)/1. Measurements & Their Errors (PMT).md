# 1. Measurements & Their Errors

## Key Definitions

**Accuracy**: How close a measurement is to the **true value**.

**Precision**: How close a measurement is to the **mean value**. Indicates magnitude of random errors only, not closeness to true value.

**Random Error**: Unpredictable variation causing a spread of values about the true value. Reduced by taking **repeat measurements**.

**Systematic Error**: Causes all readings to differ from the true value by a **fixed amount**. Cannot be corrected by repeats — requires different technique or apparatus.

**Repeatable**: Same experimenter obtains the same value using the same method and equipment.

**Reproducible**: Different experimenter using different method and apparatus obtains the same results.

**Resolution**: The smallest change in a quantity that causes a visible change in the instrument reading.

**Uncertainty**: The interval within which the true value is expected to lie, with a given level of confidence.

### Deriving SI Units

SI units of derived quantities are found from their defining equation.

**Example — Force**: $F = ma$
$$\text{Units of } F = \text{kg} \times \text{m s}^{-2} = \text{kg m s}^{-2} \text{ (or N)}$$

**Example — Voltage**: $V = \frac{E}{Q}$

- Energy: $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ → units: $\text{kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-2}$
- Charge: $Q = It$ → units: A s
- Therefore: $V = \frac{\text{kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-2}}{\text{A s}} = \text{kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-3}\text{ A}^{-1}$

### Unit Conversions

**Electron volts to joules**:
$$1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

Example: Convert 76 MeV to joules:
1. MeV → eV: $76 \times 10^6$ eV
2. eV → J: $76 \times 10^6 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} = 1.216 \times 10^{-11}$ J

**Kilowatt-hours to joules**:
$$1 \text{ kWh} = 1000 \times 3600 = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J} = 3.6 \text{ MJ}$$

## 3.1.2 Limitation of Physical Measurements

### Random Errors

- Affect **precision** (spread about mean)
- Cannot be eliminated entirely
- Example: Electronic noise in electrical instruments

**Reducing random errors**:
- Take **at least 3 repeats** and calculate a **mean** (also identifies anomalies)
- Use **computers/data loggers/cameras** to reduce human error and enable smaller intervals
- Use **appropriate equipment** with higher resolution (e.g., micrometer 0.1 mm vs ruler 1 mm)

### Systematic Errors

- Affect **accuracy**
- Cause all results to be **too high or too low by the same amount**
- Examples: **Zero error** (unzeroed balance), **parallax error** (reading scale at angle)

**Reducing systematic errors**:
- **Calibrate** apparatus by measuring a known value
- In radiation experiments, measure and subtract **background radiation**
- Read the **meniscus at eye level** to reduce parallax error
- Use **controls** in experiments

### Types of Uncertainty

**Absolute Uncertainty**: Fixed quantity, e.g., $7 \pm 0.6$ V

**Fractional Uncertainty**: Fraction of measurement, e.g., $7 \pm \frac{3}{35}$ V

**Percentage Uncertainty**: Percentage of measurement, e.g., $7 \pm 8.6\%$ V

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{uncertainty}}{\text{value}} \times 100$$

To reduce percentage/fractional uncertainty → **measure larger quantities**.

### Readings vs Measurements

**Reading**: Single value found (e.g., thermometer)
- Uncertainty = $\pm$ half the smallest division
- Example: Thermometer with 1°C divisions → uncertainty = ±0.5°C

**Measurement**: Difference between two readings (e.g., ruler)
- Uncertainty = at least $\pm 1$ smallest division (both ends contribute)
- Example: Ruler → ±0.5 mm at each end = ±1 mm total

**Digital readings/given values**: Uncertainty is $\pm$ the last significant digit (e.g., 3.2 ± 0.1 V)

**Repeated data**: Uncertainty = half the range
$$\text{Mean} \pm \frac{\text{range}}{2}$$

### Reducing Uncertainty

- **Fix one end** of a ruler (only one reading uncertainty)
- **Measure multiple instances**: e.g., time 10 pendulum swings, then divide result and uncertainty by 10

**Uncertainties should have the same number of significant figures as the data.**

### Combining Uncertainties

**Adding/subtracting data → ADD ABSOLUTE UNCERTAINTIES**

Example: Temperature change from $298 \pm 0.5$ K to $273 \pm 0.5$ K
$$\Delta T = 25 \text{ K}, \quad \text{Uncertainty} = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1 \text{ K}$$
$$\Delta T = 25 \pm 1 \text{ K}$$

**Multiplying/dividing data → ADD PERCENTAGE UNCERTAINTIES**

Example: $F = 91 \pm 3$ N, $m = 7 \pm 0.2$ kg. Find $a = F/m$.
$$a = \frac{91}{7} = 13 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$
$$\% \text{ uncertainty} = \frac{3}{91} \times 100 + \frac{0.2}{7} \times 100 = 3.3\% + 2.9\% = 6.2\%$$
$$a = 13 \pm 0.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

**Raising to a power → MULTIPLY PERCENTAGE UNCERTAINTY BY POWER**

Example: Radius $r = 5 \pm 0.3$ cm. Find uncertainty in area $A = \pi r^2$.
$$\% \text{ uncertainty in } r = \frac{0.3}{5} \times 100 = 6\%$$
$$\% \text{ uncertainty in } A = 6\% \times 2 = 12\%$$
$$A = 78.5 \pm 12\% \text{ cm}^2$$

### Uncertainties and Graphs

**Error bars**: Represent uncertainty on graphs (extend equally on both sides of point)

**Line of best fit**: Must pass through all error bars (excluding anomalies)

**Uncertainty in gradient**:
1. Draw **steepest** and **shallowest** lines of worst fit through all error bars
2. Calculate gradients of best and worst fit lines

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{|\text{best gradient} - \text{worst gradient}|}{\text{best gradient}} \times 100\%$$

**Alternative method**:
$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{max gradient} - \text{min gradient}}{2} \times 100\%$$

**Uncertainty in y-intercept**:
$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{|\text{best y-intercept} - \text{worst y-intercept}|}{\text{best y-intercept}} \times 100\%$$

## 3.1.3 Estimation of Physical Quantities

### Orders of Magnitude

**Orders of magnitude**: Powers of ten describing the size of an object or used to compare sizes.

- Diameter of nuclei: ~$10^{-14}$ m
- 100 m is **two orders of magnitude** greater than 1 m

**Finding order of magnitude**: Calculate the value and express only as a power of ten.

Example: Hydrogen atom diameter = $1.06 \times 10^{-10}$ m. Find area to nearest order of magnitude.
$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times (0.53 \times 10^{-10})^2 = 8.82 \times 10^{-21} \text{ m}^2 \approx 1 \times 10^{-20} \text{ m}^2$$
Order of magnitude = $10^{-20}$ m²

### Estimation

**Estimation**: Approximating values of physical quantities to make comparisons or check if calculated values are reasonable.
