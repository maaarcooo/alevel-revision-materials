# 1.1 Use of SI Units & Their Prefixes

## SI Base Quantities

All physical quantities in physics can be derived from **seven SI base units**. Six are required for A-level:

| Quantity | SI Base Unit | Symbol |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Length | metre | m |
| Time | second | s |
| Current | ampere | A |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Amount of substance | mole | mol |

The SI system is the only system of measurement officially used in almost every country worldwide.

## Derived Units

**Derived units** are combinations of SI base units. To find the base units of a derived unit, use the defining equation.

**Newton (N)** — unit of force:
$$F = ma$$
$$\text{N} = \text{kg} \times \text{m s}^{-2} = \text{kg m s}^{-2}$$

**Joule (J)** — unit of energy:
$$E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
$$\text{J} = \text{kg} \times (\text{m s}^{-1})^2 = \text{kg m}^2 \text{ s}^{-2}$$

**Pascal (Pa)** — unit of pressure:
$$P = \frac{F}{A}$$
$$\text{Pa} = \frac{\text{kg m s}^{-2}}{\text{m}^2} = \text{kg m}^{-1} \text{ s}^{-2}$$

## Powers of Ten

Physical quantities span enormous ranges. **Powers of ten** express these values conveniently using standard prefixes.

| Prefix | Symbol | Value |
|--------|--------|-------|
| tera | T | $10^{12}$ |
| giga | G | $10^{9}$ |
| mega | M | $10^{6}$ |
| kilo | k | $10^{3}$ |
| centi | c | $10^{-2}$ |
| milli | m | $10^{-3}$ |
| micro | μ | $10^{-6}$ |
| nano | n | $10^{-9}$ |
| pico | p | $10^{-12}$ |
| femto | f | $10^{-15}$ |

**Examples:**
- 5 kN = $5 \times 10^3$ N = 5000 N
- 7 nC = $7 \times 10^{-9}$ C = 0.000000007 C

## Common Unit Conversions

### Joules and Electronvolts

The **electronvolt (eV)** is derived from $W = qV$:
$$1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} \times 1 \text{ V} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**Conversions:**
- J → eV: divide by $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$
- eV → J: multiply by $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$

### Joules and Kilowatt-hours

$$1 \text{ kW h} = 1000 \text{ W} \times 3600 \text{ s} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J} = 3.6 \text{ MJ}$$

**Conversions:**
- J → kW h: divide by $3.6 \times 10^6$
- kW h → J: multiply by $3.6 \times 10^6$

## Orders of Magnitude

An **order of magnitude** is a power of 10 that represents the scale of a quantity.

**Key points:**
- Standard rounding rules apply (e.g., $6 \times 10^8$ rounds to $10^9$)
- One order of magnitude larger = **10 times larger**
- Two orders of magnitude larger = **100 times larger** ($10^2$)

**Examples of scale:**
| Object | Approximate Length | Order of Magnitude |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Observable universe edge | $4.4 \times 10^{26}$ m | $10^{26}$ m |
| Earth to Neptune | $4.5 \times 10^{12}$ m | $10^{12}$ m |
| London to Cape Town | $9.7 \times 10^{6}$ m | $10^{7}$ m |
| Adult human height | 1.7 m | $10^{0}$ m |
| Ant | $9 \times 10^{-4}$ m | $10^{-3}$ m |
| Bacterium | $2 \times 10^{-6}$ m | $10^{-6}$ m |
| Atom diameter | $10^{-10}$ m | $10^{-10}$ m |

### Estimation Technique

To estimate a quantity:
1. Identify relevant physical relationships
2. Use known approximate values
3. Calculate and express as an order of magnitude

**Example:** Energy to climb stairs
$$E = mgh \approx 70 \text{ kg} \times 10 \text{ N kg}^{-1} \times 3 \text{ m} = 2100 \text{ J}$$
Order of magnitude: $\sim 10^3$ J
