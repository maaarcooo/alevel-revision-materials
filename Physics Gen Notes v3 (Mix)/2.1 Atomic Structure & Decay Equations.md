# 2.1 Atomic Structure & Decay Equations

## Atomic Structure

All matter is made from **atoms**, which contain three types of particles: **protons**, **neutrons**, and **electrons**.

**Protons** and **neutrons** are found in the **nucleus**; **electrons** orbit the nucleus.

### Particle Properties (SI Units)

| Particle | Charge / C | Mass / kg |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| Proton | $+1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ | $1.67(3) \times 10^{-27}$ |
| Neutron | $0$ | $1.67(5) \times 10^{-27}$ |
| Electron | $-1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ | $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ |

### Relative Properties

| Particle | Relative Charge | Relative Mass |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Proton | +1 | 1 |
| Neutron | 0 | 1 |
| Electron | −1 | 1/2000 (negligible) |

A stable atom is **neutral** — it has equal numbers of protons and electrons, so the overall charge is zero.

**Key distinction**: The **atom** = nucleus + electrons. The **nucleus** = protons + neutrons only.

---

## Specific Charge

**Specific charge**: The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass.

$$\text{specific charge} = \frac{Q}{m}$$

- Units: **coulombs per kilogram (C kg⁻¹)**
- Specific charge of electron: $e/m_e = 1.76 \times 10^{11}$ C kg⁻¹
- Specific charge of proton: $e/m_p = 9.58 \times 10^{7}$ C kg⁻¹

### Calculating Specific Charge

**For an ion:**
- Charge = Number of electrons added/removed × $(1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C)
- Mass = Number of nucleons × $(1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg)

**For a nucleus:**
- Charge = Number of protons × $(1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C)
- Mass = Number of nucleons × $(1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg)

**Sign convention:**
- Gain of electrons → negative specific charge
- Loss of electrons → positive specific charge

## Nucleon & Proton Number

### $^A_Z X$ Notation

- **A** = **nucleon number** (mass number) = total protons + neutrons
- **Z** = **proton number** (atomic number) = total protons
- **X** = chemical symbol

Number of neutrons = A − Z

### Isotopes

**Isotopes**: Nuclei with the **same number of protons** but **different numbers of neutrons**.

- Same proton number (Z)
- Different nucleon number (A)
- Same chemical element

Example: Hydrogen isotopes — $^1_1$H (hydrogen), $^2_1$H (deuterium), $^3_1$H (tritium)

Some isotopes are **unstable** due to an imbalance of neutrons and protons — they undergo radioactive decay.

### Isotopic Data

**Isotopic data**: The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance.

**Relative atomic mass** can be calculated from percentage abundance:
$$\text{Relative atomic mass} = \sum (\text{mass number} \times \text{abundance})$$

**Carbon dating** uses the ratio of stable $^{12}$C to unstable $^{14}$C to determine the age of samples.

## Strong Nuclear Force

### Why It's Needed

In the nucleus:
- **Electrostatic repulsion** between protons (repulsive)
- **Gravitational attraction** between nucleons (negligible — gravity is the weakest fundamental force)

Without another force, the nucleus would fly apart. The **strong nuclear force** holds the nucleus together.

### Properties of the Strong Nuclear Force

The strong nuclear force acts between **quarks** (which make up protons and neutrons).

| Separation | Force Behaviour |
|------------|-----------------|
| < 0.5 fm | Highly **repulsive** |
| 0.5 – 3.0 fm | **Attractive** (maximum at ~1.0 fm) |
| > 3.0 fm | Zero / no effect |

**Key values:**
- Typical nucleon separation: ~1.0 fm
- Equilibrium position (resultant force = 0): ~0.5 fm
- Range: 0.5 to 3.0 fm (very short range)
- 1 fm = 1 femtometre = $1 \times 10^{-15}$ m

### Comparison with Electrostatic Force

| Property | Strong Nuclear Force | Electrostatic Force |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Range | Very short (0.5–3.0 fm) | Much larger |
| Charge dependence | Independent of charge | Depends on charge |
| Acts on | All nucleons (p-p, n-n, p-n) | Only charged particles |

- Between 0.5–3.0 fm, strong force attraction >> electrostatic repulsion
- Electrostatic repulsion becomes significant when proton separation > ~2.5 fm
- Equilibrium for protons (strong = electrostatic): ~0.7 fm

## Alpha & Beta Decay

Unstable nuclei become more stable through **radioactive decay**, emitting radiation.

### Alpha (α) Decay

**Alpha particle**: 2 protons + 2 neutrons = helium nucleus $^4_2$He

Common in large, unstable nuclei with too many nucleons.

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow ^{A-4}_{Z-2} Y + ^4_2 \alpha$$

**Effect on nucleus:**
- Proton number decreases by 2
- Nucleon number decreases by 4
- Neutron number decreases by 2

Example: $^{212}_{84}$Po → $^{208}_{82}$Pb + $^4_2 \alpha$

### Beta-Minus (β⁻) Decay

**Beta-minus particle**: High-energy **electron** emitted from the nucleus.

A **neutron turns into a proton**, emitting an electron and an **anti-electron neutrino** ($\bar{\nu}_e$).

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow ^A_{Z+1} Y + ^0_{-1} \beta + ^0_0 \bar{\nu}_e$$

**Effect on nucleus:**
- Proton number increases by 1
- Nucleon number remains the same

Example: $^{14}_6$C → $^{14}_7$N + $^0_{-1} \beta$ + $\bar{\nu}_e$

### Beta-Plus (β⁺) Decay

**Beta-plus particle**: High-energy **positron** emitted from the nucleus.

A **proton turns into a neutron**, emitting a positron and an **electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$).

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow ^A_{Z-1} Y + ^0_{+1} \beta + ^0_0 \nu_e$$

**Effect on nucleus:**
- Proton number decreases by 1
- Nucleon number remains the same

Example: $^{6}_6$C → $^{6}_5$B + $^0_{+1} \beta$ + $\nu_e$

### Neutrino Emission

**Electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$): Subatomic particle with no charge and negligible mass.

- **β⁻ decay** produces an **anti-electron neutrino** ($\bar{\nu}_e$)
- **β⁺ decay** produces an **electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$)

**Evidence for neutrinos:**
- Alpha particles have **discrete energies** (specific values)
- Beta particles have a **continuous range of energies**
- Energy is shared between the beta particle and neutrino
- Conservation of energy and momentum requires the neutrino

## Particles, Antiparticles & Photons

### Antimatter

Every particle has an **antimatter** counterpart with:
- **Opposite charge**
- **Same mass**
- **Same rest mass-energy**

| Matter | Symbol | Charge | Antimatter | Symbol | Charge |
|--------|--------|--------|------------|--------|--------|
| Electron | e⁻ | −1 | Positron | e⁺ | +1 |
| Proton | p | +1 | Antiproton | $\bar{p}$ | −1 |
| Neutron | n | 0 | Antineutron | $\bar{n}$ | 0 |
| Neutrino | ν | 0 | Antineutrino | $\bar{\nu}$ | 0 |

Antiparticles have a **bar** above their symbol (except positron).

### Rest Mass-Energy

| Particle | Mass / kg | Rest Mass-Energy / MeV |
|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| Proton/antiproton | $1.67(3) \times 10^{-27}$ | 938.257 |
| Neutron/antineutron | $1.67(5) \times 10^{-27}$ | 939.551 |
| Electron/positron | $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ | 0.510999 |
| Neutrino/antineutrino | 0 | 0 |

### The Photon Model

**Photon**: A massless "packet" or "quantum" of electromagnetic energy.

Energy is transferred in **discrete packets**, not continuously.

**Photon energy:**
$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Where:
- $E$ = photon energy (J)
- $h$ = Planck's constant = $6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s
- $f$ = frequency (Hz)
- $c$ = speed of light = $3.0 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)

**Key relationships:**
- Higher frequency → higher photon energy
- Longer wavelength → lower photon energy

## Annihilation & Pair Production

### Annihilation

**Annihilation**: A particle and its antiparticle collide, converting their mass into **two gamma-ray photons**.

Examples: electron-positron, proton-antiproton

**Minimum energy of one photon** = rest mass-energy of one particle:
$$E_{min} = hf_{min} = E_0$$

The two photons travel in **opposite directions** to conserve momentum.

### Pair Production

**Pair production**: A high-energy photon converts its energy into a particle-antiparticle pair.

Requires interaction with a nucleus.

**Minimum photon energy** = total rest mass-energy of both particles:
$$E_{min} = hf_{min} = 2E_0$$

The particle and antiparticle move in **opposite directions** to conserve momentum.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation |
|----------|----------|
| Specific charge | $\displaystyle \frac{Q}{m}$ |
| Photon energy | $E = hf = \displaystyle\frac{hc}{\lambda}$ |
| Min. energy for annihilation (per photon) | $E_{min} = E_0$ |
| Min. energy for pair production | $E_{min} = 2E_0$ |

## Exam Tips

- Don't confuse **nucleon number** with **neutron number**: nucleon = protons + neutrons
- After 3 fm, the strong force is **zero**, not "negligible"
- For specific charge of ions: charge depends on electrons **gained/lost**, mass depends on **nucleons**
- β⁺ decay: **p**lus = **p**roton → neutron (memory aid)
- Always convert MeV to J when using Planck's constant in calculations: $1 \text{ MeV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-13}$ J
