# 2.2 Classification of Particles

## Hadrons

**Hadrons** are subatomic particles made up of **quarks**. They can feel the **strong nuclear force**.

Two classes of hadrons:

- **Baryons**: Made of 3 quarks (e.g., protons, neutrons)
- **Mesons**: Made of a quark-antiquark pair (e.g., pions, kaons)

**Anti-hadrons**:

- **Anti-baryons**: Made of 3 antiquarks (e.g., antiproton, antineutron)
- **Anti-mesons**: Made of a quark-antiquark pair (quark and antiquark swap roles)

Key points:

- Quarks are never found in isolation — always in pairs or groups of three
- All baryons and mesons have **integer charges** (e.g., +1e, −1e, 0)
- Baryons contain either all quarks or all antiquarks — never a mixture

---

## Baryons

### Baryon Number

The **baryon number (B)** is a quantum number that counts baryons in an interaction.

| Particle Type | Baryon Number |
|---------------|---------------|
| Baryon | B = +1 |
| Anti-baryon | B = −1 |
| Non-baryon (mesons, leptons) | B = 0 |

**Baryon number is conserved** in all interactions.

Quark baryon numbers:

- Up (u), down (d), strange (s) quarks: $B = +\frac{1}{3}$
- Anti-up, anti-down, anti-strange quarks: $B = -\frac{1}{3}$

### The Proton as a Baryon

- The **proton is the most stable baryon** (longest half-life)
- All other baryons eventually decay into protons
- Stability arises because the proton is the **lightest baryon**
- Proton decay would violate baryon number conservation
- Theoretical proton half-life: ~$10^{32}$ years (unconfirmed)

## Mesons

### Pions (π-mesons)

**Pions** contain only **up and down quarks** — strangeness S = 0.

| Particle | Quark Composition | Charge | Strangeness |
|----------|-------------------|--------|-------------|
| π⁺ | $u\bar{d}$ | +1 | 0 |
| π⁻ | $d\bar{u}$ | −1 | 0 |
| π⁰ | $u\bar{u}$ or $d\bar{d}$ | 0 | 0 |

Properties:

- Antiparticle of π⁺ is π⁻ (and vice versa)
- π⁰ is its own antiparticle
- Pions are the **lightest mesons** — more stable than other mesons
- First discovered in **cosmic rays**

### Pions as Exchange Particles

- The **pion** is the exchange particle of the **strong nuclear force** between baryons
- Pions mediate the strong force that binds protons and neutrons in the nucleus
- The pion created during exchange is a **virtual particle** (temporary violation of energy conservation)

**Note**: Gluons mediate the strong interaction between quarks (not required for exam). Pions mediate the strong nuclear force between nucleons.

### Kaons (K-mesons)

**Kaons** contain a **strange quark** paired with up or down quarks.

| Particle | Quark Composition | Charge | Strangeness |
|----------|-------------------|--------|-------------|
| K⁺ | $u\bar{s}$ | +1 | +1 |
| K⁻ | $s\bar{u}$ | −1 | −1 |
| K⁰ | $d\bar{s}$ | 0 | +1 |
| $\bar{K}^0$ | $s\bar{d}$ | 0 | −1 |

Properties:

- Antiparticle of K⁺ is K⁻
- Unlike π⁰, the neutral kaon K⁰ is **not** its own antiparticle
- Kaons are heavy and unstable — decay into pions
- Unusually **long lifetimes** (characteristic of strange particles)

### Kaon Production and Decay

**Production** (strong interaction):

- Kaons are produced in pairs — strangeness is conserved
- Example: $p + p \rightarrow p + p + K^+ + K^-$

**Decay** (weak interaction):

- Example: $K^0 \rightarrow \pi^+ + \pi^-$
- Strangeness is **not conserved** in weak interactions

## Leptons

**Leptons** are **fundamental (elementary) particles** — not made of quarks.

Leptons interact via:

- Weak interaction
- Electromagnetic interaction (if charged)
- Gravitational interaction

Leptons do **not** interact via the strong nuclear force.

### Common Leptons

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Mass |
|----------|--------|--------|------|
| Electron | e⁻ | −1e | ~0.0005 u |
| Electron neutrino | νₑ | 0 | ~0 |
| Muon | μ⁻ | −1e | ~0.1 u |
| Muon neutrino | νμ | 0 | ~0 |

- The **muon** is similar to the electron but heavier
- **Neutrinos** are the most abundant leptons — no charge, negligible mass
- Quarks are fundamental but **not leptons** (quarks interact via the strong force)

### Lepton Number

The **lepton number (L)** is a quantum number conserved in all interactions.

| Particle Type | Lepton Number |
|---------------|---------------|
| Lepton (e⁻, μ⁻, νₑ, νμ) | L = +1 |
| Anti-lepton (e⁺, μ⁺, $\bar{ν}_e$, $\bar{ν}_μ$) | L = −1 |
| Non-lepton (baryons, mesons) | L = 0 |

**Electron lepton number (Lₑ)** and **muon lepton number (Lμ)** are conserved separately.

### Muon Decay

- Muons (μ⁻) decay into electrons
- Anti-muons (μ⁺) decay into positrons
- Occurs via the **weak interaction** (W⁻ boson exchange)

Muon decay equation:

$$\mu^- \rightarrow e^- + \bar{\nu}_e + \nu_\mu$$

Conservation check:

- Charge: −1 = −1 + 0 + 0 ✓
- Electron lepton number: 0 = +1 − 1 + 0 ✓
- Muon lepton number: +1 = 0 + 0 + 1 ✓

## Quarks and Antiquarks

### Properties of Quarks

The three main quark flavours:

| Quark | Symbol | Charge | Baryon Number | Strangeness |
|-------|--------|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Up | u | $+\frac{2}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | 0 |
| Down | d | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | 0 |
| Strange | s | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | −1 |

### Properties of Antiquarks

| Antiquark | Symbol | Charge | Baryon Number | Strangeness |
|-----------|--------|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Anti-up | $\bar{u}$ | $-\frac{2}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | 0 |
| Anti-down | $\bar{d}$ | $+\frac{1}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | 0 |
| Anti-strange | $\bar{s}$ | $+\frac{1}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | +1 |

Antiquarks have **opposite** charge, baryon number, and strangeness to their quark counterparts.

### Quark Combinations

**Baryons** (3 quarks):

| Particle | Quark Composition | Charge |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| Proton (p) | uud | $+\frac{2}{3}+\frac{2}{3}-\frac{1}{3} = +1$ |
| Neutron (n) | udd | $+\frac{2}{3}-\frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{3} = 0$ |

**Mesons** (quark + antiquark):

| Particle | Quark Composition | Charge |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| π⁺ | $u\bar{d}$ | +1 |
| π⁻ | $d\bar{u}$ | −1 |
| π⁰ | $u\bar{u}$ or $d\bar{d}$ | 0 |
| K⁺ | $u\bar{s}$ | +1 |
| K⁻ | $s\bar{u}$ | −1 |
| K⁰ | $d\bar{s}$ | 0 |

**Exam tip**: Quark combinations for protons, neutrons, pions, and kaons must be memorised.

## Strange Quarks and Strangeness

### Strange Particles

**Strange particles** contain a strange or anti-strange quark.

Properties:

- **Produced** via the **strong interaction**
- **Decay** via the **weak interaction**
- Produced in **quark-antiquark pairs** (strangeness conserved in production)

### Strangeness (S)

**Strangeness** is a quantum number:

| Quark Content | Strangeness |
|---------------|-------------|
| Anti-strange quark ($\bar{s}$) | S = +1 |
| Strange quark (s) | S = −1 |
| No strange quarks | S = 0 |

Conservation rules:

- Strangeness is **conserved** in strong and electromagnetic interactions
- Strangeness is **not conserved** in weak interactions
- In weak interactions, strangeness can change by 0, +1, or −1

## Conservation Laws Summary

| Quantity | Strong | Electromagnetic | Weak |
|----------|--------|-----------------|------|
| Charge | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Baryon number | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Lepton number | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Strangeness | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ (can change by 0, ±1) |

## Collaborative Efforts in Particle Physics

### Particle Accelerators

- **Particle accelerators** collide particles at very high energies (near speed of light) to:
  - Produce new particles
  - Reveal inner structure of particles
- Can be **linear** or **circular**
- Circular accelerators require powerful **superconducting magnets**
- Large-scale **international collaboration** provides funding and expertise

### The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)

- World's largest and highest-energy particle collider
- Located at **CERN**, Geneva (France-Switzerland border)
- ~2500 scientists from 110 nationalities

Four main detectors:

| Detector | Research Focus |
|----------|----------------|
| ATLAS | Fundamental particles, dark matter, extra dimensions |
| CMS | Same as ATLAS (different magnet system) |
| ALICE | Quark-gluon plasma |
| LHCb | Matter-antimatter asymmetry, bottom quark physics |

**Validation example**: Both ATLAS and CMS independently detected the **Higgs boson** (2012), validating the theory through repeated experiments.

## Key Equations and Values

Proton charge calculation:

$$q_p = \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = +1e$$

Neutron charge calculation:

$$q_n = \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = 0$$

Beta-minus decay (baryon number conservation):

$$n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

$$B: 1 = 1 + 0 + 0$$ ✓
