# Limitation of Physical Measurements

## Sources of Uncertainty

### Random & Systematic Errors

All measurements aim to find the **true value** of a quantity, but it is impossible to obtain this exactly. There is always a degree of **uncertainty**, which is an estimate of the difference between a measurement reading and the true value. Two types of measurement error lead to uncertainty: **random errors** and **systematic errors**.

**Random errors** cause unpredictable fluctuations in an instrument's readings due to uncontrollable factors such as environmental conditions. They affect the **precision** of measurements, producing a wider spread of results about the mean value. To reduce random error, repeat measurements several times and calculate an average.

**Systematic errors** arise from faulty instruments or flaws in the experimental method. They are repeated consistently every time the instrument is used or the method is followed, affecting the **accuracy** of all readings obtained. To reduce systematic errors, instruments should be **recalibrated** or replaced with different instruments, and corrections or adjustments should be made to the experimental technique.

### Zero Error

A **zero error** is a type of systematic error that occurs when an instrument gives a non-zero reading when the true reading is zero. This introduces a fixed offset into all readings, which must be accounted for when recording results.

### Precision & Accuracy

**Precision** describes how close repeated measurements are to each other, i.e. how little spread there is about the mean value. If repeated readings are very similar or identical, the measurement is precise. Measurements recorded to a greater number of decimal places are said to be more **precise** than those recorded to a whole number.

**Accuracy** describes how close a measurement is to the true value. Accuracy can be increased by repeating measurements and calculating a mean. Repeating measurements also helps identify **anomalies** (outliers), which can be omitted from the final results.

Precision and accuracy are independent: measurements can be precise but not accurate (e.g. a miscalibrated instrument giving tightly clustered but offset readings), accurate but not precise, both, or neither.

### Repeatability

A measurement is **repeatable** if the original experimenter repeats the investigation using the same method and equipment and obtains the same results.

### Reproducibility

A measurement is **reproducible** if the investigation is repeated by a different person, or using different equipment or techniques, and the same results are obtained.

### Resolution

**Resolution** is the smallest change in the quantity being measured that gives a perceptible change in the reading of a measuring instrument. For example, a wristwatch has a resolution of 1 s, whereas a digital stopclock typically has a resolution of 10 ms (0.01 s). In imaging, resolution refers to the ability to distinguish two separate structures rather than seeing them as a single blurred entity.

## Calculating Uncertainties

### Uncertainty

There is always a degree of **uncertainty** in any measurement. It can be thought of as the difference between the actual reading (affected by equipment or technique) and the true value.

Uncertainties are **not** the same as errors. Errors are issues with equipment or methodology that cause a reading to differ from the true value. The uncertainty is a range of values around a measurement within which the true value is expected to lie, and is an **estimate**.

### Types of Uncertainty

**Absolute uncertainty** expresses the uncertainty as a fixed quantity in the same units as the measurement.

**Fractional uncertainty** expresses the uncertainty as a fraction of the measured value:

$$\text{Fractional uncertainty} = \frac{\Delta x}{x}$$

**Percentage uncertainty** expresses the uncertainty as a percentage of the measured value:

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\Delta x}{x} \times 100\%$$

### Finding Uncertainties

| Situation | Uncertainty |
|---|---|
| Single reading (e.g. thermometer, analogue meter) | $\pm$ half the smallest division |
| Single measurement (e.g. ruler used to measure length between two points) | At least $\pm 1$ smallest division |
| Repeated data | Half the range: $\pm \frac{1}{2}(\text{largest} - \text{smallest value})$ |
| Digital instrument readings | $\pm$ the last significant digit (unless otherwise quoted) |

**Worked example (ammeter reading):** For an analogue ammeter with smallest division 0.2 mA reading $I = 1.6$ mA:

- Absolute uncertainty: $\Delta I = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.2 = 0.1$ mA, so $I = 1.6 \pm 0.1$ mA
- Fractional uncertainty: $\frac{0.1}{1.6} = \frac{1}{16}$
- Percentage uncertainty: $\frac{0.1}{1.6} \times 100\% = 6.2\%$

### Combining Uncertainties

**Adding or subtracting quantities:** add the absolute uncertainties.

For $d_1 - d_2$ where $d_1 = 55.0 \pm 0.5$ cm and $d_2 = 21.0 \pm 0.7$ cm:

$$d_1 - d_2 = 34.0 \pm (0.5 + 0.7) = 34.0 \pm 1.2 \text{ cm}$$

**Multiplying or dividing quantities:** add the fractional (or percentage) uncertainties.

For $v = \frac{s}{t}$ where $s = 50.0 \pm 0.1$ m and $t = 5.00 \pm 0.05$ s:

$$v = \frac{50.0}{5.00} = 10.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

$$\frac{\Delta v}{v} = \frac{\Delta s}{s} + \frac{\Delta t}{t} = \frac{0.1}{50.0} + \frac{0.05}{5.00} = 0.002 + 0.01 = 0.012$$

$$\Delta v = 10.0 \times 0.012 = 0.12 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

So $v = 10.0 \pm 0.12$ m s$^{-1}$.

**Raising to a power:** multiply the percentage uncertainty by the power.

For the volume of a sphere $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ with $r = 2.50 \pm 0.02$ cm:

$$\frac{\Delta V}{V} = 3 \times \frac{\Delta r}{r} = 3 \times \frac{0.02}{2.50} = 0.024$$

Absolute uncertainty: $\Delta V = 65.5 \times 0.024 = 1.57$ cm$^3$. Percentage uncertainty: $0.024 \times 100\% = 2.4\%$.

### Worked Example: Percentage Uncertainty in Repeated Data

For angular frequency values: 0.154, 0.153, 0.159, 0.147, 0.152 rad s$^{-1}$:

1. Mean: $\bar{\omega} = \frac{0.154 + 0.153 + 0.159 + 0.147 + 0.152}{5} = 0.153$ rad s$^{-1}$
2. Uncertainty (half the range): $\Delta\omega = \frac{1}{2}(0.159 - 0.147) = 0.006$ rad s$^{-1}$
3. Percentage uncertainty: $\frac{0.006}{0.153} \times 100\% = 3.92\% \approx 4\%$

Result: $\omega = 0.153$ rad s$^{-1}$ $\pm 4\%$

### Significant Figures for Uncertainties

- Absolute uncertainties should be quoted to **one significant figure**
- Fractional and percentage uncertainties should be quoted to **no more than two significant figures**
- The final result must then be rounded to match the precision of the absolute uncertainty
- The uncertainty in constants (such as $\pi$) is taken to be zero
- Absolute uncertainties (denoted $\Delta$) carry the same units as the quantity; percentage uncertainties have no units

Uncertainties in trigonometric and logarithmic functions are not tested in the AQA A-level exam.

## Determining Uncertainties from Graphs

### Error Bars

The uncertainty in a measurement can be represented on a graph as an **error bar**, drawn above and below the plotted point (or from side to side for horizontal uncertainties). Error bars show the **absolute uncertainty** of the plotted values. They are usually drawn vertically (for $y$-values) but can also be drawn horizontally (for $x$-values).

### Determining Uncertainty in the Gradient

To find the uncertainty in a gradient, draw two lines on the graph:

1. The **best line of best fit**, which passes as close to all the data points as possible
2. The **worst line of best fit** (or "worst fit"), which is either the steepest or shallowest line that still passes through all the error bars

The percentage uncertainty in the **gradient** is:

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{best gradient} - \text{worst gradient}}{\text{best gradient}} \times 100\%$$

The percentage uncertainty in the **y-intercept** is:

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{best y-intercept} - \text{worst y-intercept}}{\text{best y-intercept}} \times 100\%$$

**Worked example (Force vs Extension):** From a graph of Extension/mm against Force/N:

- Best gradient: $\frac{26 - 6}{80 - 0} = 0.25$ mm N$^{-1}$
- Worst gradient: $\frac{27 - 4.7}{80 - 0} = 0.28$ mm N$^{-1}$
- Percentage uncertainty: $\frac{0.28 - 0.25}{0.25} \times 100\% = 12\%$

### Graph Drawing Rules (Exam Technique)

- Use a sensible scale that fills as much of the available paper as possible
- Label axes with both the quantity name and its unit
- Plot points precisely, to within 0.5 small squares
- The best fit line should leave a roughly equal number of points above and below it
- Draw error bars accurately to reflect the stated uncertainties

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation |
|---|---|
| Percentage uncertainty | $\frac{\Delta x}{x} \times 100\%$ |
| Fractional uncertainty | $\frac{\Delta x}{x}$ |
| Uncertainty in repeated data | $\pm \frac{1}{2}(\text{largest} - \text{smallest value})$ |
| Combining (add/subtract) | Add absolute uncertainties: $\Delta z = \Delta x + \Delta y$ |
| Combining (multiply/divide) | Add fractional uncertainties: $\frac{\Delta z}{z} = \frac{\Delta x}{x} + \frac{\Delta y}{y}$ |
| Combining (power $n$) | Multiply fractional uncertainty by $n$: $\frac{\Delta z}{z} = n \cdot \frac{\Delta x}{x}$ |
| Uncertainty in gradient | $\frac{\text{best gradient} - \text{worst gradient}}{\text{best gradient}} \times 100\%$ |
| Uncertainty in y-intercept | $\frac{\text{best y-intercept} - \text{worst y-intercept}}{\text{best y-intercept}} \times 100\%$ |
