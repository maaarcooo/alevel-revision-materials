# Atomic Structure & Decay Equations

## Atomic Structure

All matter is made from **atoms**, which contain three types of particle: **protons**, **neutrons**, and **electrons**. Protons and neutrons are found in the **nucleus**, while electrons orbit the nucleus.

### Particle Properties (SI Units)

| Particle | Charge / C | Mass / kg |
|---|---|---|
| Proton | $+1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ | $1.67(3) \times 10^{-27}$ |
| Neutron | $0$ | $1.67(5) \times 10^{-27}$ |
| Electron | $-1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ | $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ |

### Relative Properties

| Particle | Relative Charge | Relative Mass |
|---|---|---|
| Proton | $+1$ | $1$ |
| Neutron | $0$ | $1$ |
| Electron | $-1$ | $1/2000$ (negligible) |

A stable atom is **neutral** (no overall charge) because it has an equal number of protons and electrons, whose charges cancel.

> **Exam tip:** Do not confuse "atom" and "nucleus". The atom includes the nucleus and electrons. The nucleus contains only protons and neutrons.

### Specific Charge

In physics, **specific** refers to a quantity expressed per unit mass. **Specific charge** is defined as the ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass:

$$\text{specific charge} = \frac{Q}{m}$$

Specific charge is measured in **coulombs per kilogram** ($\text{C kg}^{-1}$).

Datasheet values (given as charge/mass ratio):

- Specific charge of the electron: $e/m_e = 1.76 \times 10^{11} \ \text{C kg}^{-1}$
- Specific charge of the proton: $e/m_p = 9.58 \times 10^{7} \ \text{C kg}^{-1}$

### Calculating Specific Charge

The method differs depending on whether you are finding the specific charge of an **ion** or a **nucleus**.

**For an ion:**
- Charge = number of electrons gained or lost $\times \ 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C (negative if gained, positive if lost)
- Mass = nucleon (mass) number $\times \ 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

**For a nucleus:**
- Charge = number of protons $\times \ 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C (always positive)
- Mass = nucleon (mass) number $\times \ 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

The key distinction: an ion's charge comes from the imbalance of electrons relative to a neutral atom, while a nucleus's charge comes entirely from its protons (electrons are not part of the nucleus).

**Inline example (ion):** $^{24}_{12}\text{Mg}$ gains 2 electrons, forming $^{24}_{12}\text{Mg}^{2-}$. Total charge = $2 \times (-1.60 \times 10^{-19}) = -3.2 \times 10^{-19}$ C. Total mass = $24 \times 1.67 \times 10^{-27} = 4.0 \times 10^{-26}$ kg. Specific charge = $\frac{-3.2 \times 10^{-19}}{4.0 \times 10^{-26}} = -8.0 \times 10^{6} \ \text{C kg}^{-1}$.

> **Exam tip:** Gaining electrons gives a **negative** specific charge. Losing electrons gives a **positive** specific charge.

## Nucleon & Proton Number

### $^A_Z X$ Notation

A nucleus is described using $^A_Z X$ notation, where $X$ is the chemical symbol of the element.

- **Nucleon number** $A$ (also called **mass number**) = total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
- **Proton number** $Z$ (also called **atomic number**) = total number of protons in the nucleus

The number of neutrons is found by $N = A - Z$.

> **Exam tip:** The periodic table is ordered by atomic number $Z$. Do not confuse mass number $A$ with atomic number $Z$.

### Isotopes

**Elements** are defined by a fixed number of protons. **Isotopes** are defined as nuclei that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Isotopes of an element therefore share the same proton number $Z$ but have different nucleon numbers $A$.

For example, hydrogen has three isotopes: hydrogen ($^1_1\text{H}$, 0 neutrons), **deuterium** ($^2_1\text{H}$, 1 neutron), and **tritium** ($^3_1\text{H}$, 2 neutrons).

Some isotopes are **unstable** because of an imbalance of neutrons and protons. These decay by emitting radiation to achieve a more stable form, on timescales ranging from nanoseconds to hundreds of thousands of years.

**Identifying isotopes from data:** Two nuclei are isotopes if they have the same number of protons (calculated as $A - N$) but different numbers of neutrons. For example, nuclei with ($A = 37$, $N = 20$) and ($A = 35$, $N = 18$) both have $Z = 17$, so they are isotopes of chlorine.

### Isotopic Data

**Isotopic data** is defined as the relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance. The **relative atomic mass** of an element can be calculated from the relative abundance of each isotope using a weighted average.

For a sample of oxygen containing $^{16}\text{O}$ (99.76%), $^{17}\text{O}$ (0.04%), and $^{18}\text{O}$ (0.20%):

$$\text{Relative atomic mass} = (16 \times 0.9976) + (17 \times 0.0004) + (18 \times 0.002) = 16.00 \ (2 \ \text{d.p.})$$

The percentage abundance of isotopes in a sample can be obtained using a **mass spectrometer**.

A common application of isotopic data is **carbon dating** of archaeological artefacts. This uses the ratio of stable $^{12}\text{C}$ to unstable $^{14}\text{C}$. The age of dead tissue is determined by comparing this ratio to that in living tissue, since $^{14}\text{C}$ decays after the organism dies while $^{12}\text{C}$ remains constant.

## Strong Nuclear Force

### Forces in the Nucleus

Inside a nucleus, two forces act on the nucleons:

- **Electrostatic repulsion** between protons due to their positive charge (repulsive)
- **Gravitational attraction** between nucleons due to their mass (attractive, but negligible compared to the electrostatic force since gravity is the weakest fundamental force)

If these were the only forces, the nucleus could not hold together. There must therefore be a stronger attractive force acting between all nucleons: the **strong nuclear force** (also called the **strong interaction**).

The strong nuclear force acts between particles called **quarks**. Since protons and neutrons are made of quarks, the quark-level interaction keeps nucleons bound within the nucleus.

### Properties of the Strong Nuclear Force

The strong nuclear force varies with the separation between two nucleons:

| Separation | Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Below $\sim 0.5$ fm | Highly **repulsive** (prevents nucleons from being pushed into each other) |
| $\sim 0.5$ fm | **Equilibrium position** (resultant force = 0) |
| $\sim 1.0$ fm | **Maximum attraction** (typical nucleon separation) |
| $0.5$ to $3.0$ fm | **Attractive**, holding the nucleus together |
| Above $3.0$ fm | **Zero** (the force has no effect) |

The strong nuclear force has a very short range ($0.5$ to $3.0$ fm), where 1 fm (femtometre) = $1 \times 10^{-15}$ m.

> **Exam tip:** After 3 fm, state that the strong force becomes "zero" or "has no effect", not that it is "negligible". Remember the key values: range = 0.5 to 3.0 fm, typical separation $\approx$ 1.0 fm.

### Comparison of Electrostatic and Strong Forces

Between separations of 0.5 and 3.0 fm, the strong nuclear force is far more powerful than the electrostatic repulsion. The electrostatic force has a much **larger range** than the strong force, but it only becomes significant when proton separation exceeds about 2.5 fm.

The electrostatic force depends on charge, so it only acts between protons. The strong nuclear force is **not influenced by charge**, so its strength is roughly the same between all nucleon pairs (proton-proton, neutron-neutron, proton-neutron) within the 0.5 to 3.0 fm range.

The **equilibrium position for protons**, where the electrostatic repulsive force and strong attractive force balance, occurs at a separation of about **0.7 fm** (slightly larger than the 0.5 fm equilibrium for the strong force alone, because the electrostatic repulsion shifts it outward).

## Alpha & Beta Decay

Unstable nuclei become more stable through **radioactive decay**. The three main decay mechanisms are **alpha decay**, **beta-minus decay**, and **beta-plus decay**. In each, the original nucleus is called the **parent nucleus** and the resulting nucleus is the **daughter nucleus**.

### Alpha Decay

**Alpha decay** occurs in large, unstable nuclei with too many nucleons. The nucleus emits an **alpha particle** ($\alpha$), which consists of **2 protons and 2 neutrons** (equivalent to a helium nucleus $^4_2\text{He}$).

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow \ ^{A-4}_{Z-2} Y + \ ^4_2 \alpha$$

When an alpha particle is emitted:
- **Proton number decreases by 2**
- **Nucleon number decreases by 4**

Example: $^{212}_{84}\text{Po} \rightarrow \ ^{208}_{82}\text{Pb} + \ ^4_2\alpha$

> **Exam tip:** In alpha decay, the nucleon number decreases by 4, but the number of neutrons decreases by only 2.

### Beta-Minus ($\beta^-$) Decay

A **beta-minus particle** is a high-energy **electron** emitted from the nucleus. In $\beta^-$ decay, a **neutron turns into a proton**, emitting an electron and an **anti-electron neutrino** ($\bar{\nu}_e$):

$$n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow \ ^{A}_{Z+1} Y + \ ^{0}_{-1}\beta + \ ^{0}_{0}\bar{\nu}_e$$

When a $\beta^-$ particle is emitted:
- **Proton number increases by 1**
- **Nucleon number remains the same**

Example: $^{14}_{6}\text{C} \rightarrow \ ^{14}_{7}\text{N} + \ ^{0}_{-1}\beta + \ ^{0}_{0}\bar{\nu}_e$

### Beta-Plus ($\beta^+$) Decay

A **beta-plus particle** is a high-energy **positron** (anti-electron) emitted from the nucleus. In $\beta^+$ decay, a **proton turns into a neutron**, emitting a positron and an **electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$):

$$p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$$

$$^A_Z X \rightarrow \ ^{A}_{Z-1} Y + \ ^{0}_{+1}\beta + \ ^{0}_{0}\nu_e$$

When a $\beta^+$ particle is emitted:
- **Proton number decreases by 1**
- **Nucleon number remains the same**

Example: $^{6}_{6}\text{C} \rightarrow \ ^{6}_{5}\text{B} + \ ^{0}_{+1}\beta + \ ^{0}_{0}\nu_e$

> **Exam tip:** Think of beta "**p**lus" as the "**p**roton" that turns into a neutron.

### Neutrino Emission

An **electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$) is a subatomic particle with **no charge** and **negligible mass**. Its antiparticle is the **anti-electron neutrino** ($\bar{\nu}_e$), denoted by a bar over the symbol.

- **Electron anti-neutrinos** ($\bar{\nu}_e$) are produced during $\beta^-$ decay
- **Electron neutrinos** ($\nu_e$) are produced during $\beta^+$ decay

The neutrino was hypothesised to account for the **conservation of energy** in beta decay. Alpha particles have **discrete energies** (sharp spikes on a number vs kinetic energy graph), but beta particles have a **continuous range of energies** (a smooth curve). This continuous spectrum arises because the energy released in beta decay is **shared between the beta particle and the neutrino** (or anti-neutrino) in varying proportions. This was one of the first clues to the neutrino's existence.

The principle of **conservation of momentum and energy** applies in both alpha and beta emission.

## Particles, Antiparticles & Photons

### Antimatter

All particles of matter have an **antimatter** counterpart that is identical in mass but has **opposite charge**.

| Matter | Symbol | Charge | Antimatter | Symbol | Charge |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Electron | $e^-$ | $-1$ | **Positron** | $e^+$ | $+1$ |
| Proton | $p$ | $+1$ | **Antiproton** | $\bar{p}$ | $-1$ |
| Neutron | $n$ | $0$ | **Antineutron** | $\bar{n}$ | $0$ |
| Neutrino | $\nu$ | $0$ | **Antineutrino** | $\bar{\nu}$ | $0$ |

Except for the electron/positron pair, antiparticles are named with the prefix "**anti-**" and denoted by a **bar** ($\bar{\ }$) above the particle symbol.

### Properties of Antiparticles

A particle and its antiparticle have:
- **Opposite charges**
- The **same mass**
- The **same rest mass-energy**

**Rest mass-energy** is the energy equivalent to the mass of a particle when it is at rest.

| Particle / Antiparticle | Mass / kg | Rest Mass Energy / MeV |
|---|---|---|
| Proton / Antiproton | $1.67(3) \times 10^{-27}$ | $938.257$ |
| Neutron / Antineutron | $1.67(5) \times 10^{-27}$ | $939.551$ |
| Electron / Positron | $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ | $0.510999$ |
| Neutrino / Antineutrino | $0$ | $0$ |

> **Exam tip:** The datasheet provides masses in kg and rest mass-energies in MeV for the proton, neutron, electron, and neutrino.

### The Photon Model

**Photons** are fundamental particles that make up all forms of electromagnetic radiation. A **photon** is defined as a **massless "packet" or "quantum" of electromagnetic energy**. Energy is transferred not continuously but as **discrete** packets, each carrying a specific amount of energy.

### Calculating Photon Energy

$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

where $E$ = photon energy (J), $h$ = **Planck's constant** (J s), $f$ = frequency (Hz), $c$ = **speed of light** (m s$^{-1}$), and $\lambda$ = wavelength (m).

Key relationships:
- Higher frequency $\rightarrow$ higher photon energy
- Photon energy is **inversely proportional** to wavelength
- A long-wavelength photon has lower energy than a short-wavelength photon

**Inline example (photon count):** Light of wavelength 490 nm and power 3.6 mW is fully absorbed by a surface. Energy per photon: $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3.0 \times 10^8)}{490 \times 10^{-9}} = 4.06 \times 10^{-19}$ J. Photons per second: $\frac{3.6 \times 10^{-3}}{4.06 \times 10^{-19}} = 8.9 \times 10^{15}$ s$^{-1}$. In 2.0 s: $8.9 \times 10^{15} \times 2 = 1.8 \times 10^{16}$ photons.

### Annihilation

When a particle meets its corresponding antiparticle, they **annihilate**. **Annihilation** is the destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair, converting their combined mass into energy in the form of **two gamma-ray photons** emitted in **opposite directions** (to conserve momentum).

Common annihilation pairs: proton-antiproton, electron-positron.

The **minimum energy of one photon** produced equals the rest mass-energy of one of the particles:

$$E_{\text{min}} = hf_{\text{min}} = E_0$$

where $E_0$ is the rest mass-energy of one particle (or antiparticle).

### Pair Production

**Pair production** is the opposite of annihilation: a high-energy photon spontaneously converts its energy into a **particle-antiparticle pair**. This occurs when the photon interacts with a **nucleus**. The particle and antiparticle move apart in **opposite directions** to conserve momentum.

The **minimum photon energy** required equals the **total rest mass-energy** of both particles produced:

$$E_{\text{min}} = hf_{\text{min}} = 2E_0$$

**Inline example (annihilation wavelength):** For proton-antiproton annihilation, the minimum energy of one photon = $938.257$ MeV $= 938.257 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-13} = 1.501 \times 10^{-10}$ J. Maximum wavelength: $\lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{hc}{E_{\text{min}}} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3.0 \times 10^8)}{1.501 \times 10^{-10}} = 1.32 \times 10^{-15}$ m.

> **Exam tip:** Since Planck's constant is in joules, always convert rest mass-energy from MeV to J before substituting. Use $1 \ \text{MeV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-13}$ J.
