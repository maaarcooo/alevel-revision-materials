# Classification of Particles

## Hadrons

**Hadrons** are subatomic particles made up of **quarks**. Because they contain quarks, hadrons experience the **strong nuclear force**. Hadrons are divided into two classes:

- **Baryons** consist of three quarks (e.g. protons, neutrons).
- **Mesons** consist of a quark and an anti-quark pair (e.g. pions, kaons).

**Anti-hadrons** mirror this structure:

- **Anti-baryons** consist of three anti-quarks (e.g. anti-protons, anti-neutrons).
- **Anti-mesons** consist of a quark and an anti-quark pair, but with each quark swapped for its antiparticle and vice versa compared to the corresponding meson.

Quarks have never been observed in isolation. They are always found in pairs (mesons) or groups of three (baryons). All baryons and mesons carry integer (whole number) charges such as $+1e$, $-1e$, or $0$. This means baryons must be composed of either all quarks or all anti-quarks. A mixed combination like $\bar{u}ud$ does not exist because it would produce a non-integer baryon number.

For mesons, the anti-particle is still a quark-antiquark pair, but with the quark and anti-quark roles reversed. For example, if a meson is $u\bar{d}$, its antiparticle is $\bar{u}d$.

**Method: Determining quark combination from charge.** Since a baryon has three quarks, first rule out meson options (two-quark compositions). Then check whether the particle is a baryon (all quarks) or anti-baryon (all anti-quarks). Finally, sum the quark charges to match the known particle charge. For example, the $\Delta^{++}$ baryon has charge $+2$: the only combination giving $+\frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} = +2$ is $uuu$.

## Baryons

### Baryon Number

The **baryon number**, $B$, counts the number of baryons in an interaction.

| Particle type | Baryon number |
|---|---|
| Baryon | $B = +1$ |
| Anti-baryon | $B = -1$ |
| Non-baryon (mesons, leptons) | $B = 0$ |

Baryon number is a **quantum number** and is **conserved in all interactions**. This conservation law determines whether a given interaction can occur.

At the quark level, each up ($u$), down ($d$), and strange ($s$) quark has $B = +\frac{1}{3}$. Each corresponding anti-quark ($\bar{u}$, $\bar{d}$, $\bar{s}$) has $B = -\frac{1}{3}$. These values are provided on the data sheet.

This is why baryons contain only quarks (three quarks give $B = 3 \times \frac{1}{3} = +1$) and anti-baryons contain only anti-quarks ($B = 3 \times (-\frac{1}{3}) = -1$). A mixed combination such as $u\bar{d}d$ would give $B = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$, which is not an integer and therefore forbidden.

**Method: Checking baryon number conservation.** For $\beta^-$ decay: $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$. Baryon numbers: $1 = 1 + 0 + 0$. Both sides equal 1, so baryon number is conserved.

### The Proton as a Baryon

The **proton** is the most stable baryon because it is the **lightest baryon**. It has the **longest half-life** of any baryon, and all heavier baryons eventually decay into protons. Radioactive decay occurs when heavier particles decay into lighter ones, so a proton cannot decay into any lighter baryon without violating baryon number conservation. The proton's half-life is theorised to be around $10^{32}$ years, and experiments are still underway to detect proton decay.

## Mesons

### Pions

**Pions** ($\pi$-mesons) exist in three charge states: $\pi^+$, $\pi^-$, and $\pi^0$. They contain only **up** and **down** quarks (and their anti-quarks), so they all have **zero strangeness** ($S = 0$). Pions are the **lightest mesons**, making them more stable than other mesons. They were originally discovered in **cosmic rays** and can be observed in a **cloud chamber**.

The antiparticle of $\pi^+$ is $\pi^-$ (and vice versa). The $\pi^0$ is its own antiparticle.

| Particle | Quark composition | Relative charge | Strangeness |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\pi^+$ | $u\bar{d}$ | $+\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = +1$ | $S = 0$ |
| $\pi^-$ | $d\bar{u}$ | $-\frac{1}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = -1$ | $S = 0$ |
| $\pi^0$ | $u\bar{u}$ or $d\bar{d}$ | $0$ | $S = 0$ |

### Pions as Exchange Particles

The **strong nuclear force** is one of the four fundamental interactions. It keeps protons and neutrons bound together in a nucleus. Fundamental interactions are mediated by **exchange particles**. The **pion** is the exchange particle of the strong nuclear force between **baryons** (i.e. between nucleons). In a nucleus, protons and neutrons exchange pions to exhibit the strong force. Pions are said to **mediate** the strong nuclear force. The pion created during exchange is a **virtual particle**, representing a temporary violation of energy and mass conservation that is not directly observed.

For reference (not required for the AQA exam): **gluons** are the exchange particle of the strong interaction between **quarks**, while **pions** mediate the strong nuclear force between **nucleons**. Collectively, these are referred to as the **strong force**.

### Kaons

**Kaons** (K-mesons) exist as $K^+$, $K^-$, and $K^0$. They contain a **strange quark**, which can be paired with either an up or down quark, giving kaons the property of **strangeness**.

The antiparticle of $K^+$ is $K^-$ (and vice versa). Unlike $\pi^0$, the neutral kaon $K^0$ is **not** its own antiparticle (its antiparticle is $\bar{K}^0$).

Kaons are heavier and more unstable than pions. They normally decay into **pions**. Despite being unstable, kaons have unusually **long lifetimes** compared to other mesons, which is characteristic of particles containing strange quarks.

| Particle | Quark composition | Relative charge | Strangeness |
|---|---|---|---|
| $K^+$ | $u\bar{s}$ | $+\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = +1$ | $S = +1$ |
| $K^-$ | $s\bar{u}$ | $-\frac{1}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = -1$ | $S = -1$ |
| $K^0$ | $d\bar{s}$ | $-\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = 0$ | $S = +1$ |
| $\bar{K}^0$ | $s\bar{d}$ | $-\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = 0$ | $S = -1$ |

### Kaon Production and Decay

Kaons are **produced** via the **strong interaction**, where strangeness is always conserved. They are produced in pairs (pair production). Example:

$$p + p \rightarrow p + p + K^+ + K^-$$

Strangeness on both sides is zero ($S = 0$ on the left, $S = +1 + (-1) = 0$ on the right), so strangeness is conserved.

Kaons **decay** via the **weak interaction**. Example:

$$K^0 \rightarrow \pi^+ + \pi^-$$

The $K^0$ has $S = +1$, but pions have $S = 0$, so strangeness is **not conserved**. This confirms the decay occurs through the weak interaction.

## Leptons

### Leptons and Antileptons

**Leptons** are **fundamental** (elementary) particles, meaning they are not made up of quarks or any other sub-particles. Leptons interact via the **weak**, **gravitational**, and **electromagnetic** interactions. They do **not** interact via the strong nuclear force.

The most common leptons are:

| Lepton | Symbol | Charge | Mass |
|---|---|---|---|
| Electron | $e^-$ | $-1e$ | $\approx 0.0005\text{ u}$ |
| Electron neutrino | $\nu_e$ | $0$ | $\approx 0$ |
| Muon | $\mu^-$ | $-1e$ | $\approx 0.1\text{ u}$ |
| Muon neutrino | $\nu_\mu$ | $0$ | $\approx 0$ |

The **muon** is similar to the electron but approximately 200 times heavier. Both carry charge $-1e$. **Neutrinos** are the most abundant leptons in the universe and have **no charge** and **negligible mass**.

Although quarks are also fundamental particles, they are not classified as leptons because quarks interact with the strong force, whereas leptons do not.

### Lepton Number

The **lepton number**, $L$, counts the number of leptons in an interaction.

| Particle type | Lepton number |
|---|---|
| Lepton ($e^-$, $\mu^-$, $\nu_e$, $\nu_\mu$) | $L = +1$ |
| Anti-lepton ($e^+$, $\mu^+$, $\bar{\nu}_e$, $\bar{\nu}_\mu$) | $L = -1$ |
| Non-lepton (baryons, mesons) | $L = 0$ |

Lepton number is a quantum number and is **conserved in all interactions**.

**Method: Using lepton number to identify unknown particles.** In the decay $n + \mu^+ \rightarrow p + X$, assign lepton numbers: $0 + (-1) = 0 + X$, giving $X = -1$. Since $X$ has $L = -1$, it is an **anti-neutrino**.

Note: The lepton number of each particle is provided on the data sheet. Quarks have $L = 0$.

### Muon Decay

**Muons** ($\mu^-$) typically decay into an **electron**. **Anti-muons** ($\mu^+$) typically decay into a **positron**. Muon decay occurs through the **weak interaction**, identified by the exchange of a $W^-$ boson on a Feynman diagram.

The muon decay equation is:

$$\mu^- \rightarrow e^- + \bar{\nu}_e + \nu_\mu$$

**Method: Verifying conservation laws in muon decay.**

Charge ($q$): $-1 = -1 + 0 + 0$ . Conserved.

Electron lepton number ($L_e$): $0 = +1 + (-1) + 0$ . Conserved.

Muon lepton number ($L_\mu$): $+1 = 0 + 0 + 1$ . Conserved.

All three quantities balance, confirming muon decay satisfies these conservation laws.

## Quarks and Antiquarks

### Properties of Quarks

The three most common **flavours** of quarks are **up** ($u$), **down** ($d$), and **strange** ($s$). The majority of hadrons are built from combinations of these quarks. Each quark has a charge, baryon number, and strangeness (all provided on the data sheet).

| Quark | Charge | Baryon number | Strangeness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up ($u$) | $+\frac{2}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | $0$ |
| Down ($d$) | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | $0$ |
| Strange ($s$) | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ | $+\frac{1}{3}$ | $-1$ |

The charge, baryon number, and strangeness of a hadron are each determined by summing the corresponding values of its constituent quarks. For example, a proton ($uud$): charge $= +\frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = +1e$.

### Properties of Anti-quarks

Each quark has a corresponding **anti-quark** with identical mass but **opposite** charge, baryon number, and strangeness.

| Anti-quark | Charge | Baryon number | Strangeness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anti-up ($\bar{u}$) | $-\frac{2}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | $0$ |
| Anti-down ($\bar{d}$) | $+\frac{1}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | $0$ |
| Anti-strange ($\bar{s}$) | $+\frac{1}{3}e$ | $-\frac{1}{3}$ | $+1$ |

The strangeness values $S = -1$ for the strange quark and $S = +1$ for the anti-strange quark are unique to the strange quark flavour. Up and down quarks (and their anti-quarks) have $S = 0$.

**Method: Deducing quark structure from properties.** The $K^-$ has charge $-1$ and strangeness $-1$. It is a meson (quark-antiquark pair). Strangeness $-1$ requires a strange quark ($s$, charge $-\frac{1}{3}$). The remaining charge must be $-1 - (-\frac{1}{3}) = -\frac{2}{3}$, which is the anti-up quark ($\bar{u}$). Therefore $K^- = s\bar{u}$.

### Quark Combinations

**Baryons:**

| Baryon | Quark composition |
|---|---|
| Proton ($p$) | $uud$ |
| Neutron ($n$) | $udd$ |

**Mesons (Pions):**

| Meson | Quark composition |
|---|---|
| $\pi^+$ | $u\bar{d}$ |
| $\pi^-$ | $\bar{u}d$ |
| $\pi^0$ | $u\bar{u}$ or $d\bar{d}$ |

**Mesons (Kaons):**

| Meson | Quark composition |
|---|---|
| $K^+$ | $u\bar{s}$ |
| $K^-$ | $\bar{u}s$ |
| $K^0$ | $d\bar{s}$ or $\bar{d}s$ |

The quark combinations for protons, neutrons, pions, and kaons must be memorised for the exam. Quark charges are on the data sheet, so anti-quark charges can be derived by taking opposite signs.

**Inline example: counting quarks in a nucleus.** For ${}^{56}_{26}\text{Fe}$: 26 protons ($uud$, 2 up quarks each) and 30 neutrons ($udd$, 1 up quark each). Total up quarks $= 26 \times 2 + 30 \times 1 = 82$.

## Strange Quarks

### Strange Particles

**Strange particles** are particles containing a **strange** or **anti-strange** quark (e.g. kaons). Strange particles are always:

- **Produced** through the **strong interaction**
- **Decay** through the **weak interaction**
- Produced in quark-antiquark **pairs** (e.g. $s$ and $\bar{s}$ appear together)

In production via the strong interaction, the exchange particle is a **gluon**. In decay via the weak interaction, the exchange particle is a **W boson** ($W^+$ or $W^-$).

### Strangeness

**Strangeness**, $S$, is a quantum number that is **conserved in every interaction except the weak interaction**.

| Quark content | Strangeness |
|---|---|
| Contains anti-strange quark ($\bar{s}$) | $S = +1$ |
| Contains strange quark ($s$) | $S = -1$ |
| No strange or anti-strange quark | $S = 0$ |

In weak interactions, strangeness can change by $0$, $+1$, or $-1$. This constraint means that strange particles must always be **produced in pairs** via the strong interaction (so that the total strangeness is conserved in production).

**Method: Proving a decay occurs via the weak interaction.** The sigma baryon $\Sigma^+$ ($suu$) decays as $\Sigma^+ \rightarrow \pi^0 + p$. The $\Sigma^+$ has $S = -1$ (contains an $s$ quark). The $\pi^0$ and $p$ both have $S = 0$. Strangeness changes from $-1$ to $0$, a change of $+1$. Since strangeness is conserved in all interactions except the weak interaction, this decay must proceed via the **weak interaction**.

## Collaborative Efforts in Particle Physics

The field of particle physics evolves as new theories are developed and tested. The process follows the scientific method: physicists **hypothesise** the existence of a new particle and its properties (e.g. the neutrino was hypothesised to account for energy conservation in beta decay), experiments are carried out to find the particle, different experiments are combined to suggest a discovery, and if confirmed by repeated experiments that reduce **experimental uncertainty**, the theory is **validated** and accepted by the scientific community.

### Particle Accelerators

Particle physics experiments use complex machines called **particle accelerators** to collide particles at very high speeds (close to the **speed of light**) in order to produce new particles and reveal the inner structure of existing ones. Accelerators can be **linear** or **circular**. Circular accelerators require extremely powerful **superconducting magnets** to maintain the circular orbits of high-speed particles.

The expense and complexity of these machines requires large groups of scientists and engineers from around the world to **collaborate**, providing the funding and expertise needed to design, construct, and operate experiments.

### The Large Hadron Collider

The **Large Hadron Collider** (LHC) at **CERN** (Geneva, France-Switzerland border) is the world's largest and highest-energy particle collider. Around 2500 scientists from 110 nationalities work there. Two proton beams are accelerated in opposite directions and collide at four main detectors: **ATLAS**, **CMS**, **ALICE**, and **LHCb**. ATLAS and CMS are the largest, researching fundamental particles, dark matter, and physics beyond the Standard Model. Both ATLAS and CMS independently detected the **Higgs Boson** (2012), demonstrating that a theory is only validated when it can be confirmed by independent, repeated experiments.

The structure of the LHC is not tested in the exam, but students should be able to discuss how the scale of these experiments demonstrates that particle physics research demands extensive international collaboration.
