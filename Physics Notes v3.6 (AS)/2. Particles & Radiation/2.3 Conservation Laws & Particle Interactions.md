# Conservation Laws & Particle Interactions

## The Four Fundamental Interactions

There are four **fundamental interactions** (or **fundamental forces**):

| Force | Relative strength | Range | Affects | Exchange particle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strong nuclear | Strongest | $\sim 10^{-15}$ m | Hadrons only | Pion ($\pi^+, \pi^-, \pi^0$) / Gluon |
| Electromagnetic | ŌĆö | Infinite | Charged particles only | Virtual photon ($\gamma$) |
| Weak nuclear | ŌĆö | Up to $10^{-18}$ m | All particles | $W^+, W^-$ or $Z^0$ boson |
| Gravity | Weakest | Infinite | Particles with mass | Graviton (theorised, not yet discovered) |

Gravity is so weak that it has no noticeable effect at the particle scale and plays no part in particle interactions.

### Exchange (Virtual) Particles

When two particles interact, there cannot be instantaneous action at a distance. Instead, a **virtual particle** (also called an **exchange particle**) is created. Virtual particles exist only briefly and carry the fundamental force between the interacting particles. Exchange particles are collectively known as **gauge bosons**.

A skateboard analogy illustrates how exchange particles mediate forces. Two people on skateboards throwing a ball between them move apart (modelling **repulsion**), whereas throwing a boomerang causes them to move together (modelling **attraction**).

---

## The Electromagnetic Force

The electromagnetic force acts only between **charged** particles. It is carried by the **virtual photon** ($\gamma$), which has the following properties:

- Zero mass
- Zero charge
- It is its own antiparticle

Electromagnetic interactions occur whenever two charged particles interact. For example, when two electrons approach each other, a virtual photon is exchanged between them, producing electrostatic **repulsion**. The electromagnetic force is also responsible for binding electrons to atoms, due to the attractive force between negative electrons and the positive nucleus.

---

## Hadrons and the Strong Nuclear Force

**Hadrons** are particles composed of quarks, so they are subject to the **strong** interaction. The exchange particle of the strong interaction depends on the scale:

- The **pion** ($\pi$) mediates the strong force between nucleons
- The **gluon** ($g$) mediates the strong force between individual quarks

Because leptons are not made of quarks, they cannot interact via the strong force.

---

## The Weak Interaction

The weak interaction is responsible for **radioactive decay** of atoms. It is mediated by the $W^-$, $W^+$, or $Z^0$ boson, with the specific boson depending on the type of interaction. The weak interaction affects **all** particles.

### $\beta^-$ Decay

In $\beta^-$ decay, a **neutron** turns into a **proton**, emitting an **electron** and an **anti-electron neutrino**:

$$n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

The exchange particle is the $W^-$ boson. At the quark level, a **down** quark transforms into an **up** quark. The $W^-$ boson carries away the negative charge of the down quark, providing the charge for the electron and anti-neutrino.

### $\beta^+$ Decay

In $\beta^+$ decay, a **proton** turns into a **neutron**, emitting a **positron** and an **electron neutrino**:

$$p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$$

The exchange particle is the $W^+$ boson. At the quark level, an **up** quark transforms into a **down** quark. The $W^+$ boson carries away the positive charge of the up quark, providing the charge for the positron and neutrino.

**Exam tip:** The sign of the $W$ boson matches the type of beta decay. $W^-$ is exchanged in $\beta^-$ decay and $W^+$ in $\beta^+$ decay.

### Electron Capture and Electron-Proton Collisions

Both electron capture and electron-proton collisions share the same decay equation:

$$p + e^- \rightarrow n + \nu_e$$

**Electron capture** occurs when an atomic electron is absorbed by a proton in the nucleus, producing a neutron and an electron neutrino. In this process the **proton** acts on the electron, so the exchange particle is the $W^+$ boson (transferred from the proton side to the lepton side).

**Electron-proton collisions** are similar: an electron collides with a proton, producing a neutron and an electron neutrino. Here the **electron** acts on the proton, so the exchange particle is the $W^-$ boson (transferred from the electron side to the hadron side).

The two Feynman diagrams are identical in structure except for the sign and direction of the $W$ boson.

## Feynman Diagrams

**Feynman diagrams** are a way of visualising particle interactions and the exchange particles involved.

### Rules for Feynman Diagrams

- The y-axis represents **time** and the x-axis represents **space**
- A **vertex** is where particle lines and exchange-particle lines meet, representing a point of interaction (electromagnetic, weak, or strong)
- Incoming particles enter at the **bottom**, outgoing particles leave at the **top**
- Particles are represented by **straight lines** with **arrows** pointing forward in time
- Exchange particles are represented by **wavy lines** with no arrows on the line itself
- The transfer of exchange particles is from left to right unless indicated by an arrow above the wavy line
- **Hadrons/quarks** appear on the left and **leptons** on the right of a vertex; they must never meet at the same vertex
- **Charge**, **baryon number**, and **lepton number** must be conserved at each vertex
- Lines must **not** cross over

### Key Feynman Diagrams

**Electromagnetic repulsion (e.g. electron-electron):** Two electrons enter at the bottom left and right, exchange a virtual photon ($\gamma$) horizontally between them, and exit at the top diverging away from each other.

**$\beta^-$ decay:** A neutron enters at the bottom left and exits as a proton at the top left. At the vertex, a $W^-$ boson is emitted horizontally to the right, where it decays into an electron ($e^-$, upper right) and an anti-electron neutrino ($\bar{\nu}_e$, lower right).

**$\beta^+$ decay:** A proton enters at the bottom left and exits as a neutron at the top left. At the vertex, a $W^+$ boson is emitted horizontally to the right, where it decays into a positron ($e^+$, upper right) and an electron neutrino ($\nu_e$, lower right).

**Electron capture:** A proton enters at the bottom left and an electron enters at the bottom right. A $W^+$ boson is exchanged from left to right (indicated by an arrow above the wavy line). The proton becomes a neutron (top left) and the electron becomes an electron neutrino (top right).

**Electron-proton collision:** Same layout as electron capture, but now a $W^-$ boson is exchanged from right to left (arrow above the wavy line points from the electron side to the proton side). The products are the same: neutron and electron neutrino.

### Quark-Level Feynman Diagrams

In $\beta^-$ decay, the neutron's quark content changes from $udd$ to $uud$ (proton). The transforming down quark emits a $W^-$ boson, which then decays into $e^-$ and $\bar{\nu}_e$. The quark equation is:

$$d \rightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

In $\beta^+$ decay, the proton's quark content changes from $uud$ to $udd$ (neutron). The transforming up quark emits a $W^+$ boson, which then decays into $e^+$ and $\nu_e$. The quark equation is:

$$u \rightarrow d + e^+ + \nu_e$$

**Exam tip:** The most common mistakes in Feynman diagram questions are missing arrows on charged gauge bosons or on particle lines. All particles must be labelled accurately, though you are not required to sketch and label the space/time axes.

## Application of Conservation Laws

All particle interactions must obey conservation of:

- **Charge** ($Q$)
- **Baryon number** ($B$)
- **Lepton number** ($L$)
- **Strangeness** ($S$)
- **Energy** (mass-energy)
- **Momentum**

**Strangeness** does not need to be conserved in **weak interactions**. In a weak interaction, strangeness can change by $0$, $+1$, or $-1$.

**Quantum numbers** such as $Q$, $B$, $L$, and $S$ can only take discrete values (e.g. $0, +1, -1, +\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{3}$).

To determine whether a particle interaction is permitted, check whether each quantum number is equal on both sides of the equation. If even one quantity (apart from strangeness in a weak interaction) is not conserved, the interaction **cannot occur**.

### Worked method: checking conservation laws

For the kaon decay $K^+ \rightarrow \mu^+ + \bar{\nu}_\mu$:

| Quantity | LHS | RHS | Conserved? |
|---|---|---|---|
| $Q$ | $+1$ | $+1 + 0 = +1$ | Yes |
| $B$ | $0$ | $0 + 0 = 0$ | Yes |
| $L$ | $0$ | $-1 + 1 = 0$ | Yes |
| $S$ | $+1$ | $0 + 0 = 0$ | No |

$Q$, $B$, and $L$ are conserved but $S$ is not, so this decay must proceed via the **weak interaction** (the only interaction in which strangeness need not be conserved).

### Worked method: $\Lambda^0$ decay check

For $\Lambda^0 \rightarrow \pi^- + e^+ + \nu_e$ (where $\Lambda^0$ has quark content $uds$):

| Quantity | LHS | RHS | Conserved? |
|---|---|---|---|
| $Q$ | $0$ | $-1 + 1 + 0 = 0$ | Yes |
| $S$ | $-1$ | $0 + 0 + 0 = 0$ | No |
| $B$ | $+1$ | $0 + 0 + 0 = 0$ | No |
| $L$ | $0$ | $0 + (-1) + 1 = 0$ | Yes |

Baryon number is **not** conserved, so this interaction is **not permitted**.

### Worked method: charge conservation in $\beta^-$ decay via the quark model

For $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$:

- Neutron quark composition: $udd$. Total charge: $+\tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} = 0$.
- Proton quark composition: $uud$. Total charge: $+\tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} = +1$.
- Electron charge: $-1$. Anti-neutrino charge: $0$. RHS total: $+1 - 1 + 0 = 0$.
- Both sides equal $0$, so charge is conserved.

**Exam tip:** Quantum numbers for exotic particles will be given in the question. Standard particle properties can be found on the data sheet or deduced from quark content.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Description |
|---|---|
| $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$ | $\beta^-$ decay |
| $p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$ | $\beta^+$ decay |
| $p + e^- \rightarrow n + \nu_e$ | Electron capture / electron-proton collision |
| $d \rightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$ | $\beta^-$ decay at quark level |
| $u \rightarrow d + e^+ + \nu_e$ | $\beta^+$ decay at quark level |
