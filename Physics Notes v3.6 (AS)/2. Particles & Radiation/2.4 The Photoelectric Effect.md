# The Photoelectric Effect

## The Electronvolt

The **electronvolt** (eV) is a unit used to express very small energies, because quantum energies are much smaller than 1 joule.

It is derived from the definition of **potential difference**:

$$V = \frac{E}{Q}$$

where $V$ = potential difference (V), $E$ = energy (J), $Q$ = charge (C).

When an electron (charge $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C) travels through a potential difference of 1 V, the energy transferred is:

$$E = QV = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \times 1 = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

An electronvolt is therefore defined as **the energy gained by an electron travelling through a potential difference of one volt**:

$$1 \text{ eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

To convert between units:

- eV $\to$ J: **multiply** by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$
- J $\to$ eV: **divide** by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$

### Relation to Kinetic Energy

When a charged particle accelerates from rest through a potential difference, the energy gained equals the kinetic energy acquired:

$$eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

where $e$ = charge of an electron ($1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C), $V$ = potential difference (V), $m$ = mass of the particle (kg), $v$ = velocity of the particle (m s$^{-1}$).

Rearranging for speed:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2eV}{m}}$$

**Inline example (photon energy in eV).** For light of wavelength 700 nm: calculate $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3.0 \times 10^{8})}{700 \times 10^{-9}} = 2.84 \times 10^{-19}$ J, then convert to eV by dividing: $\frac{2.84 \times 10^{-19}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}} = 1.78$ eV. This confirms "about 1.8 eV."

**Exam tip.** The value of 1 eV is given on the data sheet, but converting fluently between eV and J is essential for speed on this topic.

## Threshold Frequency & Work Function

### Threshold Frequency

The **photoelectric effect** is the phenomenon in which electrons are emitted from the surface of a metal upon the absorption of electromagnetic radiation. Electrons removed in this way are called **photoelectrons**.

The photoelectric effect provides key evidence that light behaves as a particle: light is **quantised**, meaning it is carried in discrete packets (photons). Each electron can absorb only a single photon, so only photons with frequencies above a **threshold frequency** can cause emission. No amount of low-frequency photons will release an electron, regardless of intensity.

**Diagram description.** Light rays strike a metal surface. Electrons within the metal absorb individual photons and, if the photon energy is sufficient, are ejected from the surface as photoelectrons.

### Threshold Frequency & Wavelength

The **threshold frequency** $f_0$ is defined as **the minimum frequency of incident electromagnetic radiation required to remove a photoelectron from the surface of a metal**.

The **threshold wavelength** $\lambda_0$ is defined as **the longest wavelength of incident electromagnetic radiation that would remove a photoelectron from the surface of a metal**.

They are related by the wave equation with $v = c$ (speed of light):

$$c = f_0 \lambda_0$$

Both $f_0$ and $\lambda_0$ are properties of the material and vary from metal to metal.

| Metal | Threshold Frequency $f_0$ / Hz | Threshold Wavelength $\lambda_0$ / nm |
|---|---|---|
| Sodium | $4.40 \times 10^{14}$ | 682 |
| Potassium | $5.56 \times 10^{14}$ | 540 |
| Zinc | $1.02 \times 10^{15}$ | 294 |
| Iron | $1.04 \times 10^{15}$ | 289 |
| Copper | $1.13 \times 10^{15}$ | 266 |
| Gold | $1.23 \times 10^{15}$ | 244 |
| Silver | $9.71 \times 10^{15}$ | 30.9 |

**Exam tip (coconut shy analogy).** Throwing many table tennis balls (low-frequency photons) at a coconut will never dislodge it. A single shot from a pistol (a high-frequency photon) knocks it off immediately. The number of projectiles does not matter; only the energy per projectile determines whether emission occurs.

### The Work Function

The **work function** $\Phi$ (or threshold energy) of a material is defined as **the minimum energy required to release a photoelectron from the surface of a metal**.

Electrons in a metal can be modelled as sitting inside an "**energy well**." The depth of the well equals $\Phi$. A single electron absorbs one photon, and can only escape if the photon energy $\geq \Phi$.

Different metals have different work functions because their surface electrons are bound with different strengths:

- A more tightly bound electron requires more energy to escape (deeper well, larger $\Phi$).
- A less tightly bound electron requires less energy (shallower well, smaller $\Phi$).

**Alkali metals** (e.g. sodium, potassium) have threshold frequencies in the **visible light** region because the attractive forces between surface electrons and positive metal ions are relatively weak.

**Transition metals** (e.g. zinc, iron) have threshold frequencies in the **ultraviolet** region because these attractive forces are much stronger.

### Stopping Potential

The **stopping potential** $V_s$ is defined as **the potential difference required to stop photoelectron emission from occurring**.

**Experimental setup.** Photons strike the **emitter plate**, causing photoelectrons to be released. These electrons cross a vacuum gap and are collected at the **collector plate**, producing a **photoelectric current**. A variable power supply is connected so that the emitter plate is made positive, opposing the electron motion across the gap.

As the opposing p.d. increases, fewer electrons have enough kinetic energy to reach the collector. At the stopping potential $V_s$, no electrons can cross the gap. At this point, the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons equals the work done against the electric field:

$$E_{k(\text{max})} = eV_s$$

where $e$ = charge on an electron, $V_s$ = stopping potential.

**Exam tip.** The stopping voltage is technically negative (it opposes electron motion), but in calculations only its magnitude matters.

### Intensity and Stopping Potential

Increasing the **intensity** of incident radiation increases the number of photons striking the metal per second, and therefore increases the number of photoelectrons emitted (i.e. the **photoelectric current** increases). However, the stopping potential remains the same, showing that **intensity does not affect the kinetic energy of the photoelectrons**.

The maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons depends only on:

- The **frequency** (or wavelength) of the incident photons
- The **work function** of the metal

**Graph description.** A plot of photoelectric current against p.d. across the plates shows two curves (high and low intensity UV). Both curves reach zero current at the same stopping potential $-V_s$, but the high-intensity curve saturates at a higher current.

**Changing frequency at constant intensity.** If frequency increases while intensity stays constant, the photoelectric current decreases. This is because intensity $I = \frac{nhf}{tA}$, where $n$ is the number of photons. If $f$ increases and $I$ is fixed, then $n$ must decrease, so fewer photoelectrons are emitted per second.

**Changing frequency at constant number of photons.** If the number of photons per second is held constant and frequency changes, the photoelectric current remains constant (same number of photons means same number of photoelectrons), but the kinetic energy of each photoelectron changes.

## The Photoelectric Equation

The energy of a photon is $E = hf$. Photons with frequencies above the threshold frequency carry more energy than the work function. An amount of energy equal to $\Phi$ is used to release the photoelectron; the remainder is transferred as kinetic energy.

The **photoelectric equation** is:

$$hf = \Phi + E_{k(\text{max})}$$

equivalently:

$$hf = \Phi + \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{max}}^2$$

where $h$ = Planck's constant (J s), $f$ = frequency of incident radiation (Hz), $\Phi$ = work function of the material (J), $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{max}}^2 = E_{k(\text{max})}$ = maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons (J).

Key consequences of this equation:

- If $hf < \Phi$, no electrons are emitted.
- At the threshold frequency $f_0$, emission just begins and $E_{k(\text{max})} = 0$, so $hf_0 = \Phi$.
- $E_{k(\text{max})}$ depends only on the frequency of the incident photon, not on intensity.
- The majority of photoelectrons have kinetic energies less than $E_{k(\text{max})}$.

**Exam tip.** When using this equation, $hf$, $\Phi$, and $E_{k(\text{max})}$ must all be in the same units (joules). Convert any values given in eV by multiplying by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$.

### Graphical Representation of Work Function

Rearranging the photoelectric equation into straight-line form $y = mx + c$:

$$E_{k(\text{max})} = hf - \Phi$$

A graph of $E_{k(\text{max})}$ (y-axis) against $f$ (x-axis) gives:

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gradient | Planck's constant $h$ |
| y-intercept | $-\Phi$ (negative of the work function) |
| x-intercept | Threshold frequency $f_0$ |
| Region below $f_0$ | No electrons emitted |

**Inline example (work function from a graph).** For sodium, reading $f_0 = 4 \times 10^{14}$ Hz from the x-intercept: $\Phi = hf_0 = (6.63 \times 10^{-34})(4 \times 10^{14}) = 2.652 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= \frac{2.652 \times 10^{-19}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}} = 1.66$ eV.

### Maximum Kinetic Energy

**Kinetic energy and intensity.** The kinetic energy of photoelectrons is **independent of the intensity** of incident radiation, because each electron can absorb only one photon. Kinetic energy depends only on the **frequency** of the incident radiation. Increasing intensity increases the **number** of photoelectrons emitted, not their individual energies.

**Why is the kinetic energy a maximum?** All electrons at a given frequency absorb the same photon energy, but the energy required to remove an electron varies depending on its depth below the surface. **Surface electrons** require the least energy to escape (just the work function), so they emerge with the maximum kinetic energy. Electrons deeper in the metal use some absorbed energy to reach the surface, leaving less kinetic energy after escape.

### Photoelectric Current

The **photoelectric current** measures the number of photoelectrons emitted per second, calculated as the number of electrons emitted multiplied by the charge on one electron.

Photoelectric current is **proportional to the intensity** of the incident radiation (for $f > f_0$), because intensity is proportional to the number of photons striking the metal per second, and each photoelectron absorbs a single photon.

**Graph descriptions (three key sketch graphs):**

1. **$E_{k(\text{max})}$ vs light frequency:** a straight line starting from $f_0$ on the x-axis, with gradient $h$, and no emission for $f < f_0$.
2. **$E_{k(\text{max})}$ vs light intensity (for $f > f_0$):** a horizontal line, showing kinetic energy is independent of intensity.
3. **Photoelectric current vs light intensity (for $f > f_0$):** a straight line through the origin, showing current is proportional to intensity.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $1 \text{ eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$ | Electronvolt definition |
| $V = \frac{E}{Q}$ | Potential difference |
| $eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ | KE gained by electron accelerated from rest |
| $v = \sqrt{\frac{2eV}{m}}$ | Speed of electron after acceleration |
| $E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$ | Photon energy |
| $c = f\lambda$ | Wave equation (for light) |
| $hf = \Phi + E_{k(\text{max})}$ | Photoelectric equation |
| $hf_0 = \Phi$ | Threshold condition |
| $E_{k(\text{max})} = eV_s$ | Max KE from stopping potential |
| $I = \frac{nhf}{tA}$ | Intensity in terms of photon count |
