# Stationary Waves

## Stationary Waves

**Standing waves** (stationary waves) are produced by the **superposition** of two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in **opposite directions**. This is usually achieved by a travelling wave and its reflection. The superposition produces a wave pattern in which the peaks and troughs do not move. Stationary waves **store** energy, unlike progressive waves which **transfer** energy.

### Comparing Progressive and Stationary Waves

| Property | Progressive Waves | Stationary Waves |
|---|---|---|
| Amplitude | All points have the same amplitude (in turn) | Each point has a different amplitude depending on the amount of superposition |
| Phase | Points exactly one wavelength apart are in phase. Phase of points within one wavelength ranges from $0°$ to $360°$ | Points between nodes are in phase. Points on either side of a node are out of phase (antiphase) |
| Energy | Transferred along the wave | Stored, not transferred |
| Nodes and antinodes | Does not have nodes or antinodes | Has nodes and antinodes |
| Wave speed | The speed at which the wave moves through a medium | Each point oscillates at a different speed. The overall wave does not move |

### Nodes and Antinodes

A stationary wave is made up of **nodes** and **antinodes**.

A **node** is a point of no vibration (zero displacement at all times). An **antinode** is a point of maximum amplitude.

Nodes and antinodes do not move along the string. Nodes are fixed in position and antinodes only move in the vertical direction.

The phase difference between any two points on a stationary wave is either exactly in phase ($0°$) or exactly in antiphase ($180°$):

- Points between adjacent nodes are **in phase** with each other.
- Points separated by an **odd** number of nodes are **out of phase** (antiphase).
- Points separated by an **even** number of nodes are **in phase**.

The distance between two adjacent nodes (or two adjacent antinodes) is $\frac{\lambda}{2}$. The total string length $L$ accommodates only a portion of the full wavelength, depending on the harmonic.

**Examiner tip:** In exam questions, string lengths will only be whole or half numbers of wavelengths. Count the loops (antinodes) carefully: for example, a pattern of 3 nodes and 2 antinodes spans one full wavelength, while 2 nodes and 3 antinodes spans $\frac{3\lambda}{2}$.

**Worked example (method: counting wavelengths and identifying nodes/antinodes).** A stretched string shows a stationary wave pattern spanning length $L$. To determine how many wavelengths fit in $L$, count the number of half-loops: if there are 5 half-loops, $L = \frac{5\lambda}{2}$, i.e. $2\frac{1}{2}$ wavelengths. Points of zero displacement are nodes and points of maximum displacement are antinodes.

## Formation of Stationary Waves

### The Principle of Superposition

The **principle of superposition** states: when two or more waves with the same frequency arrive at a point, the resultant displacement is the sum of the displacements of each wave.

When two waves with the same frequency and amplitude arrive at a point, they superpose either:

- **In phase**, causing **constructive interference**: peaks and troughs align on both waves, and the resultant wave has double the amplitude ($2A$).
- **In anti-phase**, causing **destructive interference**: peaks of one wave align with troughs of the other, and the resultant wave has zero amplitude.

The principle of superposition applies to all types of waves: transverse and longitudinal, progressive and stationary.

### The Formation of Stationary Waves

A stationary wave is formed when two waves travelling in opposite directions along the same line with the same frequency superpose. The waves must have the same wavelength and a similar amplitude.

As a result of superposition, a resultant wave is produced:

- At the **nodes**, the two waves are in anti-phase, so destructive interference occurs and the waves cancel each other out.
- At the **antinodes**, the two waves are in phase, so constructive interference occurs and the waves add together.

Each point on the stationary wave has a **different** amplitude, unlike a progressive wave where each point has the **same** amplitude.

The formation can be visualised as two identical waves approaching from opposite directions. As they pass through each other, the resultant alternates between full constructive interference (maximum displacement envelope) and full destructive interference (flat line), but the nodes and antinodes remain at fixed positions.

### Examples of Stationary Waves

**Stretched strings.** Vibrations caused by stationary waves on a stretched string produce **sound**. This is how stringed instruments such as guitars and violins work. It can be demonstrated using a string under tension fixed at one end and driven by an oscillator at the other. One end is attached to the oscillator, the other passes over a pulley with a hanging mass to keep the string taut. The string is clamped at both sides and the frequency is adjusted until standing waves form. At specific frequencies, known as **resonant frequencies**, a whole number of half wavelengths fits on the length of the string. As the resonant frequencies are reached, standing waves with different numbers of nodes and antinodes form.

**Microwaves.** A microwave source is placed in line with a metal reflecting plate, with a small probe detector between the two. The reflector can be moved to vary the stationary wave pattern. Moving the detector along the line between source and reflector picks up the minima (nodes) and maxima (antinodes) of the stationary wave pattern.

**Sound waves.** Stationary sound waves can form inside an air column, which is how instruments such as clarinets and organs work. This is demonstrated by placing fine powder inside a horizontal tube with a loudspeaker at the open end. At certain frequencies, the powder collects into evenly spaced heaps at the positions of nodes (zero disturbance), leaving gaps at the antinodes. For a stationary wave to form in the air column, there must be a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open (loudspeaker) end.

**Examiner tip:** In exam answers, always refer back to the specific experimental context. For example: the wave produced by a loudspeaker reflects at the closed end of a tube. This reflected wave, with the same frequency, overlaps the initial wave to create a stationary wave.

## Harmonics

Stationary waves can have different wave patterns known as **harmonics**. These depend on the **frequency** of the vibration and the **situation** in which they are created (e.g. string fixed at both ends, pipe open or closed). As the frequency is increased, higher harmonics appear.

### Harmonics on a String

When a stationary wave is formed on a string fixed at both ends, the simplest pattern is a single loop consisting of two nodes (at each fixed end) and one antinode (in the centre). This is the **first harmonic** (or **fundamental frequency**).

The resonant frequencies depend on the string length $L$ and the wave speed $v$, and can be calculated using the wave equation.

**First harmonic ($n = 1$):** One loop (half a wavelength fits on the string).

$$\lambda_1 = 2L \qquad f_1 = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{v}{2L}$$

**Second harmonic ($n = 2$):** Two loops, three nodes, two antinodes.

$$\lambda_2 = L \qquad f_2 = \frac{v}{L} = 2f_1$$

**Third harmonic ($n = 3$):** Three loops, four nodes, three antinodes.

$$\lambda_3 = \frac{2L}{3} \qquad f_3 = \frac{3v}{2L} = 3f_1$$

**General pattern for the $n$th harmonic:** $n$ antinodes and $n + 1$ nodes.

$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \qquad f_n = \frac{nv}{2L} = n \, f_1$$

The frequency of any harmonic is an integer multiple of the first harmonic: $f_n = n \times f_1$.

**Worked example (method: using the harmonic relationship).** A string vibrating in the third harmonic has $f_3 = 150$ Hz. Since $f_3 = 3f_1$, the fundamental is $f_1 = 150 \div 3 = 50$ Hz. The fifth harmonic is $f_5 = 5f_1 = 5 \times 50 = 250$ Hz.

**Examiner tip:** Match the correct wavelength to the harmonic number. The first harmonic ($n = 1$) is the lowest frequency, with half a wavelength on the string. The second harmonic ($n = 2$) has a full wavelength on the string.

### Frequency of the First Harmonic

The speed of a wave on a string with two fixed ends is:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$

where $T$ is the tension in the string (N) and $\mu$ is the mass per unit length of the string (kg m$^{-1}$).

For the first harmonic, $\lambda = 2L$, so $v = f \times 2L$. Combining these:

$$f = \frac{1}{2L}\sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$

where $f$ is frequency (Hz) and $L$ is the length of the vibrating string (m).

**Worked example (method: applying the first-harmonic formula with unit conversions).** A guitar string has mass $3.2 \text{ g}$ and total length $90 \text{ cm}$. It is tightened to $T = 65 \text{ N}$ between two bridges $75 \text{ cm}$ apart. First, calculate $\mu$: $\mu = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-3}}{90 \times 10^{-2}} = 3.56 \times 10^{-3}$ kg m$^{-1}$. Note that the vibrating length $L = 75 \times 10^{-2}$ m (bridge-to-bridge distance, not total string length). Then:

$$f = \frac{1}{2 \times 0.75}\sqrt{\frac{65}{3.56 \times 10^{-3}}} = 90 \text{ Hz (2 s.f.)}$$

## Required Practical: Investigating Stationary Waves

### Aims of the Experiment

Measure how the frequency of the first harmonic is affected by changing one of:

- The length of the string
- The tension in the string
- Strings with different values of mass per unit length

**Variables:**

| If varying... | Independent variable | Dependent variable | Control variables |
|---|---|---|---|
| Length | Length of string | Frequency of first harmonic | Same masses (tension), same string ($\mu$) |
| Tension | Mass on hanger (tension) | Frequency of first harmonic | Same string length, same string ($\mu$) |
| Mass per unit length | Different strings ($\mu$) | Frequency of first harmonic | Same masses (tension), same string length |

### Equipment List

| Apparatus | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Signal generator | Operates the vibration generator and measures the frequency of the first harmonic |
| Vibration generator | Connected to the signal generator to produce the stationary wave |
| Retort stand | Provides a stable fixed end on the table |
| G clamp or 2 kg mass | Stabilises the retort stand |
| 2.0 m of string | The medium on which the stationary wave is observed |
| Pulley | Allows masses to hang vertically, introducing less friction than the table edge |
| Wooden bridge | Provides the other fixed end, position varied to change string length |
| Mass hanger + 100 g masses | Hung from the pulley to vary the tension in the string |
| Metre ruler | Measures the length of the string |
| Top-pan balance | Measures the mass of the string |

**Resolution of measuring equipment:** metre ruler = 1 mm, signal generator $\approx$ 10 nHz, top-pan balance = 0.005 g.

### Method (Example: Varying Length)

1. Set up the apparatus: attach one end of the string to the vibration generator and pass the other end over the bench pulley. Attach the mass hanger.
2. Adjust the position of the wooden bridge so that the vibrating length $L$ is measured from the vibration generator to the bridge using a metre ruler.
3. Turn on the signal generator to set the string oscillating.
4. Increase the frequency until the first harmonic is observed (a single loop with maximum amplitude). Read the frequency from the signal generator.
5. Repeat the procedure with different lengths.
6. Repeat the frequency readings at least two more times at each length and take the mean.
7. Measure the tension in the string: $T = mg$, where $m$ is the hanging mass and $g = 9.81$ N kg$^{-1}$.
8. Measure the mass per unit length: take a known length of string (1 m is ideal), weigh it on the balance, and calculate $\mu = \frac{\text{mass of string}}{\text{length of string}}$.

### Analysing the Results

For the first harmonic, $\lambda = 2L$, so $v = f \times 2L$. Rearranging:

$$f = \frac{v}{2L} = \frac{v}{2}\left(\frac{1}{L}\right)$$

Comparing to $y = mx$:

- $y = f$ (Hz)
- $x = \frac{1}{L}$ (m$^{-1}$)
- Gradient $= \frac{v}{2}$ (m s$^{-1}$)

Plot $f$ against $\frac{1}{L}$, draw a line of best fit, and calculate the gradient. The wave speed is:

$$v = 2 \times \text{gradient}$$

Verify this experimentally determined wave speed against the theoretical value:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$

Assess uncertainties in length and frequency measurements and propagate them to determine the uncertainty in the wave speed.

### Evaluating the Experiment

**Systematic errors:**

- An oscilloscope can verify the signal generator's frequency readings.
- The signal generator should be left on for about 20 minutes to stabilise before taking measurements.
- Use as large a length as possible (measurements should span a suitable range, e.g. 20 cm intervals over at least 1.0 m) to improve the resolution of measurements.

**Random errors:**

- The sharpness of resonance makes it difficult to judge exactly when the first harmonic is achieved. Adjust the frequency while watching a node closely (rather than trying to judge the antinode amplitude, since the wave moves fast). This gives the most precise reading of the resonant frequency.
- To reduce random error in frequency, use this repeat procedure: find the first harmonic by increasing frequency and note the value. Then increase past it and gradually reduce until the first harmonic reappears, noting the second value. Repeat once more for a third reading, and average all three.

### Safety Considerations

- Use a rubber string rather than a metal wire, which could snap under tension.
- If a metal wire is used, wear safety goggles to protect the eyes.
- Stand well away from the hanging masses in case they fall.
- Place a crash mat or soft surface beneath the masses to cushion any fall.

### Worked Example: Calculating Frequencies and Uncertainties

A wire has mass 0.16 g, weighed length 1.0 m, and distance between fixed ends 0.4 m. Tension is varied by adding masses to a hanger.

**Step 1 (mass per unit length):**

$$\mu = \frac{0.16 \times 10^{-3}}{1.0} = 0.16 \times 10^{-3} \text{ kg m}^{-1}$$

**Step 2 (calculate frequency for each tension).** For a hanging mass of 0.20 kg: $T = 0.20 \times 9.81 = 1.96$ N.

$$f = \frac{1}{2 \times 0.4}\sqrt{\frac{1.96}{0.16 \times 10^{-3}}} = 138 \text{ Hz (140 Hz to 2 s.f.)}$$

The same method gives, for masses 0.40 to 1.60 kg: 200, 240, 280, 310, 340, 370, 390 Hz.

**Step 3 (uncertainties in each quantity):**

- **Mass on hanger:** maximum uncertainty $\approx 0.005$ kg per mass. Each slot mass contributes $\pm 0.005$ kg, and these add. For 0.20 kg: $\frac{0.005}{0.20} \times 100\% = 2.5\%$. For 0.40 kg (two masses): absolute uncertainty $= 0.01$ kg, giving $\frac{0.01}{0.40} \times 100\% = 2.5\%$.
- **Length (distance between fixed ends):** ruler resolution is 1 mm, but practical errors of up to 1 cm are possible. For $L = 0.4$ m: $\frac{0.01}{0.4} \times 100\% = 2.5\%$.
- **Mass per unit length ($\mu$):** combining ruler and balance uncertainties: $\frac{\Delta \mu}{\mu} = \frac{\Delta m}{m} + \frac{\Delta l}{l}$, giving $\Delta \mu = 0.16 \times \left(\frac{0.005}{0.16} + \frac{0.001}{1.0}\right) = 0.005$ g/m. Percentage uncertainty: $\frac{0.005}{0.16} \times 100\% = 3\%$.

**Step 4 (uncertainty in calculated frequency).** Since tension and $\mu$ appear under a square root (power of $\frac{1}{2}$), their percentage uncertainties are halved before combining. Total percentage uncertainty in $f$:

$$\% \text{ uncertainty in } f = \% \text{ in } L + \frac{1}{2}(\% \text{ in } T) + \frac{1}{2}(\% \text{ in } \mu) = 2.5 + 1.25 + 1.5 = 5.25\%$$

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wave speed on a string | $v = \sqrt{\dfrac{T}{\mu}}$ | $T$ = tension (N), $\mu$ = mass per unit length (kg m$^{-1}$) |
| Frequency of the $n$th harmonic | $f_n = \dfrac{nv}{2L}$ | $n$ = harmonic number, $L$ = vibrating length |
| Frequency of the first harmonic | $f_1 = \dfrac{1}{2L}\sqrt{\dfrac{T}{\mu}}$ | Combines wave speed and first harmonic wavelength |
| Harmonic relationship | $f_n = n \, f_1$ | Frequency of any harmonic is $n$ times the fundamental |
| Wavelength of the $n$th harmonic | $\lambda_n = \dfrac{2L}{n}$ | For a string fixed at both ends |
| First harmonic wavelength | $\lambda_1 = 2L$ | One half-wavelength fits on the string |
| Wave equation | $v = f\lambda$ | General relation used throughout |
| Node-to-node spacing | $\dfrac{\lambda}{2}$ | Distance between adjacent nodes or adjacent antinodes |
