# Scalars & Vectors

## Scalars & Vectors

All physical quantities are classified as either a **scalar** or a **vector**.

A **scalar** is a quantity that has magnitude only. Mass is a scalar because it has a size but no associated direction.

A **vector** is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Weight is a vector because it is a force with both a size and a direction (downwards).

### Distance and Displacement

**Distance** is a measure of how far an object has travelled, regardless of direction. It is the total length of the path taken, so it has magnitude but no direction. Distance is therefore a scalar quantity.

**Displacement** is a measure of how far it is between two points in space, including the direction. It is the length and direction of a straight line drawn from the starting point to the finishing point, so it has both magnitude and direction. Displacement is therefore a vector quantity.

When a student travels to school, the total distance includes every twist and turn of the roads taken. The displacement is a single straight line from home to school, regardless of obstacles such as buildings, lakes or motorways along the way. Distance and displacement are generally different for any journey that is not a straight line.

### Speed and Velocity

**Speed** is the distance travelled per unit time, regardless of direction. It describes how fast an object is moving but not the direction it is travelling in. Speed has magnitude but no direction, so it is a scalar quantity.

**Velocity** is the displacement per unit time, including the direction. It describes how fast an object is moving and which direction it is travelling in. Velocity has both magnitude and direction, so it is a vector quantity.

An object can have a constant speed but a changing velocity if it is changing direction (e.g. uniform circular motion).

### Combining Vectors

**Vectors** are represented by arrows. The arrowhead indicates the direction and the length of the arrow represents the magnitude.

Vectors can be combined by adding them to produce the **resultant vector** (sometimes called the "net" vector, e.g. the net force).

Two methods are used to add vectors:

**Calculation** is used when the vectors are perpendicular. **Scale drawing** is used when the vectors are not perpendicular.

### Vector Calculation

At A-level, vector calculations are limited to two vectors at right angles. The combined vectors form a right-angled triangle.

The magnitude of the resultant vector $R$ is found using Pythagoras' theorem:

$$R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

where $a$ and $b$ are the magnitudes of the two perpendicular vectors.

The procedure is: (1) add the two vectors $a$ and $b$, (2) link them head-to-tail, (3) draw the resultant from the tail of $a$ to the head of $b$, (4) calculate $R$ using Pythagoras' theorem.

The direction of the resultant vector is found using trigonometry. The angle $\theta$ from the horizontal or vertical is calculated with whichever ratio (sine, cosine, or tangent) fits the known sides:

$$\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$$

When $\theta$ is measured from the horizontal: $\tan\theta = \frac{b}{a}$. When $\theta$ is measured from the vertical: $\tan\theta = \frac{a}{b}$ (where $a$ is horizontal and $b$ is vertical). The question will imply which angle is required.

### Scale Drawing

When two vectors are not at right angles, the resultant vector is found using a scale drawing:

**Step 1:** Link the vectors head-to-tail if they are not already.
**Step 2:** Draw the resultant vector using the triangle or parallelogram method.
**Step 3:** Measure the length of the resultant vector with a ruler.
**Step 4:** Measure the angle of the resultant vector (from North if it is a bearing) with a protractor.

A scale is provided (e.g. 1 cm = 1 km). The final answer must always be converted back to the real units using the scale. For example, with a scale of 1 cm = 2 km, a measured length of 5 cm corresponds to a resultant of 10 km.

### Triangle and Parallelogram Methods

**Triangle method:** (1) Link the vectors head-to-tail. (2) The resultant vector is formed by connecting the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector.

**Parallelogram method:** (1) Link the vectors tail-to-tail. (2) Complete the resulting parallelogram. (3) The resultant vector is the diagonal of the parallelogram drawn from the shared tail point.

### Vector Subtraction

To subtract vector $\mathbf{b}$ from vector $\mathbf{a}$ ($\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}$), first reverse the direction of $\mathbf{b}$ to obtain $-\mathbf{b}$, then add $\mathbf{a}$ and $-\mathbf{b}$ using either the triangle or parallelogram method as normal.

### Worked Example: Hiker Displacement

A hiker walks 6 km due east and 10 km due north.

**Magnitude:** $R = \sqrt{6^2 + 10^2} = \sqrt{36 + 100} = \sqrt{136} \approx 11.7$ km

**Direction from the horizontal:** $\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{10}{6}\right) = 59°$ from the horizontal (i.e. from due east, towards north).

This illustrates the standard method: draw the right-angled triangle, apply Pythagoras for magnitude, then use $\tan\theta$ for direction.

## Resolving Vectors

**Resolving** a vector is the reverse of combining vectors. A single resultant vector is split into two **components** that, in combination, have the same effect as the original vector.

For a vector of magnitude $F$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal:

$$F_x = F\cos\theta \quad \text{(horizontal component)}$$

$$F_y = F\sin\theta \quad \text{(vertical component)}$$

The component adjacent to the angle $\theta$ always uses $\cos\theta$, and the component opposite $\theta$ always uses $\sin\theta$ (the "cos sandwich" memory aid: $\cos\theta$ is sandwiched between the angle and its adjacent side).

### Forces on an Inclined Plane

An **inclined plane** is a flat surface tilted at an angle $\theta$ to the horizontal. For objects on slopes, it is more useful to resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to the slope rather than horizontally and vertically.

The **weight** $W$ acts vertically downwards. The **normal (reaction) force** $R$ acts perpendicular to the slope surface and away from it.

The weight $W$ is resolved into two components:

$$W\cos\theta \quad \text{perpendicular to the slope}$$

$$W\sin\theta \quad \text{parallel to the slope (directed down the slope)}$$

If there is no friction, the component $W\sin\theta$ is the unbalanced force that causes the object to accelerate down the slope. The object does not move perpendicular to the slope, so the normal force balances the perpendicular component: $R = W\cos\theta$.

### Worked Example: Helicopter Lift

A helicopter provides a lift of 250 kN with blades tilted at 15° from the vertical.

The angle is measured from the vertical, so the vertical component is adjacent to the angle and uses cosine, while the horizontal component is opposite and uses sine:

**Vertical component:** $250 \times \cos 15° = 242$ kN

**Horizontal component:** $250 \times \sin 15° = 64.7$ kN

### Equilibrium

**Coplanar forces** can be represented by vector triangles. Forces are in **equilibrium** if an object is either at rest or moving at constant velocity.

When forces are in equilibrium, the vector triangle formed by joining the forces head-to-tail is **closed** (the vectors form a closed path with no gap). This means the resultant force is zero.

The most common forces acting on objects are weight, normal reaction force, tension (from cords and strings), and friction.

To construct a closed vector triangle for an object in equilibrium: (1) draw all forces on a free-body diagram, (2) represent all forces as acting from a single point, (3) rearrange them into a closed vector triangle, keeping the same magnitude and direction for each force, and ensuring all arrows follow a continuous path around the triangle.

### Worked Example: Hanging Weight

A weight hangs in equilibrium from a cable at point X with tensions $T_1$ and $T_2$ in the cables. To find the correct force diagram: (1) identify the direction of all forces ($T_1$ and $T_2$ act along their cables away from X, weight $W$ acts vertically downwards), (2) arrange these into a vector triangle keeping the same magnitudes and directions, (3) check that the arrows form a closed path (each vector's head meets the next vector's tail). The correct answer is the triangle where all three vectors follow a continuous closed loop.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation |
|---|---|
| Resultant of two perpendicular vectors | $R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ |
| Direction of resultant (from horizontal) | $\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right)$ |
| Horizontal component of vector $F$ | $F_x = F\cos\theta$ |
| Vertical component of vector $F$ | $F_y = F\sin\theta$ |
| Weight component perpendicular to slope | $W\cos\theta$ |
| Weight component parallel to slope | $W\sin\theta$ |
| Normal force on frictionless inclined plane | $R = W\cos\theta$ |
