# Moments

## Moments

### What is a Moment?

A **moment** is the turning effect of a force. Moments arise when forces cause objects to **rotate** about some **pivot**.

The moment of a force is defined as:

$$\text{Moment} = F \times d$$

where $F$ is the force in newtons (N) and $d$ is the **perpendicular distance** from the pivot in metres (m).

The SI unit of moment is **newton metres (N m)**. Newton centimetres (N cm) may also be used if distance is given in centimetres.

When the force is not perpendicular to the line joining the point of application to the pivot, the perpendicular component of the distance must be used:

$$\text{Moment} = F \times d\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and the line from the pivot to the point of application.

**Everyday example:** a door handle is placed far from the hinge (pivot) to maximise the perpendicular distance for a given applied force, producing a larger moment and making the door easier to open.

**Method (from worked example):** to find the moment about a pivot, (1) identify the relevant force, converting mass to weight using $W = mg$ if needed, (2) find the perpendicular distance from that force's line of action to the pivot, (3) substitute into $\text{Moment} = F \times d$. For a 0.5 kg mass at the 80 cm mark on a metre rule pivoted at 50 cm: $W = 0.5 \times 9.81 \approx 5$ N, $d = 80 - 50 = 30$ cm, so moment $= 5 \times 30 = 150$ N cm.

**Exam tip:** draw all forces on the object and identify which are perpendicular to the distance from the pivot. Not all forces produce a turning effect, and questions may include extra forces to mislead.

## The Principle of Moments

The **principle of moments** states:

> For a system to be in **equilibrium**, the sum of **clockwise moments** about a point must equal the sum of **anticlockwise moments** about the same point.

For multiple forces acting on a beam with pivot:

$$F_{\text{cw}} \times d_{\text{cw}} = \sum F_{\text{acw}} \times d_{\text{acw}}$$

More generally, with forces $F_1$, $F_2$, $F_3$ at distances $d_1$, $d_2$, $d_3$ from the pivot, if $F_2$ acts clockwise and $F_1$, $F_3$ act anticlockwise:

$$F_2 \times d_2 = (F_1 \times d_1) + (F_3 \times d_3)$$

**Method (from worked example):** for a uniform beam, the weight acts at the **centre of gravity** (the midpoint for a uniform beam). A 5 m uniform beam of weight 40 N is pivoted 2 m from one end with a load $W$ hung from that end. The centre of gravity is at 2.5 m from either end, so it is $2.5 - 2 = 0.5$ m from the pivot on the longer side. Taking moments: clockwise moment from the beam's weight $= 40 \times 0.5 = 20$ N m, anticlockwise moment from the load $= W \times 2$ m. Setting these equal: $W = \frac{20}{2} = 10$ N.

**Exam tip:** ensure all distances use the same units. Carefully determine which forces produce clockwise or anticlockwise moments. Forces above the beam on the left of the pivot and below the beam on the right of the pivot produce anticlockwise moments, and vice versa.

## Couples

A **couple** is a pair of equal and opposite coplanar forces that produces **rotation only** (no translational motion).

A couple requires forces that are:

- Equal in magnitude
- Opposite in direction
- Perpendicular to the distance between them
- Not sharing the same line of action

Because the forces are equal and opposite, the resultant force is **zero**. By Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$), the object does **not** accelerate translationally.

Unlike the moment of a single force, the **moment of a couple does not depend on a pivot**. The moment of a couple is:

$$\text{Moment of a couple} = F \times d$$

where $F$ is the magnitude of one of the forces and $d$ is the **perpendicular distance between the lines of action** of the two forces.

**Identifying a couple:** two forces on an object form a couple only if they satisfy all three conditions above. Forces in the same direction, forces of different magnitudes, forces with a non-perpendicular separation, or forces sharing the same line of action do not form a couple.

**Exam tip:** two equal and opposite forces along the same line of action do not constitute a couple, because they have zero perpendicular separation and produce no rotation.

## Centre of Mass

### Definition

The **centre of mass** of an object is the point at which the weight of the object may be considered to act.

For a **uniform regular solid**, the centre of mass is at its geometric centre. Examples: for a person standing upright it is roughly in the middle of the body behind the navel; for a sphere it is at the centre.

For **symmetrical objects with uniform density**, the centre of mass is located at the **point of symmetry**. For shapes with multiple lines of symmetry, it is at the intersection of those lines.

### Stability

The position of the centre of mass determines an object's **stability**.

An object is **stable** when its centre of mass lies vertically above its base. If an object is tilted so that its centre of mass is no longer above its base, it will **topple**.

| Feature | Effect on Stability |
|---|---|
| Wide base | Lowers centre of mass, increases stability |
| Narrow base | Raises centre of mass, decreases stability |

The most stable objects combine a **wide base** with a **low centre of mass**.

### Centre of Gravity vs Centre of Mass

In a **uniform gravitational field**, the centre of gravity is **identical** to the centre of mass.

The centre of mass does **not** depend on the gravitational field. The centre of gravity **does** depend on the gravitational field, since $W = mg$ varies with $g$.

In a **non-uniform gravitational field** (e.g. an object in space between two bodies), the centre of gravity is displaced towards the region of stronger gravitational field. For example, the Moon's centre of gravity is shifted slightly towards the Earth relative to its centre of mass, because the side of the Moon facing Earth experiences a stronger gravitational pull.

**Exam tip:** the centre of mass is a hypothetical point that can lie inside or outside the body. It shifts as the shape of the body changes (e.g. a person's centre of mass is lower when leaning forward than when standing upright).

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Moment of a force | $\text{Moment} = F \times d$ | $d$ is perpendicular distance from pivot |
| Moment (non-perpendicular) | $\text{Moment} = F \times d\cos\theta$ | $\theta$ is angle between force direction and the line to the pivot |
| Principle of moments | $\sum F_{\text{cw}} d_{\text{cw}} = \sum F_{\text{acw}} d_{\text{acw}}$ | Condition for rotational equilibrium |
| Moment of a couple | $\text{Moment} = F \times d$ | $d$ is perpendicular distance between lines of action; no pivot dependence |
| Weight | $W = mg$ | Used to find force from mass in moment calculations |
