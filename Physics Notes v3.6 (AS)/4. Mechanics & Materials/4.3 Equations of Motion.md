# Equations of Motion

## Motion Along a Straight Line

### Displacement, Speed, Velocity & Acceleration

**Scalar quantities** have magnitude (size) only.

**Distance** is the total length between two points. **Speed** is the total distance travelled per unit of time. Both are scalars.

**Vector quantities** have both magnitude and direction.

**Displacement** is the distance of an object from a fixed point in a specified direction. **Velocity** is the rate of change of displacement of an object. **Acceleration** is the rate of change of velocity of an object.

Speed and velocity are measured in metres per second ($\text{m s}^{-1}$). Acceleration is measured in metres per second squared ($\text{m s}^{-2}$).

$$v = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \qquad a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$

where $\Delta s$ = change in displacement, $\Delta v$ = change in velocity, and $\Delta t$ = change in time.

**Worked method (unit conversion and acceleration):** A car accelerates uniformly from rest to $150 \text{ km h}^{-1}$ in 6.2 s. Convert the speed: $150 \text{ km h}^{-1} = \frac{150 \times 10^3}{3600} = 41.67 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Then $a = \frac{41.67}{6.2} = 6.7 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.

### Average & Instantaneous Speed

The **instantaneous speed** (or velocity) is the speed (or velocity) of an object at any given point in time. This applies whether the object moves at constant velocity or is accelerating.

On a **displacement-time graph**, an accelerating object appears as a curved line (since velocity is changing). To find the instantaneous velocity at a particular time, draw a **tangent** to the curve at that time and calculate the **gradient** of that tangent.

The **average speed** (or velocity) is the total distance (or displacement) divided by the total time. On a displacement-time graph, divide the total displacement (y-axis) by the total time (x-axis). This works for both curved and straight lines.

**Worked method (average speed):** A cyclist travels 20 m at constant speed then decelerates for 5 m to a stop, taking 3.5 s total. Average speed $= \frac{20 + 5}{3.5} = 7.1 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.

## Motion Graphs

### Uniform & Non-Uniform Acceleration Graphs

Three types of motion graph are used: displacement-time, velocity-time, and acceleration-time.

#### Displacement-Time Graph

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Gradient** (slope) | Velocity |
| **y-intercept** | Initial displacement |
| Diagonal straight line | Constant velocity |
| Positive slope | Motion in positive direction |
| Negative slope | Motion in negative direction |
| Curved line | Acceleration (non-uniform velocity) |
| Horizontal line (zero slope) | Object at rest |
| Area under curve | Meaningless |

A displacement-time graph can show positive or negative displacement values. A distance-time graph only has positive values.

The three key graph shapes are: a straight diagonal line (constant velocity), a curve with increasing gradient (increasing velocity at constant acceleration), and a curve with increasingly steep gradient (velocity increasing with increasing acceleration).

#### Velocity-Time Graph

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Slope** | Acceleration |
| **y-intercept** | Initial velocity |
| Straight line | Uniform (constant) acceleration |
| Positive slope | Increase in velocity in positive direction |
| Negative slope | Increase in velocity in negative direction |
| Curved line | Non-uniform acceleration |
| Horizontal line (zero slope) | Constant velocity |
| **Area under curve** | Displacement or distance travelled |

A velocity-time graph can show positive or negative velocity values. A speed-time graph only has positive values.

The four key graph shapes are: a horizontal line (constant velocity), a straight line from the origin (uniform acceleration from rest), a curve with decreasing gradient (increasing acceleration), and a curve with increasing gradient tending to horizontal (decreasing acceleration / deceleration).

#### Acceleration-Time Graph

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Slope | Meaningless |
| **y-intercept** | Initial acceleration |
| Horizontal line (zero slope) | Constant acceleration |
| **Area under curve** | Change in velocity |

The three key graph shapes are: a horizontal line along $a = 0$ (constant velocity, zero acceleration), a horizontal line at some positive $a$ value (constant acceleration, increasing velocity), and an upward-sloping line (increasing acceleration).

### Gradients and Areas Summary

| Graph type | Gradient gives | Area under curve gives |
|---|---|---|
| Displacement-time | Velocity | (meaningless) |
| Velocity-time | Acceleration | Displacement |
| Acceleration-time | (meaningless) | Change in velocity |

### Motion of a Bouncing Ball

For a bouncing ball in a uniform gravitational field (assuming no air resistance), the acceleration due to gravity $g$ is always directed downwards, regardless of the ball's direction of travel. The ball's velocity is positive when travelling upwards (if upwards is defined as positive) and negative when travelling downwards.

Ignoring air resistance, the ball reaches the same height every time before bouncing again. When travelling upwards, the ball has a positive velocity that decreases (decelerates) until it reaches its highest point.

**At the highest point (A):** The ball is at maximum displacement. It momentarily has zero velocity. The velocity changes from positive to negative as the ball reverses direction. The acceleration $g$ remains constant and directed vertically downwards.

**At the lowest point (B, on the ground):** The ball is at minimum displacement. Its velocity changes instantaneously from negative to positive, but the speed (magnitude of velocity) remains the same. This instantaneous change in direction produces a momentary spike in acceleration (since $a = \Delta v / \Delta t$ and $\Delta t$ is very small).

On a displacement-time graph, bouncing motion appears as a series of inverted parabolas, each returning to $s = 0$ at the bounce point. On a velocity-time graph, the motion appears as a series of straight lines with negative gradient (slope $= -g$), with discontinuities at each bounce where velocity jumps from negative to positive.

On an acceleration-time graph, acceleration is constant at $-9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ throughout, with sharp spikes at each bounce point where the contact force acts.

**Worked method (v-t graph area):** For a vehicle with uniform acceleration, the displacement at 40 s equals the area under the velocity-time graph. If the graph is a triangle with base 40 s and height 105 km h$^{-1}$, first convert velocity: $\frac{105}{3600} = 0.0292 \text{ km s}^{-1}$. Then displacement $= \frac{1}{2} \times 40 \times 0.0292 = 0.6 \text{ km} = 600 \text{ m}$. The area can often be decomposed into triangles, rectangles, and trapezoids for calculation.

## SUVAT Equations

The **SUVAT equations** are equations of motion for objects moving in a straight line with **constant acceleration**. They contain five variables:

| Symbol | Quantity | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| $s$ | Displacement | m |
| $u$ | Initial velocity | $\text{m s}^{-1}$ |
| $v$ | Final velocity | $\text{m s}^{-1}$ |
| $a$ | Acceleration | $\text{m s}^{-2}$ |
| $t$ | Time | s |

The four SUVAT equations (all given on the data sheet):

$$v = u + at$$

$$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

$$s = \frac{(v + u)}{2} \, t$$

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

All variables except time $t$ are vectors, meaning they can be positive or negative depending on direction. The sign convention must be consistent throughout a problem: if upwards is positive, then $s$ upwards is positive and $a$ due to gravity is negative.

**Key phrases and their meanings:**

- "Starts from rest" or initial velocity not mentioned: $u = 0$
- "Starts from rest at $x = 0$ when $t = 0$": object begins stationary at the origin
- "Falling due to gravity" (with no other forces): $a = g = 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$

**SUVAT applicability:** These equations apply only for constant acceleration in a straight line, for example an object falling in a uniform gravitational field without air resistance.

### How to Use the SUVAT Equations

**Step 1:** List all known and unknown variables. Use the context to deduce implicit values (e.g. $u = 0$ for "starts from rest", $a = \pm 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ for vertical motion under gravity).

**Step 2:** Choose the equation that links the three known quantities to the one unknown. Each equation omits one of the five SUVAT variables.

**Step 3:** Convert to SI units, substitute into the equation, and rearrange to solve.

Some problems require splitting the motion into two stages and applying SUVAT twice (e.g. two masses connected over a pulley where one mass stops before the other).

**Worked method (choosing the right equation):** A train passes marker 1 at $u = 50 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, must pass marker 2 at $v = 10 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, and must take at least $t = 20$ s. Find $s$. Three known quantities are $u$, $v$, $t$, and the unknown is $s$. The equation linking $u$, $v$, $t$, $s$ (omitting $a$) is $s = \frac{(v+u)}{2}t$. Substituting: $s = \frac{(50+10)}{2} \times 20 = 600 \text{ m}$.

## Projectile Motion

A **projectile** is a particle moving freely (non-powered) under gravity in a two-dimensional plane. Examples include throwing a ball, jumping off a diving board, and hitting a baseball.

An object enters projectile motion with a **resultant velocity** $u$ at an angle $\theta$ to the horizontal. The motion is analysed by resolving into independent horizontal and vertical components.

### Key Projectile Quantities

**Time of flight** (total time): the total time the projectile is in the air. For a symmetric trajectory (launch and landing at the same height), the time to maximum height is half the total time.

**Maximum height attained:** the height at which the projectile is momentarily at rest, occurring when the vertical velocity component $= 0$. For symmetric trajectories, this occurs at half the total time.

**Range:** the total horizontal distance travelled by the projectile.

### Horizontal and Vertical Components

The trajectory consists of a **vertical** component and a **horizontal** component. These are **independent** of each other, meaning changes in one do not affect the other.

| Quantity | Horizontal Component | Vertical Component |
|---|---|---|
| Displacement | Maximum range at end of motion (total time elapsed). Half the range at maximum height (half time elapsed). | Maximum height at top of motion (half time elapsed) |
| Velocity | **Constant** throughout | **Zero** at maximum height |
| Acceleration | **Zero** (velocity is constant horizontally) | $g = 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. Positive when falling towards Earth, negative when moving away from Earth |

### Resolving Velocity Components

Using trigonometry, the resultant velocity $u$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal resolves as:

$$u_x = u \cos\theta \quad \text{(horizontal component)}$$

$$u_y = u \sin\theta \quad \text{(vertical component)}$$

### Deriving Key Equations with SUVAT

**Time of flight:** Vertically, $u = u\sin\theta$, $v = 0$ at maximum height, $a = -g$. Using $v = u + at$: $0 = u\sin\theta - gt$, so $t = \frac{u\sin\theta}{g}$ to maximum height. Total time of flight:

$$T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$$

**Maximum height:** Vertically, $u = u\sin\theta$, $v = 0$, $a = -g$, $s = H$. Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $0 = (u\sin\theta)^2 - 2gH$, so:

$$H = \frac{(u\sin\theta)^2}{2g}$$

**Range:** Horizontally, velocity is constant at $u\cos\theta$, so $R = u\cos\theta \times T = u\cos\theta \times \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$. Using the identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$:

$$R = \frac{u^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}$$

### Effects of Air Resistance on Projectile Motion

Any object moving through air experiences **air resistance**, a drag force caused by friction between air molecules and the object, acting opposite to the direction of motion.

The drag force has two effects on projectile motion: its horizontal component reduces horizontal velocity and therefore the range, and its vertical component reduces maximum height and produces a steeper descent path. Overall, both range and maximum height are reduced compared to the no-air-resistance case.

### Factors Affecting Motion Through a Fluid

A **fluid** describes both liquids and gases. The two main factors affecting motion through a fluid are **mass** and **surface area**. A larger surface area produces greater air resistance. A greater mass produces a greater weight.

### Solving Projectile Problems

Three scenarios arise: **vertical projection** (free fall), **horizontal projection** (launched horizontally from a height), and **projection at an angle** (the most common).

**Worked method (vertical projection):** A feather falls from rest in a vacuum tube for $t = 0.5$ s. Only vertical motion matters: $u = 0$, $a = 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$, $t = 0.5$ s. Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$: $L = \frac{1}{2} \times 9.81 \times 0.5^2 = 1.2 \text{ m}$.

**Worked method (horizontal projection):** A motorcycle takes off horizontally from 1.25 m height, landing 10 m away. Vertically: $s = 1.25$ m, $u = 0$, $a = 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. Using $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$: $t = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 1.25}{9.81}} = 0.5$ s. Horizontally: $a = 0$, $s = 10$ m, $t = 0.5$ s, so $v = \frac{10}{0.5} = 20 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.

**Worked method (projection at angle):** A ball is thrown at $12 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ at $50°$. For maximum height, consider vertical motion only: $u = 12\sin 50°$, $v = 0$, $a = -9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ rearranged to $s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}$: $H = \frac{0 - (12\sin 50°)^2}{2 \times (-9.81)} = \frac{(12\sin 50°)^2}{19.62} = 4.3 \text{ m}$.

## Drag Forces

**Drag forces** oppose the motion of an object moving through a fluid (gas or liquid). Examples include **friction** and **air resistance**.

Properties of drag forces: they always act in the **opposite** direction to motion, they can never speed up an object or start it moving, they slow an object down or keep it moving at constant speed, and they convert kinetic energy into heat and sound.

**Lift** is an upwards force on an object moving through a fluid, perpendicular to the fluid flow. For example, an aeroplane pushes air downwards, and by Newton's third law, the air exerts an equal and opposite upward force (lift) on the wings.

A key property of drag is that it **increases with the speed** of the object. When the driving force exceeds friction, the object accelerates. When driving force equals friction, velocity is constant. When friction exceeds the driving force, the object decelerates.

**Worked method (finding friction):** A car of mass 800 kg has a driving force of 3 kN and acceleration $2.0 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. By Newton's second law: $F_{\text{net}} = ma = 800 \times 2.0 = 1600$ N. Since $F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{drive}} - F_{\text{friction}}$: $1600 = 3000 - F_{\text{friction}}$, so $F_{\text{friction}} = 1400$ N.

### Air Resistance

**Air resistance** is a drag force experienced by objects moving through air. At walking pace, a person rarely notices it, but swimming at the same pace uses far more energy because air is 800 times **less dense** than water.

Air resistance increases with speed, but other factors also affect it: **cross-sectional area**, **shape**, **altitude**, **temperature**, and **humidity**.

Design considerations to reduce air resistance: racing cars use streamlined shapes with curved, angled fronts. Aeroplanes fly at high altitude where air is less dense. Racing cyclists adopt a streamlined posture and use aerodynamically designed bicycles, clothing, and helmets.

### Air Resistance & Projectile Motion

Air resistance decreases the horizontal component of a projectile's velocity, reducing both its range and maximum height compared to the case with no air resistance. The descent path becomes steeper than the ascent.

The angle and speed of release are varied depending on the goal. In sports such as long jump or javelin, an optimum launch angle (accounting for air resistance) maximises the range. In gymnastics or ski jumping, a large initial vertical velocity is used to maximise height and flight time.

If a question states air resistance is **negligible**, treat the body as having no drag forces.

## Terminal Velocity

For a body in **free fall**, the only force acting initially is its weight, and its acceleration is $g$ (due to gravity alone).

As the body accelerates and its speed increases, the **drag force increases** with speed. By Newton's second law ($F = ma$), the increasing drag reduces the resultant force, which in turn reduces the acceleration.

When the drag force equals the gravitational pull (weight), the resultant force is zero, so acceleration is zero. The body then falls at a constant velocity called the **terminal velocity**. This is the maximum velocity the object can reach during free fall through a fluid.

On a velocity-time graph, the motion appears as a curve: initially steep (high acceleration, small drag), gradually flattening (decreasing acceleration as drag grows), and eventually becoming horizontal (zero acceleration at terminal velocity). The gradient of the v-t graph equals acceleration, which decreases from $g$ to zero.

**Worked method (skydiver problem):** Two skydivers A (heavier) and B (lighter) want to join up in free fall. B should jump first. Reasoning: A has greater mass and greater weight. Terminal velocity is reached when weight equals drag. Since A's weight is larger, A needs a higher speed (greater drag) to reach equilibrium, meaning A has a higher terminal velocity and will fall faster. A will therefore catch up to B.

A common misconception is that skydivers appear to move upwards when their parachute opens. They do not move upwards; they **decelerate** to a new, lower terminal velocity because the parachute greatly increases the surface area and therefore the drag force.

## Required Practical: Determination of $g$

### Aims

To determine the acceleration due to gravity $g$ by measuring the time for a ball-bearing to fall through a known height, then using a SUVAT equation to calculate acceleration.

### Variables

| Variable type | Variable |
|---|---|
| Independent | Height, $h$ |
| Dependent | Time, $t$ |
| Control | Same steel ball-bearing, same electromagnet, distance between ball-bearing and top of glass tube |

### Equipment

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Metre ruler (resolution 1 mm) | Measure distance between light gates |
| Steel ball-bearing | Object to drop (must be magnetic) |
| Electromagnet | Release ball-bearing from a consistent height each time |
| Two light gates | Detect the ball and measure fall time over a set distance |
| Timer (resolution 0.01 s) | Record time between light gate triggers |
| Tall clamp stand (retort stand) | Support glass tube and electromagnet in vertical alignment |
| Glass tube | Guide ball-bearing vertically downwards |
| Cushion | Catch ball-bearing to prevent damage to surface/ball |

### Method

1. Attach the electromagnet to the top of a tall clamp stand. Do not switch on the current until setup is complete.
2. Place the glass tube directly underneath the electromagnet, leaving space for the ball-bearing. Ensure the tube faces directly downwards.
3. Attach both light gates around the glass tube at a starting separation of around 10 cm.
4. Measure the distance between the two light gates as the height $h$ using a metre ruler.
5. Place a cushion directly underneath the glass tube to catch the ball-bearing.
6. Switch on the electromagnet current and place the ball-bearing underneath so it is held in place.
7. Switch off the electromagnet current. The ball-bearing drops.
8. The timer starts when the ball passes through the first light gate.
9. The timer stops when the ball passes through the second light gate.
10. Record the time $t$.
11. Increase $h$ (e.g. by 5 cm intervals) and repeat. Use at least 5 to 10 values of $h$.
12. Repeat at least 3 times for each value of $h$ and calculate an average $t$.

### Analysis of Results

The known quantities are: $s = h$, time $= t$, initial velocity $= u$, acceleration $= g$.

Starting from $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ and rearranging:

$$2s = 2ut + at^2$$

$$\frac{2s}{t} = 2u + at$$

Substituting $s = h$ and $a = g$:

$$\frac{2h}{t} = gt + 2u$$

This has the form $y = mx + c$, where:

| Straight line term | Experimental quantity |
|---|---|
| $y$ | $\frac{2h}{t}$ ($\text{m s}^{-1}$) |
| $x$ | $t$ (s) |
| Gradient $m$ | $g$ ($\text{m s}^{-2}$) |
| y-intercept $c$ | $2u$ |

Plot $\frac{2h}{t}$ on the y-axis against $t$ on the x-axis, draw a line of best fit, and calculate the gradient to obtain $g$.

### Calculating Error in $g$

Compare the gradient of the **line of best fit** to the **line of worst fit** (the steepest or shallowest line that still passes through all error bars).

To construct error bars: the error in $h$ is half the resolution of the metre ruler (typically $\pm 0.5$ mm). The error in $t$ is half the range of the repeated measurements. The percentage error in $\frac{2h}{t}$ is found by adding the percentage uncertainties of $h$ and $t$.

The fractional uncertainty in $g$ is:

$$\text{fractional uncertainty} = \frac{|\text{gradient}_{\text{best fit}} - \text{gradient}_{\text{worst fit}}|}{\text{gradient}_{\text{best fit}}}$$

### Evaluating the Experiment

**Systematic errors:** Residual magnetism after the electromagnet is switched off may delay the ball's release, causing $t$ to be recorded as longer than the true value.

**Random errors:** Large uncertainty in $h$ from using a metre ruler with 1 mm precision. Parallax error when reading $h$. The ball may not fall accurately through the centre of each light gate. These are reduced by repeating each measurement 3 to 5 times and averaging.

### Safety Considerations

The electromagnet requires an electrical current, so no water should be near the apparatus. Switch on the current only after setup is complete to reduce electrocution risk. Use a cushion or soft surface to catch the ball-bearing. Secure the tall clamp stand to the bench with a G-clamp to prevent it from toppling.

**Worked method (calculating $g$ from data):** Given data for heights 0.10 m to 0.60 m with three time repeats each, calculate average $t$ and $\frac{2h}{t}$ for each height. Plot $\frac{2h}{t}$ against $t$, draw a line of best fit, and find the gradient. Using points $(0, 0.125)$ and $(0.36, 3.50)$: $g = \frac{3.50 - 0.125}{0.36 - 0} = 9.38 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.
