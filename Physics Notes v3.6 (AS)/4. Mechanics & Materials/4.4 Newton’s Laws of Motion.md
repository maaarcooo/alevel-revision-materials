# Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's laws of motion describe the relationship between the forces acting on objects and the motion of those objects.

---

## Newton's First Law

Newton's First Law states:

> *An object will remain at rest or move with constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force.*

This has two consequences: a stationary object stays stationary, and an object moving at constant velocity continues at that velocity, unless a **resultant force** acts.

A resultant force is required to **change the motion** of an object, meaning to speed it up, slow it down, or change its direction.

If the forces on a body are **balanced** (resultant force = 0), the body is either at rest or moving at constant velocity.

Because force is a vector, it is convenient to resolve into **horizontal** and **vertical** components. When forces are balanced, forces acting to the left equal those to the right, and forces acting upward equal those acting downward.

The **resultant force** is the vector sum of all forces acting on the body.

**Worked example (car at constant velocity):** A car moves at constant velocity with a driving force $D = 6 \text{ kN}$. Since velocity is constant, the resultant force is zero, so the frictional force $F$ must equal the driving force: $F = 6 \text{ kN}$.

**Exam note:** The term "body" in Newton's first law simply means "object." Newton's first law was revolutionary because the prevailing belief was that moving objects naturally slow to a stop. The idea that an object can move with no resultant force is still a common misconception.

---

## Newton's Second Law

Newton's Second Law states:

> *The resultant force acting on an object with a constant mass is directly proportional to its acceleration.*

$$F = ma$$

where $F$ is the resultant force (N), $m$ is the mass (kg), and $a$ is the acceleration (m s$^{-2}$).

The **acceleration** always acts in the **same direction** as the resultant force.

When **unbalanced forces** act on an object, the object experiences a resultant force. If this resultant force acts along the direction of motion, the object speeds up or slows down. If it acts at an angle to the direction of motion, the object changes direction.

### Resultant Force

**Force** is a vector quantity with both **magnitude** and **direction**. The resultant force is the **vector sum** of all forces acting on the body.

By convention, if the object is in motion, the positive direction is taken as the direction of motion. Resultant forces can therefore be positive (in the direction of motion) or negative (opposing motion).

For example, consider three carts with the following horizontal forces (taking rightward as positive):

| Forces applied | Resultant force |
|---|---|
| 30 N right only | $30 + 0 = 30$ N (right) |
| 50 N right, 20 N left | $50 + (-20) = 30$ N (right) |
| 10 N right, 10 N left | $10 + (-10) = 0$ N (equilibrium) |
| 30 N right, 40 N left | $30 + (-40) = -10$ N (left) |

If the resultant force acts at an angle to the direction of motion, its magnitude and direction can be found by combining vectors or using scale drawings.

### Acceleration

**Acceleration** is a vector quantity with both **magnitude** and **direction**. If the resultant force is in the direction of motion, the acceleration is **positive**. If it opposes motion, the acceleration is **negative** (deceleration). In all cases, acceleration is in the same direction as the resultant force.

**Exam note:** A resultant force opposing an object's motion causes it to decelerate, not to suddenly travel backwards.

If no drag forces are present, the acceleration of a falling object is independent of its mass. This was demonstrated by astronauts on the Moon who dropped a hammer and a feather from equal heights simultaneously and found they hit the ground at the same time (no air resistance on the Moon).

### Worked Example: Rocket

A rocket has an upward thrust of 15 MN, weight 8 MN, and air resistance 500 kN. Mass $= 0.8 \times 10^5$ kg.

**(a) Resultant force:**

Taking upward as positive:

$$F = (15 \times 10^6) + (-8 \times 10^6) + (-500 \times 10^3) = 6.5 \times 10^6 \text{ N} = 6.5 \text{ MN (upwards)}$$

The positive value confirms the resultant force acts upward (direction of motion).

**(b) Acceleration:**

$$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{6.5 \times 10^6}{0.8 \times 10^5} = 81 \text{ m s}^{-2} \text{ (upwards)}$$

Acceleration is in the same direction as the resultant force.

**Exam note (sign convention):** It is standard to treat the direction of motion as positive. Forward, upward, and rightward are conventionally positive. For stationary objects, any consistent choice is acceptable.

### Newton's Second Law and Momentum

Newton's second law can also be stated in terms of **momentum**:

> *The resultant force on an object is equal to its rate of change of momentum.*

The change in momentum is in the same direction as the resultant force.

The two forms of Newton's second law are equivalent, derived as follows:

$$p = mv$$

$$\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = m \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$

Since $a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$:

$$F = m \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = ma$$

**Worked example (skateboard):** A girl on a skateboard increases speed from 1 m s$^{-1}$ to 4 m s$^{-1}$ in 2.5 s with a driving force of 72 N. Using the momentum form of Newton's second law:

$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{m(v_f - v_i)}{\Delta t}$$

$$72 = \frac{m(4 - 1)}{2.5} \implies m = \frac{72 \times 2.5}{3} = 60 \text{ kg}$$

## Newton's Third Law

Newton's first and second laws deal with multiple forces acting on a **single object**. Newton's third law deals with the forces arising when **two objects** interact.

Newton's Third Law states:

> *If Object A exerts a force on Object B, then Object B will exert a force on Object A which is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.*

When two objects interact, the forces arise in pairs called **third-law pairs**. A Newton's third law force pair must satisfy all four criteria:

| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Type | Same type of force |
| Magnitude | Equal |
| Direction | Opposite |
| Objects | Acting on **different** objects |

### Walking Example

When walking, the foot pushes backward on the ground, and the ground pushes forward on the foot. Both forces are **normal contact forces**, equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects (foot and ground).

**Exam note (common error):** The force the foot exerts on the ground is a normal contact force, not weight. Its magnitude equals the person's weight (for one foot on the ground), which causes confusion. If you are considering the weight of the person (gravitational pull of Earth on person), the third-law pair is the gravitational pull of the person on the Earth, not the normal contact force from the ground. A useful framework: describe the force as a push or a pull first, then identify "Object A pushes/pulls on Object B, and Object B pushes/pulls on Object A."

### Worked Example: Textbook on a Lab Bench

A free-body diagram of a book on a bench shows two forces on the book: weight (downward) and the normal reaction force (upward). Student A claims this is Newton's third law. Student B claims it is Newton's first law.

**Student B is correct.** The two forces shown fail the third-law criteria on two counts: they are not the same type (weight is gravitational, reaction is a contact force), and they both act on the same object (the book). The free-body diagram shows multiple forces on one object, which is the domain of Newton's first law: the forces are balanced, so the book remains at rest.

To identify the actual third-law pairs in this situation:

- **Contact pair:** The book pushes down on the table (normal contact force), and the table pushes up on the book (normal contact force). Same type, equal magnitude, opposite direction, different objects.
- **Gravitational pair:** The Earth pulls on the book (gravitational force/weight), and the book pulls on the Earth (gravitational force). Same type, equal magnitude, opposite direction, different objects.

**Exam note:** Two forces of equal magnitude in opposite directions do not automatically form a third-law pair. All four criteria must be met.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Meaning | Units |
|---|---|---|
| $F = ma$ | Resultant force = mass $\times$ acceleration | N, kg, m s$^{-2}$ |
| $F = \dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$ | Resultant force = rate of change of momentum | N, kg m s$^{-1}$, s |
| $p = mv$ | Momentum = mass $\times$ velocity | kg m s$^{-1}$, kg, m s$^{-1}$ |
| $a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$ | Acceleration = rate of change of velocity | m s$^{-2}$, m s$^{-1}$, s |
