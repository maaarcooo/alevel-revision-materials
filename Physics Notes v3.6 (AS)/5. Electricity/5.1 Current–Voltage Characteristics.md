# Current–Voltage Characteristics

## Basics of Electricity

### Electric Current & Potential Difference

A simple electrical circuit requires a **conductor** through which **current** can flow and a source of **potential difference**.

### Electric Current

**Electric current** is defined as the rate of flow of electric charge. Current is measured in **amperes** (**A**), where $1 \text{ A} = 1 \text{ C s}^{-1}$, meaning one ampere corresponds to one coulomb of charge flowing per second.

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$$

where $I$ is the current (A), $\Delta Q$ is the charge flowing (C), and $\Delta t$ is the time interval (s).

Current flows when a circuit is complete, connecting the positive and negative terminals of a source such as a cell. In electric circuits, the current is a flow of **electrons**. **Conventional current** is defined as the flow of positive charge, running from the **positive** terminal to the **negative** terminal of a cell. This is the **opposite** of the direction of electron flow: because electrons are **negatively** charged, they flow from the **negative** terminal to the **positive** terminal.

Current is measured using an **ammeter**, which must be connected in **series** with the component being measured.

### Potential Difference

**Potential difference** (p.d.) is defined as the electrical work done per unit charge flowing between two points. It is measured in **volts** (**V**), where $1 \text{ V} = 1 \text{ J C}^{-1}$, meaning one volt corresponds to the transfer of one joule of electrical energy per coulomb of charge.

$$V = \frac{W}{Q}$$

where $V$ is the potential difference (V), $W$ is the electrical work done by the charge (J), and $Q$ is the charge flow (C).

A cell creates a potential difference through the **separation of charge**: one terminal has an excess of positive charge and the other an excess of negative charge. Electrons are **repelled** by the negative terminal and **attracted** to the positive terminal, so when a wire connects the terminals, the potential difference **causes** the flow of electrons (current).

As electrons flow through a cell, they **gain** energy. For example, in a 12 V cell, every coulomb of charge passing through gains 12 J of energy. As electrons flow through the external circuit, they **lose** energy, transferring it to the wires and components. After leaving the 12 V cell, each coulomb of charge transfers 12 J of energy to the circuit.

Potential difference is measured using a **voltmeter**, which must be connected in **parallel** with the component being measured.

**Exam tip:** Potential difference is not the same as energy. It is the energy per unit charge. Since $W = QV$, two batteries may have the same voltage but store different amounts of energy, meaning one can move more charge than the other.

### Resistance

**Resistance** is defined as the opposition of a component to the flow of electric current through it. The resistance $R$ of a conductor is defined as the ratio of the potential difference $V$ across it to the current $I$ in it:

$$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

where $R$ is resistance ($\Omega$), $V$ is the potential difference across the conductor (V), and $I$ is the current in the conductor (A).

Resistance is measured in **ohms** ($\Omega$). A resistance of $1 \text{ } \Omega$ means a potential difference of 1 V produces a current of 1 A through the component.

The resistance of a component controls the size of the current in a circuit. For a given potential difference across a component, the **higher** the resistance the **lower** the current, and the **lower** the resistance the **higher** the current.

All electrical components, including wires, possess some resistance. Wires are often made from copper because of its **low** electrical resistance, making it a **good conductor**.

**Exam tip:** In exam questions, the resistance of wires is taken to be **zero**.

## Current–Voltage Characteristics

### Ohm's Law

**Ohm's law** states: for a conductor at a constant temperature, the current through it is proportional to the potential difference across it. Constant temperature implies constant resistance. This is expressed as:

$$V = IR$$

The relationship between potential difference and current for a component (such as a fixed resistor) can be investigated using a circuit containing a voltage supply, the component under test with a **voltmeter** in parallel across it, an **ammeter** in series, and a **variable resistor** to adjust the current and p.d. in the circuit.

By adjusting the variable resistor, different pairs of current and potential difference values are recorded. For a fixed resistor, plotting current against potential difference produces a **straight line** through the origin.

The gradient of an $I$–$V$ graph equals $\frac{1}{R}$, so the resistance can be found from $R = \frac{1}{\text{gradient}}$.

An electrical component **obeys Ohm's law** if its graph of current against potential difference is a **straight line through the origin**. A resistor obeys Ohm's law. A filament lamp does **not** obey Ohm's law. This applies to any metal wires, provided the current is not large enough to increase their temperature.

**Worked example (method: deducing R–V graph from an I–V characteristic).** Given an $I$–$V$ graph that is initially linear then curves upward with increasing gradient: because $R = V/I$, the initial straight-line portion means constant resistance (horizontal $R$–$V$ line), while the increasing gradient means decreasing resistance (the $R$–$V$ line curves downward). The correct $R$–$V$ graph starts horizontal then falls.

### I–V Characteristics

As the potential difference across a component is increased, the current also increases (by Ohm's law). The precise relationship differs for different components and is shown on an **I–V graph**.

| Component | I–V Graph Shape | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Ohmic conductor** (e.g. resistor) | Straight line through the origin | $I \propto V$; constant gradient; constant resistance |
| **Semiconductor diode** | Horizontal line (left), sharp upward curve (right) | Conducts in one direction only; **forward bias** gives sharply increasing $I$; **reverse bias** gives zero $I$ |
| **Filament lamp** | S-shaped curve through the origin | Current increases at a proportionally slower rate than p.d.; symmetric for positive and negative p.d. |

**Ohmic conductor:** The $I$–$V$ graph is a straight line through the origin, showing current is **directly proportional** to potential difference. Resistance is constant.

**Semiconductor diode:** A diode allows current to flow in only one specific direction. In **forward bias** (current in the direction of the arrowhead symbol), there is a sharp increase in current and potential difference, shown on the right side of the graph. In **reverse bias** (diode switched around), the diode does not conduct, giving zero current, shown on the left side of the graph.

**Filament lamp:** The $I$–$V$ graph shows current increasing at a proportionally slower rate than the potential difference. As the current increases, the **temperature** of the filament **increases**. Since the filament is a metal, higher temperature causes an **increase** in **resistance**. This increased resistance opposes the current, causing it to **increase** at a **slower rate**. Where the graph is a straight line (at low voltages), the resistance is constant. As the graph curves, the resistance is increasing. The filament lamp obeys Ohm's law for small voltages only.

**Worked example (method: identifying components from I–V graphs).** Given two $I$–$V$ graphs: a linear graph (X) indicates constant gradient and therefore constant resistance, characteristic of a **resistor**. A graph (Y) that starts with zero gradient then increases rapidly indicates initially infinite resistance that then decreases sharply, characteristic of a **semiconductor diode** (current flows in one direction only).

**Exam tip:** Be confident drawing the $I$–$V$ characteristics for each component from memory, as exam questions may require sketching them.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Variables |
|---|---|---|
| Electric current | $I = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$ | $I$: current (A), $\Delta Q$: charge (C), $\Delta t$: time (s) |
| Potential difference | $V = \dfrac{W}{Q}$ | $V$: p.d. (V), $W$: work done (J), $Q$: charge (C) |
| Resistance (Ohm's law) | $R = \dfrac{V}{I}$ or $V = IR$ | $R$: resistance ($\Omega$), $V$: p.d. (V), $I$: current (A) |
| Work done | $W = QV$ | $W$: energy (J), $Q$: charge (C), $V$: p.d. (V) |
