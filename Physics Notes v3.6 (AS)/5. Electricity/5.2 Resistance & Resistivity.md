# Resistance & Resistivity

## Resistivity

All materials have some **resistance** to the flow of charge. As **free electrons** move through a metal wire, they collide with the positive metal **ions** in the lattice. On each **collision**, the electrons **transfer** some or all of their **kinetic energy** to the ions, producing electrical **heating**.

Since **current** is the **flow** of **charge**, ions resisting the flow of electrons cause resistance. Resistance depends on three factors: the **length** of the wire, the **cross-sectional area** through which current passes, and the **resistivity** of the material.

The resistance equation is:

$$R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$$

where $R$ is resistance ($\Omega$), $\rho$ is resistivity ($\Omega$ m), $L$ is length (m), and $A$ is cross-sectional area (m$^2$).

From this equation:

- The **longer** the wire, the **greater** its resistance (directly proportional).
- The **thicker** the wire, the **smaller** its resistance (inversely proportional to area).

Doubling the length doubles the resistance. Doubling the cross-sectional area halves the resistance.

**Resistivity** is a property that describes the extent to which a material opposes the flow of electric current through it. It is a property of the material itself (not the shape or size of a sample) and is dependent on temperature. Resistivity is measured in $\Omega$ m.

| Category | Material | Resistivity / $\Omega$ m |
|---|---|---|
| Metals | Copper | $1.7 \times 10^{-8}$ |
| Metals | Gold | $2.4 \times 10^{-8}$ |
| Metals | Aluminium | $2.6 \times 10^{-8}$ |
| Semiconductors | Germanium | $0.6$ |
| Semiconductors | Silicon | $2.3 \times 10^{3}$ |
| Insulators | Glass | $10^{12}$ |
| Insulators | Sulfur | $10^{15}$ |

The higher the resistivity of a material, the higher its resistance. Copper is used for electrical wires because its low resistivity at room temperature allows current to flow through it very easily. Insulators have such high resistivity that virtually no current flows through them.

**Worked example method (comparing conductors of different geometry):** To determine which of two conducting cylinders is the better conductor, calculate $R = \rho L / A$ for each, using $A = \pi(d/2)^2$ for circular cross-sections. The cylinder with the lower resistance is the better conductor. For a copper cylinder (diameter 5 mm, length 8 mm, $\rho = 1.7 \times 10^{-8}$ $\Omega$ m) this gives $R = 6.8 \times 10^{-6}$ $\Omega$, and for an aluminium cylinder (diameter 10 mm, length 16 mm, $\rho = 2.6 \times 10^{-8}$ $\Omega$ m) this gives $R = 5.3 \times 10^{-6}$ $\Omega$. Despite having higher resistivity, the aluminium cylinder has lower resistance because of its larger cross-sectional area relative to its length.

**Exam tip:** Resistivity values will always be given in the question. If the cross-sectional area is circular (e.g. a wire), area is proportional to the diameter squared ($A = \pi d^2 / 4$). If the diameter doubles, the area quadruples, causing the resistance to drop to a quarter.

## Resistance in a Thermistor

### Temperature and Resistance

All solids are made up of vibrating atoms. The higher the temperature, the faster these atoms vibrate. Electric current is the flow of free electrons in a material. The electrons collide with the vibrating atoms, which impede their flow, so the current **decreases**.

If current decreases (at constant voltage), resistance increases (from $V = IR$). Therefore, for a **metallic conductor** which obeys Ohm's law:

- An **increase** in temperature causes an **increase** in resistance.
- A **decrease** in temperature causes a **decrease** in resistance.

This is **not** the case for a **thermistor**. For a thermistor, an **increase** in temperature causes a **decrease** in resistance.

### Applications of a Thermistor

A **thermistor** is a non-ohmic conductor and sensory resistor whose resistance varies with temperature. Most thermistors are **negative temperature coefficient (NTC)** components, meaning that if temperature increases, resistance decreases (and vice versa).

The temperature-resistance graph for an NTC thermistor shows an exponential-type decrease: resistance is high at low temperatures and falls steeply at first, then more gradually as temperature rises.

Thermistors are used as temperature sensors in circuits for ovens, fire alarms, and digital thermometers.

- As the thermistor gets **hotter**, its resistance **decreases**.
- As the thermistor gets **cooler**, its resistance **increases**.

**Worked example method (thermistor in a series circuit):** When a thermistor is connected in series with a fixed resistor $R$ and a battery, and the temperature of the thermistor decreases, its resistance increases. The total circuit resistance increases, so the current through the whole series circuit decreases. Applying $V = IR$ to the thermistor: since its resistance has increased while the (now smaller) current is the same through both components, the p.d. across the thermistor increases. Conversely, the p.d. across $R$ decreases because $R$ now takes a smaller proportion of the cell's emf. This is a key principle for potential divider questions involving thermistors.

## Superconductivity

All materials have some resistivity, even good electrical conductors such as copper and silver. Resistance means that when electricity flows through a material, it heats up and electrical energy is wasted as thermal energy.

The resistivity of a material can be lowered by lowering its temperature. If a material is cooled below a temperature called the **critical temperature**, its resistivity disappears entirely. It is now a **superconductor**.

A **superconductor** (or superconducting material) is defined as: **a material with no resistance below a critical temperature**.

The **critical temperature** (also called the **transition temperature**) is defined as: **the temperature at which a material becomes superconducting**.

A common superconducting material is mercury, which has a critical temperature of 4.2 K.

On a graph of electrical resistivity against temperature, a normal metal shows resistivity decreasing gradually and roughly linearly as temperature falls, but never reaching zero. A superconductor follows a similar curve above $T_c$, but at $T_c$ the resistivity drops abruptly to exactly zero and remains zero for all temperatures below $T_c$.

Superconductivity is a property of only certain materials.

### Applications of Superconductors

Superconductors are useful for applications that require large electric currents, particularly for the production of strong magnetic fields and the reduction of energy loss/dissipation in the transmission of electric power. Applications include: MRI scanners, transformers and generators (fewer fire risks), motors, monorail trains, **maglev** (magnetic levitation) trains, particle accelerators (which need large magnetic fields to accelerate particles), fusion reactors, electromagnets, power/electrical cables, and microchips.

Maglev trains require extremely strong electromagnets both on the train and on the rails to levitate the train due to its large mass. They can reach speeds up to 603 km/h. Maglev train systems currently exist in Japan, South Korea, and China.

**Exam tip:** Superconductivity occurs when there is **no** resistance. Avoid writing that there is "a little" resistance or "thermal" conductivity, which are not correct descriptions.

## Required Practical: Investigating Resistivity

### Aims of the Experiment

The aim is to determine the resistivity of a 2 metre constantan wire.

**Variables:**

| Variable type | Variable | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| Independent | Length $L$ of the wire | m |
| Dependent | Current $I$ through the wire | A |
| Control | Voltage through the wire, material of the wire | V, — |

### Equipment

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Ammeter | To determine the current through the wire |
| Voltmeter | To determine the voltage across the wire |
| 2.0 m of constantan wire (22–36 swg) | To calculate its resistivity |
| Flying lead | A wire with a crocodile clip at one end to allow connection at any point along the test wire |
| Metre ruler | To measure the length of the wire |
| Micrometer screw gauge | To measure the diameter of the wire |
| Power supply | To provide the voltage through the wire |

**Resolution of measuring equipment:** metre ruler = 1 mm, micrometer screw gauge = 0.01 mm, voltmeter = 0.1 V, ammeter = 0.01 A.

### Method

1. Measure the diameter of the constantan wire using a micrometer. Take the measurement 5–10 times at random points along the wire and calculate the mean diameter.
2. Set up the equipment so the wire is taped or clamped to the ruler with one end of the circuit attached to the wire at the 0 mark. The ammeter is connected in series and the voltmeter in parallel to the wire.
3. Attach the flying lead to the test wire at 0.25 m.
4. Set the power supply at a voltage of 6.0 V. Check this is the voltage through the wire on the voltmeter.
5. Read and record the current from the ammeter, then switch off the current immediately after the reading. This prevents the wire from heating up and changing the resistivity.
6. Vary the distance between the fixed end of the wire and the flying lead in 0.25 m intervals (0.25, 0.50, 0.75 m, etc.) until the full 2.0 m length. The starting length and intervals can be changed (e.g. start at 0.1 m in 0.1 m intervals), as long as there are 8–10 readings.
7. Record the current for each length at least 3 times and calculate an average current $I$.
8. For each length, calculate the average resistance using $R = V / I$.

### Analysis of Results

The resistivity of the wire is $\rho = RA / L$. Rearranging gives:

$$R = \frac{\rho}{A} \cdot L$$

Comparing to the equation of a straight line $y = mx$: $y = R$, $x = L$, and the gradient $m = \rho / A$.

Therefore, to find resistivity:

1. Plot a graph of length $L$ (x-axis) against average resistance $R$ (y-axis).
2. Draw a line of best fit.
3. Calculate the gradient $\Delta R / \Delta L$.
4. Multiply the gradient by the cross-sectional area:

$$\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$$

The cross-sectional area is calculated from the mean diameter:

$$A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4}$$

**Worked example method:** Given experimental current data at 8 lengths from 0.25 m to 2.00 m (with 3 repeats each), and 10 diameter measurements averaging 0.191 mm: first calculate average current at each length and then $R = V/I$ for each. Calculate $A = \pi(0.191 \times 10^{-3})^2 / 4 = 2.87 \times 10^{-8}$ m$^2$. Plot $R$ against $L$, draw a line of best fit, and take the gradient from the line (not from data points). Using gradient points (0.3, 5.00) and (1.7, 27.00) gives gradient $= 22.00 / 1.40 = 15.71$ $\Omega$ m$^{-1}$. Then $\rho = 15.71 \times 2.87 \times 10^{-8} = 4.51 \times 10^{-7}$ $\Omega$ m.

### Evaluating the Experiment

**Systematic errors:**

- The fixed end of the wire attached to the circuit must start at 0 on the ruler, otherwise this introduces a zero error in length measurements.

**Random errors:**

- Only allow small currents to flow through the wire. Resistivity depends on temperature, and current flowing through the wire increases its temperature, affecting resistance and resistivity. Temperature is kept constant and low by using small currents.
- The current should be switched off between readings so the temperature does not change the resistance.
- Take at least 5–10 measurements of the diameter with the micrometer screw gauge and calculate an average to reduce random errors.

### Safety Considerations

- With high current and thin wire, the wire becomes very hot. Never touch the wire directly when the circuit is switched on.
- Switch off the power supply immediately if you smell burning.
- Ensure no liquids are close to the equipment, as this could damage the electrical equipment.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Resistance (from resistivity) | $R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$ | $\rho$ in $\Omega$ m, $L$ in m, $A$ in m$^2$ |
| Resistivity (from experiment) | $\rho = \frac{RA}{L}$ | Rearrangement of the above |
| Resistivity (from graph) | $\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$ | Where gradient is from $R$ vs $L$ graph |
| Cross-sectional area (circular wire) | $A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4}$ | $d$ = diameter in m |
| Resistance (from Ohm's law) | $R = \frac{V}{I}$ | Used in the required practical |
