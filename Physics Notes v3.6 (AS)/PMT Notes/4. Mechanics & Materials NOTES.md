# Section 4: Mechanics and Materials

## 3.4.1 Force, Energy and Momentum

### 3.4.1.1 Scalars and Vectors

A **scalar** is a quantity with magnitude only. A **vector** is a quantity with both magnitude and direction.

| Scalars | Vectors |
|---|---|
| Distance, speed, mass, temperature | Displacement, velocity, force/weight, acceleration |

**Adding vectors** uses two methods depending on geometry:

**Calculation (perpendicular vectors):** Use Pythagoras to find the resultant magnitude and trigonometry for direction. For two perpendicular forces of 12 N and 5 N: $R = \sqrt{12^2 + 5^2} = 13$ N, with $\theta = \tan^{-1}(5/12) = 22.6°$ from the horizontal. Always state what the angle is measured from.

**Scale drawing (non-perpendicular vectors):** Draw each vector tip-to-tail at the correct angle using a ruler and protractor, then measure the resultant's length (converting via scale) and direction.

**Resolving vectors** is the reverse of addition: splitting a single vector $V$ into two perpendicular components using trigonometry.

$$x = V\cos\theta \qquad y = V\sin\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the vector and the horizontal. The rule: if you move from the original vector *through* the angle to reach the component, use $\cos$; if you move *away* from the angle, use $\sin$.

**Inclined plane example:** A 50 N weight on a plane inclined at 15° resolves into a component parallel to the plane $= 50\sin 15° = 12.9$ N (moving away from the angle) and a component perpendicular to the plane $= 50\cos 15° = 48.3$ N (moving through the angle). The angle between the weight vector and the perpendicular-to-plane direction equals the angle of inclination.

**Equilibrium** requires the sum of all forces acting on an object to be zero (no resultant force). An object in equilibrium is either at rest or moving at constant velocity (Newton's first law). You can demonstrate equilibrium by resolving all forces into horizontal and vertical components and showing each sums to zero, or, for exactly three forces, by drawing them as a **closed triangle** in a scale diagram.

### 3.4.1.2 Moments

The **moment** of a force about a point is defined as:

$$\text{Moment} = F \times d$$

where $F$ is the force and $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot.

A **couple** is a pair of **coplanar** (in the same plane) forces that are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, acting through different lines of action. A couple produces rotation without translation.

$$\text{Moment of a couple} = F \times d$$

where $d$ is the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of the two forces.

The **principle of moments** states that for an object in equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about that point.

**Worked method:** When taking moments, choose a pivot at the point where an unknown force acts, so that force's moment is zero and it drops out of the calculation. For example, a beam with forces 50 N and 35 N at 2 m and 3 m from point A, balanced by force $F$ at 3.5 m from A: taking moments about A gives $(2 \times 50) + (3 \times 35) = 3.5F$, so $F = 205/3.5 = 58.6$ N.

The **centre of mass** is the single point through which all of an object's mass can be said to act. For a **uniform** object, the centre of mass lies at its geometric centre.

### 3.4.1.3 Motion Along a Straight Line

**Speed** is a scalar quantity describing how quickly an object travels.

**Displacement** ($s$) is the distance from the starting position in a specified direction (vector).

**Velocity** ($v$) is the rate of change of displacement: $v = \Delta s / \Delta t$.

**Acceleration** ($a$) is the rate of change of velocity: $a = \Delta v / \Delta t$.

**Instantaneous velocity** is the velocity at a specific moment, found from the gradient of a tangent to a displacement-time graph at that instant. **Average velocity** is the total displacement divided by the total time taken.

**Uniform acceleration** means acceleration is constant over time.

| Graph type | Gradient gives | Area under gives |
|---|---|---|
| Displacement-time | Velocity | -- |
| Velocity-time | Acceleration | Displacement |
| Acceleration-time | -- | Change in velocity |

A non-uniform acceleration on a velocity-time graph produces a curve; a uniform acceleration produces a straight line.

**SUVAT equations** (valid only for uniform acceleration):

$$v = u + at$$

$$s = \left(\frac{u + v}{2}\right)t$$

$$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

where $s$ = displacement, $u$ = initial velocity, $v$ = final velocity, $a$ = acceleration, $t$ = time.

**Strategy:** List known values of $s$, $u$, $v$, $a$, $t$ and identify the unknown, then select the equation that contains exactly those variables.

**Worked method (dropped stone, $s = 50$ m):** $u = 0$, $a = 9.81$ m/s². Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $v^2 = 0 + 2(9.81)(50) = 981$, so $v = 31.3$ m/s. Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$: $50 = \frac{1}{2}(9.81)t^2$, so $t = 3.19$ s.

### 3.4.1.4 Projectile Motion

The vertical and horizontal components of a projectile's motion are **independent** and can be evaluated separately using SUVAT, with constant acceleration ($a = -g$ vertically, $a = 0$ horizontally when air resistance is neglected).

**Method:** Resolve the initial velocity into components ($u_x = u\cos\theta$, $u_y = u\sin\theta$), then apply SUVAT to each direction independently. Maximum height occurs when the vertical component of velocity equals zero. For a projectile launched from and returning to the same level, the total flight time equals twice the time to reach maximum height.

**Worked method (ball at 20 m/s, 60° from horizontal):** $u_y = 20\sin 60° = 17.3$ m/s, $u_x = 20\cos 60° = 10$ m/s. At max height, $v_y = 0$: using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ gives $0 = 17.3^2 - 2(9.81)s$, so $s = 15.3$ m. Using $v = u + at$: $0 = 17.3 - 9.81t$, so $t = 1.76$ s to peak, giving total flight time $= 3.5$ s.

**Free fall** is motion under gravity alone, with acceleration $g = 9.81$ m/s².

**Friction** (also called **drag** or **air resistance** in fluids) opposes motion and converts kinetic energy into heat and sound. The magnitude of air resistance increases as the speed of the object increases.

**Lift** is an upward force on objects moving through a fluid, caused by the object redirecting fluid flow. It acts perpendicular to the direction of fluid flow.

**Terminal speed** (or terminal velocity) occurs when resistive forces equal the driving forces, producing zero resultant force and therefore zero acceleration. The object then moves at constant speed. A skydiver illustrates this: initially weight exceeds air resistance so the skydiver accelerates; as speed increases, air resistance grows until it equals weight, at which point terminal velocity is reached.

With air resistance, a projectile reaches its maximum height sooner, and both its maximum height and horizontal range decrease compared to the ideal (no air resistance) case.

### 3.4.1.5 Newton's Laws of Motion

**Newton's first law:** An object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant force.

**Newton's second law:** The resultant force on an object equals the rate of change of its momentum. For constant mass this gives:

$$F = ma$$

where $F$ is the resultant force, $m$ is mass, and $a$ is acceleration.

**Newton's third law:** If object A exerts a force on object B, then B exerts a force on A that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. The two forces act on different objects, are of the same type, and exist simultaneously.

A **free-body diagram** shows all forces acting on a single object as labelled arrows. Equal-length arrows indicate balanced forces (equilibrium / constant velocity); unequal arrows indicate a resultant force and therefore acceleration.

**Worked method (ball, weight 100 N, pushed right 75 N, resisted left 10 N):** Mass $= W/g = 100/9.81 = 10.2$ kg. Resultant horizontal force $= 75 - 10 = 65$ N to the right. The vertical forces (weight and normal reaction, each 100 N) cancel. $a = F/m = 65/10.2 = 6.4$ m/s².

### 3.4.1.6 Momentum

**Momentum** is the product of an object's mass and velocity:

$$p = mv$$

Momentum is a vector quantity, measured in kg m/s.

**Conservation of momentum:** The total momentum of a system is conserved provided no external forces act. Total momentum before an event equals total momentum after.

**Worked method (collision):** A 500 kg car at 4 m/s collides with a stationary 1500 kg truck; they join together. Total momentum before $= (500 \times 4) + (1500 \times 0) = 2000$ kg m/s. After: $2000 = 2000 \times V$, so $V = 1$ m/s.

From Newton's second law, force equals the rate of change of momentum:

$$F = \frac{\Delta(mv)}{\Delta t}$$

Rearranging gives the **impulse** equation:

$$F\Delta t = \Delta(mv)$$

**Impulse** ($F\Delta t$) is the change in momentum of an object. It equals the area under a force-time graph.

**Worked method (impulse):** A 100 N force acts for 0.5 s on a ball. Change in momentum $= 100 \times 0.5 = 50$ kg m/s.

**Car safety applications:** Crumple zones, seat belts, and airbags all increase the impact time $\Delta t$ over which the passenger decelerates. Since $F = \Delta(mv)/\Delta t$, a longer impact time reduces the force on the passenger, reducing injury severity.

| Collision type | Momentum conserved? | Kinetic energy conserved? |
|---|---|---|
| **Elastic** | Yes | Yes |
| **Inelastic** | Yes | No (KE converted to heat, sound, deformation, etc.) |

If the colliding objects stick together, the collision is always inelastic. An **explosion** is also inelastic because kinetic energy after the event is greater than before (chemical/internal energy is converted to KE), even though momentum is still conserved (total momentum remains zero if the system was initially at rest).

### 3.4.1.6 Work, Energy and Power

> ⚠ Check: source labels this section 3.4.1.6, the same number as Momentum above; the AQA specification numbers it 3.4.1.7.

**Work done** ($W$) is the force causing motion multiplied by the distance travelled in the direction of the force:

$$W = Fs\cos\theta$$

where $s$ is the distance travelled and $\theta$ is the angle between the direction of the force and the direction of motion. When the force varies, work done equals the area under a **force-displacement graph**.

**Power** ($P$) is the rate of energy transfer:

$$P = \frac{\Delta W}{\Delta t} = Fv$$

This follows because $P = F \times \Delta s / \Delta t = Fv$.

**Efficiency** is the ratio of useful energy (or power) output to total energy (or power) input:

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful output power}}{\text{input power}}$$

Multiply by 100 to express as a percentage.

---

### 3.4.1.7 Conservation of Energy

The **principle of conservation of energy** states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between forms. In a closed system, total energy remains constant:

$$\text{Total energy in} = \text{Total energy out}$$

Key energy formulae:

$$\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h \qquad \text{(change in gravitational potential energy)}$$

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \qquad \text{(kinetic energy)}$$

When an object rises, kinetic energy transfers to gravitational potential energy; when it falls, the reverse occurs. In reality, work is done against resistive forces (e.g. air resistance), so the kinetic energy given to an object is not fully converted to gravitational potential energy at its peak: some energy is dissipated as heat to the surroundings.

**Worked method (pendulum):** A 0.5 kg pendulum rises 0.1 m at maximum amplitude. $\Delta E_p = 0.5 \times 9.81 \times 0.1 = 0.4905$ J. At the equilibrium position (lowest point), all GPE has been converted to KE (ignoring air resistance): $\frac{1}{2}(0.5)v^2 = 0.4905$, giving $v^2 = 1.962$, so $v = 1.4$ m/s.

## 3.4.2 Materials

### 3.4.2.1 Bulk Properties of Solids

**Density** is the mass per unit volume of a material ($\rho = m/V$), measuring how compact a substance is.

**Hooke's law** states that the extension of an elastic object is directly proportional to the force applied, up to the **limit of proportionality**. On a force-extension graph this appears as a straight line through the origin.

$$F = k\Delta L$$

where $k$ is the **spring constant** (a measure of the **stiffness** of the object, in N/m) and $\Delta L$ is the extension.

The **limit of proportionality** (P) is the point beyond which Hooke's law no longer holds (force and extension are no longer proportional). The **elastic limit** (E) lies just beyond P: below it, the material deforms elastically (returns to original shape when force is removed); beyond it, **plastic** deformation occurs (permanent deformation).

**Elastic behaviour:** The material returns to its original shape when the deforming force is removed. No permanent deformation occurs.

**Plastic behaviour:** The material does not return to its original shape when the deforming force is removed. Permanent deformation remains.

**Tensile stress** is the force applied per unit cross-sectional area:

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \qquad \text{(unit: Pa)}$$

**Tensile strain** is the ratio of extension to original length:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \qquad \text{(dimensionless, no unit)}$$

**Elastic strain energy** is the energy stored in a stretched or compressed material. Because the force is variable during stretching, $W = Fs\cos\theta$ cannot be used directly. Instead, elastic strain energy equals the area under a force-extension graph:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}F\Delta L$$

(This formula applies in the linear, Hooke's law region where the graph is a straight line.)

**Breaking stress** is the maximum stress a material can withstand before fracture. Its value depends on conditions such as temperature.

**Force-extension graph behaviours:**

| Behaviour | Description |
|---|---|
| **Plastic** | Large extension as load increases, especially beyond the elastic limit |
| **Brittle** | Very little extension before fracture occurs at low strain |

**Loading and unloading beyond the elastic limit:** The unloading curve does not return to the origin but runs parallel to the loading line (same gradient, same stiffness). The area between the loading and unloading lines represents the work done to permanently deform the material.

For **elastic** deformation, all work done is stored as elastic strain energy and fully recoverable. For **plastic** deformation, work is done to move atoms apart permanently, and this energy is dissipated as heat rather than stored.

**Safety applications:** Car crumple zones absorb kinetic energy through plastic deformation. Seat belts stretch to convert the passenger's kinetic energy into elastic strain energy, increasing impact time.

When a vertically hung spring with a mass is stretched and released, elastic strain energy converts back to kinetic energy, which then transfers to gravitational potential energy as the mass rises.

**Stress-strain graphs** describe the behaviour of a *material* (not a specific object). The highest point on the graph is the **ultimate tensile stress** (UTS), the maximum stress the material can withstand. The shape indicates whether a material is **ductile** (large plastic deformation before fracture), **brittle** (fractures with little deformation), or **plastic** (large permanent deformation).

---

### 3.4.2.2 Young Modulus

The **Young modulus** ($E$) quantifies the stiffness of a material. Up to the limit of proportionality, stress is proportional to strain, so their ratio is constant:

$$E = \frac{\text{Tensile Stress}}{\text{Tensile Strain}} = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}$$

Substituting the definitions of stress and strain:

$$E = \frac{FL}{\Delta L \cdot A}$$

where $F$ is the applied force, $L$ is the original length, $\Delta L$ is the extension, and $A$ is the cross-sectional area. The unit is the Pascal (Pa).

The Young modulus can be determined from a stress-strain graph by calculating the **gradient** of the straight (linear) portion of the graph.
