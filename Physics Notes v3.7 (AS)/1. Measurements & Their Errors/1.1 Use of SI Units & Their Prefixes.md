# Use of SI Units & Their Prefixes

## SI Units

### SI Base Quantities

Every unit in physics can be reduced to combinations of seven **SI base units**, the internationally standardised system of measurement used in almost every country. Only six of these are required for the AQA A-Level course:

| Quantity | SI Base Unit | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Length | metre | m |
| Time | second | s |
| Current | ampere | A |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Amount of substance | mole | mol |

### Derived Units

**Derived units** are constructed from the base units using the defining equation of the quantity. To find the base units of any derived quantity, substitute base units into its definition.

**Newton (N)** — the unit of force:

$$F = ma \implies \text{N} = \text{kg} \times \text{m s}^{-2} = \text{kg m s}^{-2}$$

**Joule (J)** — the unit of energy (using kinetic energy $E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$, where $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is dimensionless):

$$\text{J} = \text{kg} \times (\text{m s}^{-1})^2 = \text{kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-2}$$

**Pascal (Pa)** — the unit of pressure:

$$P = \frac{F}{A} \implies \text{Pa} = \frac{\text{kg m s}^{-2}}{\text{m}^2} = \text{kg m}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-2}$$

The method generalises to any derived unit: write the defining equation, replace every quantity with its base units, and simplify.

## Powers of Ten

### Prefixes

Physical quantities span an enormous range. The diameter of an atom is roughly $10^{-10}$ m while the width of a galaxy can reach $10^{21}$ m, a span of 31 orders of magnitude. Standard prefixes attached to units avoid writing out long strings of zeros.

| Prefix | Abbreviation | Value |
|---|---|---|
| tera | T | $10^{12}$ |
| giga | G | $10^{9}$ |
| mega | M | $10^{6}$ |
| kilo | k | $10^{3}$ |
| centi | c | $10^{-2}$ |
| milli | m | $10^{-3}$ |
| micro | $\mu$ | $10^{-6}$ |
| nano | n | $10^{-9}$ |
| pico | p | $10^{-12}$ |
| femto | f | $10^{-15}$ |

Converting with prefixes: replace the prefix with the corresponding power of ten. For example, $5\;\text{kN} = 5 \times 10^{3}\;\text{N}$ and $7\;\text{nC} = 7 \times 10^{-9}\;\text{C}$.

> **Exam tip:** Mishandling prefixes (e.g. confusing milli and micro, or forgetting to convert before substituting into equations) is one of the most common sources of lost marks.

### Common Unit Conversions

#### Joules and electronvolts

The **electronvolt (eV)** is defined from $W = qV$: the energy gained by a single electron charge accelerated through 1 V.

$$1\;\text{eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\;\text{C} \times 1\;\text{V} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\;\text{J}$$

To convert J $\to$ eV: divide by $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$. To convert eV $\to$ J: multiply by $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$.

**Worked example — ionisation energy of hydrogen:**

$$E = 2.176 \times 10^{-18}\;\text{J} \implies \frac{2.176 \times 10^{-18}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 13.6\;\text{eV}$$

#### Joules and kilowatt-hours

Expand the compound unit step by step: $1\;\text{kW h} = 1000\;\text{W} \times 3600\;\text{s} = 3.6 \times 10^{6}\;\text{J} = 3.6\;\text{MJ}$ (using $\text{power} = \text{energy} / \text{time}$, so $1\;\text{W} = 1\;\text{J s}^{-1}$).

To convert J $\to$ kW h: divide by $3.6 \times 10^{6}$. To convert kW h $\to$ J: multiply by $3.6 \times 10^{6}$.

## Estimating Physical Quantities

### Orders of Magnitude

The **order of magnitude** of a quantity is the power of ten closest to its value when written in scientific notation. It provides a quick way to compare sizes without worrying about exact figures.

Key rules for determining order of magnitude: if the coefficient is below 5, keep the exponent (e.g. $3 \times 10^{8} \to 10^{8}$); if the coefficient is 5 or above, round up (e.g. $6 \times 10^{8} \to 10^{9}$).

A quantity is **one order of magnitude larger** than another if it is roughly ten times bigger. Two orders of magnitude means roughly $10^{2} = 100$ times bigger, and so on. The difference in orders of magnitude between two quantities gives the factor-of-ten ratio between them (e.g. $10^{6}$ vs $10^{2}$: difference of 4, so the first is $10^{4} = 10\,000$ times larger).

**Scientific notation** expresses any number as a coefficient between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of ten, avoiding unwieldy strings of zeros (e.g. diameter of the Milky Way $\approx 1 \times 10^{21}\;\text{m}$, order of magnitude $10^{21}$).

#### Orders of Magnitude — Lengths

| Object | Approximate Length | Order of Magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| Observable universe (edge) | $4.40 \times 10^{26}\;\text{m}$ | $10^{26}\;\text{m}$ |
| Earth to Neptune | $4.5 \times 10^{12}\;\text{m}$ | $10^{12}\;\text{m}$ |
| London to Cape Town | $9.7 \times 10^{6}\;\text{m}$ | $10^{7}\;\text{m}$ |
| Adult human height | $1.7\;\text{m}$ | $10^{0}\;\text{m}$ |
| Length of an ant | $9 \times 10^{-4}\;\text{m}$ | $10^{-3}\;\text{m}$ |
| Length of a bacterium | $2 \times 10^{-6}\;\text{m}$ | $10^{-6}\;\text{m}$ |

### Estimating Physical Quantities

The table below lists approximate values that are useful for estimation questions in exams.

| Quantity | Approximate Value |
|---|---|
| Diameter of an atom | $10^{-10}\;\text{m}$ |
| Wavelength of UV light | $10\;\text{nm}$ |
| Height of an adult human | $2\;\text{m}$ |
| Earth–Sun distance (1 AU) | $1.5 \times 10^{11}\;\text{m}$ |
| Mass of a hydrogen atom | $10^{-27}\;\text{kg}$ |
| Mass of an adult human | $70\;\text{kg}$ |
| Mass of a car | $1000\;\text{kg}$ |
| Seconds in a day | $90\,000\;\text{s}$ |
| Seconds in a year | $3 \times 10^{7}\;\text{s}$ |
| Speed of sound in air | $300\;\text{m s}^{-1}$ |
| Power of a lightbulb | $60\;\text{W}$ |
| Atmospheric pressure | $1 \times 10^{5}\;\text{Pa}$ |

**Worked example — estimating with orders of magnitude:**

*Surface temperature of the Sun:* approximately 6000 K, giving an order of magnitude of $\sim 10^{4}\;\text{K}$.

*Power of a standard lightbulb:* approximately 60 W, giving an order of magnitude of $\sim 10^{2}\;\text{W}$.

*Volume of a room:* model as a cube of side length 3 m. $V = 3^{3} = 27\;\text{m}^{3}$, order of magnitude $\sim 10^{1}\;\text{m}^{3}$.

**Worked example — energy to climb a flight of stairs:**

Use $E = mgh$. Estimate the mass of an adult as $\sim 70\;\text{kg}$, gravitational field strength $g \approx 10\;\text{N kg}^{-1}$, and the height of a staircase as $\sim 3\;\text{m}$:

$$E \approx 70 \times 10 \times 3 = 2100\;\text{J}$$
