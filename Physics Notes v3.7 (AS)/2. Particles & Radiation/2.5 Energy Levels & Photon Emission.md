# Energy Levels & Photon Emission

## Collisions of Electrons with Atoms

### Ionisation & Excitation

Electrons in an atom occupy discrete **energy levels**. Electrons occupy the lowest possible energy level as this is the most **stable** configuration for the atom.

When an electron **absorbs** or **emits** a photon, it can move between energy levels or be removed from the atom entirely.

**Excitation** is when an electron absorbs enough energy to move up to a higher energy level. When an electron moves to a higher energy level, the atom is said to be in an **excited state**. To excite an electron, it must **absorb** a photon with energy exactly equal to the difference between two energy levels.

Electrons can return to a lower energy level by **de-excitation**. To de-excite, an electron must **emit** a photon whose energy equals the difference between the two levels.

**Ionisation** is when an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged. When an electron is removed from an atom, the atom becomes **ionised**. An electron can be removed from any energy level it occupies. The **ionisation energy** of an atom is the **minimum** energy required to remove an electron from the **ground state** of the atom.

### Fluorescent Tube

**Fluorescence** occurs when an electron in an atomic orbital absorbs energy from an interaction with a photon or a collision with another electron.

**Fluorescent tubes** are partially evacuated glass tubes filled with low-pressure mercury vapour with a **phosphor** coating on the glass. They operate on the basis of excitation and de-excitation of electrons leading to the emission of visible light.

The process works as follows:

1. A **high voltage** is applied across the tube, causing electrons to flow from the cathode to the anode, producing an electron beam.
2. These beam electrons **collide** with electrons in the mercury atoms, **transferring kinetic energy** in the collision.
3. The atomic electrons in the **mercury** atoms gain enough energy for **ionisation** to occur.
4. A high energy state is unstable, so high energy electrons **de-excite** back to their original **ground state**.
5. As they de-excite, the electrons release energy by **emitting photons** in the **UV** range of wavelengths.
6. The UV photons collide with **electrons** in the atoms of the **phosphor** coating and **excite** them into a higher energy level.
7. As these phosphor electrons **de-excite**, they do so **in stages**, emitting photons in the **visible light** range of wavelengths.

The key distinction is that mercury atoms emit UV photons (single large transition), while phosphor atoms emit visible light photons (multiple smaller transitions, hence the staged de-excitation).

## Energy Levels & Photon Emission

### Line Spectra & Energy Levels

Energy levels are represented as a series of horizontal lines on an energy level diagram:

- The line at the bottom with the greatest negative energy represents the **ground state** ($n = 1$).
- The lines above the ground state with decreasing (less negative) energies represent **excited states** ($n = 2, 3, 4, ...$).
- The line at the top, usually $0 \text{ eV}$ or infinity $\infty$, represents the **ionisation energy**.

**Hydrogen atom energy levels:**

| Level ($n$) | Energy (eV) |
|---|---|
| 1 (ground state) | $-13.6$ |
| 2 | $-3.40$ |
| 3 | $-1.51$ |
| 4 | $-0.85$ |
| 5 | $-0.54$ |
| 6 | $\approx 0$ |
| $\infty$ (ionisation) | $0$ |

A photon is emitted when an electron moves from a higher energy state to a lower energy state. The energy of the emitted photon is equal to the difference in energy between the energy levels in the transition.

### Line Spectra

**Line spectra** occur when excited atoms emit light of certain wavelengths which correspond to different colours. The emitted light is observed as a series of **lines** with spaces in between. These series of lines are called **line or atomic spectra**.

Each element produces a unique set of spectral lines. No two elements emit the same set of spectral lines, so elements can be identified by their line spectrum.

There are two types of line spectra: **emission spectra** and **absorption spectra**.

### Emission Spectra

When an electron transitions from a higher energy level to a lower energy level, this results in the **emission** of a photon. Each transition corresponds to a different wavelength of light, producing a line in the spectrum.

The resulting emission spectrum contains a set of **discrete wavelengths**, represented by coloured lines on a black background.

Each emitted photon has a wavelength associated with a discrete change in energy:

$$\Delta E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

where $\Delta E$ = change in energy level (J), $h$ = Planck's constant (J s), $f$ = frequency of photon (Hz), $c$ = speed of light (m s$^{-1}$), and $\lambda$ = wavelength of the photon (m).

**Line spectra provide evidence** that electrons in atoms can only transition between **discrete energy levels**.

### Absorption Spectra

An atom can be raised to an excited state by the **absorption** of a photon. When white light passes through a **cool, low pressure gas**, light of certain wavelengths is found to be missing.

An **absorption spectrum** consists of a **continuous spectrum** containing all the colours with **dark lines** at certain wavelengths. These dark lines correspond exactly to the differences in energy levels in an atom.

When excited electrons return to lower levels, the photons are emitted **in all directions** rather than in the original direction of the white light. Therefore, some wavelengths appear to be missing from the transmitted beam.

The wavelengths missing from an absorption spectrum are the **same** as the corresponding **emission spectra** of the same element.

### Difference in Discrete Energy Levels

The difference between two energy levels equals a specific photon energy:

$$\Delta E = hf = E_2 - E_1$$

where $E_1$ = energy of the higher level (J), $E_2$ = energy of the lower level (J), $h$ = Planck's constant (J s), and $f$ = frequency of photon (Hz).

⚠ Check: the source labels $E_1$ as the higher level and $E_2$ as the lower level, which is unconventional. Standard treatment typically uses $E_2$ for the higher level. In practice, the assignment does not matter as long as you take the magnitude of the difference.

Using the wave equation, the wavelength of the emitted or absorbed radiation can be related to the energy difference:

$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E_2 - E_1}$$

The larger the energy difference $\Delta E$ between two levels, the shorter the wavelength $\lambda$, and vice versa.

**Worked example method (energy level transitions):** Given a set of energy levels, the longest wavelength photon corresponds to the smallest energy gap (adjacent levels near the top of the diagram). The shortest wavelength photon corresponds to the largest energy gap (widest separation, typically between the highest and lowest available levels). To calculate wavelength: find $\Delta E$ in eV, convert to joules by multiplying by $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$, then apply $\lambda = hc / \Delta E$. For instance, from levels $-0.54$ eV and $-3.4$ eV: $\Delta E = (3.4 - 0.54) = 2.86$ eV $= 2.86 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J, giving $\lambda = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{2.86 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 435$ nm.

## Wave-Particle Duality

### Wave-Particle Duality

Light can behave as a particle (photons) **and** as a wave. This phenomenon is called **wave-particle duality**.

Light interacts with matter (such as electrons) **as a particle**. The evidence for this is provided by the **photoelectric effect**.

Light propagates through space **as a wave**. The evidence for this comes from the **diffraction** and **interference** of light in **Young's Double-Slit Experiment**.

**Diffraction** occurs when waves pass through a gap or around an obstacle and the waves spread out. When light is passed through a slit with a width of similar size to the wavelength, the wave spreads out forming a diffraction pattern.

### Light as a Particle

Einstein proposed that light can be described as quanta of energy that behave as particles, called **photons**. The photon model of light explains that:

- Electromagnetic waves carry energy in discrete packets called photons.
- The energy of photons is quantised according to $E = hf$.
- In the photoelectric effect, each electron can absorb only a single photon, so only frequencies above the **threshold frequency** will emit a photoelectron.

| Wave theory prediction | Why it is wrong (photon model) |
|---|---|
| Any frequency of light can cause photoelectric emission if exposure time is long enough | Photoelectrons are released immediately if the frequency is above the threshold for that metal |
| The energy absorbed by each electron increases gradually with each wave | Energy is absorbed instantaneously. Photoelectrons are either emitted or not emitted after exposure to light |
| The kinetic energy of emitted electrons should increase with radiation intensity | If the intensity is increased, more photoelectrons are emitted per second (but maximum kinetic energy is unchanged) |

If the frequency of incident light is above the threshold and the intensity is increased, **more photoelectrons are emitted per second**. The wave theory explains interference and diffraction well but fails to explain the photoelectric effect.

### Electron Diffraction

Louis de Broglie discovered that **matter** can behave as a **wave**. Experiments show that a **diffraction pattern** is produced when a beam of electrons is directed at a thin graphite film. Diffraction is a property of **waves** and cannot be explained by describing electrons as particles.

To observe diffraction of electrons, they must be focused through a gap similar in size to their **de Broglie wavelength**, such as an atomic lattice. Graphite film is ideal because of its crystalline structure: the gaps between neighbouring planes of atoms in the crystals act as slits, allowing the electron waves to spread out and create a diffraction pattern.

The diffraction pattern is observed on the screen as a series of **concentric rings**, similar to the pattern produced when light passes through a diffraction grating. If electrons acted purely as particles, no pattern would be observed and particles would be distributed uniformly across the screen.

Observations:

- A **larger** accelerating voltage **reduces** the diameter of a given ring.
- A **lower** accelerating voltage **increases** the diameter of the rings.

### Investigating Electron Diffraction

Electron diffraction tubes can investigate the wave properties of electrons. Electrons are accelerated in an electron gun to a high potential (e.g. 5000 V) and directed through a thin film of graphite. The electrons diffract from the gaps between carbon atoms and produce a circular pattern on a fluorescent screen made from phosphor.

Increasing the voltage between the anode and cathode causes the energy, and hence speed, of the electrons to increase. The kinetic energy of the electrons is proportional to the voltage across the anode-cathode:

$$E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = eV$$

where $e$ is the electron charge and $V$ is the accelerating potential difference.

## The de Broglie Wavelength

The **de Broglie equation** relates **wavelength** (a wave property) to **momentum** (a matter property).

In electron diffraction: increasing the **speed** of electrons (and hence their **momentum**) causes the angle of **diffraction** to decrease (seen as a decrease in the diameter of the diffraction rings). This agrees with wave theory, where the angle of diffraction decreases as wavelength decreases. Therefore, the **greater** the **momentum** of the particle, the **smaller** the **de Broglie wavelength**.

De Broglie's equation:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$

In terms of the speed of a moving particle ($p = mv$):

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$$

Relating momentum and kinetic energy:

$$E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{p^2}{2m} \quad \Rightarrow \quad p = \sqrt{2mE}$$

Combining with the de Broglie equation:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mE}}$$

where $\lambda$ = de Broglie wavelength (m), $h$ = Planck's constant (J s), $p$ = momentum (kg m s$^{-1}$), $E$ = kinetic energy (J), $m$ = mass (kg), $v$ = speed (m s$^{-1}$).

**Worked example method (comparing de Broglie wavelengths):** When a proton and electron are accelerated through the same potential difference, they gain the same kinetic energy $E$. Since $\lambda = h / \sqrt{2mE}$ and $h$ and $E$ are identical, $\lambda \propto 1/\sqrt{m}$. The ratio $\lambda_p / \lambda_e = \sqrt{m_e / m_p} = \sqrt{9.11 \times 10^{-31} / 1.67 \times 10^{-27}} = 0.0234$. The proton's de Broglie wavelength is about 43 times smaller than the electron's because the proton has much greater mass and therefore greater momentum at the same kinetic energy.

## Diffraction Effects of Momentum

When electrons pass through a slit similar in size to their de Broglie wavelength, they exhibit diffraction, a property of waves. The regular spacing of atoms in a **crystalline solid** acts as a **diffraction grating**, scattering the electrons in a predictable manner. The observed diffraction pattern can be used to deduce the structure of the crystal.

**High energy electrons** have a **shorter wavelength** and can therefore be used to investigate the **size of the nucleus** of an atom (as opposed to the arrangement of atoms in a crystal).

### Momentum of Electrons

From $\lambda = h / (mv)$:

- A **smaller** momentum results in a **longer** wavelength.
- A **larger** momentum results in a **shorter** wavelength.

### Kinetic Energy of Electrons

The speed of an electron can be increased by increasing the accelerating voltage (potential difference). If the electron speed and therefore kinetic energy is increased:

- The wavelength of the wave will **decrease**.
- The diffraction rings will appear **closer** together.

The higher the kinetic energy, the higher the momentum, hence the **shorter** the de Broglie wavelength.

### Radius of the Diffraction Pattern

The radius of the diffraction pattern depends on the wavelength:

- The **longer** the wavelength, the more the wave spreads out, producing a **larger radius**.
- The **shorter** the wavelength, the **smaller the radius** produced.

Electrons with smaller momentum produce a more diffuse diffraction pattern (larger rings).

**Worked example method (diffraction angle from accelerating voltage):** Given a potential difference $V$ and atomic spacing $d$, find the de Broglie wavelength by: (1) calculate $E_k = eV$, (2) find speed from $v = \sqrt{2E_k/m}$, (3) find $\lambda = h/(mv)$, (4) apply the diffraction equation $d \sin\theta = n\lambda$. For the first minimum, $n = 1/2$, giving $\sin\theta = \lambda / (2d)$. For example, with $V = 4$ kV and $d = 1.4 \times 10^{-10}$ m: $E_k = 6.4 \times 10^{-16}$ J, $v = 3.748 \times 10^7$ m s$^{-1}$, $\lambda = 1.942 \times 10^{-11}$ m, $\sin\theta = 0.0694$, $\theta = 4.0°$.

## Analysis of the Nature of Matter

### The Evolving Field of Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is a relatively new field compared to classical mechanics (Newton's laws, wave theory). Around 1900, discoveries such as the electron and the gamma photon began to conflict with existing models of matter. New theories emerged from **Max Planck**, **Niels Bohr**, and **Albert Einstein**, who are seen as the pioneers of **Quantum Theory**.

The understanding of quantum mechanics has changed over time:

- Scientists use existing models (e.g. wave theory) to make predictions.
- Sometimes experimental results do not fit the existing model (e.g. **the photoelectric effect** could not be explained by wave theory).
- Scientists then change the model to explain the new evidence (e.g. **wave-particle duality**).

### Development of Scientific Theories

When a new theory is suggested (such as de Broglie's idea of particles acting as waves), it must be **evaluated**. Other scientists evaluate the theory in a process called **peer review**. When enough experimental evidence supports the theory, it is **validated**. De Broglie's theory is currently accepted as correct until any conflicting evidence is found.

### Peer Review

Scientists across the world form a **scientific community**. Scientific claims from new research published in journals must be peer-reviewed by other experts in that field.

During peer review, scientists check:

- **Validity**: does the research achieve what it claims? Is the method appropriate and have errors been addressed?
- **Originality**: are the results new, or has similar research already been carried out? If so, has prior work been credited?
- **Significance**: are the findings important or ground-breaking?

### Evaluating Scientific Claims

Claims from research that is not peer-reviewed should always be questioned. Problems may include the method used, the accuracy of results, or the conclusions drawn. Research should also be checked to confirm it is:

- **Repeatable** by the same scientists who carried it out.
- **Reproducible** by other teams of scientists.
