# Longitudinal & Transverse Waves

## Progressive Waves

A **progressive wave** is a wave that transfers energy from one point to another without transferring the medium itself.

### Properties of a Progressive Wave

**Displacement** ($x$) is the distance of a point on the wave from its **equilibrium position**. It is a vector quantity and can be positive or negative. Measured in metres (m).

**Amplitude** ($A$) is the maximum displacement of a particle in the wave from its equilibrium position. Measured in metres (m).

**Wavelength** ($\lambda$) is the distance between points on successive oscillations of the wave that are in phase (e.g. crest to crest, trough to trough). Measured in metres (m).

**Period** ($T$), or time period, is the time taken for one complete oscillation or cycle of the wave. Measured in seconds (s). On a displacement-time graph, the period is the horizontal distance between two successive equivalent points (e.g. two adjacent peaks).

**Frequency** ($f$) is the number of complete oscillations per unit time. Measured in **hertz** (Hz) or $\text{s}^{-1}$. Related to period by:

$$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

**Speed** ($v$) is the distance travelled by the wave per unit time. Measured in $\text{m s}^{-1}$.

### The Wave Equation

The **wave equation** links speed, frequency, and wavelength and applies to both transverse and longitudinal waves:

$$v = f\lambda$$

For a wave of constant speed, wavelength and frequency are inversely related: increasing the wavelength decreases the frequency, and vice versa.

**Worked example method (finding wavelength from a displacement-time graph):** Given $v = 340 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ and $T = 0.28 \text{ ms}$, first calculate $f = 1/T = 1/(0.28 \times 10^{-3}) = 3571.43 \text{ Hz}$, then rearrange the wave equation: $\lambda = v/f = 340/3571.43 = 0.095 \text{ m}$ (2 s.f.).

**Exam note:** The wave equation is sometimes written $c = f\lambda$, but $c$ conventionally denotes the speed of light ($3 \times 10^8 \text{ m s}^{-1}$). Only electromagnetic waves travel at this speed, so use $v$ for any wave speed that is not the speed of light.

### Phase Difference

The **phase difference** between two waves (or two points on the same wave) is a measure of how much one point or wave is in front of or behind another. It is found from the relative position of the crests or troughs of two waves of the same frequency.

- **In phase**: crests (or troughs) are aligned. Phase difference = $0$ (equivalently $360°$ or $2\pi$ rad).
- **In antiphase**: the crest of one wave aligns with the trough of another. Phase difference = $180°$ or $\pi$ rad.

If one wave reaches a given point in its cycle earlier than another, it **leads**; the other wave **lags** by the same amount.

Phase difference is measured in **fractions of a wavelength**, **degrees**, or **radians**. The conversion is:

$$\text{Phase difference (degrees)} = \text{fraction of } \lambda \times 360°$$

$$\text{Phase difference (radians)} = \text{fraction of } \lambda \times 2\pi$$

For example, a $\frac{1}{4}\lambda$ shift corresponds to $\frac{1}{4} \times 360° = 90°$ or $\frac{1}{4} \times 2\pi = \frac{\pi}{2}$ rad.

**Worked example method (phase difference from a displacement-distance diagram):** For plane waves on water with a measured peak-to-peak distance of 7.50 mm and a span of 25 cm covering $3\frac{3}{4}$ wavelengths: the amplitude is half the peak-to-peak value, $7.50/2 = 3.75 \text{ mm}$; the wavelength is $25 \text{ cm} \div 3.75 = 6.67 \text{ cm}$; and two points separated by $\frac{1}{2}\lambda$ have a phase difference of $\frac{1}{2} \times 360° = 180°$ (antiphase).

**Exam note:** When labelling wavelength or period on a diagram, arrows must span from the very top of one crest to the very top of the next (or equivalent points). Too-short arrows lose marks. Similarly, amplitude arrows must go from the equilibrium line to the peak, not from trough to peak.

## Longitudinal & Transverse Waves

In **mechanical waves**, particles oscillate about fixed points. Waves are classified into two types, distinguished by the direction of vibration relative to the direction of travel.

### Transverse Waves

A **transverse wave** is a wave in which the particles oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave travel (and energy transfer).

Transverse waves display **crests** (peaks) and **troughs**. They can be demonstrated using a rope, where the rope vibrates up and down while the wave pulse travels horizontally along it. Transverse waves **can** be polarised.

Examples of transverse waves: electromagnetic waves (e.g. radio, visible light, UV), vibrations on a guitar string, waves on a string, seismic S-waves.

### Longitudinal Waves

A **longitudinal wave** is a wave in which the particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave travel (and energy transfer).

Longitudinal waves display **compressions** (regions of increased pressure, where particles are close together) and **rarefactions** (regions of decreased pressure, where particles are spread apart). They can be demonstrated using a slinky spring, where the coils vibrate back and forth along the same axis that the wave travels. Longitudinal waves **cannot** be polarised.

Energy is transmitted through the medium as follows: particles vibrate when given energy, compressions cause nearby particles to vibrate with more energy, producing a new compression further along the medium. However, the particles themselves do not travel with the wave; they return to their equilibrium positions.

A **wavelength** on a longitudinal wave is the distance between two successive compressions (or two successive rarefactions).

Examples of longitudinal waves: sound waves, ultrasound waves, waves through a slinky coil, seismic P-waves.

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Transverse | Longitudinal |
|---|---|---|
| Direction of oscillation | Perpendicular to wave travel | Parallel to wave travel |
| Visible features | Crests and troughs | Compressions and rarefactions |
| Can be polarised? | Yes | No |
| Demonstration | Rope | Slinky spring |
| Examples | EM waves, S-waves, guitar strings | Sound, ultrasound, P-waves |

**Exam note:** The definitions of transverse and longitudinal waves are frequently asked in exam questions. Ensure you state the direction of oscillation relative to the direction of wave travel (and energy transfer).

## Polarisation

**Polarisation** is when particle oscillations occur in a single plane perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.

Polarisation can only occur in **transverse waves**, because transverse waves oscillate in any plane perpendicular to the propagation direction. When a transverse wave is **polarised**, its vibrations are restricted to one direction, but these vibrations remain perpendicular to the direction of propagation and energy transfer. Longitudinal waves cannot be polarised because they oscillate parallel to the direction of travel.

An unpolarised transverse wave has oscillations in all planes perpendicular to the propagation direction. A vertically polarised wave oscillates only in the vertical plane, and a horizontally polarised wave oscillates only in the horizontal plane.

### Polarisers and Polarising Filters

Waves can be polarised by passing through a **polariser** (or **polarising filter**), which only allows oscillations in a certain plane (its **transmission axis**) to be transmitted. When an unpolarised wave passes through a polariser, it emerges polarised in the plane of the transmission axis. If a wave that is already polarised passes through a filter whose transmission axis is perpendicular to the wave's plane of oscillation, none of the wave is transmitted.

Light can also be polarised through **reflection**, **refraction**, and **scattering**.

### Investigating Light Intensity with Two Polarisers

Place an unpolarised light source in front of two identical polarising filters, A and B:

**Transmission axes parallel:** Filter A polarises the light in a certain axis. All of the polarised light passes through filter B unaffected, so the **maximum** intensity of light is transmitted.

**Rotating filter B:** As filter B is rotated, the intensity of the transmitted light changes periodically depending on the angle B is rotated through.

**Transmission axes perpendicular (90° apart):** Filter A polarises the light, but none of the polarised light passes through filter B because its transmission axis is perpendicular. The **minimum** intensity (zero) is transmitted.

The graph of transmitted light intensity against angle as the second polariser is rotated through 360° shows: maxima at 0°, 180°, and 360° (when the transmission axes are parallel), and zero intensity at 90° and 270° (when the transmission axes are perpendicular). The curve follows a $\cos^2\theta$ shape. *(Beyond source: the $\cos^2\theta$ dependence is Malus's Law.)*

In practice, the intensity of an unpolarised electromagnetic wave **reduces** after passing through a polarising filter. *(Beyond source: a single ideal polariser transmits exactly half the intensity of incident unpolarised light.)*

**Exam note:** You may be expected to describe the intensity variation or sketch the intensity-angle graph for light with two polarisers, but you will not be required to perform Malus's Law calculations.

**Worked example method (identifying polarisation scenarios):** Polarisation requires a transverse wave and a mechanism that restricts oscillations to one plane. Radio waves (transverse) passing through a metal grid can be polarised, so this is correct. Sound and ultrasound are longitudinal and cannot be polarised. Diffraction does not cause polarisation; polarisation occurs through reflection, refraction, or scattering.

### Applications of Polarisers

**Polaroid sunglasses** contain lenses with polarising filters whose transmission axes are **vertically oriented**, blocking horizontally polarised light. When light reflects from a horizontal reflective surface (e.g. water, a wet road), it undergoes **partial plane polarisation**, with a proportion of the reflected light oscillating more in the horizontal plane than the vertical. The vertically oriented polarising filter eliminates this partially polarised glare, allowing objects beneath the water surface to be viewed more clearly.

**Polaroid photography** works on the same principle. Polarising filters reduce glare and intensify colour on bright days. They also enable photographs of objects underwater: the reflected light from the water surface is partially horizontally polarised and is eliminated by the polarising lens, while the light from the underwater object is **refracted** (not reflected) by the surface, so it is **not** plane-polarised and passes through the filter. The underwater object therefore appears more intense than the glare in the photograph.

**Polarisation of radio and microwave signals:** Radio and television services are broadcast either horizontally polarised or vertically polarised. The reception aerial must be mounted accordingly: flat (horizontal) or on its side (vertical), depending on the transmitter it points towards and the polarity of the services being broadcast. An incorrectly oriented aerial will not pick up the signal.
