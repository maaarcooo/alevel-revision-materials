# Interference

## Path Difference & Coherence

### Superposition and Interference

When two or more waves arrive at the same point and overlap, their displacements combine. This process is called **superposition**.

The **principle of superposition** states that when two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point is equal to the sum of the displacements of the individual waves.

The superposition of **surface water waves** illustrates this: where waves overlap there are regions of zero displacement (flat water) and regions of increased displacement (higher peaks). On a vertical displacement-displacement graph, superposition can be analysed by adding the individual wave amplitudes point by point, often producing a larger resultant amplitude.

**Interference** is the effect of superposition. It is most clearly observed when waves overlap either in **phase** or in **antiphase**:

- When two waves are in **antiphase**, their combined effect causes them to cancel each other out. This is **destructive interference**, and the resultant amplitude is zero (for equal-amplitude waves).
- When two waves are in **phase**, their combined effect produces a larger resultant amplitude (double the individual amplitude for equal-amplitude waves). This is **constructive interference**.

At points where two waves are neither fully in phase nor fully in antiphase, the resultant amplitude lies somewhere between the two extremes. Individual wave displacements may be positive or negative and are combined in the same way as other **vector** quantities.

### Coherence

For waves to produce a stable pattern of constructive and destructive interference, they must be **coherent**. Coherence requires two conditions:

- The waves have the **same frequency**.
- The waves maintain a **constant phase difference**.

If a wave undergoes an abrupt change in phase, the phase difference between the two waves is no longer constant, so they are **not coherent** and cannot produce a sustained interference pattern.

Examples of coherent light sources that produce observable interference include **monochromatic laser** light and sound waves from two nearby **speakers** emitting sound of the same frequency. Non-coherent sources (such as filament bulbs or sodium lamps) produce **white light** containing a mixture of wavelengths and random phase changes.

### Path Difference

**Path difference** is defined as the difference in distance travelled by the two waves from their sources to the point where they meet.

Path difference versus phase difference:

- **Path difference** compares the amount of progress made by waves along a path (measured in metres or wavelengths).
- **Phase difference** compares the relative position between the phases (peaks and troughs) of coherent waves that are normally travelling parallel to each other at a given point (measured in degrees or radians).

The path difference between two coherent waves determines whether constructive or destructive interference occurs where they meet. Path difference is generally expressed in multiples of wavelength. When counting wavelengths, one full wavelength corresponds to the wave going through a complete cycle (up, down, and back to the starting position).

**Example from source diagram:** Two sources $S_1$ and $S_2$ emit waves towards points $P_1$ and $P_2$. The number of wavelengths from each source to each point:

| Path | Distance |
|---|---|
| $S_1 \to P_1$ | $6\lambda$ |
| $S_2 \to P_1$ | $6.5\lambda$ |
| $S_1 \to P_2$ | $7\lambda$ |
| $S_2 \to P_2$ | $6\lambda$ |

At $P_1$: path difference $= 6.5\lambda - 6\lambda = \frac{\lambda}{2}$ (half wavelength, so **destructive** interference).

At $P_2$: path difference $= 7\lambda - 6\lambda = \lambda$ (whole wavelength, so **constructive** interference).

### Conditions for Constructive and Destructive Interference

For waves emitted by two coherent sources very close together:

**Constructive interference** occurs when:

$$\text{path difference} = n\lambda$$

**Destructive interference** occurs when:

$$\text{path difference} = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda$$

Where $\lambda$ is the wavelength in metres (m) and $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ (any non-negative integer).

### Wavefront Diagrams

Wave behaviour can also be represented using a **wavefront diagram**, where each curved line represents a wavefront (a peak or a trough).

On a wavefront diagram:

- Where a **peak meets a peak** (blue dot in source), **constructive interference** occurs.
- Where a **trough meets a trough** (yellow dot), **constructive interference** also occurs.
- Constructive interference occurs along **lines of maximum displacement**.
- Where a **peak meets a trough** (green dot), **destructive interference** occurs.

By counting the number of wavelengths from each source to a given point on the wavefront diagram, you can determine whether constructive or destructive interference occurs there.

**Example from wavefront diagram:** At point $P$, the number of crests from source $S_1 = 4\lambda$ and from source $S_2 = 6\lambda$. Path difference $= 6\lambda - 4\lambda = 2\lambda$, which is a whole number of wavelengths, so constructive interference occurs at $P$.

**Worked example (interference at points X, Y, Z):** For coherent waves from two-point sources starting from equilibrium:

- Point X: both peaks overlap. Path difference $= 5.5\lambda - 4.5\lambda = \lambda$ → **constructive**.
- Point Y: both troughs overlap. Path difference $= 3.75\lambda - 3.75\lambda = 0$ → **constructive**.
- Point Z: peak of one wave meets trough of the other. Path difference $= 4\lambda - 3.5\lambda = \frac{\lambda}{2}$ → **destructive**.

Answer: **B** (Constructive, Constructive, Destructive).

**Exam tip:** A path difference is the difference between the **length** of the two paths, not the distance between the two paths. Think of constructive interference as "building" the wave and destructive interference as "destroying" the wave.

## Demonstrating Interference

### Interference & Diffraction of a Laser

Lasers are ideal for analysing diffraction and intensity patterns because they produce light that is:

- **Coherent**: constant phase difference and same frequency
- **Monochromatic**: same wavelength

The diffraction pattern produced by a laser on a screen consists of areas of **constructive interference** (the **bright** strips or **fringes**) and areas of **destructive interference** (the **dark fringes**). As the number of slits in a grating increases (from 1 to 2 to 3, etc.), the bright maxima become narrower and more sharply defined, with wider dark regions between them.

### Safety Issues with Lasers

Lasers produce a very **high-energy beam of light** that can cause **permanent eye damage** or blindness. Precautions when using lasers:

- **Never** look directly at a laser or its reflection
- Do not shine the laser towards a person
- Do not allow the beam to **reflect** from shiny surfaces into anyone's eyes
- Wear laser safety **goggles**
- Place a **"laser on"** warning light outside the room
- Stand **behind** the laser

### Sound & EM Wave Interference

**Using Sound Waves:** Two-source interference can be demonstrated with two speakers emitting a **coherent sound** (same frequency, constant phase difference). Sound waves are longitudinal, made up of **compressions** and **rarefactions**:

- **Constructive interference** occurs when compressions and rarefactions from each wave **line up**, and the sound appears **louder**.
- **Destructive interference** occurs when a compression from one wave lines up with a rarefaction from the other and vice versa. The waves cancel, and **zero sound** is heard.

This principle is the technology behind **noise-cancelling headphones**.

**Using Microwaves:** Two-source interference for microwaves (and other EM waves) can be detected with a moveable **microwave detector**. The detector picks up **maximum amplitude** or intensity in regions of constructive interference and **minimum or zero amplitude** (no signal) in regions of destructive interference.

### Intensity Variation with Amplitude

The **intensity** of a wave (its power per unit area) is proportional to the **energy transferred** by the wave. The energy transferred by a wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude. Therefore:

$$I \propto A^2$$

Where $I$ = intensity of the wave in W m$^{-2}$ and $A$ = amplitude of the wave in metres (m).

**Worked example method (speaker interference):** Two in-phase speakers play a 280 Hz note. A student walks 3.0 m from speaker A towards speaker B.

1. Calculate wavelength: $\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{340}{280} = 1.2$ m
2. Apply destructive interference condition: path difference $= \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda$
3. For $n = 0$: path difference $= \frac{\lambda}{2} = 0.6$ m (first quiet spot)
4. For $n = 1$: path difference $= \frac{3\lambda}{2} = 1.8$ m (second quiet spot)
5. For $n = 2$: path difference $= \frac{5\lambda}{2} = 3.0$ m (third quiet spot)

The student hears **3 quiet spots** in 3.0 m.

## Young's Double-Slit Experiment

### Double Slit Interference

**Young's double-slit experiment** produces both a **diffraction** and an **interference pattern** using either two coherent wave sources or a single wave source passing through a **double slit**.

In the typical setup:

- A laser light source is placed behind a **single slit**, which diffracts the light so it illuminates both slits of the double slit.
- The light from the double slits is then diffracted, producing a **diffraction pattern** of bright and dark fringes on a screen placed far away.
- **Constructive interference** between light rays forms **bright** strips (also called **fringes**, interference fringes, or **maxima**) on the screen.
- **Destructive interference** forms **dark** strips (also called dark fringes or **minima**).

Each bright fringe is **identical** in width and intensity. The fringes are all separated by dark **narrow** bands of destructive interference.

### Double Slit Interference Pattern

The Young's double-slit interference pattern shows regions of constructive and destructive interference:

- Each **bright fringe** is a peak of equal **maximum intensity**.
- Each **dark fringe** is a trough or minimum of **zero intensity**.
- **Maxima** are formed by constructive interference of light.
- **Minima** are formed by destructive interference of light.

The wave from slit $S_2$ has to travel slightly further than that from $S_1$ to reach the same point on the screen. This extra distance is the **path difference**.

For maxima (constructive): path difference $= n\lambda$

For minima (destructive): path difference $= \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda$

Where $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ is the **order** of the maximum or minimum, representing its position relative to the central maximum. $n = 0$ is the **central maximum**, $n = 1$ is the first maximum on either side, $n = 2$ the next, and so on.

**Worked example method (maxima order determination):** Two coherent sources $S_1$ and $S_2$ are 65 cm apart, with a microphone M at 150 cm from $S_1$ along the normal. The path difference at M is found using Pythagoras: $\sqrt{65^2 + 150^2} - 150 = 163 - 150 = 13$ cm. For constructive interference, $13 = n\lambda$, giving $\lambda = \frac{13}{n}$. Within the wavelength range 3.5 cm to 12.5 cm, only $n = 2$ ($\lambda = 6.5$ cm) and $n = 3$ ($\lambda = 4.3$ cm) fall within range.

### Fringe Spacing Equation

The spacing between bright (or dark) fringes is calculated using the **double slit equation**:

$$w = \frac{\lambda D}{s}$$

Where:

- $w$ = **fringe width** (distance between successive bright fringes) in m
- $\lambda$ = wavelength of the source in m
- $D$ = distance between the double slit and the screen in m (typically several metres)
- $s$ = distance between the centres of the two slits in m (typically in mm)

The fringe separation $w$ **increases** if:

- The wavelength $\lambda$ of the incident light **increases**
- The distance $D$ between screen and slits **increases**
- The slit separation $s$ **decreases**

**Worked example method (finding slit separation):** A laser of frequency 750 THz produces fringes with a separation of 15 mm between maxima P and Q (spanning 9 bright fringes) on a screen 4.5 m away. First find $\lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{750 \times 10^{12}} = 4 \times 10^{-7}$ m $= 400$ nm. The individual fringe width is $w = \frac{15 \times 10^{-3}}{9}$. Then $s = \frac{\lambda D}{w} = \frac{4 \times 10^{-7} \times 4.5}{15 \times 10^{-3} \div 9} = 1.08 \times 10^{-3}$ m $= 1.1$ mm (2 s.f.).

**Exam tip:** Since $w$, $s$, and $D$ are all distances, label the double-slit diagram clearly to avoid substituting the wrong quantity.

**Exam tip:** In an ideal interference pattern, bright fringes are equally spaced and of equal intensity. In reality, each slit also acts as a **single slit** that diffracts light, so the observed fringe intensities are modulated by the single-slit diffraction envelope. The central maximum is much brighter than the outer fringes.

### Interference Patterns with White Light

When **white light** is diffracted through a double slit, the resulting pattern differs from that produced by monochromatic light:

- Each maximum is of roughly **equal width**.
- The **central maximum is white** because every wavelength interferes constructively there.
- Two dark narrow destructive interference fringes appear on either side of the central maximum.
- All other maxima are composed of a **spectrum**.
- **Separate** diffraction patterns can be observed for each wavelength of light.
- The shortest wavelength (**violet/blue**) appears **nearest** to the central maximum because it is diffracted the **least**.
- The longest wavelength (**red**) appears **furthest** from the central maximum because it is diffracted the **most**.
- Further from the central maximum, the colours become **blurry** and the fringe spacing becomes so small that spectra eventually merge.
- As maxima move **further away** from the central maximum, the wavelengths of **blue** observed **decrease** and the wavelengths of **red** observed **increase**.

## Developing Theories of EM Radiation

The understanding of the nature of light evolved over several centuries:

| Scientist | Date | Theory / Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Isaac Newton** | 1672 | Proposed visible light is a stream of microscopic particles called **corpuscles**. Could not explain interference or diffraction, so the wave view was adopted instead. |
| **Christiaan Huygens** | 1678 | Proposed the **Wave Theory of Light** to explain diffraction and refraction. Light is described as a series of wavefronts where every point is a source of waves (**Huygens' wavelets**) that spread out and travel at the same speed as the source wave. |
| **Thomas Young** | 1801 | Devised the **double-slit experiment**, providing experimental proof that light is a wave capable of constructive and destructive interference. |
| **James Clerk Maxwell** | 1862 | Showed electric and magnetic fields obey the wave equation, meaning light consists of perpendicular oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Later, Maxwell and Hertz discovered the full **electromagnetic spectrum**. |
| **Albert Einstein** | 1905 | Discovered light behaves as a particle through the **photoelectric effect**. Described light as packets of energy called **photons**. The scientific community later understood that light exhibits both wave and particle behaviour, known as **wave-particle duality**. |

## Required Practical: Young's Slit Experiment & Diffraction Gratings

### Equipment List

| Apparatus | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Laser | Source of monochromatic light |
| Single slit | Focus the laser beam onto the double slit (optional) |
| Double slit | Diffract the beam into two sources of coherent light |
| Diffraction grating | Diffract the beam into multiple sources of coherent light |
| Metre ruler | Measure the distance between slits and screen ($D$) |
| Vernier callipers | Measure fringe width ($w$) and slit separation (if not quoted on double slit) |
| Retort stand | Support the laser and slits at the same height |
| White screen | Project the interference pattern onto |
| Set square | Ensure all components are aligned to the normal |

Resolution: metre ruler = $\pm 1$ mm, Vernier callipers = $\pm 0.01$ mm.

### Young's Double-Slit Experiment

**Aim:** Investigate the relationship between the slit-screen distance $D$ and the fringe width $w$.

**Variables:**

- Independent: distance between slits and screen, $D$
- Dependent: fringe width, $w$
- Control: wavelength of laser light $\lambda$, slit separation $s$

**Method:**

1. Fix the laser and slits to a retort stand, place the screen so $D = 0.5$ m (measured with metre ruler).
2. Darken the room and turn on the laser.
3. Measure from the central fringe across many fringes using Vernier callipers and divide by the number of fringe widths to find $w$.
4. Increase $D$ by 0.1 m and repeat, increasing each time up to around 1.5 m.
5. Repeat the experiment twice more and calculate the mean fringe width $w$ for each $D$.

**Analysing the results:** The fringe spacing equation $w = \frac{\lambda D}{s}$ can be compared to $y = mx$, where $y = w$, $x = D$, and the gradient $= \frac{\lambda}{s}$. Plot $w$ against $D$ and draw a line of best fit. The wavelength of the laser light is:

$$\lambda = s \times \text{gradient}$$

### Interference by a Diffraction Grating

**Aim:** Calculate the wavelength of the laser light using a diffraction grating.

**Variables:**

- Independent: distance between maxima, $h$
- Dependent: the angle between the normal and each order, $\theta_n$ (where $n = 1, 2, 3$, etc.)
- Control: distance between slits and screen $D$, laser wavelength $\lambda$, slit separation $d$

**Method:**

1. Place the laser on a retort stand and the diffraction grating in front of it.
2. Use a set square to ensure the beam passes through the grating at normal incidence and meets the screen perpendicularly.
3. Set the distance $D$ between grating and screen to 1.0 m using a metre ruler.
4. Darken the room and turn on the laser.
5. Identify the zero-order maximum (the central beam).
6. Measure the distance $h$ to the two nearest first-order maxima (i.e. $n = 1$, $n = 2$) using Vernier callipers.
7. Calculate the mean of these two values.
8. Measure distance $h$ for increasing orders.
9. Repeat with a diffraction grating that has a different number of slits per mm.

**Analysing the results:** The diffraction grating equation is:

$$n\lambda = d \sin \theta$$

Where $n$ = order of the diffraction pattern, $\lambda$ = wavelength (m), $d$ = distance between slits (m), and $\theta$ = angle between the normal and the maxima.

The slit spacing is found from:

$$d = \frac{1}{N}$$

Where $N$ = the number of slits per metre (m$^{-1}$).

Since the angle is not small, it must be calculated using trigonometry:

$$\tan \theta = \frac{h}{D} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{h}{D}\right)$$

Calculate a mean $\theta$ value for each order, then use $\lambda = \frac{d \sin \theta}{n}$ to find the wavelength. Compare the mean wavelength with the accepted value (typically **635 nm** for a standard school red laser).

**Worked example method (diffraction grating):** A student uses 300 slits/mm and 100 slits/mm gratings with $D = 3.75$ m (375 cm). Using cumulative $h$ values and $\theta = \tan^{-1}(h/D)$:

- 300 slits/mm, $n = 1$: $\theta_1 = \tan^{-1}(71.6/375) = 10.81°$, $\lambda = \frac{d \sin 10.81°}{1} = 625$ nm
- 300 slits/mm, $n = 2$: $\theta_2 = \tan^{-1}(151.4/375) = 21.99°$, $\lambda = \frac{d \sin 21.99°}{2} = 624$ nm
- 100 slits/mm, $n = 1$: $\theta_1 = \tan^{-1}(23.9/375) = 3.647°$, $\lambda = \frac{d \sin 3.647°}{1} = 636$ nm
- 100 slits/mm, $n = 2$: $\theta_2 = \tan^{-1}(48.4/375) = 7.354°$, $\lambda = \frac{d \sin 7.354°}{2} = 640$ nm

Mean $\lambda = \frac{625 + 624 + 636 + 640}{4} = 631$ nm. Percentage uncertainty $= \frac{635 - 631}{635} \times 100\% = 0.6\%$.

### Evaluating the Experiments

**Systematic errors:**

- Use a set square to avoid parallax error when measuring fringe width.
- A grating with more lines per mm produces greater values of $h$, lowering the percentage uncertainty.
- Measure each bright fringe distance from the centre of the bright spot.

**Random errors:**

- Fringe spacing can be subjective depending on intensity, so take multiple measurements of $w$ and $h$ (between 3 and 8) and find the average.
- Use a Vernier scale to record $w$ and $h$ to reduce percentage uncertainty.
- Reduce uncertainty in $w$ and $h$ by measuring across all visible fringes and dividing by the number of fringes.
- Increase the grating-to-screen distance $D$ to increase fringe separation (though this may decrease light intensity on the screen).
- Conduct the experiment in a darkened room so fringes are clear.

### Safety Considerations

- Lasers should be **Class 2** with a maximum output of no more than **1 mW**.
- Do not allow laser beams to shine into anyone's eyes.
- Remove reflective surfaces from the room to prevent reflected laser light reaching anyone's eyes.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Variables |
|---|---|---|
| Constructive interference condition | $\text{path difference} = n\lambda$ | $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ |
| Destructive interference condition | $\text{path difference} = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda$ | $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ |
| Double slit fringe spacing | $w = \dfrac{\lambda D}{s}$ | $w$ = fringe width (m), $\lambda$ = wavelength (m), $D$ = slit-to-screen distance (m), $s$ = slit separation (m) |
| Intensity-amplitude relation | $I \propto A^2$ | $I$ = intensity (W m$^{-2}$), $A$ = amplitude (m) |
| Diffraction grating equation | $n\lambda = d \sin \theta$ | $n$ = order, $d$ = slit spacing (m), $\theta$ = angle to normal |
| Slit spacing from lines per metre | $d = \dfrac{1}{N}$ | $N$ = number of slits per metre (m$^{-1}$) |
| Angle from grating geometry | $\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{h}{D}\right)$ | $h$ = distance from central to $n$th maximum (m), $D$ = grating-to-screen distance (m) |
| Wave equation | $v = f\lambda$ | $v$ = wave speed (m s$^{-1}$), $f$ = frequency (Hz), $\lambda$ = wavelength (m) |
