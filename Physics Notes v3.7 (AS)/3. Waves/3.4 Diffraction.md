# Diffraction

## Single Slit Diffraction

### Diffraction

**Diffraction** is the spreading out of waves after they pass through a narrow gap or around an obstruction. Diffraction effects are most noticeable when the **wavelength** of the wave and the **width of the gap** are similar in size. For light, this effect becomes significant with very narrow slits, such as those used in single or double slit experiments.

When waves pass through a narrow gap, the wavefronts curve as they spread out on the far side of the aperture. The wavefronts emerge as circular arcs from the slit rather than continuing as plane waves.

As the **gap size increases** compared to the wavelength, the waves **spread out less** after passing through the gap. Conversely, when the gap size is comparable to or smaller than the wavelength, spreading is most pronounced.

### Single Slit Diffraction Pattern

The **diffraction pattern** of light passing through a single slit is a series of light and dark fringes on a screen. The **bright fringes** are areas of **maximum intensity**, produced by the **constructive interference** of each part of the wavefront as it passes through the slit. The **dark fringes** are areas of zero or minimum **intensity**, produced by the **destructive interference** of each part of the wavefront as it passes through the slit.

The single slit pattern differs from a double slit pattern in several key ways. The **central maximum** is much **wider and brighter** than all other bright fringes, and much wider than the central maximum of a double-slit diffraction pattern. On either side of the central maximum are much **narrower and less bright** maxima. These subsidiary maxima get **dimmer** as the **order increases**.

### Single Slit Intensity Pattern

The features of the **single slit intensity** pattern are:

- The **central bright fringe** has the **greatest intensity** of any fringe and is called the **central maximum**.
- The **dark fringes** are regions with **zero intensity**.
- Moving away from the central maximum on either side, the **intensity** of each bright fringe **gets less**.

The intensity curve is a smooth envelope with a tall, broad central peak and symmetrically decreasing subsidiary peaks separated by points of zero intensity.

### Single Slit Diffraction of White Light

A source of white light diffracted through a single slit produces a **diffraction pattern** different from that of a double slit or diffraction grating.

The **central maximum** is bright white because constructive interference from all colours occurs at this position. It is much **wider and brighter** than the other bright fringes, and much wider than the central maximum of a double-slit pattern.

All other maxima are composed of a **spectrum**. **Separate** diffraction patterns can be observed for each wavelength of light within the white light:

- The shortest wavelength (violet/blue) appears **nearest** to the central maximum because it is diffracted less.
- The longest wavelength (red) appears **furthest** from the central maximum because it is diffracted more.

The colours look **blurry**, and further away from the central maximum the fringe spacing gets so small that the spectra eventually merge without any space between them. As the maxima move **further away** from the central maximum, the wavelengths of **blue** observed **decrease** and the wavelengths of **red** observed **increase**.

The white light intensity pattern compared to the monochromatic pattern:

- The central maximum is **equal** in **intensity** to that of monochromatic light.
- The **non-central** maxima are **wider** and **less intense**.
- The **fringe spacing** between maxima **gets smaller**.
- The proportion of **red wavelengths** in the pattern **increases** with increasing order $n = 1, 2, 3 \ldots$
- The proportion of **blue wavelengths decreases** with increasing order.

### Single Slit Diffraction and Young's Fringes

In Young's double slit experiment, light passing through the two slits interferes, but each slit also diffracts light like a single slit. The resulting observed pattern is a combination of both effects:

- The **double-slit** interference pattern alone has **equally** spaced intensity peaks with maxima of **equal** intensity.
- The **single-slit** intensity pattern has a distinctive **central** maximum and **subsequent** maxima at lower intensity.
- The **combined** intensity pattern has equally spaced bright fringes modulated within a single slit **"envelope"**. The double-slit fringes nearest the centre of the pattern are brightest, and those further out are progressively dimmer because they sit under the falling single-slit envelope.

### Changes in Wavelength

When the **wavelength** passing through the gap is increased, the wave **diffracts more**. This increases the **angle of diffraction** of the waves as they pass through the slit, so the **width** of the **bright maxima** is also **increased**.

- **Red** light, with the **longest wavelength** of visible light, produces a diffraction pattern with **wide fringes**.
- **Blue** light, with a much **shorter wavelength**, produces a diffraction pattern with **narrow fringes**.

If a blue laser is replaced with a red laser, there is **more diffraction** through the single slit, so the fringes in the intensity pattern are **wider**.

### Changes in Slit Width

If the slit is made **narrower**:

- The **angle** of diffraction is **greater**, so the waves spread out more beyond the slit.
- The **intensity** of the maxima **decreases**.
- The **width** of the central maximum **increases**.
- The **spacing** between fringes is **wider**.

Conversely, when the slits are made **wider**, the fringes become **narrower** (and vice versa).

### Worked Example: Matching Wavelength to Aperture

To determine which scenario best demonstrates diffraction, compare the wavelength of each wave type to the size of the gap or obstruction:

- UV radiation ($4 \times 10^{-7}$ to $1 \times 10^{-8}$ m) through a gate post: wavelength far too small relative to the gap, so negligible diffraction.
- Sound waves ($1.72 \times 10^{-2}$ to $17$ m) past a steel rod: wavelength too large relative to the rod diameter for significant diffraction.
- Radio waves ($0.1$ to $10^{6}$ m) between human hair: wavelength vastly exceeds hair thickness, so no appreciable diffraction.
- X-rays ($1 \times 10^{-8}$ to $4 \times 10^{-13}$ m) through atoms in a crystalline solid: wavelength is roughly the same as the atomic spacing, so strong diffraction occurs.

The correct answer is X-rays through a crystalline solid, because the wavelength closely matches the gap size.

**Exam tip:** When drawing diffracted waves, keep the wavelength (distance between wavefronts) constant. It is only the amplitude of the wave that changes when diffracted.

## The Diffraction Grating

### Structure and Purpose

A **diffraction grating** is a piece of **optical equipment** that creates a diffraction pattern when it diffracts monochromatic light into bright and dark fringes, or white light into its different wavelength components. It consists of a **large number** of very **thin**, **equally spaced parallel slits** carved into a glass plate.

Diffraction gratings are useful because they create a **sharper pattern** than a double slit. Their **bright fringes** are **narrower** and **brighter**, while their dark regions are wider and darker.

### The Diffraction Grating Equation

The positions of the maxima produced by a diffraction grating are given by:

$$d \sin \theta = n\lambda$$

where:

- $n$ is the **order** of the maxima (the number of the maximum away from the central maximum, with $n = 0$ at the centre)
- $d$ is the distance between adjacent slits on the grating (m)
- $\theta$ is the angle of diffraction of the light of order $n$ from the normal (°)
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light from the source (m)

The regions where **constructive interference** occurs are the regions of **maximum intensity**, just as with single slit diffraction and double-slit interference.

### Slit Spacing

Diffraction gratings come in **different sizes**, determined by the number of **lines per millimetre** (lines/mm) or lines per m, represented by the symbol $N$.

The slit spacing $d$ is calculated from $N$ using:

$$d = \frac{1}{N}$$

If $N$ is given in lines per mm then $d$ is in mm. If $N$ is given in lines per m then $d$ is in m.

### Angular Separation

The angular separation of each maximum is found by rearranging the grating equation to make $\theta$ the subject. The angle $\theta$ is measured from the centre (the normal), meaning higher orders of $n$ are at greater angles.

The angular separation between two maxima is found by subtracting the smaller angle from the larger. For example, the angular separation between the first and second order maxima at $n_1$ and $n_2$ is $\theta_2 - \theta_1$.

### Orders of Maxima

The **maximum angle of diffraction** at which maxima can be observed is when the beam is at **right angles** to the diffraction grating, i.e. $\theta = 90°$ and $\sin \theta = 1$.

The highest order of maxima visible is therefore:

$$n = \frac{d}{\lambda}$$

Since $n$ must be an integer, if the calculated value is a decimal it must be **rounded down** to find the highest visible order. For example, if $n$ is calculated as 2.7, then $n = 2$ is the highest order visible.

### Worked Example: Angle Between Second-Order Lines

A diffraction grating with slit spacing $d = 1.7\;\mu\text{m}$ is used with light of wavelength $\lambda = 550\;\text{nm}$. To find the angle $\alpha$ between the two second-order lines:

1. Use $d \sin \theta = n\lambda$ with $n = 2$.
2. Rearrange: $\sin \theta = \frac{n\lambda}{d} = \frac{2 \times 550 \times 10^{-9}}{1.7 \times 10^{-6}} = 0.647...\approx 0.65$
3. $\theta = \sin^{-1}(0.65) = 40.5°$
4. Since $\theta$ is the angle from the centre to one second-order line, the total angle between the two symmetric second-order lines is $\alpha = 2\theta = 81°$ (2 s.f.).

**Exam tip:** Take care that $\theta$ is the angle from the centre (normal), not the angle between two different orders.

### Derivation of the Diffraction Grating Equation

When light passes through the slits of a diffraction grating, the path difference at the zeroth-order maximum ($n = 0$) is zero. At the first-order maxima, constructive interference occurs and the path difference is $\lambda$. At the $n$th-order maxima, the path difference equals $n\lambda$.

From the geometry of adjacent slits separated by distance $d$, with the diffracted beam at angle $\theta$ to the normal, the path difference between adjacent rays is $d \sin \theta$. For the first-order maximum:

$$\sin \theta_1 = \frac{\lambda}{d}$$

For the second order ($n = 2$), where the path difference is $2\lambda$:

$$\sin \theta_2 = \frac{2\lambda}{d}$$

In general, for the $n$th order:

$$\sin \theta_n = \frac{n\lambda}{d}$$

Rearranging gives:

$$d \sin \theta = n\lambda$$

### Applications of Diffraction Gratings

Diffraction gratings are useful for **separating light** of different wavelengths with **high resolution**.

**Spectrometers:** Diffraction gratings are most commonly used in spectrometers, which play a crucial role in atomic physics and astrophysics. They are used to analyse light from stars and determine stellar composition, perform chemical analysis, measure red shift and rotation of stars, measure the wavelength or frequency of light from a star, and analyse the absorption and emission spectra of materials.

**X-ray crystallography:** X-rays are directed at a thin crystal sheet which acts as a diffraction grating to form a diffraction pattern. This works because the wavelength of X-rays is similar in size to the gaps between atoms. The resulting pattern can be used to measure the **atomic spacing** in certain materials.

**Monochromators:** Diffraction gratings are used to isolate a specific wavelength emitted by molecules in diseased cells from a biopsy sample, or to excite molecules in a sample with a specific wavelength of light.

**Optical fibres:** Diffraction gratings are used to select the optimum wavelength of light for transmission through an optical fibre.

**Exam tip:** You are expected to know applications of diffraction gratings, but you will not be tested on specific apparatus or techniques such as spectrometry. Avoid vague terms like "observe spectra" or "look at light from stars" and instead state specifically what is being measured or analysed.

| Equation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $d \sin \theta = n\lambda$ | Diffraction grating equation relating slit spacing, angle, order, and wavelength |
