# Refraction

## Refraction at a Plane Surface

### Refractive Index

The **refractive index** of a material is the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in that substance. It quantifies how much a material slows light down.

$$n = \frac{c}{c_s}$$

where $n$ is the refractive index (dimensionless), $c$ is the speed of light in a vacuum (m s$^{-1}$), and $c_s$ is the speed of light in the substance (m s$^{-1}$).

Since light always travels slower in a substance than in a vacuum, $n$ is always greater than 1. A material with a high refractive index is called **optically dense** and causes light to travel slower. In calculations, the refractive index of air is taken as approximately 1 (light does not slow down significantly in air compared to a vacuum). The refractive index of air is often written as $n_{\text{air}}$ or $n_1$.

### Refraction

**Refraction** is the change in direction of a wave when it passes through a boundary between media of different density. This direction change is caused by a change in speed of different parts of the wavefront as they hit the boundary: one side of the wavefront changes speed before the other side, causing the wave to turn. In optics, the word **medium** refers to a transparent material.

The **angle of incidence** ($i$) is the angle between the approaching incident ray and the **normal** (a line drawn at 90° to the boundary). The **angle of refraction** ($r$) is the angle between the ray leaving the boundary and the normal. The amount of refraction depends on the difference in optical density between the two media.

**Conditions for refraction:**

- Into a more optically dense medium (e.g. air to glass): waves travel slower, the angle of refraction is smaller than the angle of incidence, and the light bends **towards** the normal. The refracted light has a **lower speed** and a **shorter wavelength**.
- Into a less optically dense medium (e.g. glass to air): waves travel faster, the angle of refraction is larger than the angle of incidence, and the light bends **away** from the normal. The refracted light has a **higher speed** and a **longer wavelength**.

When a wave refracts, its speed and wavelength change but its **frequency remains the same** (the colour of light does not change).

When a light ray hits the boundary at exactly 90° (along the normal), the wave passes **straight through** without changing direction because the entire wavefront enters the new medium simultaneously.

**Analogy:** Imagine a car driving at an angle from a road onto a patch of mud. The wheel that hits the mud slows down first while the other wheel continues at the original speed, causing the car to turn.

## Snell's Law

**Snell's law** relates the angle of incidence to the angle of refraction at a boundary between two media:

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

where $n_1$ is the refractive index of material 1, $n_2$ is the refractive index of material 2, $\theta_1$ is the angle of incidence in material 1, and $\theta_2$ is the angle of refraction in material 2. Angles $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are always measured from the normal. The ray travels from material 1 into material 2.

This can be rearranged to give:

$$\frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{\sin \theta_1}{\sin \theta_2}$$

**Worked example method:** For a light ray entering a glass cube from air at 39° with refraction angle 25°, apply Snell's law with $n_1 = 1$ (air): $n_2 = \frac{\sin 39°}{\sin 25°} = 1.489 \approx 1.5$. In a "show that" question, give the answer to at least one more significant figure than the value stated in the question.

**Exam tips:**

- Always check that angles of incidence and refraction are measured from the normal, not from the boundary surface. If the angle from the boundary is given, subtract from 90° to get the correct angle.
- Ensure your calculator is set to degrees, not radians.

## Total Internal Reflection

### Critical Angle

The **critical angle** ($\theta_c$) is the angle of incidence in the denser medium at which the angle of refraction is exactly 90° (the refracted ray travels along the boundary). The larger the refractive index of a material, the smaller its critical angle.

Three cases arise when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium:

| Condition | Result |
|---|---|
| Angle of incidence $<$ critical angle | Ray refracts and exits the material |
| Angle of incidence $=$ critical angle | Angle of refraction $= 90°$, refracted ray travels along the boundary |
| Angle of incidence $>$ critical angle | **Total internal reflection** occurs |

### Critical Angle Equation

$$\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$

where $n_1$ is the refractive index of the denser material (the material the light is travelling in), $n_2$ is the refractive index of the less dense material, and $\theta_c$ is the critical angle of material 1. The formula finds the critical angle of the material in the denominator. This can also be written as:

$$\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{\sin \theta_1}{\sin \theta_2}$$

### Total Internal Reflection

**Total internal reflection** (TIR) is a special case of refraction that occurs when both conditions are met:

1. The angle of incidence within the denser medium is **greater than the critical angle** ($i > \theta_c$).
2. The incident refractive index $n_1$ is **greater than** the refractive index of the material at the boundary $n_2$ (i.e. $n_1 > n_2$, the light must be travelling from denser to less dense).

TIR follows the **law of reflection**: the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.

**Worked example method:** A glass cube ($n = 1.489$) is in contact with a liquid. Light enters the vertical air-glass face at 39° and refracts to 25° inside the glass. At the glass-liquid boundary, it is totally internally reflected for the first time. The refracted ray inside the glass hits the bottom face at an angle of $90° - 25° = 65°$ to the normal. Since TIR occurs for the first time at this angle, 65° is the critical angle. Applying $n_2 = n_1 \times \frac{\sin \theta_1}{\sin \theta_2} = 1.489 \times \frac{\sin 65°}{\sin 90°} = 1.35$ gives the refractive index of the liquid. After TIR, the ray reflects symmetrically (angle of incidence = angle of reflection = 65°) and exits through the opposite glass-air face at 39° (by symmetry with the entry face).

## Fibre Optics

### Structure and Operation

**Optical fibres** use total internal reflection to transmit light signals over long distances without significant loss of information or speed. Monochromatic light refracts as it enters the fibre at one end, undergoes repeated TIR against the sides of the fibre, and is refracted back out at the other end.

The three main components of a step-index optical fibre are:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Core** | An optically dense tube made of plastic or glass (higher refractive index, $n_1$) |
| **Cladding** | A layer of lower optical density surrounding the core ($n_2 < n_1$) |
| **Outer sheath** | Protective outer covering |

This is called a **step-index fibre** because the refractive index increases in a step from the outside (cladding) to the centre (core). TIR only occurs because $n_{\text{cladding}} < n_{\text{core}}$.

**Applications:** communications (telephone and internet transmission) and medical imaging (endoscopes).

### Role of the Cladding

- **Protects** the thin core from damage and scratching.
- **Prevents signal degradation** by stopping light from escaping the core, which would cause loss of information.
- Keeps signals **secure** and maintains original signal quality.
- Keeps the **core separate** from adjacent fibres, preventing information crossover between fibres.

### Material Dispersion

**Material dispersion** occurs when white light (rather than monochromatic light) is used. The white light separates into its spectral components because each wavelength travels at a different speed in the medium. Violet light has the shortest wavelength and travels slowest in the fibre, so its angle of incidence at the fibre boundary is smallest. Since angle of incidence equals angle of reflection, violet light undergoes more reflections and takes longer to travel down the fibre. This causes the beam to spread and the output pulse to broaden.

### Modal Dispersion

**Modal dispersion** occurs even with monochromatic light. Different parts of the wavefront have different angles of incidence, so each part undergoes TIR a different number of times and reaches the end of the fibre at slightly different times, causing the pulse to spread out. This effect is more prominent when the core is **wider** (more possible path differences). To reduce modal dispersion, the core must be very **narrow**.

### Pulse Broadening and Absorption

Both material and modal dispersion cause **pulse broadening**, where output pulses are longer than input pulses. This can result in the **merging of adjacent pulses**, which distorts the transmitted information and decreases the signal amplitude.

**Absorption** occurs when the fibre material absorbs part of the signal's energy, reducing the **amplitude** of the signal and leading to loss of transmitted information.

### Reducing Pulse Broadening and Absorption

**To reduce absorption:**

- Use an extremely **transparent** core material.
- Use optical fibre **repeaters** to regenerate the pulse before significant absorption occurs.

**To reduce pulse broadening:**

- Use a core that is as **narrow** as possible to minimise path length differences.
- Use a **monochromatic** light source so pulse speed is constant (eliminates material dispersion).
- Use optical fibre **repeaters** to regenerate the pulse before significant broadening occurs.
- Use a **single-mode fibre**, where only a single wavelength passes through the core, to reduce multipath modal dispersion.

**Worked example method:** For a step-index fibre where the light refracts along the core-cladding boundary (i.e. at the critical angle), given the speed of light in the core ($2.027 \times 10^8$ m s$^{-1}$) and cladding ($2.055 \times 10^8$ m s$^{-1}$): first calculate the refractive indices using $n = c/c_s$ (core: $n_1 = 1.48$, cladding: $n_2 = 1.46$). Then find the critical angle: $\theta_c = \sin^{-1}(1.46/1.48) = 80.5°$. The angle of refraction at the entry face is $90° - 80.5° = 9.5°$. Finally, apply Snell's law at the air-core boundary: $\sin \theta = n_2 \sin \theta_r = 1.48 \times \sin 9.5° = 0.2443$, so $\theta = \sin^{-1}(0.2443) = 14.1°$.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Description |
|---|---|
| $n = \dfrac{c}{c_s}$ | Refractive index of a material |
| $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$ | Snell's law |
| $\sin \theta_c = \dfrac{n_2}{n_1}$ | Critical angle ($n_1 > n_2$) |
