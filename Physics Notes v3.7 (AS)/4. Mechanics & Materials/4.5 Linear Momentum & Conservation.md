# Linear Momentum & Conservation

## Linear Momentum

**Linear momentum** is the momentum of an object moving in one dimension, defined as the product of mass and velocity:

$$p = mv$$

where $p$ is momentum in kg m s$^{-1}$, $m$ is mass in kg, and $v$ is velocity in m s$^{-1}$.

Momentum is a **vector** quantity possessing both **magnitude** and **direction**. The initial direction of motion is usually assigned the positive direction. An object travelling in the opposite direction has a negative velocity and therefore negative momentum.

A light object moving fast can carry the same momentum as a heavy object moving slowly. For example, a 60 g tennis ball at 75 m s$^{-1}$ has momentum $0.060 \times 75 = 4.5$ kg m s$^{-1}$, identical to a 3 kg brick at 1.5 m s$^{-1}$ ($3 \times 1.5 = 4.5$ kg m s$^{-1}$). On impact, both would exert a similar force (depending on the time each takes to stop).

The linear momentum of an object remains **constant** unless the system is acted upon by an external resultant force.

**Exam tip:** The SI unit of momentum is kg m s$^{-1}$. Convert mass from grams to kg (divide by 1000) and velocity from km s$^{-1}$ to m s$^{-1}$ (multiply by 1000) before substituting. The choice of positive direction is arbitrary, but signs must remain consistent throughout the calculation.

## Conservation of Momentum

### The Principle of Conservation of Momentum

The **principle of conservation of linear momentum** states: the total momentum before a collision is equal to the total momentum after a collision, provided no external force acts.

$$\text{Total momentum before} = \text{Total momentum after}$$

Because momentum is a vector, opposing momenta can cancel to give a **net momentum of zero**. An object that **rebounds** after a collision has a positive velocity before and a negative velocity after (taking the initial direction as positive).

Momentum, like energy, is **always conserved**. For two objects A and B:

$$p_{Ai} + p_{Bi} = p_{Af} + p_{Bf}$$

where each $p$ term is the momentum (in kg m s$^{-1}$) of the relevant object before ($i$) or after ($f$) the collision.

### Conservation Example: Collision with Rebound

Ball A (mass $m_A$) moves right at velocity $u_A$ and strikes stationary Ball B (mass $m_B$). After the collision, A rebounds left and B moves right. Taking rightward as positive:

$$p_{\text{before}} = m_A u_A + 0$$

$$p_{\text{after}} = -m_A v_A + m_B v_B$$

Equating by conservation:

$$m_A u_A = m_B v_B - m_A v_A$$

A stationary object has zero momentum.

### Conservation Example: Objects Stick Together

Trolley A (0.80 kg) at 3.0 m s$^{-1}$ collides with stationary trolley B (twice A's mass, so 1.60 kg). They stick together. Using conservation:

$$0.80 \times 3.0 + 0 = (0.80 + 1.60) \times v_{A+B}$$

$$v_{A+B} = \frac{2.4}{2.4} = 1.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

### External and Internal Forces

The conservation law applies only when **no external forces** act.

| Force type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **External forces** | Forces acting on the system from outside | Friction, weight |
| **Internal forces** | Forces exchanged between particles within the system | Tension in a string |

Which forces count as internal or external depends on the system boundary. Systems with no external forces are described as **closed** or **isolated**, and in such systems total momentum is conserved.

**Example:** A swimmer diving from a stationary boat. Before the dive, total momentum is zero. The diver moves forward, so the boat moves backward to conserve momentum, because no external horizontal forces act on the diver-boat system.

## Impulse

### Force and Momentum

Force can be defined as the **rate of change of momentum**:

$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$$

where $F$ is the resultant force (N), $\Delta p$ is the change in momentum (kg m s$^{-1}$), and $\Delta t$ is the time interval (s). The change in momentum is:

$$\Delta p = p_{\text{final}} - p_{\text{initial}}$$

### Direction of Forces

Force and momentum are both vectors. The force equal to the rate of change of momentum is the **resultant force**. Taking the initial direction of motion as positive:

- A force opposing the initial velocity is negative.
- The opposing force is exerted by the object the body has collided with.
- By **Newton's Third Law**, the forces on the two colliding objects are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

**Method example (car rebounding off a wall):** A 1500 kg car hits a wall at 15 m s$^{-1}$ and rebounds at 5 m s$^{-1}$, with a contact time of 3.0 s. Taking the initial direction as positive:

$$p_i = 1500 \times 15 = 22\,500 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$$

$$p_f = 1500 \times (-5) = -7500 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$$

$$\Delta p = -7500 - 22\,500 = -30\,000 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$$

$$F = \frac{-30\,000}{3.0} = -10\,000 \text{ N}$$

The negative sign indicates the force on the car acts opposite to its initial motion (i.e. the wall pushes the car back). By Newton's Third Law, the car exerts a force of +10 000 N on the wall.

**Exam tip:** In questions, look for the words "from", "acting on", or "exerted on" to identify which object experiences which force. Sketch a free-body diagram if needed.

### Definition of Impulse

**Impulse** is the effect of an external resultant force acting on an object for a short time, changing its motion. Examples include kicking a ball, catching a ball, and collisions between objects.

Impulse is the product of the force and the time for which it acts:

$$\text{Impulse} = F\Delta t$$

measured in **newton seconds** (N s).

Since $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$, rearranging gives:

$$\Delta p = F\Delta t$$

so **change in momentum equals impulse**, and impulse can be measured indirectly through the change in momentum:

$$\text{Impulse} = \Delta p = mv - mu$$

where $v$ is the final velocity and $u$ is the initial velocity (both in m s$^{-1}$).

These equations apply only when the force $F$ is **constant**.

**Impulse** is a vector quantity, always in the direction of the **resultant force**. A small force acting over a long time produces the same impulse as a large force acting over a short time.

**Exam tip:** The base units of the newton are kg m s$^{-2}$, so $F\Delta t$ has units kg m s$^{-2}$ $\times$ s = kg m s$^{-1}$, confirming that impulse and momentum share the same unit.

### Examples of Impulse

**Rain vs hail on an umbrella:** Water droplets splatter and roll off with only a small change in momentum. Hailstones have a larger mass and tend to bounce back, producing a greater change in momentum. The impulse the umbrella applies on the hailstones is therefore greater than on raindrops, meaning more force is needed to hold the umbrella upright in hail.

**Cricket fielder catching a ball:** A high-speed cricket ball has high momentum. Stopping it abruptly would produce a large force over a short time, risking injury. The fielder draws their hands back on catching, **increasing the time** over which the change in momentum occurs. This reduces the force exerted on their hands. The fielder may also step backwards to extend the deceleration time further.

**Impulse and injury prevention:** Increasing the contact time over which a momentum change occurs reduces the force experienced, lowering the risk of injury.

### Worked Method: Impulse with Direction Change

A 58 g tennis ball moves left at 30 m s$^{-1}$ and is struck back to the right at 20 m s$^{-1}$. Taking leftward as positive:

$$\text{Impulse} = m(v - u) = (58 \times 10^{-3})(-20 - 30) = -2.9 \text{ N s}$$

The negative sign means the impulse acts to the right (opposite to the initial leftward motion), which is the direction the racket pushes the ball.

**Exam tip:** If an object changes direction, the sign of its velocity must change. This is the most common error. Velocity, impulse, force, and momentum are all vectors. For instance, an impulse of 20 N s to the right is $-20$ N s when leftward is defined as positive.

## Impulse on a Force-Time Graph

In practice, forces often vary over time. When force is plotted against time, the **impulse equals the area under the force-time graph**, because impulse $= F \times \Delta t$.

The impulse is the same whether a small force acts for a long time or a large force acts for a short time (provided the area under the graph is equal).

**Finding the area:**

- If the graph is a **curve**, estimate the area by counting squares.
- If the graph consists of **straight lines**, split it into geometric shapes (rectangles, triangles, trapeziums) and sum their areas.

Since $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$, the same change in momentum spread over a longer time produces a smaller force (and vice versa).

### Worked Method: Velocity from a Force-Time Graph

A 3.0 kg ball, initially at rest, experiences a force that varies as a triangular graph peaking at 4 kN at $t = 8$ s, returning to zero at $t = 16$ s.

$$\text{Impulse} = \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times (4 \times 10^3) + \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times (4 \times 10^3) = 32 \times 10^3 \text{ N s}$$

$$32 \times 10^3 = 3.0 \times v \implies v = \frac{32 \times 10^3}{3.0} \approx 11 \text{ km s}^{-1}$$

### Impact Forces

**Impact forces** are reduced by **increasing the contact time**. This principle is applied in sport, vehicle safety, and packaging.

**In sport (cricket):** The fielder draws their hands back to extend the deceleration time and reduce the force on their hands.

**In sport (football):** Following through a kick increases the contact time between foot and ball, transferring more momentum and giving the ball a greater impulse and therefore a higher velocity.

**In packaging:** Bubble wrap and polyester padding cushion fragile items by increasing the time over which impact forces act, reducing peak force and the risk of damage.

**Method example (comparing forces):** For the same change in momentum ($\Delta p = 0.5$ kg m s$^{-1}$), a contact time of 2.0 s gives $F = 0.5/2.0 = 0.25$ N, while a contact time of 0.1 s gives $F = 0.5/0.1 = 5.0$ N. The shorter contact time delivers twenty times the force.

**Exam tip:** The area under a force-time graph may need splitting into squares, rectangles, right-angled triangles, and trapeziums. Be comfortable calculating each.

## Collisions

### Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

In both collisions and explosions, momentum is **always conserved**. However, **kinetic energy** may or may not be conserved.

| Property | Elastic collision | Inelastic collision |
|---|---|---|
| Momentum | Conserved | Conserved |
| Kinetic energy | Conserved | Not conserved |
| Typical outcome | Objects bounce apart, move in opposite directions | Objects stick together and move as one |

An **explosion** involves recoil, such as a gun firing a bullet or an unstable nucleus emitting an alpha particle and a daughter nucleus.

To determine the collision type, compare the total kinetic energy before and after using:

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

**Method example:** Trolley A (0.80 kg, 3.0 m s$^{-1}$) collides with stationary trolley B (1.60 kg). They stick together at 1.0 m s$^{-1}$.

$$E_{k,\text{before}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.80)(3.0)^2 + 0 = 3.6 \text{ J}$$

$$E_{k,\text{after}} = \frac{1}{2}(2.4)(1.0)^2 = 1.2 \text{ J}$$

Kinetic energy is not conserved ($3.6 \neq 1.2$), so this is an **inelastic** collision. The "lost" kinetic energy is converted to other forms (heat, sound, deformation).

**Exam tip:** A stationary object has $v = 0$, so its momentum and kinetic energy are both zero. When two objects stick together after a collision, treat the combined object as a single body with mass equal to the sum of the individual masses.

### Momentum Conservation Issues: Vehicle Safety

The impact force in a vehicle collision can be **decreased** by **increasing the contact time** over which the collision occurs. Vehicles use three key safety features:

**Seat belts** keep the passenger fixed to their seat during an abrupt stop. They are designed to stretch slightly, increasing the time over which the passenger's momentum reaches zero and reducing the force exerted on them.

**Airbags** deploy from the dashboard, steering wheel, and doors on collision. They act as a soft cushion, increasing the contact time over which the passenger decelerates and thereby reducing the force.

**Crumple zones** are built into the front and rear exterior of vehicles. They crush in a controlled way during a collision, increasing the time over which the vehicle's momentum reaches zero and reducing the force on the passengers. This is why even minor collisions can produce apparently heavy bodywork damage.

On a **force-time graph**, for the same impulse (same area under the curve), the effect of safety features is shown by a **lower peak force** and a **wider base** (longer contact time), compared to the taller, narrower peak without them.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Units |
|---|---|---|
| Momentum | $p = mv$ | kg m s$^{-1}$ |
| Conservation of momentum | $p_{Ai} + p_{Bi} = p_{Af} + p_{Bf}$ | kg m s$^{-1}$ |
| Force (rate of change of momentum) | $F = \dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$ | N |
| Change in momentum | $\Delta p = p_{\text{final}} - p_{\text{initial}}$ | kg m s$^{-1}$ |
| Impulse (constant force) | $\text{Impulse} = F\Delta t = \Delta p = mv - mu$ | N s |
| Impulse (variable force) | $\text{Impulse} = \text{area under } F\text{-}t \text{ graph}$ | N s |
| Kinetic energy | $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ | J |
