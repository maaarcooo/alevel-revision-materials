# Work, Energy & Power

## Work & Power

### Work Done

**Work done** by a force is equivalent to a **transfer of energy**.

The unit of work done is the newton metre: $1 \text{ N m} = 1 \text{ J}$.

The work done by a **resultant force** on a system equals the change in energy in that system.

**Mechanical work** is defined as the amount of energy transferred when an external force causes an object to move over a certain distance.

If a constant force is applied **parallel** to the direction of the object's displacement:

$$W = Fs$$

where $W$ = work done (J), $F$ = average force applied (N), $s$ = displacement (m).

When pushing a block, **work is done against friction** and energy is transferred from the person to the block. Some energy is **dissipated** to the surroundings by heating and as sound.

### Work Done at an Angle

When the force is not parallel to the displacement but acts at an angle $\theta$ to it:

$$W = Fs\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the direction of the force and the direction of motion.

When $\theta = 0$ (force parallel to motion), $\cos\theta = 1$ and the equation reduces to $W = Fs$.

For **horizontal** motion, use $\cos\theta$. For **vertical** motion, use $\sin\theta$. The component of the force that is **parallel to the displacement** is the one used to calculate work done.

**Inline example (slope problem).** A barrel of weight $W = 2.5 \times 10^3$ N sits on a frictionless slope at 40° to the horizontal. To find the work done moving it 6.0 m up the slope, resolve the weight parallel to the slope: $F = W\sin 40° = 2.5 \times 10^3 \times \sin 40° = 1607$ N. Then $W_{\text{done}} = 1607 \times 6.0 = 9.6 \times 10^3$ J.

**Exam tip.** A common mistake is using the wrong force component. If the force is not parallel to the displacement, resolve it first and use only the parallel component.

### Power

**Power** is the rate of doing **work** or the rate of **energy transfer**:

$$P = \frac{\Delta W}{\Delta t}$$

where $P$ = power (W), $\Delta W$ = work done (J), $\Delta t$ = time taken (s).

Power is increased if the energy transfer is greater or if the same energy is transferred in a shorter time.

For an object moving at **constant velocity** with a **constant force**:

$$P = Fv$$

where $F$ = force (N) and $v$ = velocity (m s$^{-1}$). The force must be in the direction of the velocity; otherwise use the component of the force in the direction of velocity.

## Area Under a Force-Displacement Graph

The work done by a force acting over a distance can be found from a **force-displacement graph**: the work done equals the **area under the force-displacement graph**.

This follows directly from $W = F \times s$: the same work is done whether there is a small force over a long displacement or a large force over a small displacement.

If the graph is not a simple shape, split it into rectangles, triangles, and trapeziums. The total work done is the sum of the individual areas.

**Inline example (composite graph).** A force-displacement graph rises linearly from 100 N to 250 N over 0 to 80 m. Split into a rectangle (base 80, height 100) and a triangle (base 80, height 150): area $= (80 \times 100) + (0.5 \times 80 \times 150) = 8000 + 6000 = 14\,000$ J.

**Exam tip.** Always check the units on the axes. Forces may be given in kN and displacements in km; convert to SI units before calculating the area to obtain work done in joules.

### Variable Forces

A **variable force** is one that changes in magnitude during the motion. This is more representative of real-life forces.

For a **constant** force, $W = Fs$ and $P = Fv$ can be used directly. For a **varying** force, these equations **cannot** be used; instead, work done must be found from the area under the force-displacement graph.

If a varying force increases, the object's acceleration increases, and vice versa.

**Inline example (suitcase problem).** A person pulls a suitcase with 150 N over the first 12 m (constant), then the force decreases linearly to 60 N over the next 13 m (total 25 m). Rectangle area: $12 \times 150 = 1800$ J. Trapezium area: $\frac{1}{2}(150 + 60) \times 13 = 1365$ J. Total work done $= 1800 + 1365 = 3165$ J.

**Exam tip.** When sketching force-displacement graphs, they do not need to be to scale, but label the key values on both axes so you can calculate areas correctly.

## Efficiency

The **efficiency** of a system measures how successfully energy is transferred.

**Efficiency** is defined as the ratio of the useful power output from a system to its total power input:

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Useful power output}}{\text{Total power input}} \times 100\%$$

Equivalently, in terms of energy:

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Useful energy output}}{\text{Total energy input}} \times 100\%$$

A **high** efficiency means most energy transferred is **useful**. A **low** efficiency means most energy transferred is **wasted**.

Which energy is useful or wasted depends on the system. In a **lightbulb**, light energy is useful and heat is wasted. In a **heater**, thermal energy is useful and sound is wasted.

**Inline example (motor efficiency).** An electric motor with 35% efficiency lifts a 7.2 kg load through 5 m in 3 s. Useful energy output $= mgh = 7.2 \times 9.81 \times 5 = 353.16$ J. Useful power output $= 353.16 \div 3 = 117.72$ W. Power input $= \frac{117.72 \times 100}{35} = 336$ W.

**Exam tip.** Efficiency can be expressed as a ratio (0 to 1) or a percentage (0% to 100%). It has **no units** since numerator and denominator share the same units. If the question asks for a ratio, give a fraction or decimal; for a percentage, multiply the ratio by 100.

## Conservation of Energy

### The Principle of Conservation of Energy

The **Principle of Conservation of Energy** states that energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transferred from one form to another.

The total amount of energy in a **closed system** remains constant, although the proportion in each form may change.

### Types of Energy

| Form | Description |
|---|---|
| **Kinetic** | Energy of a moving object |
| **Gravitational potential** | Energy gained when an object is lifted up, lost when it falls |
| **Elastic (strain)** | Energy of a stretched spring or elastic band |
| **Chemical** | Energy stored in a chemical substance |
| **Nuclear** | Energy stored within the nucleus of an atom |
| **Internal** | Energy due to an object's temperature or state (also called thermal or heat energy) |

### Energy Dissipation

No energy transfer is 100% efficient. When energy is transferred, some is **dissipated** to the surroundings. Dissipated energy is usually **wasted** energy because it cannot easily be used for a specific purpose. Common wasted energy forms are **thermal (heat), light, and sound**.

What counts as wasted depends on the system. In a **television**: electrical energy converts to light + sound (useful) + thermal energy (wasted). In a **heater**: electrical energy converts to thermal energy (useful) + sound (wasted).

### Applications of Energy Conservation

Common energy transfers:

- Falling object (in a vacuum): gravitational potential energy $\rightarrow$ kinetic energy
- Battery: chemical energy $\rightarrow$ electrical energy
- Horizontal mass on a spring: elastic potential energy $\rightarrow$ kinetic energy
- Trampoline: elastic potential energy $\rightarrow$ kinetic energy $\rightarrow$ gravitational potential energy

When **friction** or other resistive forces act:

$$\text{Loss in kinetic energy} = \text{Gain in gravitational potential energy} + \text{Work done against friction}$$

### Energy Equations

The most common equations in conservation of energy problems:

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

$$\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h$$

$$E_{\text{elastic}} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2$$

**Inline example (rollercoaster).** A rollercoaster follows the path A (top of slope) $\rightarrow$ B (bottom of loop) $\rightarrow$ C (top of loop) $\rightarrow$ D (flat ground). At A: raised up, so has GPE. A$\rightarrow$B: GPE converts to KE. B$\rightarrow$C: KE converts back to GPE as it rises. C$\rightarrow$D: GPE converts to KE descending to flat terrain. Energy sequence: GPE $\rightarrow$ KE $\rightarrow$ GPE $\rightarrow$ KE.

**Exam tip.** You may not be told the energy transfers directly. Familiarise yourself with which energy store applies to each physical situation, for instance a ball rolling downhill involves GPE to KE, and a spring releasing involves elastic PE to KE.

## Kinetic & Gravitational Potential Energy

### Kinetic Energy

**Kinetic energy** is the energy an object has due to its **motion** (or velocity). The faster an object moves, the greater its kinetic energy.

When an object falls, it gains kinetic energy (from losing gravitational potential energy).

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

where $E_k$ = kinetic energy (J), $m$ = mass (kg), $v$ = velocity (m s$^{-1}$).

**Inline example (finding new KE).** A body at 12 m s$^{-1}$ has $E_k = 1650$ J. First find mass: $m = \frac{2 \times 1650}{12^2} = \frac{3300}{144} \approx 22.9$ kg. At 45 m s$^{-1}$: $E_k = \frac{1}{2} \times 22.9 \times 45^2 \approx 23\,000$ J (2 s.f.).

**Exam tip.** In $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, only $v$ is squared, not $m$ or the $\frac{1}{2}$. If a question asks for the "loss of kinetic energy", do not include a negative sign since energy is a scalar quantity.

### Gravitational Potential Energy

**Gravitational potential energy** is energy stored in a mass due to its position in a **gravitational field**. Lifting a mass up increases its GPE; a falling mass loses GPE.

For energy changes in a **uniform gravitational field** (near the Earth's surface):

$$\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h$$

where $\Delta E_p$ = change in gravitational potential energy (J), $m$ = mass (kg), $g$ = gravitational field strength (9.81 N kg$^{-1}$), $\Delta h$ = change in height (m).

The potential energy at ground level is usually taken as zero, though any reference point can be used when calculating **changes** in GPE.

This equation is only valid in a **uniform gravitational field**. At A-level, non-uniform fields (e.g. in space) require a different treatment.

### Gravitational Potential Energy vs Height Graphs

For a ball thrown upward (ignoring air resistance), the graph of GPE against height is a **straight line** through the origin with positive gradient, showing a **linear** relationship ($\Delta E_p \propto \Delta h$ at constant $m$ and $g$). For a falling ball, the graph is a straight line with negative gradient. These graphs would be identical if GPE were plotted against time instead of height.

**Inline example (climbing stairs).** A 74 kg man climbs 5 flights of stairs, each 3.7 m high. Total height: $\Delta h = 5 \times 3.7 = 18.5$ m. $\Delta E_p = 74 \times 9.81 \times 18.5 \approx 13\,000$ J (2 s.f.).

**Exam tip.** Use the correct symbol $\Delta E_p$ in equations rather than the informal abbreviation "GPE".

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Work done (parallel force) | $W = Fs$ | Force parallel to displacement |
| Work done (angled force) | $W = Fs\cos\theta$ | $\theta$ = angle between force and displacement |
| Power | $P = \dfrac{\Delta W}{\Delta t}$ | Rate of energy transfer |
| Power (constant $F$ and $v$) | $P = Fv$ | Force in direction of velocity |
| Efficiency | $\eta = \dfrac{\text{Useful output}}{\text{Total input}} \times 100\%$ | Works with power or energy; dimensionless |
| Kinetic energy | $E_k = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ | $v$ is the only squared term |
| Gravitational PE (uniform field) | $\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h$ | Valid near Earth's surface only |
| Elastic PE | $E_{\text{elastic}} = \dfrac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2$ | $\Delta L$ = extension or compression |
| Conservation with friction | $\Delta E_k = \Delta E_p + W_{\text{friction}}$ | Loss in KE = gain in GPE + work against friction |
