# Bulk Properties of Solids

---

## Density

**Density** is the mass per unit volume of an object.

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$$

where $\rho$ = density (kg m$^{-3}$), $m$ = mass (kg), $V$ = volume (m$^3$).

Objects made from low-density materials typically have a lower mass. A balloon is less dense than a small bar of lead despite occupying a larger volume. Gases are less dense than solids because their particles are more spread out.

The units of density depend on the units chosen for mass and volume: mass in g with volume in cm$^3$ gives g cm$^{-3}$; mass in kg with volume in m$^3$ gives kg m$^{-3}$.

### Volume Formulas for Common 3D Shapes

| Shape | Volume |
|---|---|
| Sphere | $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ |
| Cube | $V = d^3$ |
| Cylinder | $V = \pi r^2 l$ |

When the volume is not given directly, calculate it from the object's dimensions using the appropriate formula.

**Worked method (density of a rectangular solid):** For a paving slab of mass 73 kg with dimensions 40 mm × 500 mm × 850 mm: calculate volume in mm$^3$ ($V = 40 \times 500 \times 850 = 1.7 \times 10^7$ mm$^3$), convert to m$^3$ using $1\text{ mm}^3 = (10^{-3})^3 = 10^{-9}\text{ m}^3$, giving $V = 1.7 \times 10^{-2}$ m$^3$, then $\rho = 73 / (1.7 \times 10^{-2}) = 4300$ kg m$^{-3}$ (2 s.f.).

### Unit Conversion Tips

When converting a larger unit to a smaller one, multiply. When converting a smaller unit to a larger one, divide. For squared or cubic conversions, square or cube the conversion factor:

- $1\text{ mm}^3 = 1/(1000)^3 = 1 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}^3$
- $1\text{ cm}^3 = 1/(100)^3 = 1 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^3$

## Hooke's Law

When a force is applied to each end of a spring or wire, it **stretches**. This occurs for any material with **elasticity**, such as a wire or a bungee rope.

A material obeys **Hooke's Law** if the extension of the material is directly proportional to the applied force (load) up to the **limit of proportionality**.

$$F = k\Delta L$$

where $F$ = force (N), $k$ = **spring constant** (N m$^{-1}$), $\Delta L$ = extension (m).

The spring constant is a property of the material being stretched and measures the **stiffness** of a material. The larger the spring constant, the stiffer the material.

Hooke's Law applies to both **extensions** (increase in length) and **compressions** (decrease in length).

The **extension** is calculated as:

$$\Delta L = \text{stretched length} - \text{unstretched length}$$

### Force-Extension Graphs

The way a material responds to a given force can be shown on a force-extension graph. Every material has a unique force-extension graph depending on how brittle or ductile it is.

A material obeying Hooke's Law is shown by a **straight line through the origin**. As more force is added beyond the proportional region, the graph starts to curve.

Key features of the force-extension graph:

**Limit of proportionality:** Beyond this point, Hooke's Law no longer holds and the extension is no longer proportional to the applied force.

**Elastic limit:** Beyond this point, the material will no longer return to its original length when the force is removed.

**Gradient:** In the linear (Hooke's Law) region, the gradient of a force-extension graph ($F$ on $y$-axis, $\Delta L$ on $x$-axis) equals the spring constant $k$. If the axes are swapped (extension on $y$-axis, force on $x$-axis), the gradient equals $\frac{1}{k}$.

**Worked method (spring constant from a graph with swapped axes):** When the graph plots length (cm) on the $y$-axis against load (N) on the $x$-axis, the spring constant is $1/\text{gradient}$. Reading two points on the linear section (e.g. $\Delta L = 0.145 - 0.10 = 0.045$ m, $\Delta F = 0.36$ N), gradient $= 0.045/0.36 = 1/8.0$ m N$^{-1}$, so $k = 8.0$ N m$^{-1}$.

**Exam tip:** Always check which quantity is on which axis before finding $k$ as the gradient. Exam questions frequently place force on the $x$-axis, making the gradient $1/k$ rather than $k$.

## Stress & Strain

Opposite forces can deform an object. If the forces stretch the object, they are **tensile forces**, which lead to two properties: **tensile stress** and **tensile strain**.

### Tensile Stress

**Tensile stress** is the force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material.

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A}$$

where $\sigma$ = tensile stress (Pa), $F$ = force applied (N), $A$ = cross-sectional area (m$^2$).

The symbol $\sigma$ is the Greek lower-case letter "sigma". Stress has units of pascals (Pa), the same as pressure.

The **ultimate tensile stress** is the maximum force per original cross-sectional area a wire can support before it breaks.

### Tensile Strain

**Tensile strain** is the extension per unit length, representing a deformation of a solid due to stress in the form of elongation or contraction.

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$$

where $\Delta L$ = extension (m), $L$ = original length (m).

Strain is **dimensionless** because it is the ratio of two lengths. It can also be expressed as a percentage (e.g. extending a 0.1 m wire by 0.005 m gives strain $= (0.005/0.1) \times 100 = 5\%$).

Since strain is a ratio, the extension and original length do not need to be in metres, as long as both are in the same units.

**Worked method (stress and strain of a brass wire):** A brass wire of length 4.50 m and radius 0.2 mm is extended to 4.53 m by a 50 N force. Cross-sectional area $A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times (0.2 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}$ m$^2$. Stress $= 50 / (1.257 \times 10^{-7}) = 400$ MPa. Extension $= 4.53 - 4.50 = 0.03$ m. Strain $= 0.03/4.50 = 6.7 \times 10^{-3}$.

### Stress-Strain Curves

**Stress-strain curves** describe material properties such as whether they are brittle or ductile, and up to what stress and strain they obey Hooke's Law and exhibit elastic or plastic behaviour. Each material has a unique stress-strain curve.

Key points on the stress-strain graph:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Limit of proportionality** | The point up to which stress is directly proportional to strain (Hooke's Law region, straight line from origin) |
| **Elastic limit** | Beyond this, the material will not return to its original shape when the force is removed |
| **Yield stress** | The stress at which the material extends plastically for no or a small increase in stress |
| **Breaking point** | The stress at which the material fractures. The stress here is the **breaking stress**, the maximum stress the material can withstand |
| **Elastic region** | From the origin up to the elastic limit. The material returns to its original shape when the force is removed |
| **Plastic region** | Beyond the elastic limit to the breaking point. The material is permanently deformed |

The **area under the straight-line (Hooke's Law) region** of a stress-strain graph equals the elastic strain energy stored per unit volume.

**Worked method (reading a stress-strain graph for copper):** The breaking stress is read from the $y$-axis at the final point on the curve (the fracture point), e.g. 190 MPa. The stress at which plastic deformation begins is where the graph starts to curve away from the straight line, e.g. 130 MPa (acceptable range typically 120-135 MPa for subjective readings).

## Elastic Strain Energy

Work has to be done to stretch a material. Before a material reaches its elastic limit (while it obeys Hooke's Law), all the work done is stored as **elastic strain energy**.

The work done (or elastic strain energy) equals the **area under the force-extension graph**. This is true whether or not the material obeys Hooke's Law.

For the region where the material **obeys** Hooke's Law, the work done is the area of a **right-angled triangle** under the graph:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}F\Delta L$$

where $E$ = elastic strain energy (J), $F$ = force (N), $\Delta L$ = extension (m).

Substituting Hooke's Law ($F = k\Delta L$) gives an alternative form:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2$$

where $k$ = spring constant (N m$^{-1}$).

For the region where the material **does not** obey Hooke's Law, the work done is the full area under the graph. Calculate this by splitting the area into separate geometric segments (triangles, trapeziums, rectangles) and summing them.

**Worked method (splitting areas):** For a graph where the Hooke's Law region ends at point X (extension 11.0 mm, force 450 N) and the material extends to point Y (extension 13.5 mm, force 600 N), split into area A (triangle from 0 to 11.0 mm) and area B (trapezium from 11.0 to 13.5 mm). Area A $= \frac{1}{2} \times 11 \times 10^{-3} \times 450 = 2.475$ J. Area B $= \frac{(450 + 600)}{2} \times 2.5 \times 10^{-3} = 1.313$ J. Total $= 3.79$ J (3 s.f.).

**Worked method (area from a linear graph):** For a spring obeying Hooke's Law with force 8.1 N at extension 40 mm, energy stored $= \frac{1}{2} \times 40 \times 10^{-3} \times 8.1 = 0.16$ J.

### Breaking Stress

As greater force is applied to a material, the **stress** increases. The **breaking stress** is the maximum stress a material can stand before it fractures (breaks).

A material with high breaking stress is considered **ductile**, meaning it can extend significantly before breaking due to plastic deformation. Copper is a common example: it is both a good electrical conductor and ductile, making it suitable for wires.

The **ultimate tensile stress (UTS)** is the maximum stress a material can withstand and is sometimes marked separately from the breaking point on a stress-strain graph. After the UTS, the stress may decrease slightly before the material finally fractures at the breaking point. The UTS and breaking stress can depend on the condition of the material, such as its temperature, which is important for engineers selecting materials for structures that may face extreme temperatures and loads.

### Spring Energy

When a vertical spring with a hanging mass extends and contracts, its total energy remains constant but converts between three forms: **elastic potential energy (EPE)**, **kinetic energy (KE)**, and **gravitational potential energy (GPE)**.

| Position | EPE | KE | GPE |
|---|---|---|---|
| A (top, spring compressed) | Some (spring slightly compressed) | Zero (stationary) | Maximum (highest point) |
| B (middle, equilibrium) | Some (spring slightly stretched) | Maximum (passing through equilibrium at maximum speed) | Some (still above ground) |
| C (bottom, spring at max extension) | Maximum (maximum extension) | Zero (stationary) | Minimum (lowest point) |

For a **horizontal** mass-spring system, there is no gravitational potential energy to consider. The spring only converts between KE and EPE.

## Elastic & Plastic Behaviour

Materials can undergo two types of deformation:

**Elastic deformation:** When the load is removed, the object returns to its original shape. This occurs in the **elastic region** of the force-extension graph (below the elastic limit).

**Plastic deformation:** The material is **permanently deformed** and will not return to its original shape or length when the load is removed. This occurs beyond the **elastic limit**, in the **plastic region** of the graph.

The **elastic region** is where extension is proportional to force (straight line). The **plastic region** is where extension is no longer proportional to force (graph curves). These regions are divided by the elastic limit. The plastic region extends from the elastic limit to the point of fracture.

### Brittle and Ductile Materials

**Brittle** materials (e.g. glass, concrete) have very little to no plastic region. They break with little elastic and insignificant plastic deformation. On a stress-strain or force-extension graph, a brittle material is represented by a straight line through the origin with no or negligible curved region before fracture.

**Ductile** materials (e.g. rubber, copper) have a larger plastic region. They stretch into a new shape before breaking. On a graph, a ductile material is represented by a straight line through the origin that then curves towards the $x$-axis before breaking.

**Worked method (unloading after plastic deformation):** A metal wire loaded to point X (elastic limit) and then to Y (near fracture) exhibits plastic behaviour between X and Y. When masses are removed, the unloading line has the **same gradient** as the original loading line (because the intermolecular forces are unchanged), but the $y$-intercept is no longer through the origin because plastic deformation has caused a **permanent extension**. The bonds in the metal are re-aligned during unloading, but the material does not return to zero extension.

**Exam tip:** Describe plastic deformation as the material being "permanently deformed" rather than saying it "does not obey Hooke's law". The latter is technically about proportionality, not permanent deformation.

## Energy Conservation

### Loading and Unloading a Metal Wire

When a metal wire is loaded beyond its limit of proportionality, it undergoes **plastic deformation**. When the force is removed (unloading), the extension decreases but the wire does not return to zero extension because it is now **permanently extended**.

The unloading line is **parallel** to the loading line (since $k$ does not change) but does not pass through the origin. The **area between** the loading and unloading lines represents the work done to permanently deform the wire.

### Loading and Unloading a Rubber Band

The force-extension graph for loading (stretching) and unloading (contracting) a rubber band produces a **hysteresis loop**. The rubber band returns to zero extension when fully unloaded, so it has no permanent extension and is **elastic**.

The loading curve lies above the unloading curve, showing the rubber band stores more strain energy during stretching than it releases during contraction.

| Area | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Area X (between the curves) | Work done in heating the rubber (increase in thermal energy) |
| Area Y (under the unloading curve) | Work done by the rubber returning to its original shape |
| Area X + Y (under the loading curve) | Total work done in stretching the rubber band |

Due to conservation of energy, the difference in strain energy between loading and unloading is transferred to **thermal energy**. A rubber band becomes warm when repeatedly stretched and contracted.

### Vehicle Suspension and Shock Absorbers

Vehicle suspension systems consist of **tyres**, **springs**, and **shock absorbers**, which provide comfortable handling and keep tyres in contact with the road.

A bump causes the wheel to move up and down perpendicular to the road surface. Without suspension, a wheel losing contact with the road would slam back down, causing large vibrations, potential damage, and loss of control.

**Shock absorbers** are elastic objects designed to absorb or dampen the compression and rebound of the springs above a vehicle's tyres. They convert **kinetic energy** (from the car's movement) into **thermal energy**, which is dissipated. The faster the suspension springs move (e.g. hitting a bump at high speed), the more resistance the shock absorber provides.
