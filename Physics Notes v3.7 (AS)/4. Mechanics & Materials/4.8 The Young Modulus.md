# The Young Modulus

## The Young Modulus

The **Young Modulus** is a measure of the ability of a material to withstand changes in length when a load is applied. It quantifies the **stiffness** of a material, which is critical for engineers selecting materials that must withstand sufficient forces without excessive deformation.

The Young Modulus is defined as the ratio of **tensile stress** to **tensile strain**:

$$E = \frac{\text{Tensile stress}}{\text{Tensile strain}} = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L}$$

where $F$ is the applied force (N), $L$ is the original length (m), $A$ is the cross-sectional area (m$^2$), and $\Delta L$ is the extension (m).

Since strain is dimensionless, the Young Modulus has units of **pascals (Pa)**. Typical values are very large, on the order of GPa.

| Material | Young's Modulus / GPa |
|---|---|
| Aluminium | 70 |
| Copper | 117 |
| Brass | 120 |
| Iron | 170 |
| Nickel | 210 |
| Steel | 110 |

**Worked example method.** Given a load, cross-sectional area, and percentage extension: (1) calculate stress $\sigma = F/A$, converting area to m$^2$ (recall $1 \text{ mm}^2 = 1 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2$); (2) convert percentage extension to strain (e.g. 0.50% = 0.005); (3) divide stress by strain to obtain $E$. For instance, a wire carrying 92 N with $A = 0.04 \text{ mm}^2$ and 0.50% extension gives $\sigma = 92 / (0.04 \times 10^{-6}) = 2.3 \times 10^9$ Pa, strain = 0.005, so $E = 2.3 \times 10^9 / 0.005 = 4.6 \times 10^{11}$ Pa.

**Exam tip:** To remember which quantity is on top, use the mnemonic "When you're stressed, you show the strain", i.e. Stress $\div$ Strain.

## The Young Modulus from Stress-Strain Graphs

The Young Modulus equals the **gradient** of a stress-strain graph in the **linear region** (the straight-line portion). This linear region is where **Hooke's Law** is obeyed.

The **area under the graph** in the linear region equals the **energy stored per unit volume** of the material.

A typical stress-strain graph starts with a straight line from the origin (gradient = Young Modulus), then curves as the material yields and deforms plastically.

**Worked example method.** To find $E$ from a stress-strain graph: identify where the linear region ends, read off the stress and strain at that point, then calculate gradient = stress / strain. For a copper wire where the linear region ends at stress $= 112.5 \times 10^6$ Pa and strain $= 0.9 \times 10^{-3}$:

$$E = \frac{112.5 \times 10^6}{0.9 \times 10^{-3}} = 1.25 \times 10^{11} \text{ Pa}$$

## Required Practical: The Young Modulus

### Aim

Measure the Young Modulus of a metal in the form of a wire, using a clamped horizontal wire over a pulley. An alternative setup uses a vertical wire attached to the ceiling with a mass hanging from it.

### Variables

The **independent variable** is the force (load) in N. The **dependent variable** is the extension in m. **Control variables** are the original length of wire, the thickness of the wire, and the metal used.

### Equipment

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Bench | Hold the equipment |
| G-clamp | Stabilise the apparatus |
| Copper wire | The test specimen |
| Metre ruler (resolution 1 mm) | Measure the length of the wire and extension |
| Pulley | Allow the mass to hang vertically with less friction than the table edge |
| Tape marker | Mark a reference point on the wire to measure extension; should touch the ruler |
| Wooden blocks | Clamp the wire to keep it taut and straight |
| Mass hanger + 100 g masses | Apply incremental loads to produce extension |
| Micrometer (resolution 0.001 mm) | Measure the diameter of the wire |

### Method

1. Measure the diameter of the wire using a micrometer screw gauge or digital callipers. Take at least 3 readings at different points along the wire and calculate an average.
2. Set up the apparatus so the wire is taut with no masses on the hanger.
3. Measure the original length of the wire using a metre ruler. Mark a reference point on the wire with tape, preferably near the beginning of the scale (e.g. at 1 cm).
4. Record the initial reading on the ruler at the reference point.
5. Add a 100 g mass onto the mass hanger.
6. Read and record the new position of the tape marker on the metre ruler.
7. Repeat by adding a 100 g mass each time (at least 5 to 10 additions), recording the new scale reading after each.

### Analysis of Results

The Young Modulus equation can be rearranged for force:

$$F = \left(\frac{E \times A}{L}\right) \Delta L$$

Comparing to $y = mx$: $y = F$, $x = \Delta L$, and the gradient $= EA/L$. Therefore:

$$E = \text{gradient} \times \frac{L}{A}$$

**Step-by-step analysis:**

1. Calculate the cross-sectional area from the average diameter: $A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4}$
2. Plot a graph of load $F$ (y-axis) against extension $\Delta L$ (x-axis). The load is found by multiplying each mass by $g = 9.81$ N kg$^{-1}$.
3. Draw a line of best fit and determine the gradient $\Delta F / \Delta L$.
4. Multiply the gradient by $L/A$ to obtain the Young Modulus.

**Worked example.** A student adds 100 g masses starting from 200 g, measuring extensions from 0.1 to 2.1 $\times 10^{-3}$ m. Additional data: $L = 1.382$ m, three diameter readings of 0.277, 0.280, and 0.275 mm give an average $d = 0.277$ mm, so $A = \pi (0.277 \times 10^{-3})^2 / 4 = 6.03 \times 10^{-8}$ m$^2$. From the force-extension graph, the gradient is:

$$\frac{\Delta F}{\Delta L} = \frac{7.75 - 2.125}{2 \times 10^{-3} - 0} = 2812.5 \text{ N m}^{-1}$$

⚠ Check: the source labels the lower force value as both "2.215" (in the formula) and "2.125" (on the graph annotation); the graph annotation of 2.125 appears consistent with the plotted line, and 2.125/2 rounded gives the "2" label shown. The numerical answer is unaffected at the stated precision.

$$E = 2812.5 \times \frac{1.382}{6.03 \times 10^{-8}} = 6.4 \times 10^{10} \text{ Pa}$$

### Evaluating the Experiment

**Systematic errors:**

A **vernier scale** can be used instead of a plain ruler for more precise extension readings, producing a more accurate value. If the wire is extended past its **elastic limit**, it will be permanently deformed. To avoid this, remove the load after each measurement and check the wire returns to its original length before continuing.

**Random errors:**

**Parallax error** can occur when reading the marker position against the ruler. Repeating the experiment for all loads and averaging the extension reduces random error. Measuring the diameter at several places along the wire and averaging reduces the uncertainty in the cross-sectional area.

### Safety Considerations

Wear **safety goggles** at all times in case the wire snaps. Place a **cushion or soft surface** directly below the mass hanger in case it falls off.

**Exam tip:** You will be expected to suggest improvements for more accurate and reliable results, such as repeating readings and using a longer length of wire (which produces larger, more measurable extensions for the same strain).

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Young Modulus | $E = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L}$ | Units: Pa |
| Tensile stress | $\sigma = \frac{F}{A}$ | Units: Pa |
| Tensile strain | $\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$ | Dimensionless |
| Cross-sectional area | $A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4}$ | From diameter $d$ |
| Young Modulus (from graph) | $E = \text{gradient of stress-strain graph}$ | Linear region only |
| Young Modulus (from practical) | $E = \text{gradient of } F\text{-}\Delta L \text{ graph} \times \frac{L}{A}$ | Gradient $= \Delta F / \Delta L$ |
| Energy per unit volume | Area under linear region of stress-strain graph | In Hooke's Law region |
