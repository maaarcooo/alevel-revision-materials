# Circuits & The Potential Divider

## Resistors in Series & Parallel

### Resistors in Series

When two or more components are connected in series, **the combined resistance is equal to the sum of the individual resistances**:

$$R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 \ldots$$

Each resistor simply adds to the total, so the combined resistance is always greater than any single resistor in the chain.

**Method example:** given $R_{total} = 60 \, \Omega$ with $R_1 = 30 \, \Omega$ and $R_3 = 10 \, \Omega$, rearrange to find $R_2 = R_{total} - R_1 - R_3 = 60 - 30 - 10 = 20 \, \Omega$.

### Resistors in Parallel

When two or more components are connected in parallel, **the reciprocal of the combined resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistances**:

$$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} \ldots$$

Adding more resistors in parallel always decreases the combined resistance, making it less than the smallest individual resistance. If two resistors of equal resistance $R$ are connected in parallel, the combined resistance is $\frac{R}{2}$.

**Method example:** three resistors $R$, $2R$, and $R$ in parallel give $\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R} + \frac{1}{2R} + \frac{1}{R} = \frac{5}{2R}$. Taking the reciprocal: $R_T = \frac{2R}{5}$. The critical final step is to flip the fraction, since the equation gives $\frac{1}{R_T}$, not $R_T$ directly.

**Common error:** forgetting to take the reciprocal at the end. The parallel equation yields $\frac{1}{R_T}$, so to obtain $R_T$ you must calculate $1 \div \text{your answer}$. On a calculator, use the $x^{-1}$ or $\frac{1}{x}$ button. The reciprocal of a value is $\frac{1}{\text{value}}$, and for a fraction you swap numerator and denominator.

## Series & Parallel Circuits

### Current

In a **series** circuit, the current is the **same** through all components.

In a **parallel** circuit, the current splits across different branches at a **junction**. The total current into a junction equals the total current out of that junction. The amount of current in each branch depends on the total resistance of the components within that branch.

### Potential Difference

In a **series** circuit, the **e.m.f** of the power supply is shared among all components in different amounts depending on their resistance.

In a **parallel** circuit, the voltage across all components in each branch equals the e.m.f of the power supply.

### Cells in Series & Parallel

Cells connected in **series**: the total voltage is the sum of the p.d. across each cell (e.g. three 2 V cells in series give 6 V).

Cells connected in **parallel**: the total voltage across the arrangement equals the voltage of a single cell (e.g. three 2 V cells in parallel give 2 V).

### Summary: Series vs Parallel

| Quantity | Series | Parallel |
|---|---|---|
| Voltage | $V_{in} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$ | $V_{in} = V_1 = V_2 = V_3$ |
| Current | $I_{in} = I_1 = I_2 = I_3$ | $I_{in} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$ |
| Resistance | $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$ | $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}$ |

## Conservation of Charge & Energy

### Conservation of Charge (Kirchhoff's First Law)

Charge is never used up or lost in a circuit. As a consequence, **the sum of the currents entering a junction always equals the sum of the currents leaving the junction**. This is **Kirchhoff's First Law**.

$$I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$$

A **junction** is a point where at least three circuit paths meet. A **branch** is a path connecting two junctions.

In a series circuit, the current is identical at every point. In a parallel circuit, the current divides at each junction, with each branch carrying a different current determined by its resistance. Kirchhoff's First Law applies at every junction independently.

**Method example (ammeter readings):** if a cell delivers 0.270 A (= 270 mA) and the circuit splits into two parallel branches, one carrying 80 mA which itself subdivides into 27 mA and $A_2$, then by Kirchhoff's First Law at that sub-junction: $A_2 = 80 - 27 = 53$ mA. At the main junction: $A_3 = 270 - 80 = 190$ mA. The ammeter in series with the cell reads the same as the cell current: $A_1 = 270$ mA.

### Conservation of Energy (Kirchhoff's Second Law)

Energy is never used up or lost in a circuit. As a consequence, **the total e.m.f. in a closed circuit equals the sum of the potential differences across each component**. This is **Kirchhoff's Second Law**.

$$E_1 + E_2 = V_1 + V_2$$

Each closed loop in a circuit can be treated as an independent series circuit. In a series circuit, the voltage is split across components in proportion to their resistance, and the sum of the voltages equals the total e.m.f. In a parallel circuit, each closed loop independently satisfies Kirchhoff's Second Law, so the voltage across each branch equals the supply e.m.f.

A **closed-circuit loop** acts as its own independent series circuit. A parallel circuit is made up of two or more such loops, each separating at a junction.

**Method example (voltmeter readings):** in a circuit with a 24 V supply, a 2 V lamp, and a 12 V lamp in one loop, Kirchhoff's Second Law gives $V_1 = 24 - 2 - 12 = 10$ V. In a different loop containing 2 V + 8 V + two identical lamps, the total p.d. for the two lamps is $24 - 2 - 8 = 14$ V, shared equally ($V_2 = 7$ V each) since they have the same resistance and carry the same current. In a third loop with just 2 V and $V_3$: $V_3 = 24 - 2 = 22$ V.

### Practical Importance

Parallel circuits are widely used in home wiring because a single power source supplies all lights and appliances with the same voltage, and if one component fails, current and voltage continue to flow through the remaining components.

**Exam tip:** annotate circuit diagrams with current direction arrows (positive to negative) and known values at every junction. Common mistakes include assuming current is the same in every parallel branch, assuming voltage is the same across every series component, or failing to check that voltages sum to the supply e.m.f.

## Electrical Energy & Power

### Energy & Power

In mechanics, **power** $P$ is defined as the **rate of doing work**. Since **potential difference** is the **work done per unit charge** and **current** is the **rate of flow of charge**, electrical power is the **rate of change of work done**:

$$P = \frac{E}{t} = \frac{W}{t}$$

where $P$ is power (W), $E$ or $W$ is energy or work done (J), and $t$ is time (s).

Power can also be expressed as:

$$P = IV$$

where $I$ is current (A) and $V$ is potential difference (V).

Substituting $V = IR$ (Ohm's Law) gives two further forms:

$$P = I^2 R \qquad \qquad P = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

For a given resistance, if the current or voltage doubles, the power increases by a factor of four (because of the squared term). Which form to use depends on whether current or voltage is given in the question.

### Energy Transferred

Rearranging gives the energy transferred in a component:

$$E = VIt$$

where $E$ is energy transferred (J), $V$ is voltage (V), $I$ is current (A), and $t$ is time (s).

**Method example:** two lamps connected in series to a 150 V supply, one rated 41 W at 135 V and the other 4.5 W at 15 V. Using $I = \frac{P}{V}$: lamp 1 needs $\frac{41}{135} = 0.3$ A and lamp 2 needs $\frac{4.5}{15} = 0.3$ A. Both require the same current, and since they are in series the same current flows through both, so both light at their normal brightness.

**Exam tip:** the mnemonic "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star, Power equals $I$ squared $R$" helps recall whether to multiply or divide by resistance. Always ensure time is in seconds when working in watts.

## Potential Divider Circuits

### Potential Divider Circuit

When two resistors are connected in series, Kirchhoff's Second Law means the p.d. of the power source is divided between them. A **potential divider** is a circuit that produces an **output voltage as a fraction of its input voltage**.

Potential dividers have three main purposes: to provide a variable potential difference, to select a specific potential difference, and to split the p.d. of a power source between two or more components. They are used widely in volume controls and sensory circuits using LDRs and thermistors.

The **potential divider equation** relates the output voltage to the input voltage and the two resistances:

$$V_{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \, V_{in}$$

This can equivalently be written as $V_{out} = \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2} \, V_{in}$ depending on which resistor $V_{out}$ is measured across. The key rule is that **the numerator must be the resistance of the resistor across which** $V_{out}$ **is taken**.

The input voltage $V_{in}$ is applied across the full series combination. The output voltage $V_{out}$ is measured across one of the resistors. Since both resistors carry the same current $I$ (series circuit), the p.d. across each is proportional to its resistance ($V = IR$). The resistor with the **larger resistance** gets a **greater share** of the p.d. If one resistor's resistance increases, it takes a greater share of the total p.d. and the other resistor's share decreases.

**Method example:** given $R_1 = 20 \, \text{k}\Omega$, $R_2 = 12 \, \text{k}\Omega$, and $V_{out} = 5.3$ V measured across $R_1$, rearranging the potential divider equation: $V_{in} = V_{out} \times \frac{R_1 + R_2}{R_1} = 5.3 \times \frac{32}{20} = 8.48 \approx 8.5$ V (2 s.f.).

### Variable Resistance Components

Variable and sensory resistors are used in potential dividers to vary the output voltage, which can cause an external component to switch on or off (e.g. a heater switching off at room temperature).

**Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs):** resistance decreases as light intensity increases (and vice versa). Used in street lights and security lights.

**Thermistors** (NTC): resistance decreases as temperature increases (and vice versa). Used in fire alarms, ovens, and digital thermometers.

From $V = IR$, the p.d. across a component in a potential divider is proportional to its resistance. So if the resistance of an LDR or thermistor decreases, the p.d. across it also decreases, and if its resistance increases, the p.d. across it increases. Since the total p.d. must equal $V_{in}$ (Kirchhoff's Second Law), a decrease in the sensory resistor's p.d. causes an increase in the fixed resistor's p.d., and vice versa.

**Method example:** a thermistor and fixed resistor $R$ in series. When the temperature decreases, the thermistor's resistance increases, so its p.d. increases. The p.d. across $R$ must therefore decrease to keep the total equal to the supply e.m.f.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 \ldots$ | Combined resistance in series |
| $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} \ldots$ | Combined resistance in parallel |
| $I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$ | Kirchhoff's First Law (current at a junction) |
| $\Sigma \, \varepsilon = \Sigma \, V$ | Kirchhoff's Second Law (e.m.f. = sum of p.d.s in a loop) |
| $P = \frac{E}{t} = \frac{W}{t}$ | Power as rate of energy transfer |
| $P = IV$ | Electrical power |
| $P = I^2 R$ | Power in terms of current and resistance |
| $P = \frac{V^2}{R}$ | Power in terms of voltage and resistance |
| $E = VIt$ | Energy transferred in a component |
| $V_{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \, V_{in}$ | Potential divider equation |
