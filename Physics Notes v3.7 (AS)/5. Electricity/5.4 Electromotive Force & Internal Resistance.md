# Electromotive Force & Internal Resistance

## Electromotive Force

When charge passes through a power supply such as a battery, it **gains** electrical energy.

**Electromotive force (e.m.f)** is defined as the amount of chemical energy converted to electrical energy per coulomb of charge when passing through a power supply:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{E}{Q}$$

where $\varepsilon$ = e.m.f (V), $E$ = energy transferred (J), and $Q$ = charge (C).

E.m.f is represented by the symbol $\varepsilon$ (Greek letter epsilon). Despite its name, it is not a force. It is measured in **volts (V)**.

E.m.f is equal to the potential difference across the cell when no current is flowing. It can be measured by connecting a high-resistance voltmeter across the terminals of the cell in an open circuit (voltmeter in parallel with the cell, no external load connected).

### Terminal Potential Difference

The **terminal potential difference (p.d.)** is the potential difference across the terminals of a cell. It is given by Ohm's law:

$$V = IR$$

where $V$ = terminal p.d. (V), $I$ = current (A), and $R$ = load resistance ($\Omega$).

If there were no internal resistance, the terminal p.d. would equal the e.m.f. In practice, since a cell has internal resistance, the terminal p.d. is always **lower** than the e.m.f.

### Lost Volts

In a closed circuit, current flows through the cell and a potential difference develops across the internal resistance. Since resistance opposes current, this reduces the energy per unit charge (voltage) available to the rest of the external circuit. This difference is called the **lost volts**, represented by lowercase $v$.

Lost volts is defined as the work done per unit charge to overcome the internal resistance inside the battery when current flows. It is the voltage lost in the cell due to internal resistance.

From conservation of energy:

$$v = \varepsilon - V = Ir$$

where $v$ = lost volts (V), $I$ = current (A), $r$ = internal resistance ($\Omega$), $\varepsilon$ = e.m.f (V), and $V$ = terminal p.d. (V).

### The E.M.F Equation

The e.m.f is the sum of the terminal p.d. and the lost volts:

$$\varepsilon = IR + Ir = I(R + r)$$

where $R$ = resistance of the external load ($\Omega$) and $r$ = internal resistance ($\Omega$). The term $IR$ represents the terminal p.d. (voltage across the load) and $Ir$ represents the lost volts (voltage across the internal resistance).

E.m.f can therefore be defined as the **total**, or **maximum**, voltage available to the circuit.

**Inline example:** A battery has $\varepsilon = 7.3$ V and $r = 0.3\;\Omega$, connected to a $9.5\;\Omega$ load. The current is $I = \frac{\varepsilon}{R + r} = \frac{7.3}{9.5 + 0.3} = 0.74\text{ A}$ (2 s.f.: 0.7 A). The lost volts are $v = Ir = 0.7 \times 0.3 = 0.2$ V (2 s.f.).

> **Exam tip:** If a question states "a battery of negligible internal resistance", the e.m.f equals the terminal voltage and no internal resistance calculations are needed. If the circuit diagram explicitly includes internal resistance, use the e.m.f equations.

## Internal Resistance

All power supplies have some resistance between their terminals. This is called **internal resistance** ($r$), defined as the resistance of the materials within the battery.

Internal resistance causes the charge circulating to dissipate some electrical energy from the power supply itself. This is why a cell becomes warm after a period of use. Over time, internal resistance causes a **loss of voltage** (energy loss) in the power supply.

### Circuit Model

A cell can be modelled as a source of e.m.f with an internal resistance connected in series. In such a circuit:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $R$ | Load resistor (external resistance) |
| $r$ | Internal resistance of the cell |
| $\varepsilon$ | E.m.f of the cell |
| $V_r$ | Lost volts (p.d. across $r$) |
| $V_R$ | P.d. across the load resistor (= terminal p.d.) |

The cell and internal resistance are enclosed within a dashed box in circuit diagrams to show they are part of the same component. The voltmeter reads the terminal p.d. $V_R$ across the external load, while the lost volts $V_r$ drop across $r$ inside the cell.

## Required Practical: Investigating EMF & Internal Resistance

### Aims

Investigate the relationship between e.m.f and internal resistance by measuring the variation of current and voltage using a variable resistor.

| Variable type | Variable |
|---|---|
| Independent | Resistance, $R$ ($\Omega$) |
| Dependent | Voltage, $V$ (V) and current, $I$ (A) |
| Control | E.m.f of the cell, internal resistance of the cell |

### Equipment

| Apparatus | Purpose |
|---|---|
| 1.5 V Cell | Provide an e.m.f to the circuit |
| Resistor (unknown value) | Act as internal resistance |
| 100 $\Omega$ Variable Resistor | Change the values of current and voltage in the circuit |
| Voltmeter (0--2 V range) | Measure voltage |
| Ammeter (0--200 mA range) | Measure current |
| Wires (at least 6 leads) | Make electrical connections |
| Switch | Open between readings to avoid running down the battery |

**Resolution of measuring equipment:** Voltmeter = 1 mV, Ammeter = 0.1 mA.

### Method

The circuit consists of the cell and the resistor labelled $r$ connected in series (treated as a single cell), in series with a switch, an ammeter, and a variable resistor $R$. A voltmeter is connected in parallel across $R$.

1. With the switch open, record the voltmeter reading $V$ (this gives the e.m.f directly since no current flows).
2. Set the variable resistor to its maximum value, close the switch, and record $V$ and $I$ from the ammeter. Open the switch between readings.
3. Vary the resistance of the variable resistor to obtain a minimum of 8--10 pairs of readings for $V$ and $I$, covering the whole range of the variable resistor.
4. Take 3 repeat readings at each resistance setting to calculate mean values of $V$ and $I$.

### Analysing the Results

Starting from $\varepsilon = I(R + r)$, expand and rearrange for $V$:

$$\varepsilon = IR + Ir = V + Ir$$

$$V = -rI + \varepsilon$$

Comparing to $y = mx + c$:

| Straight-line term | Physical quantity |
|---|---|
| $y$ | $V$ (V) |
| $x$ | $I$ (A) |
| Gradient ($m$) | $-r$ ($\Omega$) |
| $y$-intercept ($c$) | $\varepsilon$ (V) |

Plot a graph of $V$ (y-axis) against $I$ (x-axis) and draw a line of best fit. The $y$-intercept gives the e.m.f directly. The gradient is the negative of the internal resistance, so $r = -\text{gradient}$.

**Inline example:** From a dataset where the line of best fit passes through $(0,\;1.60)$ and $(66 \times 10^{-3},\;0.10)$, the gradient is $\frac{1.6 - 0.1}{0 - 66 \times 10^{-3}} = -22.7\;\Omega$ (3 s.f.). Therefore $\varepsilon = 1.60$ V and $r = 22.7\;\Omega$.

### Evaluating the Experiment

**Systematic errors:**
Only close the switch for as long as it takes to record each pair of readings. This prevents the internal resistance of the battery from changing during the experiment (prolonged current flow heats the cell and alters $r$).

**Random errors:**
Use fairly new cells, since the e.m.f and internal resistance of run-down batteries can vary during the experiment. Wait for the voltmeter and ammeter readings to stabilise before recording. Take at least 3 repeat readings for each voltage and current, and calculate a mean to reduce random errors.

### Safety Considerations

This is a low-risk experiment. Electrical components can get hot when used for prolonged periods. Switch off the power supply immediately if burning is smelled. Keep liquids away from the electrical equipment.

## Key Equations Summary

| Equation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $\varepsilon = \dfrac{E}{Q}$ | E.m.f = energy transferred per unit charge |
| $V = IR$ | Terminal p.d. (Ohm's law for external circuit) |
| $v = \varepsilon - V = Ir$ | Lost volts across internal resistance |
| $\varepsilon = IR + Ir = I(R + r)$ | E.m.f equation (conservation of energy in full circuit) |
| $V = -rI + \varepsilon$ | Rearranged form for straight-line graph analysis |
