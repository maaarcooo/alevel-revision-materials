# Measurements and their Errors

## 3.1.1 - Uses of SI Units and their Prefixes

**SI units** are the **fundamental** units of measurement. There are six SI base quantities:

| Quantity | Symbol | SI Unit |
|---|---|---|
| Mass | $m$ | kg (kilograms) |
| Length | $l$ | m (metres) |
| Time | $t$ | s (seconds) |
| Amount of substance | $n$ | mol (moles) |
| Temperature | $T$ | K (kelvin) |
| Electric current | $I$ | A (amperes) |

The SI units of any quantity can be **derived** from its defining equation.

**Example: Force.** $F = ma$, so the SI unit of force is $\text{kg} \times \text{m s}^{-2} = \text{kg m s}^{-2}$ (also known as N).

**Example: Voltage.** $V = \frac{E}{Q}$. Energy has SI units $\text{kg m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$ (from $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$). Charge $Q = It$ has units As. Therefore $V = \frac{\text{kg m}^2\text{s}^{-2}}{\text{As}} = \text{kg m}^2\text{s}^{-3}\text{A}^{-1}$.

### Prefixes

| Name | Symbol | Multiplier |
|---|---|---|
| Tera | T | $10^{12}$ |
| Giga | G | $10^{9}$ |
| Mega | M | $10^{6}$ |
| Kilo | k | $10^{3}$ |
| Centi | c | $10^{-2}$ |
| Milli | m | $10^{-3}$ |
| Micro | $\mu$ | $10^{-6}$ |
| Nano | n | $10^{-9}$ |
| Pico | p | $10^{-12}$ |
| Femto | f | $10^{-15}$ |

**Examples:** 6 pF (picofarads) = $6 \times 10^{-12}$ F. 9 G$\Omega$ (gigaohms) = $9 \times 10^{9}$ $\Omega$. 10 $\mu$m (micrometres) = $10 \times 10^{-6}$ m.

### Converting Electron Volts to Joules

$$1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**Worked example:** Convert 76 MeV to joules. First convert MeV to eV: $76 \times 10^{6}$ eV. Then convert to joules: $76 \times 10^{6} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} = 1.216 \times 10^{-11}$ J.

### Converting kWh to Joules

1 kW = 1000 J/s and 1 hour = 3600 s, so:

$$1 \text{ kWh} = 1000 \times 3600 = 3.6 \times 10^{6} \text{ J} = 3.6 \text{ MJ}$$

## 3.1.2 - Limitation of Physical Measurements

### Random and Systematic Errors

**Random errors** cause unpredictable variation between measurements, producing a spread of values about the true value. They affect **precision**. Random errors cannot be entirely eliminated.

An example is **electronic noise** in the circuit of an electrical instrument.

To reduce random errors:

- Take **at least 3 repeats** and calculate a **mean**, which also allows **anomalies** to be identified.
- Use **computers, data loggers, or cameras** to reduce human error and enable smaller measurement intervals.
- Use **appropriate equipment** with higher resolution (e.g. a micrometer at 0.1 mm resolution rather than a ruler at 1 mm).

> ⚠ Check: source states micrometer resolution as 0.1 mm; standard micrometer screw gauges have 0.01 mm resolution. 0.1 mm is the resolution of vernier calipers.

**Systematic errors** cause all readings to differ from the true value by a fixed amount (always too high or always too low). They affect **accuracy** and arise from faults in the apparatus or experimental method. Systematic errors cannot be corrected by repeat readings.

Examples include a **zero error** (a balance not zeroed correctly) and **parallax error** (reading a scale at the wrong angle).

To reduce systematic errors:

- **Calibrate** apparatus by measuring a known value (e.g. weigh a 1 kg standard mass). If the reading is wrong, the systematic error is identified.
- In radiation experiments, correct for **background radiation** by measuring it beforehand and subtracting it from results.
- Read the **meniscus** at eye level to reduce parallax error, and use **controls** in experiments.

### Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | How close a measurement is to the **true value** |
| **Precision** | How close measurements are to the **mean value**; indicates the magnitude of random errors, not closeness to the true value |
| **Repeatability** | The same experimenter, using the same method and equipment, obtains the same results |
| **Reproducibility** | A different experimenter, using a different method and equipment, obtains the same results |
| **Resolution** | The smallest change in a quantity that produces a recognisable change in the reading of an instrument |

### Uncertainty

The **uncertainty** of a measurement is the bounds within which the true value is expected to lie, e.g. $20°\text{C} \pm 2°\text{C}$ means the true value lies between 18°C and 22°C.

**Absolute uncertainty:** given as a fixed quantity, e.g. $7 \pm 0.6$ V.

**Fractional uncertainty:** given as a fraction of the measurement, e.g. $7 \pm \frac{3}{35}$ V.

**Percentage uncertainty:** given as a percentage of the measurement, e.g. $7 \pm 8.6\%$ V.

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{uncertainty}}{\text{value}} \times 100$$

To reduce percentage and fractional uncertainty, measure **larger quantities**.

### Resolution and Uncertainty

A **reading** is a single value obtained from an instrument (e.g. reading a thermometer). A **measurement** is the difference between two readings (e.g. using a ruler, where both the start and end points are judged).

- **Uncertainty in a reading** = $\pm$ half the smallest division. Example: a thermometer with smallest division 1°C has uncertainty $\pm 0.5$°C.
- **Uncertainty in a measurement** = at least $\pm 1$ smallest division (because each end contributes $\pm$ half a division, and these are added). Example: a ruler measurement has uncertainty $\pm 1$ mm.

**Digital readings** and given values have uncertainty equal to $\pm$ the last significant digit (e.g. $3.2 \pm 0.1$ V), unless quoted otherwise. The **resolution** of the instrument determines its uncertainty.

**Repeated data:** the uncertainty is half the range, expressed as:

$$\text{mean} \pm \frac{\text{range}}{2}$$

where range = largest value $-$ smallest value.

### Reducing Uncertainty

- **Fix one end** of a ruler so only one reading contributes uncertainty, rather than two.
- **Measure multiple instances** together. For example, time 10 swings of a pendulum to get $6.2 \pm 0.1$ s, then the time per swing is $0.62 \pm 0.01$ s (the uncertainty is also divided by 10).

Uncertainties should be given to the **same number of significant figures** as the data.

### Combining Uncertainties

**Adding or subtracting data: add absolute uncertainties.**

Worked example: A thermometer ($\pm 0.5$ K) reads a temperature drop from $298 \pm 0.5$ K to $273 \pm 0.5$ K. The difference is $298 - 273 = 25$ K. The combined uncertainty is $0.5 + 0.5 = 1$ K, so the result is $25 \pm 1$ K.

**Multiplying or dividing data: add percentage uncertainties.**

Worked example: A force of $91 \pm 3$ N is applied to a mass of $7 \pm 0.2$ kg. $a = F/m = 91/7 = 13$ m s$^{-2}$. Percentage uncertainties: $\frac{3}{91} \times 100 = 3.3\%$ and $\frac{0.2}{7} \times 100 = 2.9\%$. Combined: $3.3\% + 2.9\% = 6.2\%$. $6.2\%$ of 13 is 0.8, so $a = 13 \pm 0.8$ m s$^{-2}$.

**Raising to a power: multiply percentage uncertainty by the power.**

Worked example: A circle has radius $5 \pm 0.3$ cm. Area $= \pi r^2 = \pi \times 25 = 78.5$ cm$^2$. Percentage uncertainty in radius: $\frac{0.3}{5} \times 100 = 6\%$. Since area depends on $r^2$, the percentage uncertainty in area is $6\% \times 2 = 12\%$. So area $= 78.5 \pm 12\%$ cm$^2$.

### Uncertainties and Graphs

Uncertainties are represented as **error bars** on graphs. If the uncertainty is 5 mm, the error bar extends 5 squares on either side of the plotted point.

A **line of best fit** should pass through all error bars, excluding **anomalous points**.

**Uncertainty in a gradient** is found using lines of best and worst fit:

1. Draw the **steepest** and **shallowest** lines of worst fit, each passing through all error bars.
2. Calculate the gradient of both the best fit and worst fit lines.
3. The uncertainty is the difference between the best and worst gradients:

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty in gradient} = \frac{|\text{best gradient} - \text{worst gradient}|}{\text{best gradient}} \times 100\%$$

The modulus ensures the value is always positive.

**Uncertainty in the y-intercept** (when best and worst lines have different intercepts):

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty in y-intercept} = \frac{|\text{best y-intercept} - \text{worst y-intercept}|}{\text{best y-intercept}} \times 100\%$$

**Alternative method:** the source states the percentage uncertainty can also be calculated as:

$$\text{Percentage uncertainty} = \frac{\text{max gradient} - \text{min gradient}}{2} \times 100\%$$

> ⚠ Check: this formula as written gives half the range of gradients multiplied by 100%, not a true percentage uncertainty. The standard treatment divides by the best gradient: $\frac{(\text{max gradient} - \text{min gradient})/2}{\text{best gradient}} \times 100\%$.

## 3.1.3 - Estimation of Physical Quantities

**Orders of magnitude** are powers of ten used to describe or compare the size of objects.

Example: The diameter of nuclei has an order of magnitude of about $10^{-14}$ m. 100 m is two orders of magnitude greater than 1 m.

To give a value to the **nearest order of magnitude**, calculate the quantity and express it only as a power of ten.

Worked example: The diameter of a hydrogen atom is $1.06 \times 10^{-10}$ m. Find the approximate area of the atom (assuming spherical) to the nearest order of magnitude. $A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times (0.53 \times 10^{-10})^2 = 8.82 \times 10^{-21}$ m$^2$ $= 1 \times 10^{-20}$ m$^2$ (to 1 s.f.). The area to the nearest order of magnitude is $10^{-20}$ m$^2$.

**Estimation** is a skill physicists use to approximate the values of physical quantities, to make comparisons, or to check whether a calculated value is reasonable.
