# Section 5: Electricity

## 3.5.1 Current Electricity

### 3.5.1.1 Basics of Electricity

**Electric current** ($I$) is the rate of flow of charge in a circuit:

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$$

where $Q$ is charge (C) and $t$ is time (s).

**Potential difference** ($V$) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points in a circuit:

$$V = \frac{W}{Q}$$

where $W$ is the energy transferred (J) and $Q$ is charge (C).

**Resistance** ($R$) is a measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a material. It is calculated by dividing the potential difference across a component by the current flowing through it:

$$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

---

### 3.5.1.2 Current-Voltage Characteristics

**Ohm's law** states that for an **ohmic conductor**, current is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided physical conditions (e.g. temperature) are kept constant. The constant of proportionality is resistance.

You must be able to recognise and interpret current-voltage (I-V) graphs for the following components (the axes may be plotted either way round):

**Ohmic conductor:** The I-V graph is a straight line through the origin, confirming direct proportionality between $I$ and $V$. This holds only while physical conditions remain constant.

**Semiconductor diode:** The I-V graph must be considered in two regions. In **forward bias**, negligible current flows until the **threshold voltage** is reached (the minimum voltage needed to allow current to flow); beyond this point current rises steeply. In **reverse bias**, the resistance of the diode is extremely high so only a very small current can flow.

**Filament lamp:** The filament is a metal wire that heats up as current increases. At very low currents the wire has not heated significantly, so Ohm's law is approximately obeyed and the graph is roughly linear. As current increases (in either direction), the wire's temperature rises, its resistance increases, and the graph curves away from a straight line. The graph is symmetric about the origin.

**Ammeter and voltmeter assumptions:** Unless a question states otherwise, an **ammeter** is modelled as having zero resistance (so it does not affect the current it measures) and a **voltmeter** is modelled as having infinite resistance (so no current flows through it and its measurement of p.d. is exact).

---

### 3.5.1.3 Resistivity

**Resistivity** ($\rho$) is a property of a material that quantifies how easily it conducts electricity. It is defined as the product of resistance and cross-sectional area divided by the length of the material:

$$\rho = \frac{RA}{L}$$

Resistivity gives the resistance of a specimen of length 1 m and cross-sectional area 1 m$^2$, making it useful for comparing different materials regardless of their physical dimensions. Resistivity depends on environmental factors such as temperature.

**Effect of temperature on metals:** When the temperature of a metal conductor increases, its resistance increases. The metal atoms gain kinetic energy and vibrate more, causing the charge carriers (electrons) to collide with atoms more frequently. This slows the electrons, reducing current and therefore increasing resistance (since $R = V/I$).

**Thermistors:** A **thermistor** is a temperature-sensitive semiconductor whose resistance decreases as temperature increases. This is because increasing temperature causes electrons to be emitted from atoms, increasing the number of charge carriers. The greater number of carriers increases the current, so resistance decreases. The resistance-temperature graph of a thermistor shows a steep decrease that levels off at higher temperatures.

One application of a thermistor is as a **temperature sensor** in a circuit, which can trigger an event once the temperature drops to or reaches a certain value, for example turning on heating when room temperature falls below a set point.

**Light Dependent Resistor (LDR):** An **LDR** is a light-sensitive semiconductor whose resistance decreases as light intensity increases (and increases as light intensity decreases).

**Superconductors:** A **superconductor** is a material that has zero resistivity when its temperature is at or below a characteristic value called the **critical temperature**. The critical temperature depends on the material; most known superconductors have critical temperatures close to 0 K ($-273\,°\text{C}$). Since resistivity is zero, resistance is also zero. Applications of superconductors include:

- **Power cables** that would eliminate energy loss through heating during transmission.
- **Strong magnetic fields** that would not require a constant power source. These could be used in maglev trains (removing friction between train and rail) and in medical applications.

### 3.5.1.4 Circuits

**Resistors in series:** The total resistance is the sum of the individual resistances:

$$R_T = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots$$

**Resistors in parallel:** The reciprocal of the total resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistances:

$$\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots$$

Both rules may be needed in a single circuit. For example, a circuit with a 4 $\Omega$ and 16 $\Omega$ resistor in parallel, connected in series with a 10 $\Omega$ and 3 $\Omega$ resistor:

1. Parallel combination: $\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{5}{16}$, so $R_T = 3.2\;\Omega$.
2. Series total: $R_T = 10 + 3 + 3.2 = 16.2\;\Omega$.

**Power** ($P$) is the rate of transfer of energy:

$$P = \frac{E}{t}$$

where $E$ is energy transferred (J) and $t$ is time (s). Combining $P = VI$ with $V = IR$ gives three equivalent forms:

$$P = VI = \frac{V^2}{R} = I^2 R$$

Since power is energy per unit time, the energy transferred is:

$$E = VIt$$

**Worked method (power and energy):** A lamp rated at 60 W is connected to a 240 V supply. Energy transferred in 2 minutes: $E = Pt = 60 \times 120 = 7200\;\text{J}$. Current through the lamp: $I = P/V = 60/240 = 0.25\;\text{A}$.

**Series circuit rules:**

- The current is the same at all points in the circuit.
- The supply p.d. is shared across all elements; the total sum of the voltages across all elements equals the supply p.d.

**Parallel circuit rules:**

- The sum of the currents in each set of parallel branches equals the total current.
- The potential difference across each branch is the same.

**Cells in series:** The total voltage is the sum of the individual cell voltages: $V_T = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 + \ldots$

**Identical cells in parallel:** The total voltage equals the voltage of one cell, because the current is split equally between branches: $V_T = V_1 = V_2 = V_3 = \ldots$

**Kirchhoff's laws** describe charge and energy conservation in DC circuits:

- **Kirchhoff's first law:** The total current flowing into a junction equals the total current flowing out of that junction (conservation of charge).
- **Kirchhoff's second law:** The sum of all the voltages around a closed loop in a series circuit equals the supply voltage (conservation of energy).

### 3.5.1.5 Potential Divider

A **potential divider** is a circuit with two or more resistors in series connected across a voltage source, used to produce a required fraction of the source potential difference. The source p.d. is split in the ratio of the resistances: for two resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$ in series across a source of voltage $V_S$, the output p.d. across $R_2$ is:

$$V_{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \times V_S$$

*(Beyond source: the formula $V_{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \times V_S$ is not stated explicitly in the source but follows directly from the source's statement that p.d. is split in the ratio of resistances, combined with $V = IR$.)*

A **variable potential divider** uses a variable resistor for one of the series resistors. By varying its resistance, the output p.d. can be varied. For example, if the resistance across $R_1$ increases, total resistance increases, circuit current decreases, and $V_{out} = IR_2$ decreases.

**Sensor circuits:** Replacing the variable resistor with a thermistor or LDR creates a sensor circuit.

- **LDR as $R_1$:** If light intensity falls, $R_1$ (LDR resistance) increases, so total resistance increases, circuit current decreases, and the p.d. across $R_2$ (i.e. $V_{out}$) decreases.

> ⚠ Check: source states "resistance across R₂ decreases" when describing this effect; standard treatment is that $R_2$ is a fixed resistor so its resistance does not change — it is the p.d. across $R_2$ that decreases due to the reduced circuit current.

- **Swapping positions:** If the positions of the LDR and fixed resistor are swapped (so $V_{out}$ is now across the LDR), then $V_{out}$ increases as light intensity decreases. This arrangement could trigger a light bulb to switch on when a threshold voltage is reached.

- **Thermistor as $R_1$:** Similarly, a thermistor can be used to create a temperature-dependent output voltage, triggering events when temperature crosses a set value.

### 3.5.1.6 Electromotive Force and Internal Resistance

Batteries have an **internal resistance** ($r$), caused by electrons colliding with atoms inside the battery. This means some energy is dissipated within the source before charge even leaves the battery. Internal resistance is represented as a small resistor inside the battery symbol in circuit diagrams.

**Electromotive force** (emf, $\varepsilon$) is the energy transferred by a cell per coulomb of charge that passes through it:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{E}{Q}$$

The total resistance in a circuit with internal resistance is the sum of the load resistance ($R$) and internal resistance ($r$):

$$R_T = R + r$$

Since $V = IR$, the emf is the product of the total resistance and the circuit current:

$$\varepsilon = IR + Ir = I(R + r)$$

The p.d. across the external load resistance $R$ is the **terminal p.d.** ($V = IR$). The p.d. across the internal resistance $r$ is the **lost volts** ($v = Ir$), representing the energy wasted within the cell per coulomb of charge.

Therefore:

$$\varepsilon = V + v$$

**Measuring emf:** The emf of a battery can be measured by connecting a voltmeter across the cell when no current is flowing (open circuit). In this case $v = Ir = 0$, so the voltmeter reading equals $\varepsilon$.

**Worked method 1 (finding current):** A cell has emf = 5 V, lost volts = 2 V, and $R = 10\;\Omega$.

- Terminal p.d.: $V = \varepsilon - v = 5 - 2 = 3\;\text{V}$.
- Current: $I = V/R = 3/10 = 0.3\;\text{A}$.

**Worked method 2 (finding lost volts and internal resistance):** A cell has emf = 10 V, current = 2 A, $R_1 = 3\;\Omega$ and $R_2 = 0.5\;\Omega$.

> ⚠ Check: source text states $R_1 = 3.5\;\Omega$ but the calculation and circuit diagram both use $R_1 = 3\;\Omega$. The working below follows the calculation as given in the source (using $R_1 = 3\;\Omega$).

- Terminal p.d.: $V_1 = IR_1 = 2 \times 3 = 6\;\text{V}$, $V_2 = IR_2 = 2 \times 0.5 = 1\;\text{V}$, so $V = 6 + 1 = 7\;\text{V}$.
- Lost volts: $v = \varepsilon - V = 10 - 7 = 3\;\text{V}$.
- Internal resistance: $r = v/I = 3/2 = 1.5\;\Omega$.

> ⚠ Check: source writes units as $\Omega$ for the intermediate voltage values ($6\;\Omega$, $1\;\Omega$); the correct unit is V.

## Key Equations Summary

| Quantity | Equation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Current | $I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$ | Charge per unit time |
| Potential difference | $V = \frac{W}{Q}$ | Energy transferred per unit charge |
| Resistance | $R = \frac{V}{I}$ | Ohm's law (for ohmic conductors, $V \propto I$) |
| Resistivity | $\rho = \frac{RA}{L}$ | Material property; depends on temperature |
| Power | $P = VI = \frac{V^2}{R} = I^2 R$ | Rate of energy transfer |
| Energy transferred | $E = VIt$ | Also $E = Pt$ |
| Resistors in series | $R_T = R_1 + R_2 + \ldots$ | Same current through each |
| Resistors in parallel | $\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \ldots$ | Same p.d. across each |
| Potential divider | $V_{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \times V_S$ | Output across $R_2$ |
| EMF | $\varepsilon = \frac{E}{Q}$ | Energy per coulomb from source |
| EMF equation | $\varepsilon = I(R + r) = V + v$ | Terminal p.d. + lost volts |
| Terminal p.d. | $V = IR$ | p.d. across external resistance |
| Lost volts | $v = Ir$ | p.d. across internal resistance |
| Cells in series | $V_T = V_1 + V_2 + \ldots$ | Voltages add |
| Identical cells in parallel | $V_T = V_1 = V_2 = \ldots$ | Voltage equals one cell |
