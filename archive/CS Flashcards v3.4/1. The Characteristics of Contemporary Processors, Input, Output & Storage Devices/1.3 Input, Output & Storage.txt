What is an input device? | A hardware device used to put data and information into a computer system
A hardware device that sends data into a computer for processing — what is this called? | An input device
What is an output device? | A hardware device that receives information from a computer system and presents it to the user
A hardware device that presents processed information to the user — what is this called? | An output device
What is a keyboard used for? | Entering text and commands into a computer by pressing keys
What does a webcam do? | Captures video input, often used for video conferencing or recording videos
What is a magnetic stripe reader used for? | Reading data from magnetic stripes on cards such as credit and debit cards
What does a barcode reader do? | Scans barcodes to read information, typically used in retail checkouts and inventory management
What is a scanner used for? | Digitising physical documents or images into a format the computer can process
What input device would a graphic designer use for drawing directly onto a computer? | A graphics tablet
What are biometric devices used for? | Verifying a user's identity using features such as fingerprints or facial recognition
A device such as a fingerprint scanner used for security authentication — what type of device is this? | A biometric device
What do speakers do as output devices? | Output audio from a computer, such as music, sound effects, or voice
What does a printer do? | Produces a hard copy of digital documents or images on paper
What does a projector do? | Projects the computer's visual display onto a large screen or wall
What is a Braille display? | A specialised output device that presents information in Braille, allowing visually impaired users to read text from a computer
What is a plotter used for? | Printing large, high-quality diagrams and designs, often used in engineering or architecture
Give an example of a device that is both an input and output device | A touchscreen — it outputs visual information like a monitor while also accepting touch input
What factors should be considered when choosing an input device? | Speed of input, accuracy of input, cost, and relevance to the task
What four factors should be considered when choosing a storage device? | Storage capacity needed, performance/speed needs, portability, and cost
What are the three main types of secondary storage? | Magnetic, optical, and flash
How do optical storage devices read and write data? | Using lasers — binary data is represented by portions of the disc that either reflect or scatter the laser light
On a CD surface, what are pits? | Recessed portions of the disc that scatter light, representing a binary 0
On a CD surface, what are lands? | Flat portions of the disc that reflect light, representing a binary 1
The recessed portions of a CD that scatter laser light are called [...] | pits
The flat portions of a CD that reflect laser light are called [...] | lands
What does CD stand for? | Compact disc
What type of data are CDs most commonly used to store? | Audio files (though they can also store text and digital images)
Name two disadvantages of CDs | Easily damaged by scratches, and limited storage capacity with relatively slow transfer speeds
What is the difference between CD-R and CD-RW? | CD-R can only be written to once, while CD-RW can be written to multiple times
What is a CD-ROM? | A CD that is written to during production and can then only be read from, not rewritten
What does DVD stand for? | Digital versatile disc (or digital video disc)
How do DVDs compare to CDs in terms of storage capacity? | DVDs have a higher storage capacity than CDs
What type of content are DVDs best suited for storing? | Digital videos, which require more storage space than audio files or photos
How does Blu-ray compare to DVD in terms of storage capacity? | Blu-ray discs have more than five times the storage capacity of traditional DVDs
What are Blu-ray discs particularly useful for? | Storing high-resolution films
What is the typical storage capacity of a CD? | 700MB
What is the typical storage capacity of a DVD? | 4.7GB to 9GB
What is the typical storage capacity of a Blu-ray disc? | 25GB to 50GB
How do magnetic storage devices represent binary data? | Using two magnetic states: polarised (magnetic poles aligned, representing 1) and unpolarised (magnetic poles randomly scattered, representing 0)
A region of magnetic material where all poles are aligned and can be read by a read/write head is in what state? | Polarised
What is the most common type of magnetic storage? | Hard disk drives (HDDs)
What does HDD stand for? | Hard disk drive
What is the typical storage capacity of a consumer-grade HDD? | 500GB to 2TB (some up to 5TB)
How does a hard disk drive work? | Rotating magnetic platters spin at high speeds under a read/write head on an actuating arm, allowing the head to access any part of the platter
Why do most HDDs have multiple platters? | To maximise storage capacity by stacking platters on top of each other
Name two disadvantages of HDDs | Somewhat slow data transfer speeds, and susceptibility to damage from movement due to brittle platters and moving parts
Explain why HDDs are susceptible to physical damage | HDDs contain brittle spinning platters and moving parts (actuating arm), so sudden movement or shocks can cause the read/write head to scratch or damage the platters
What was magnetic tape used for? | Recording and storing computer data, popular from the 1950s through to the 1980s
Why was magnetic tape eventually superseded? | It was bulky, and more modern technologies like ROM cartridges offered better alternatives
What is a floppy disk? | A thin magnetic disk enclosed in a plastic casing, used for exchanging small amounts of data portably
What was the typical storage capacity of a floppy disk? | Around 1MB, though some later versions reached up to 200MB
What two key properties make flash storage advantageous? | It is fast and compact
What technology does flash storage use to store data? | Silicon semiconductors forming NAND and NOR logic gates that store electrical charge in one of two states (high or low)
In flash storage, what do the two electrical charge states (high and low) represent? | Binary values True and False (1 and 0)
How is data organised in flash memory? | In blocks, which are combined to form pages
Which flash memory logic gate is preferred for storing small quantities of data such as executable code? | NOR
Which flash memory logic gate is preferred for storing larger files like photos and videos? | NAND
The preferred logic gate for small data like code in flash memory is [...] | NOR
The preferred logic gate for large files like photos/videos in flash memory is [...] | NAND
What does it mean that flash memory is non-volatile? | It retains its stored data even when power is lost
Flash memory can be erased and reprogrammed [...] | electronically
How does flash memory differ from RAM in terms of volatility? | Flash memory is non-volatile (retains data without power), while RAM is volatile (loses data when power is lost)
Why is flash memory more expensive than magnetic and optical storage? | As a general rule, flash memory costs more per gigabyte than other storage methods
What does SSD stand for? | Solid state drive
Why are SSDs more resistant to physical damage than HDDs? | SSDs have no moving parts, unlike HDDs which contain spinning platters and actuating arms
Name two advantages of SSDs over HDDs | Higher data transfer speeds, and greater resistance to damage from movement (no moving parts)
What is the primary disadvantage of SSDs? | Their higher cost per gigabyte compared to HDDs (though this is decreasing over time)
Explain why SSDs have a limited lifespan | Every time a page is written to, the voltage required to write to it increases. Over time, this required voltage becomes too high for the page to be written to
What is the typical storage capacity of a consumer-grade SSD? | 120GB to 4TB
How does the portability of a USB flash drive compare to other storage devices? | Very high portability due to being small and lightweight
What is RAM? | Random access memory — a type of fast main memory used to store data and programs the computer is currently using
A type of fast, volatile main memory used to hold data and programs currently in use — what is this? | RAM (Random Access Memory)
Why does RAM speed up a computer's execution? | RAM has higher access speeds than even flash memory, allowing the processor to quickly access currently needed data
Why is RAM volatile? | It loses all stored information when power is lost
Why is RAM not used to store personal files permanently? | Because RAM is volatile — it loses its contents when the computer is powered off
Why do computers typically have only 4-8GB of RAM compared to hundreds of GB of secondary storage? | RAM is more expensive per gigabyte than secondary storage devices
What is ROM? | Read only memory — non-volatile memory that cannot be modified after being programmed, used for storing fixed instructions
Non-volatile memory that stores fixed instructions and cannot be modified after programming — what is this? | ROM (Read Only Memory)
What is ROM typically used to store? | Fixed sequences of instructions such as the computer's startup (bootstrap) routine or BIOS/firmware
What is the bootstrap routine? | The fixed startup sequence stored in ROM that initialises the computer's hardware when it is powered on
What does BIOS stand for? | Basic Input/Output System
How does RAM differ from ROM in terms of volatility? | RAM is volatile (loses contents when power is off), while ROM is non-volatile (retains contents without power)
How does RAM differ from ROM in terms of read/write capability? | RAM can be both read from and written to, while ROM can only be read from (not modified)
What is the difference between primary and secondary storage? | Primary storage (RAM and ROM) is used by the OS to run the computer and store currently needed instructions/data. Secondary storage (HDDs, SSDs, optical discs) stores user data like files, images, and videos long-term
What is virtual storage? | Storing information remotely so it can be accessed by any computer with access to the same system, typically over the Internet
Storing data remotely on servers accessed over the Internet, also known as cloud storage — what is this called? | Virtual storage
Give two examples of virtual storage services | Google Drive and Microsoft OneDrive
Virtual storage is often an abstraction of [...] acting like one | multiple drives
Explain how virtual storage physically works behind the scenes | Data stored "in the cloud" is actually stored across hundreds of hard drives or SSDs in data centres, formatted to appear as a single piece of storage
Name two advantages of virtual storage | Accessibility from any Internet-connected device, and scalability (easy to increase storage capacity as needs grow)
Name two disadvantages of virtual storage | Dependence on Internet connection speed, and ongoing/high costs
What is the difference between virtual storage and virtual memory? | Virtual storage is data stored remotely on the cloud. Virtual memory is when secondary storage is used as an extension of RAM when RAM capacity is exceeded
Why is virtual storage becoming more popular over time? | As Internet speeds increase, the convenience of accessing files from any Internet-connected device makes cloud storage more practical
What benefit does data redundancy provide in virtual storage? | It protects against data loss due to hardware failure, since data is backed up across multiple servers
How does magnetic storage compare to flash storage in terms of cost per gigabyte? | Magnetic storage has a lower cost per gigabyte than flash storage
How does optical storage compare to flash storage in terms of read/write speed? | Flash storage has much faster read/write speeds than optical storage
How do HDDs compare to SSDs in terms of durability? | SSDs are more durable because they have no moving parts, while HDDs are susceptible to damage from shocks or falls due to their moving parts
