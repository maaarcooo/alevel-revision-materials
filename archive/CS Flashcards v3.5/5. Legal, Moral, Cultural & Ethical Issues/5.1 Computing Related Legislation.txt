What is the Data Protection Act (DPA)? | A law that protects personal data from being misused
A law that protects personal data from being misused — what is it called? | The Data Protection Act (DPA)
What is personal data? | Any data that can be used to identify a living person, or data that can be used in combination with other data to identify a living person
Give four examples of personal data | Name, address, date of birth, race, religion
What is a data subject? | An individual who can be identified by personal data
An individual who can be identified by personal data is called a [...] | data subject
Does the Data Protection Act only apply to data stored on computers? | No, it also applies to information stored in organised paper filing systems
Name three exemptions to the Data Protection Act | Domestic purposes (e.g. personal photos or letters), law enforcement (e.g. police investigating crime), intelligence services processing (e.g. MI5)
Why is law enforcement exempt from the DPA? | So that suspects cannot request to see the evidence held against them during a criminal investigation
State DPA Principle 1 | Personal data must be processed fairly and lawfully
What does DPA Principle 1 (fairly and lawfully processed) mean for a company? | The company must be clear about what personal data they collect and what they intend to use it for, having a lawful reason for holding the data
State DPA Principle 2 | Personal data must be obtained only for specified and lawful purposes
What does DPA Principle 2 (specified and lawful purposes) mean for a company? | The company cannot use personal data for any purpose other than what was originally stated, and cannot pass data on without permission
Give an example of a breach of DPA Principle 2 | A company collects a phone number for delivery updates but then uses it to market new products without consent
State DPA Principle 3 | Personal data must be adequate, relevant and not excessive
What does DPA Principle 3 (adequate, relevant, not excessive) mean for a company? | The company cannot request personal data that they do not need for the stated purpose
Give an example of a breach of DPA Principle 3 | A bank asking customers about their previous holidays when opening a bank account
State DPA Principle 4 | Personal data must be kept accurate and up to date
What does DPA Principle 4 (accurate and up to date) mean for a data subject? | If a company holds incorrect or outdated personal data, the data subject has the right to have it corrected or deleted
State DPA Principle 5 | Personal data will not be kept for longer than is necessary
What does DPA Principle 5 (not kept longer than necessary) mean for a company? | The company must delete personal data once they no longer have a need for it
State DPA Principle 6 | Personal data must be processed in line with the rights of data subjects
What does DPA Principle 6 (processed in line with people's rights) mean for a company? | If requested, the company must provide a data subject with all the personal data they hold on them
State DPA Principle 7 | Personal data must be held securely
What does DPA Principle 7 (held securely) mean for a company? | The company must ensure personal data is secured (e.g. usernames and passwords) and backed up to prevent accidental loss
State DPA Principle 8 | Personal data must not be transferred to countries outside the European Economic Area unless those countries have similar data protection laws
What does DPA Principle 8 (transfer restrictions) mean for a company? | The company cannot send personal data outside the EEA unless the destination country has been deemed by the European Commission to provide adequate data protection
What is a Data Controller? | A member of staff appointed by a company who is responsible for ensuring the Data Protection principles are not breached and for communicating with the Information Commissioner
A company must appoint a [...] to ensure the DPA principles are followed | Data Controller
What is a Subject Access Request (SAR)? | A request from a data subject to a company for a copy of all personal data held about them
A request from a data subject to receive a copy of all data held about them is called a [...] | Subject Access Request (SAR)
What must a company do when it receives a Subject Access Request? | Send a copy of the subject's data securely after verifying the identity of the subject
Name four rights of a data subject under the DPA | Right to be informed about data collection, right to access their personal data, right to have inaccurate data corrected, right to be forgotten in certain circumstances
What is the right to data portability? | The right of a data subject to obtain and reuse their personal data for their own purposes across different services
What replaced the Data Protection Act 1998? | The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018, as of 2018
Name four actions a company should take to prevent a data breach | Implement multi-factor authentication, use role-based access controls, maintain effective security monitoring, carry out regular penetration testing
What was British Airways fined for their GDPR breach? | £20 million, after data security failings enabled unauthorised access to personal and payment data of over 400,000 customers
What is the Computer Misuse Act (CMA)? | A law concerning the malicious use of computers, originally created to ensure computer hacking was covered by law
A law that was originally created to ensure computer hacking was covered by law — what is it called? | The Computer Misuse Act 1990 (CMA)
How many primary offences does the Computer Misuse Act define? | Three
What is Offence 1 under the Computer Misuse Act? | Unauthorised access to computer materials (e.g. finding a teacher's password and opening their files)
What is Offence 2 under the Computer Misuse Act? | Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences (e.g. accessing a teacher's computer with the intent to change your test marks)
What is Offence 3 under the Computer Misuse Act? | Unauthorised modification of computer files (e.g. accessing a teacher's computer and actually changing your test marks)
How does CMA Offence 1 differ from CMA Offence 2? | Offence 1 is simply gaining unauthorised access to computer materials. Offence 2 involves gaining unauthorised access with the specific intent to commit a further crime, carrying harsher consequences
How does CMA Offence 2 differ from CMA Offence 3? | Offence 2 is unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences (planning). Offence 3 is actually carrying out the unauthorised modification of computer files (execution)
Which CMA offence carries the most severe consequences? | Offence 3 (unauthorised modification of computer files), with penalties increasing from Offence 1 through to Offence 3
What punishment can result from breaching the Computer Misuse Act? | Imprisonment (for all three offences)
Name two technical measures that can help prevent unauthorised access under the CMA | Digital signatures/certificates (to verify identity) and firewalls (to prevent external unauthorised access and DoS/DDoS attacks)
What is the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988? | A law that protects the intellectual property of individuals and companies, making it illegal to copy, modify or distribute software or other intellectual property without permission
A law that protects intellectual property and makes it illegal to copy, modify or distribute work without permission — what is it called? | The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
When does copyright protection apply to an original work? | Automatically from the moment of creation — no registration is required
How long does copyright last? | 25 to 70 years after the death of the creator, depending on the type of work
In 1992, what was added to the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act? | Computer programs were included as a type of literary work, meaning copyright automatically applies to code
Whose responsibility is it to take action if their copyrighted work has been copied? | The individual copyright holder must take action themselves under the Act
Name three primary breaches of the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act | Copying an original work, issuing copies to the public, performing/showing/playing the work in public
Name three secondary breaches of the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act | Importing a copy of original work, possessing or dealing with a copy, providing means to make copies
How do primary breaches differ from secondary breaches under the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act? | Primary breaches involve directly infringing copyright (e.g. copying, distributing, performing the work). Secondary breaches involve facilitating or profiting from infringement (e.g. importing copies, providing equipment to make copies)
How does peer-to-peer streaming relate to the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act? | It can breach the act because it prevents copyright owners from receiving income from their work (e.g. sharing downloaded Netflix videos with others)
Why is it important to use the full name "Copyright, Designs and Patents Act" in exams? | OCR may not award marks if a shortened version like "Copyright Act" is used
What is the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA)? | A law that legislates how organisations can monitor electronic communications, covering investigation, surveillance and interception of communications by public bodies
A law that covers investigation, surveillance and interception of electronic communications by public bodies — what is it called? | The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA)
Under RIPA, what can organisations like MI6 legally do? | Wiretap online conversations in the interests of national security, provided a warrant has been issued
What does RIPA require of internet service providers (ISPs) and mobile phone companies? | They must give up customer information on request from an authorised authority, and ensure their networks have sufficient hardware to facilitate surveillance
Why is RIPA considered controversial? | Its powers extend to small agencies like local councils, some feel it is an invasion of privacy, and it has been used for purposes beyond monitoring criminal/terrorist activity (e.g. monitoring dog walkers, fly tippers, school catchment areas)
Give an example of RIPA being used controversially | Local councils used RIPA to carry out over 55,000 days of covert surveillance over 5 years, including monitoring people walking dogs, feeding pigeons and fly-tipping
What key technical terms should be used when describing RIPA in an exam? | "Surveillance" (the use of technology to gather information on people) and "communication" (done electronically)
How does RIPA impact businesses? | Businesses must provide access to digital communications or data when asked, and must implement hardware and software that facilitates storage of digital communications
Does RIPA allow mass surveillance of communications? | Yes, RIPA permits mass surveillance of communications by authorised public bodies
Can evidence obtained through RIPA interception be revealed in court? | No, RIPA prevents the existence of interception activities being revealed in court
How does the Data Protection Act differ from the Computer Misuse Act? | The DPA protects personal data from being misused by organisations that store it. The CMA protects computer systems from being accessed or modified without authorisation
How does the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act differ from the Computer Misuse Act? | The CDPA protects intellectual property (creative works, software) from being copied or distributed without permission. The CMA protects computer systems from unauthorised access, intent to commit offences, and unauthorised modification
How does the Data Protection Act differ from RIPA? | The DPA restricts how organisations collect, use and store personal data, protecting individual privacy. RIPA grants public bodies legal powers to monitor and intercept electronic communications, potentially overriding some individual privacy
Under RIPA, what must be obtained before surveillance can take place? | A warrant must be issued by an authorised authority
Which organisation has the most freedom under RIPA? | GCHQ is amongst those with the most freedom to conduct surveillance
What is the role of the Information Commissioner in relation to the DPA? | The Information Commissioner oversees compliance with data protection legislation, and data subjects can complain to them if they believe their rights have been violated
