What are ethics in the context of computing? | Values held by a community and how they impact different groups of people in society
Values held by a community that impact different groups in society — what term describes this? | Ethics
What are morals in the context of computing? | A personal code of conduct encompassing how we choose to behave, including decisions made at the expense of others
A personal code of conduct governing how an individual chooses to behave — what term describes this? | Morals
How do ethics differ from morals? | Ethics concern community values and their societal impact, while morals are about an individual's personal code of conduct and personal choices
Why is it important to consider moral, ethical, environmental, social, and cultural implications of computing? | Computers are integral to almost every aspect of daily life, so identifying these issues is the first step to resolving them
Name two advantages of computers in the workforce | They improve efficiency (e.g. reducing delivery times and manufacturing costs) and reduce strain on workers by removing repetitive and tedious tasks
Give an example of how Amazon uses robots in the workforce | Amazon uses robots in warehouses to package and transfer goods between stations, reducing delivery times and unit labour costs
What is Effidence's PostBot? | A robot that navigates through neighbourhoods to deliver letters and parcels door-to-door
What type of unemployment does computerisation of the workforce primarily cause? | Structural unemployment — workers lose jobs because their skills no longer match available roles
Why might the unemployment caused by computerisation not be permanent? | Higher consumer demand due to cheaper services may create employment in other sections of the business
What can governments do to reduce the impact of computerisation on workers? | Invest in retraining programmes so displaced workers can change occupation
Which type of jobs has computerisation hit the hardest? | Middle-income manufacturing jobs, where entire production lines have been replaced by robots
What shift in job types has resulted from the computerisation of manufacturing? | A shift from middle-income manufacturing jobs toward low-income service jobs
Give two examples of non-manufacturing jobs that have been replaced by computers | Library assistants and taxi drivers
What is a major downside of a highly computerised workplace? | High dependence on computers — if something goes wrong, there is a major loss of output
How has computerisation affected demand for computing-related jobs? | Demand has increased — software developers are needed for embedded systems and network engineers to maintain networks
What new skill expectation do employers now have due to computerisation? | Workers are expected to be able to confidently and productively use computers
Give two examples of services now offered exclusively online | Online shopping and online banking
How have exclusively online services reduced business costs? | Reduced costs of renting physical space and cut labour costs as fewer front-facing retail staff and bank clerks are needed
What new jobs have grown as a result of online services? | Delivery drivers and web developers
What is automated decision making? | The process of using algorithms and computer systems to make decisions without direct human intervention, based on data analysis, pattern recognition, and predefined rules
The process of using algorithms to make decisions without direct human intervention — what is this called? | Automated decision making
How is automated decision making used in social media? | Algorithms determine what content users see on their feeds, based on their interactions and inferred interests from their connections
What is the danger of tailoring social media feeds solely to users' existing interests? | It creates a filter bubble where users' beliefs are never challenged, leading to a close-minded society
A situation where social media algorithms only reinforce a user's existing beliefs, preventing exposure to alternative viewpoints — what is this called? | A filter bubble (or echo chamber)
Give two examples of application processes where algorithmic decision-making has been used | Determining suitability for mortgages/loans and screening job applicants
Why might relying entirely on algorithms for decisions about people be unfair? | Algorithms do not consider extenuating circumstances and cannot process information with the same consideration of contextual factors that humans can
How can algorithmic decision making benefit employers in recruitment? | It screens candidates faster for desired qualities before the interview stage, improving productivity and convenience
How is automated decision making used in driverless cars? | Algorithms make driving decisions faster than humans can react, potentially saving lives
How is automated decision making used in stock market trading? | Algorithms analyse past trends to model future changes, allowing traders to make rapid decisions
Give two examples of systems that use fast automated decision making to respond to real-time changes | Plant automation systems and power distribution systems
Why must algorithms be thoroughly tested before deployment? | To produce high-quality software that is free of bias against any group of people and works with factually correct data
Why can algorithms not be assumed to be free of bias? | They are designed by software developers who may introduce their own biases into the system
What does it mean to say algorithms should not remain a "black box"? | The decisions algorithms make must be justified and transparent, with some level of human input to ensure fairness
What ethical dilemma does algorithmic decision making raise for driverless cars? | How to decide who should be harmed if a scenario arises where either a pedestrian or the driver must be harmed (the trolley problem)
In the context of driverless cars, what additional ethical question arises from automated harm decisions? | Who is responsible for the consequences of the decision — the manufacturer, the programmer, or the owner?
What is the COMPAS AI program used for? | Predicting a defendant's potential for future criminal behaviour in the US judicial system
What criticism has been made of the COMPAS program? | It uses undisclosed factors, AI cannot fully understand human behaviour nuances, and it may reflect human biases
What is artificial intelligence (AI)? | The ability of a computer to replicate human intelligence, cognitive ability, and grasp abstract concepts
The ability of a computer to replicate human intelligence, cognitive ability, and grasp abstract concepts — what is this called? | Artificial intelligence (AI)
What cognitive functions do AI systems simulate? | Learning, problem-solving, reasoning, perception, and decision-making
What are expert systems? | Systems (also known as knowledge-based systems) that replicate the knowledge and experience of an expert in a particular subject
Systems that replicate the knowledge and experience of a human expert in a particular subject — what are these called? | Expert systems (or knowledge-based systems)
What are the three components of an expert system? | A knowledge base (set of facts and rules), an inference engine (built from the rules), and a user interface to interrogate it
What is a knowledge base in an expert system? | A set of facts and rules about a particular domain that the expert system uses to make decisions
What is an inference engine in an expert system? | The component that applies the rules from the knowledge base to given facts to draw conclusions or diagnoses
How is AI used in medicine? | In expert systems to draw connections between illnesses to form diagnoses
What are neural networks in AI? | Systems that replicate biological neural networks by learning from a set of training data, then applying that knowledge to new data sets
Systems that learn from training data and apply that knowledge to new data, replicating how biological neurons work — what are these called? | Neural networks
Give two applications of neural networks | Pattern detection and detecting financial fraud
Give two examples of AI voice recognition systems in smart homes | Google Home and Amazon's Alexa
What privacy concern do smart home voice assistants raise? | They must be constantly switched on to function, meaning they are always listening, which raises questions about privacy
What accountability question does AI raise? | Who is responsible when things go wrong — the AI system, its developers, or the user?
Why are artists concerned about AI? | Their work is being used without consent to train AI systems, leading to potential copyright infringements
What did UNESCO produce regarding AI in 2021? | The first global standard on AI ethics — the "Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence", adopted by all 193 member states
Why might AI algorithms show bias? | If they are trained on biased datasets, leading to discrimination against certain demographic groups
How did Amazon's AI recruitment tool demonstrate algorithmic bias? | It was trained on 10 years of mostly male applicant data, so it taught itself that male candidates were preferable and penalised CVs containing the word "women"
What is censorship? | The act of suppressing or controlling the content that people are able to view, publish, and access
The act of suppressing the content people are able to view, publish, and access — what is this called? | Censorship
Why do some countries use censorship? | To block out other political opinions, protect national security, restrict harmful content, preserve cultural norms, or combat disinformation
What does the UK block through ISPs? | Websites with content associated with terrorism and extremist political beliefs
What is the danger of censorship being misused? | It could be used to block alternative political beliefs, pushing a particular ideology rather than protecting the country
What is the concept of a "Free Internet"? | The idea that nothing should be filtered online, in line with the principle of freedom of speech
Who created the internet and what was the original vision? | Tim Berners-Lee created the internet as a space where people could share and mutually benefit from resources
Why do most people agree some censorship is necessary online? | For national security purposes and to filter offensive comments and extremist propaganda
Give an example of censorship at an institutional level | Schools blocking unsuitable material from pupils, or workplaces blocking distracting websites to maintain productivity
How can censorship exacerbate the digital divide? | By widening the gap in access to information and online resources for marginalised communities with restricted internet access
What is the digital divide? | The unequal distribution of access to technology and the internet among different communities, regions, and socioeconomic groups
The unequal distribution of access to technology and the internet among different groups in society — what term describes this? | The digital divide
How do computers monitor behaviour in the workplace? | Employers track the websites and applications workers are accessing and the time spent on them to monitor productivity
Give three examples of monitoring technologies | CCTV cameras, GPS tracking, and email/phone call monitoring
How are CCTV cameras used for security purposes? | To detect crime (from speeding to violence), trace criminal activity, and deter unlawful behaviour
What are ankle monitors used for? | Tracking the location of people under house arrest
What is the argument in favour of surveillance like ankle monitors? | It is a necessary measure to deter people from committing crime and ensure public safety
What is the argument against surveillance like ankle monitors? | It is unethical and contravenes basic human rights
Why might mass surveillance lead to self-censorship? | People may fear expressing dissenting opinions if they feel constantly watched, stifling open dialogue
What is big data? | Large amounts of data collected from a number of sources that can be analysed to identify patterns and make inferences
Large amounts of data from numerous sources that can be analysed for patterns — what is this called? | Big data
What is data mining? | The process of analysing big data to make inferences about people's behaviour, likes, dislikes, and to identify previously unknown connections between variables
The process of analysing large datasets to extract patterns and inferences about people's behaviour — what is this called? | Data mining
Give three uses of data mining | Informing personalised political propaganda, targeted advertising, and identifying previously unknown connections between variables
Give an example of how data mining could be used against an individual | An insurance company could discover a person is at high risk of developing a disease and deny them insurance or charge a higher premium
Why is the Data Protection Act 1998 considered insufficient today? | It no longer adequately protects public welfare given the scale of modern data collection, which is why the GDPR was enforced from May 2018
What is the GDPR? | The General Data Protection Regulation — a data protection law enforced from May 2018 that strengthens individuals' rights over their personal data
Why is there pressure on large firms regarding data collection? | To be more transparent about what data they collect, how it is used, and to make this clearer to users
What is piracy in the context of computing? | The unauthorised copying, use, distribution, or reproduction of copyrighted content such as software, music, or films
The unauthorised copying and distribution of copyrighted content — what is this called? | Piracy
Why is piracy considered a form of theft? | It involves using someone's creative output without permission, directly infringing on their intellectual property rights
Give three types of piracy | Software piracy (unauthorised copying/distribution of software), music and film piracy (via peer-to-peer networks), and eBook piracy (unauthorised distribution of digital books)
How has the internet made piracy easier? | It has made it easier than ever to source copyrighted content such as music, films, and software online
What economic impact does piracy have? | It inflicts financial losses on creators and industries, which can lead to job losses and slow economic development
What is offensive communication? | Any form of online harassment, including cyber-bullying or stalking
What is the Malicious Communications Act (1988)? | A law that makes it a criminal offence to send indecent or offensive messages to anyone online, with offenders traceable by law enforcement
A law making it a criminal offence to send indecent or offensive messages online — what is this called? | The Malicious Communications Act (1988)
How did Steve Jobs propose to combat music piracy? | By creating a better legal service (iTunes) that was easy to use and reasonably priced, arguing piracy was a service issue not a pricing issue
What is the Equality Act 2010 in the context of web design? | A law making it illegal to discriminate against providing a service to a certain group of people, requiring websites to be accessible to all
Why must website layout consider the target audience's culture? | Different cultures read in different directions (e.g. English left-to-right, Arabic right-to-left), affecting where menus and navigation elements should be placed
In English-speaking countries, where are website menus typically displayed? | On the left-hand side of the page
Why would a website for Arabic-speaking users place menus on the right? | Because Arabic is read from right to left, so placing navigation on the right matches the natural reading direction
Give three ways websites should accommodate users with visual impairments | Provide the ability to enlarge text, alter contrast, include alternative text (alt text) for images, and offer a screen magnifier option
What should be provided for users with hearing impairments on a website? | Transcripts of audio files
What is a colour paradigm in web design? | The systematic use of colours in UX design to elicit specific responses from users, taking into account how colours are interpreted in different cultures
Why must web developers consider colour carefully? | Colours have different meanings in different cultures — e.g. white represents mourning in the Middle East but purity in Western cultures, and red signifies danger in the West but luck in China
Why is a neutral colour scheme often chosen for international websites? | To avoid negative cultural connotations — green is commonly used as it represents luck and nature in many cultures
Why is Unicode preferred over ASCII for international websites? | ASCII uses only 7 bits and cannot represent all characters in languages like Chinese, whereas Unicode can represent over a million characters
ASCII uses 7 bits — why is this insufficient for a global website? | It cannot represent all characters in languages with large character sets (e.g. Chinese), limiting the website's accessibility
What character encoding standard supports most of the world's writing systems? | Unicode
What is e-waste? | Discarded electronic or electrical devices that can cause environmental and health problems if improperly disposed of
Discarded electronic devices containing toxic materials that cause environmental harm if improperly disposed of — what is this called? | E-waste
Name two toxic substances found in computer components | Mercury and radioactive isotopes
Why is shipping e-waste to developing countries considered unethical? | These countries often have lower environmental standards, and the toxic materials contaminate local water supplies and harm communities
How does increased computerisation contribute to global warming? | More electricity is required to power devices, which uses non-renewable fossil fuels that emit greenhouse gases into the atmosphere
What is the throw-away attitude in relation to technology? | The tendency for people to frequently discard devices due to affordability and the pressure to keep up with trends, increasing e-waste
Give two examples of environmentally friendly technologies | Smart home systems (using temperature and motion sensors to reduce energy waste) and Sleep/Stand-by features on computers and laptops
How do smart home systems reduce energy consumption? | They use temperature sensors to determine when heating should be switched on and motion sensors to switch off lights when rooms are empty
How can new car engine technology reduce environmental impact? | Engines designed to prevent idling reduce unnecessary emissions
What is environmental equity? | The fair distribution of environmental benefits and burdens among different social groups and communities
Why do lower-income communities bear a disproportionate burden of e-waste pollution? | They are more likely to live near disposal sites and have less access to energy-efficient technologies and recycling facilities
How does data mining differ from big data? | Big data refers to the large datasets collected from many sources, while data mining is the process of analysing those datasets to extract patterns and inferences
How does censorship differ from monitoring behaviour? | Censorship controls or suppresses what content people can access, while monitoring behaviour observes and records people's activities without necessarily restricting them
How do expert systems differ from neural networks? | Expert systems use predefined rules and facts in a knowledge base to make decisions, while neural networks learn patterns from training data and apply that learning to new data
How does structural unemployment differ from general unemployment? | Structural unemployment occurs when workers' skills become obsolete due to changes in the economy (like automation), not due to a lack of jobs overall
How does the Data Protection Act 1998 relate to the GDPR? | The DPA 1998 was the earlier UK data protection law that became insufficient for modern data collection, leading to the GDPR being enforced from May 2018 as a stronger replacement
What moral dilemma do AI developers face regarding accountability? | They must consider the potential consequences of their creations, including privacy, job displacement, social inequality, and ensuring AI systems avoid biases that lead to discrimination
Why is informed consent important when analysing personal information? | It respects individuals' autonomy to control information about themselves, which is a fundamental moral principle
What is algorithmic transparency? | The ethical principle that automated decision-making algorithms should be interpretable and understandable to ensure accountability and prevent hidden biases
The principle that algorithms should be interpretable and understandable to prevent hidden biases — what is this called? | Algorithmic transparency
Why is a feedback loop important for automated decision-making systems? | Continuous data collection and evaluation of real-world impact allows iterative improvement of algorithms and correction of errors or biases
What is localisation in software development? | Adapting an application to different languages and regions without engineering changes, including translation, appropriate symbols, and understanding local norms
