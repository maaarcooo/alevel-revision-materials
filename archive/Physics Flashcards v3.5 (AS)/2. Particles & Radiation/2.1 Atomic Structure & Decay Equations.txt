What three types of particle make up an atom? | Protons, neutrons and electrons
Where are protons and neutrons found in an atom? | In the nucleus
Where are electrons found in an atom? | Orbiting the nucleus
What is the charge of a proton in coulombs? | +1.60 x 10^-19 C
What is the charge of a neutron? | 0 (neutral)
What is the charge of an electron in coulombs? | -1.60 x 10^-19 C
What is the mass of a proton in kg? | 1.67(3) x 10^-27 kg
What is the mass of a neutron in kg? | 1.67(5) x 10^-27 kg
What is the mass of an electron in kg? | 9.11 x 10^-31 kg
What is the relative charge of a proton? | +1
What is the relative charge of an electron? | -1
What is the relative mass of a proton? | 1
What is the relative mass of a neutron? | 1
What is the relative mass of an electron? | 1/2000 (negligible)
Explain why a stable atom has no overall charge | Protons and electrons have equal magnitude but opposite charge, and a stable atom has equal numbers of both, so the charges cancel to zero
A particle with relative charge +1 and relative mass 1 is a [...] | proton
A particle with relative charge 0 and relative mass 1 is a [...] | neutron
A particle with relative charge -1 and negligible relative mass is a [...] | electron
What is specific charge? | The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass
The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass is called what? | Specific charge
What is the equation for specific charge? | Specific charge = Q / m (charge divided by mass)
What are the units of specific charge? | Coulombs per kilogram (C kg^-1)
What is the specific charge of the electron? | 1.76 x 10^11 C kg^-1
What is the specific charge of the proton? | 9.58 x 10^7 C kg^-1
How do you calculate the total charge of an ion? | Total number of electrons added or removed x (1.60 x 10^-19 C)
How do you calculate the total mass of an ion or nucleus? | Total number of nucleons x (1.67 x 10^-27 kg)
How do you calculate the total charge of a nucleus? | Total charge of the protons x (1.60 x 10^-19 C)
How does calculating specific charge differ between an ion and a nucleus? | For an ion, charge comes from electrons gained/lost. For a nucleus, charge comes from the protons only (no electrons involved)
If an ion gains electrons, is its specific charge positive or negative? | Negative (because electrons have negative charge)
If an ion loses electrons, is its specific charge positive or negative? | Positive (the remaining protons give a net positive charge)
How does an 'atom' differ from a 'nucleus'? | An atom consists of the nucleus plus electrons. The nucleus consists only of the protons and neutrons in the centre of the atom
In AZX notation, what does the top number A represent? | The nucleon number (mass number) — total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
In AZX notation, what does the bottom number Z represent? | The proton number (atomic number) — total number of protons in the nucleus
What is the nucleon number (A)? | The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus is called what? | The nucleon number (or mass number), A
What is the proton number (Z)? | The total number of protons in the nucleus
The total number of protons in the nucleus is called what? | The proton number (or atomic number), Z
How do you calculate the number of neutrons in a nucleus? | Neutrons = nucleon number (A) - proton number (Z)
What defines which element an atom is? | The number of protons (proton number)
What are isotopes? | Nuclei that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
Nuclei with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons are called what? | Isotopes
How does the nucleon number of isotopes of the same element compare? | Isotopes have different nucleon numbers (because they have different numbers of neutrons)
Name the two isotopes of hydrogen | Deuterium (1 proton, 1 neutron) and tritium (1 proton, 2 neutrons)
Why are some isotopes unstable? | They have an imbalance of neutrons and protons, causing them to decay and emit radiation to achieve a more stable form
What is isotopic data? | The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance
The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element in a substance is called what? | Isotopic data
How can the percentage abundance of different isotopes be measured? | Using a mass spectrometer
What is a common use of isotopic data? | Carbon dating of archaeological artefacts, which compares the ratio of stable carbon-12 to unstable carbon-14
How is carbon dating carried out? | The ratio of carbon-12 to carbon-14 in a dead tissue sample is compared to the ratio in a living tissue sample to determine the age of the sample
What two forces act on protons within a nucleus? | Repulsive electrostatic force (between protons due to positive charge) and attractive strong nuclear force (between all nucleons)
Why is gravitational force negligible inside the nucleus? | Gravity is the weakest of the fundamental forces, so it has a negligible effect compared to the electrostatic repulsion between protons
What is the strong nuclear force? | An attractive force acting between all nucleons that is stronger than the electrostatic repulsion, holding the nucleus together
What holds the nucleus together despite the electrostatic repulsion between protons? | The strong nuclear force
The strong nuclear force acts between particles called [...] | quarks
Explain why the strong nuclear force acts between protons and neutrons | Protons and neutrons are made up of quarks, and the strong nuclear force acts between quarks, so the interaction between quarks in the nucleons keeps them bound within the nucleus
At what separation is the strong nuclear force repulsive? | Below approximately 0.5 fm
At what range of separations is the strong nuclear force attractive? | From approximately 0.5 fm to 3.0 fm
At what separation does the strong nuclear force have its maximum attractive value? | Approximately 1.0 fm (typical nucleon separation)
At what separation is the equilibrium position of the strong nuclear force (resultant force = 0)? | Approximately 0.5 fm
What happens to the strong nuclear force beyond 3.0 fm? | It becomes zero / has no effect
How does the range of the strong nuclear force compare to the electrostatic force? | The strong nuclear force has a very small range (0.5 to 3.0 fm), while the electrostatic force has a much larger range
Does the strong nuclear force depend on charge? | No, it is roughly the same strength between all nucleon pairs (proton-proton, neutron-neutron, proton-neutron) for separations between 0.5 and 3.0 fm
At separations between 0.5 and 3.0 fm, how does the strong force compare to the electrostatic force? | The attraction of the strong force is far more powerful than the repulsion of the electrostatic force
At what separation does the equilibrium position occur for two protons (where electrostatic repulsion equals strong attraction)? | Approximately 0.7 fm
How large is 1 femtometre (fm) in metres? | 1 x 10^-15 m
How do the strong nuclear force and electrostatic force differ in their dependence on charge? | The electrostatic force is influenced by charge (acts only between charged particles), whereas the strong nuclear force is not charge-dependent (acts between all nucleon types)
What is radioactive decay? | The process by which unstable nuclei become more stable by emitting radiation
Name three common types of radioactive decay | Alpha decay, beta-minus decay, and beta-plus decay
What does an alpha particle consist of? | 2 protons and 2 neutrons (equivalent to a helium nucleus)
An alpha particle is equivalent to the nucleus of which element? | Helium (He-4)
In alpha decay, what happens to the proton number of the parent nucleus? | It decreases by 2
In alpha decay, what happens to the nucleon number of the parent nucleus? | It decreases by 4
Write the general equation for alpha decay | (A, Z)X → (A-4, Z-2)Y + (4, 2)alpha
Why is alpha decay common in large nuclei? | Large nuclei are unstable because they have too many nucleons (protons and neutrons), so emitting an alpha particle reduces both
What is a beta-minus particle? | A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus
In beta-minus decay, what nuclear transformation occurs? | A neutron turns into a proton, emitting an electron and an anti-electron neutrino
In beta-minus decay, what happens to the proton number? | It increases by 1
In beta-minus decay, what happens to the nucleon number? | It remains the same
Write the general equation for beta-minus decay | (A, Z)X → (A, Z+1)Y + (0, -1)beta + (0, 0)anti-neutrino_e
What is a beta-plus particle? | A high-energy positron emitted from the nucleus
In beta-plus decay, what nuclear transformation occurs? | A proton turns into a neutron, emitting a positron (anti-electron) and an electron neutrino
In beta-plus decay, what happens to the proton number? | It decreases by 1
In beta-plus decay, what happens to the nucleon number? | It remains the same
Write the general equation for beta-plus decay | (A, Z)X → (A, Z-1)Y + (0, +1)beta + (0, 0)neutrino_e
How does beta-minus decay differ from beta-plus decay? | In beta-minus, a neutron becomes a proton (emitting an electron and anti-neutrino). In beta-plus, a proton becomes a neutron (emitting a positron and neutrino)
What type of neutrino is emitted in beta-minus decay? | Anti-electron neutrino
What type of neutrino is emitted in beta-plus decay? | Electron neutrino
What is an electron neutrino? | A subatomic particle with no charge and negligible mass emitted from the nucleus during beta decay
Why was the neutrino hypothesised to exist? | To account for the conservation of energy in beta decay (beta particles have a continuous energy spectrum, so the missing energy must be carried by another particle)
How does the energy spectrum of alpha particles differ from that of beta particles? | Alpha particles have discrete (fixed) energy values, while beta particles have a continuous range of energies
Explain why beta particles have a continuous range of energies | The energy released in beta decay is shared between the beta particle and the neutrino (or anti-neutrino) in varying proportions
What conservation laws apply in alpha and beta decay? | Conservation of momentum and conservation of energy
What is antimatter? | Particles that are identical to their matter counterparts but have opposite charge
How are antiparticles named and symbolised (for particles other than the electron)? | They have the same name with the prefix 'anti-' and a line (bar) above the particle symbol
What is the antiparticle of the electron? | The positron (e+)
What is the antiparticle of the proton? | The antiproton (p-bar)
What is the antiparticle of the neutron? | The antineutron (n-bar)
What is the antiparticle of the neutrino? | The antineutrino (with a bar above the symbol)
What three properties do corresponding matter and antimatter particles share? | Same mass, same rest mass-energy, but opposite charges
What is rest mass-energy? | The energy equivalent to the mass of a particle when it is at rest
What is the rest mass-energy of a proton (or antiproton)? | 938.257 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of a neutron (or antineutron)? | 939.551 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of an electron (or positron)? | 0.510999 MeV
What is the rest mass-energy of a neutrino (or antineutrino)? | 0 MeV
What is a photon? | A massless packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy
A massless quantum of electromagnetic energy is called what? | A photon
What equation gives the energy of a photon in terms of frequency? | E = hf
What equation gives the energy of a photon in terms of wavelength? | E = hc / lambda
In E = hf, what does h represent and what are its units? | Planck's constant, measured in joule-seconds (J s)
How does photon energy relate to frequency? | Photon energy is directly proportional to frequency — higher frequency means higher energy
How does photon energy relate to wavelength? | Photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength — shorter wavelength means higher energy
What is annihilation? | The destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair when they collide, converting their mass into two gamma-ray photons
The destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair producing two gamma-ray photons is called what? | Annihilation
Why are two photons produced in annihilation rather than one? | To conserve momentum, the two photons must travel in opposite directions
What is the minimum energy of one photon produced in annihilation? | The rest mass-energy of one of the particles: E_min = hf_min = E (rest mass-energy of one particle)
What is pair production? | The creation of a particle-antiparticle pair when a high-energy photon spontaneously converts its energy into mass
The creation of a particle-antiparticle pair from a high-energy photon is called what? | Pair production
What is the minimum energy a photon must have for pair production? | The total rest mass-energy of both particles produced: E_min = 2E (twice the rest mass-energy of one particle)
How does pair production differ from annihilation in terms of energy requirements? | Pair production requires a photon with energy equal to the rest mass-energy of BOTH particles (2E). Annihilation produces two photons each with minimum energy equal to the rest mass-energy of ONE particle (E)
Why must pair production occur near a nucleus? | To conserve momentum (the nucleus absorbs recoil momentum)
In pair production, why do the particle and antiparticle move apart in opposite directions? | To conserve momentum
How does the minimum photon energy for annihilation compare to that for pair production of the same particle type? | Annihilation minimum per photon is E (one particle's rest mass-energy). Pair production minimum for the incoming photon is 2E (both particles' rest mass-energy)
What is the conversion factor between MeV and joules? | 1 MeV = 1.60 x 10^-13 J
When using E = hf or E = hc/lambda with rest mass-energies, what unit conversion is essential? | Rest mass-energy must be converted from MeV to joules (J), since Planck's constant h is in J s
