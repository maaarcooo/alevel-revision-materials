What are hadrons? | Subatomic particles that are made up of quarks
Subatomic particles made up of quarks are called what? | Hadrons
Why can hadrons feel the strong nuclear force? | Because they are made up of quarks, which interact via the strong force
What are the two classes of hadrons? | Baryons and mesons
How many quarks make up a baryon? | Three quarks
How many quarks make up a meson? | Two (a quark and an anti-quark pair)
A particle made of three quarks is classified as what? | A baryon
A particle made of a quark and an anti-quark pair is classified as what? | A meson
Name the two most common baryons | Protons and neutrons
Name the two most common mesons | Pions and kaons
What is an anti-baryon made of? | Three anti-quarks
What is an anti-meson made of? | A quark and an anti-quark pair (same structure as a meson, but the quark becomes the anti-quark and vice versa)
How does the anti-particle of a meson differ from the original meson? | The quark becomes the anti-quark and the anti-quark becomes the quark
Can quarks exist in isolation? | No, quarks have never been discovered on their own, only in pairs or groups of three
Why must all quarks in a baryon be either all quarks or all anti-quarks? | Because a mixture of quarks and anti-quarks would give a baryon number that is not a whole number (integer), and all baryons/mesons must have integer charges
Why would the combination u-bar, u, d not form a valid baryon? | Because baryons cannot contain a mix of quarks and anti-quarks, only all quarks or all anti-quarks
What is the baryon number, B? | A quantum number representing the number of baryons in an interaction
What is the baryon number of a baryon? | B = +1
What is the baryon number of an anti-baryon? | B = -1
What is the baryon number of a particle that is not a baryon (e.g. meson or lepton)? | B = 0
Is baryon number conserved in all interactions? | Yes, baryon number is conserved in all interactions
What is the baryon number of a single quark (u, d, or s)? | +1/3
What is the baryon number of a single anti-quark? | -1/3
Why is the proton the most stable baryon? | Because it is the lightest baryon, and radioactive decay occurs when heavier particles decay into lighter ones
What do all other baryons eventually decay into? | The proton
The baryon with the longest half-life is the [...] | proton
What is the theorised half-life of the proton? | Approximately 10^32 years
Why would proton decay violate conservation laws? | Because there is no lighter baryon for it to decay into, so baryon number could not be conserved
Show that baryon number is conserved in beta-minus decay: n -> p + e- + anti-v_e | Baryon numbers: n = +1, p = +1, e- = 0, anti-v_e = 0. Left side: +1. Right side: +1 + 0 + 0 = +1. Conserved.
What are pions (pi-mesons)? | Mesons that can be positive (pi+), negative (pi-), or neutral (pi0), containing only up and down quarks
What is the quark composition of a pi+ pion? | u d-bar (up quark and anti-down quark)
What is the quark composition of a pi- pion? | d u-bar (down quark and anti-up quark)
What is the quark composition of a pi0 pion? | u u-bar or d d-bar (up and anti-up, or down and anti-down)
What is the strangeness of all pions? | S = 0 (they contain only up and down quarks)
What is the antiparticle of the positive pion (pi+)? | The negative pion (pi-)
What is unique about the neutral pion (pi0) regarding its antiparticle? | The neutral pion is its own antiparticle
Why are pions more stable than other types of mesons? | Because pions are the lightest mesons
Where were pions originally discovered? | In cosmic rays
What is the exchange particle of the strong nuclear force between baryons? | The pion
What role do pions play in a nucleus? | Protons and neutrons exchange pions to mediate (bring about) the strong nuclear force that holds them together
How does the role of gluons differ from the role of pions in the strong force? | Gluons bind quarks together (strong interaction), while pions bind nucleons together (strong nuclear force)
What are kaons (K-mesons)? | Mesons that can be positive (K+), negative (K-), or neutral (K0), containing a strange quark paired with an up or down quark
Why do kaons have a property called strangeness? | Because they contain a strange quark
What is the quark composition of a K+ kaon? | u s-bar (up quark and anti-strange quark)
What is the quark composition of a K- kaon? | s u-bar (strange quark and anti-up quark)
What is the quark composition of a K0 kaon? | d s-bar (down quark and anti-strange quark)
What is the quark composition of an anti-K0 kaon? | s d-bar (strange quark and anti-down quark)
What is the strangeness of a K+ kaon? | S = +1
What is the strangeness of a K- kaon? | S = -1
What is the strangeness of a K0 kaon? | S = +1
What is the strangeness of an anti-K0 kaon? | S = -1
How does the neutral kaon differ from the neutral pion regarding antiparticles? | The neutral kaon is not its own antiparticle, whereas the neutral pion is
What do kaons normally decay into? | Pions
Why do kaons have unusually long lifetimes compared to other mesons? | This is characteristic of particles containing strange quarks
Through which interaction are kaons produced? | The strong interaction (where strangeness is conserved)
Through which interaction do kaons decay? | The weak interaction
Give an example of kaon pair production | p + p -> p + p + K+ + K- (strangeness is conserved as the total strangeness is zero on both sides)
Why are kaons always produced in pairs? | Because strangeness must be conserved in the strong interaction, so a strange quark and anti-strange quark are produced together
Give an example of kaon decay | K0 -> pi+ + pi-
Why is strangeness not conserved in kaon decay? | Because kaons decay via the weak interaction, and strangeness is not conserved in weak interactions
How do pions and kaons differ in quark content? | Pions contain only up and down quarks (S = 0), while kaons contain a strange quark paired with up or down quarks (S = +/-1)
What are leptons? | A group of fundamental (elementary) particles that are not made up of quarks
Fundamental particles that are not made up of quarks and do not interact via the strong nuclear force are called what? | Leptons
Through which interactions can leptons interact? | The weak, gravitational, and electromagnetic interactions
Can leptons interact via the strong nuclear force? | No
Name the four most common leptons | Electron (e-), electron neutrino (v_e), muon (mu-), muon neutrino (v_mu)
How is the muon similar to the electron? | Both have a charge of -1e, but the muon is heavier
What is the approximate mass of an electron in atomic mass units? | About 0.0005 u
What is the approximate mass of a muon in atomic mass units? | About 0.1 u
What charge do neutrinos have? | No charge (zero)
What mass do neutrinos have? | Negligible mass (almost zero)
Neutrinos are the most abundant [...] in the universe | leptons
Why are quarks not classed as leptons even though they are also fundamental particles? | Because leptons do not interact with the strong force, while quarks do
What is the lepton number, L? | A quantum number representing the number of leptons in an interaction
What is the lepton number of a lepton? | L = +1
What is the lepton number of an anti-lepton? | L = -1
What is the lepton number of a particle that is not a lepton? | L = 0
Is lepton number conserved in all interactions? | Yes
In the decay pi+ -> mu+ + v_mu, which particle is the anti-lepton? | The anti-muon (mu+), because it is the antiparticle of the muon
Why is the muon neutrino (v_mu) in the decay pi+ -> mu+ + v_mu not an anti-lepton? | Because it is a muon neutrino, not an anti-muon neutrino. Only anti-particles of leptons have L = -1
What do muons (mu-) typically decay into? | An electron (plus an anti-electron neutrino and a muon neutrino)
What do anti-muons (mu+) typically decay into? | A positron (plus an electron neutrino and an anti-muon neutrino)
Through which interaction does muon decay occur? | The weak interaction
What exchange particle is involved in muon decay? | The W- boson
Write the equation for muon decay | mu- -> e- + anti-v_e + v_mu
In muon decay, how is electron lepton number conserved? | Left side: L_e = 0 (muon). Right side: L_e = +1 (electron) + (-1) (anti-electron neutrino) + 0 (muon neutrino) = 0. Conserved.
In muon decay, how is muon lepton number conserved? | Left side: L_mu = +1 (muon). Right side: L_mu = 0 (electron) + 0 (anti-electron neutrino) + 1 (muon neutrino) = +1. Conserved.
What are the three most common flavours of quarks? | Up (u), down (d), and strange (s)
What is the charge of an up quark? | +2/3 e
What is the charge of a down quark? | -1/3 e
What is the charge of a strange quark? | -1/3 e
What is the baryon number of each quark (u, d, s)? | +1/3
What is the strangeness of an up quark? | 0
What is the strangeness of a down quark? | 0
What is the strangeness of a strange quark? | -1
What is the charge of an anti-up quark? | -2/3 e
What is the charge of an anti-down quark? | +1/3 e
What is the charge of an anti-strange quark? | +1/3 e
What is the baryon number of each anti-quark? | -1/3
What is the strangeness of an anti-strange quark? | +1
How do anti-quarks compare to quarks in their properties? | Anti-quarks have opposite charge, opposite baryon number, and opposite strangeness to their corresponding quarks
What is the quark composition of a proton? | uud (two up quarks and one down quark)
What is the quark composition of a neutron? | udd (one up quark and two down quarks)
Verify the charge of a proton from its quark composition | +2/3 + 2/3 - 1/3 = +1e
Verify the charge of a neutron from its quark composition | +2/3 - 1/3 - 1/3 = 0
How do the quark compositions of a proton and neutron differ? | Proton = uud (two up, one down). Neutron = udd (one up, two down). The neutron has one extra down quark replacing an up quark.
What are strange particles? | Particles that contain a strange or anti-strange quark (e.g. kaons)
Strange particles are always produced through which interaction? | The strong interaction
Strange particles always decay through which interaction? | The weak interaction
Why are strange particles always produced in pairs? | Because strangeness is conserved in the strong interaction, so a strange quark and anti-strange quark must be created together
What is strangeness (S)? | A quantum number that depends on whether a particle contains a strange quark, anti-strange quark, or no strange quarks
A quantum number assigned to particles based on whether they contain strange or anti-strange quarks is called what? | Strangeness (S)
What is the strangeness of a particle containing an anti-strange quark? | S = +1
What is the strangeness of a particle containing a strange quark? | S = -1
What is the strangeness of a particle with no strange quarks? | S = 0
In which interactions is strangeness conserved? | In all interactions except the weak interaction
By how much can strangeness change in weak interactions? | By 0, +1, or -1
How can you prove that a decay occurs via the weak interaction using strangeness? | If strangeness is not conserved (changes by +/-1) in the decay, it must occur via the weak interaction, since strangeness is conserved in all other interactions
How does baryon number conservation differ from strangeness conservation? | Baryon number is conserved in all interactions. Strangeness is conserved in all interactions except the weak interaction.
How does lepton number conservation differ from strangeness conservation? | Lepton number is conserved in all interactions. Strangeness is conserved in all interactions except the weak interaction.
What are particle accelerators used for? | To collide particles at very high speeds to produce new particles and reveal the inner structure of particles
Why do particles in accelerators need to travel close to the speed of light? | Because the collisions need very high energies to produce new particles
What are the two types of particle accelerator? | Linear and circular
Why do circular particle accelerators require superconducting magnets? | To keep the high-speed particles in a circular orbit
Why does particle physics research require international collaboration? | Because the expensive and complex equipment requires large groups of scientists and engineers from around the world to provide funding and expertise
What is the LHC? | The Large Hadron Collider, the world's largest and highest-energy particle collider, based at CERN in Geneva
Name the four main detectors at the LHC | ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, and LHCb
Which two LHC detectors both detected the Higgs Boson? | ATLAS and CMS
Why is the detection of the Higgs Boson by both ATLAS and CMS significant for the scientific method? | It demonstrates that a theory is only validated after it can be confirmed by independent, repeated experiments, reducing experimental uncertainty
Why was the neutrino originally hypothesised to exist? | To account for energy conservation in beta decay
