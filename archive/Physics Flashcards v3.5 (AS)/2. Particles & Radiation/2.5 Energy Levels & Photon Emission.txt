What are energy levels? | Discrete energy states that electrons in an atom can occupy
The discrete energy states that electrons occupy in an atom are called what? | Energy levels
Why do electrons occupy the lowest possible energy level? | Because this is the most stable configuration for the atom
What is excitation? | When an electron absorbs enough energy to move up to a higher energy level
When an electron absorbs energy and moves to a higher energy level, this process is called what? | Excitation
What must an electron absorb to be excited to a higher energy level? | A photon
When an electron moves to a higher energy level, the atom is said to be in what state? | An excited state
What is de-excitation? | When an electron moves back down to a lower energy level by emitting a photon
When an electron drops to a lower energy level by emitting a photon, this process is called what? | De-excitation
What is ionisation? | When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged
When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged, this is called what? | Ionisation
What happens to an atom when an electron is removed from it? | The atom becomes ionised (positively charged)
What is the ionisation energy of an atom? | The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of an atom
The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state — what quantity is this? | Ionisation energy
From which energy level is the ionisation energy defined? | The ground state (lowest energy level)
How does excitation differ from ionisation? | In excitation, an electron moves to a higher energy level but remains bound to the atom. In ionisation, an electron is completely removed from the atom, leaving it charged
What is fluorescence? | When an electron in an atomic orbital absorbs energy from a photon interaction or electron collision, then re-emits photons as it de-excites
What are fluorescent tubes filled with? | Low-pressure mercury vapour, inside a partially evacuated glass tube with a phosphor coating
Explain how a fluorescent tube produces visible light (first stage) | A high voltage accelerates electrons from cathode to anode. These beam electrons collide with mercury atom electrons, transferring kinetic energy and causing ionisation
Explain how a fluorescent tube produces visible light (second stage) | Mercury electrons de-excite back to the ground state and emit UV photons
Explain how a fluorescent tube produces visible light (final stage) | UV photons collide with electrons in the phosphor coating, exciting them. These phosphor electrons de-excite in stages, emitting photons in the visible light range
Why do phosphor electrons emit visible light rather than UV? | They de-excite in stages (multiple smaller energy transitions), so each emitted photon has less energy and therefore a longer wavelength, falling in the visible range
What type of radiation do mercury atoms emit when their electrons de-excite? | Ultraviolet (UV) photons
What is the role of the phosphor coating in a fluorescent tube? | It absorbs UV photons and re-emits the energy as visible light through staged de-excitation of its electrons
How are energy levels represented in an energy level diagram? | As a series of horizontal lines, with the ground state at the bottom (most negative energy) and the ionisation level at the top (0 eV)
What does the bottom line in an energy level diagram represent? | The ground state — the lowest energy level with the greatest negative energy value
What do the lines above the ground state in an energy level diagram represent? | Excited states, with decreasing negative energy values as you go higher
What does the top line (0 eV or infinity) in an energy level diagram represent? | The ionisation energy level
What is the ground state energy of a hydrogen atom? | -13.6 eV
What is the energy of the n = 2 level in hydrogen? | -3.40 eV
What is the energy of the n = 3 level in hydrogen? | -1.51 eV
When is a photon emitted by an atom? | When an electron transitions from a higher energy level to a lower energy level
The energy of an emitted photon equals what? | The difference in energy between the two energy levels involved in the transition
What are line spectra? | Spectra produced when excited atoms emit light of certain discrete wavelengths, corresponding to different colours
Discrete spectra produced when excited atoms emit light at specific wavelengths are called what? | Line spectra (or atomic spectra)
Why does each element produce a unique set of spectral lines? | Because each element has a unique set of energy levels, so the energy differences (and therefore photon wavelengths) are unique to that element
How can elements be identified using light? | By their unique line spectrum — no two elements produce the same set of spectral lines
What are the two types of line spectra? | Emission spectra and absorption spectra
What is an emission spectrum? | A set of discrete coloured lines on a black background, produced when electrons transition from higher to lower energy levels and emit photons
A set of coloured lines on a black background, each corresponding to a specific wavelength — what type of spectrum is this? | An emission spectrum
What is the equation relating photon energy to the change in energy level? | ΔE = hf = hc/λ
In the equation ΔE = hf, what does ΔE represent? | The change in energy level (J)
In the equation ΔE = hf, what does h represent? | Planck's constant (J s)
In the equation ΔE = hf, what does f represent? | Frequency of the photon (Hz)
What evidence do line spectra provide about atomic structure? | That electrons in atoms can only transition between discrete energy levels (energy is quantised)
What is an absorption spectrum? | A continuous spectrum (all colours) with dark lines at certain wavelengths, produced when white light passes through a cool, low-pressure gas
A continuous spectrum with dark lines at specific wavelengths — what type of spectrum is this? | An absorption spectrum
What conditions are needed to produce an absorption spectrum? | White light passing through a cool, low-pressure gas
Why do dark lines appear in an absorption spectrum? | Electrons in the gas absorb photons of specific wavelengths to become excited. When they de-excite, photons are re-emitted in all directions rather than the original direction, so those wavelengths appear missing
What is the relationship between an element's absorption and emission spectra? | The dark lines in the absorption spectrum correspond to exactly the same wavelengths as the coloured lines in the emission spectrum
What is the equation for the energy difference between two discrete energy levels? | ΔE = hf = E₂ - E₁
In the equation ΔE = E₂ - E₁, what do E₁ and E₂ represent? | E₁ = energy of the higher level (J), E₂ = energy of the lower level (J). The assignment does not matter as long as you calculate the difference
What is the equation relating wavelength to the energy difference between two levels? | λ = hc / (E₂ - E₁)
How does the energy difference between two levels affect the wavelength of the emitted photon? | The larger the energy difference ΔE, the shorter the wavelength λ (they are inversely proportional)
A large energy level transition produces a photon with what kind of wavelength? | A short wavelength (high frequency, high energy)
A small energy level transition produces a photon with what kind of wavelength? | A long wavelength (low frequency, low energy)
How do you convert energy from eV to joules? | Multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
How do you convert energy from joules to eV? | Divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
How does emission spectra differ from absorption spectra in appearance? | Emission spectra show coloured lines on a black background. Absorption spectra show dark lines on a continuous coloured background
What is wave-particle duality? | The phenomenon where light (and matter) can behave both as a wave and as a particle
The phenomenon where light exhibits both wave and particle behaviour is called what? | Wave-particle duality
What evidence supports light behaving as a particle? | The photoelectric effect
What evidence supports light behaving as a wave? | Diffraction and interference of light, demonstrated by Young's Double-Slit Experiment
What is diffraction? | When waves pass through a gap or around an obstacle and spread out
How does the photon model explain the photoelectric effect? | Each electron absorbs only a single photon. Only photons with frequency above the threshold frequency have enough energy to release a photoelectron, which happens instantaneously
Why does the wave theory of light fail to explain the photoelectric effect? | Wave theory predicts that any frequency should cause emission given enough time, that energy absorption is gradual, and that kinetic energy should increase with intensity — none of which are observed
What does the wave theory of light incorrectly predict about the threshold frequency? | That any frequency of light can cause photoelectric emission if the exposure time is long enough
What does the wave theory incorrectly predict about the kinetic energy of photoelectrons? | That kinetic energy should increase with radiation intensity. In reality, increasing intensity only increases the number of photoelectrons per second, not their kinetic energy
What happens when light intensity is increased above the threshold frequency in the photoelectric effect? | More photoelectrons are emitted per second, but the maximum kinetic energy of each photoelectron stays the same
What is the equation for photon energy? | E = hf
Who discovered that matter can behave as a wave? | Louis de Broglie
What experimental evidence shows that electrons have wave properties? | A diffraction pattern (concentric rings) is produced when a beam of electrons is directed at a thin graphite film
Why is graphite film used in electron diffraction experiments? | Its crystalline structure has gaps between neighbouring atomic planes that are similar in size to the de Broglie wavelength of electrons, acting as a diffraction grating
What pattern is observed on the screen in an electron diffraction experiment? | A series of concentric rings (a diffraction pattern)
What would be observed if electrons behaved only as particles (not waves) when fired at graphite? | A uniform distribution across the screen, with no diffraction pattern
What happens to the diffraction ring diameter when the accelerating voltage is increased in an electron diffraction experiment? | The diameter decreases (rings get closer together)
Explain why increasing the accelerating voltage decreases the diffraction ring diameter | Higher voltage gives electrons more kinetic energy and speed, increasing their momentum and decreasing their de Broglie wavelength. A shorter wavelength produces less diffraction, so the rings are smaller
What is the relationship between the kinetic energy of an accelerated electron and the potential difference? | Ek = ½mv² = eV
What is the de Broglie equation? | λ = h/p, where λ is the de Broglie wavelength, h is Planck's constant, and p is momentum
The equation λ = h/p is known as what? | The de Broglie equation
What does the de Broglie equation relate? | Wavelength (a wave property) to momentum (a particle property)
Write the de Broglie wavelength in terms of mass and speed | λ = h/(mv)
Write the de Broglie wavelength in terms of kinetic energy | λ = h/√(2mE)
How can momentum be expressed in terms of kinetic energy and mass? | p = √(2mE)
How does the de Broglie wavelength change as a particle's momentum increases? | It decreases — greater momentum means a smaller de Broglie wavelength
How does the de Broglie wavelength change as a particle's momentum decreases? | It increases — smaller momentum means a larger de Broglie wavelength
Why does a proton have a shorter de Broglie wavelength than an electron at the same kinetic energy? | The proton has a much greater mass, so it has greater momentum (p = √(2mE)), and since λ = h/p, greater momentum means a shorter wavelength
In the equation λ = h/(mv), what does m represent? | Mass of the particle (kg)
In the equation λ = h/(mv), what does v represent? | Speed of the particle (m s⁻¹)
What must be true about the gap size for electron diffraction to be observed? | The gap must be similar in size to the de Broglie wavelength of the electrons (such as the spacing in an atomic lattice)
What happens to the electron diffraction pattern when electron speed is increased? | The diffraction rings get closer together (smaller diameter) because the de Broglie wavelength decreases
How does the radius of a diffraction pattern relate to wavelength? | Longer wavelength produces a larger radius (more spreading). Shorter wavelength produces a smaller radius
Electrons with smaller momentum produce what kind of diffraction pattern? | A more diffuse pattern with larger ring diameter (because smaller momentum means longer wavelength and more diffraction)
Why can high-energy electrons be used to investigate the size of an atomic nucleus? | High-energy electrons have a very short de Broglie wavelength, comparable to nuclear dimensions, allowing them to diffract from the nucleus
What is the regular spacing of atoms in a crystalline solid analogous to in optics? | A diffraction grating — it scatters electrons in a predictable pattern that can reveal the crystal structure
What can the observed electron diffraction pattern be used to determine? | The structure of the crystal producing the pattern
Who are considered the pioneers of quantum theory? | Max Planck, Niels Bohr, and Albert Einstein
Why did scientists need to develop quantum theory around 1900? | Discoveries such as the electron and gamma photon conflicted with existing classical models of the nature of matter
Explain why scientific models sometimes need to change | When experimental results do not match predictions from the existing model (e.g. the photoelectric effect contradicting wave theory), the model must be revised to explain the new evidence
What is peer review? | The process where scientific claims from published research are evaluated by other expert scientists in that field
What three things must peer reviewers check? | Validity (is the method appropriate?), originality (are results new?), and significance (are findings important?)
Why should non-peer-reviewed scientific claims be questioned? | There could be problems with the method, accuracy of results, or conclusions drawn that have not been independently checked
What is the difference between repeatable and reproducible results? | Repeatable means the same scientists can achieve the same results again. Reproducible means other independent teams of scientists can achieve the same results
What must happen before a new scientific theory like de Broglie's is accepted? | It must be evaluated through peer review, and enough experimental evidence must be found to support and validate it
