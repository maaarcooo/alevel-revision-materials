What is superposition? | The combination of two or more waves overlapping at the same point, where their amplitudes combine
When two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point equals what? | The sum of the displacements of the individual waves (principle of superposition)
State the principle of superposition | When two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point is equal to the sum of the displacements of the individual waves
What is interference? | The effect of the superposition of two or more waves
What is constructive interference? | When two waves meet in phase, their combined effect makes the resultant wave amplitude larger
What is destructive interference? | When two waves meet in antiphase, their combined effect means they cancel each other out
Two waves arriving in phase produce what type of interference? | Constructive interference
Two waves arriving in antiphase produce what type of interference? | Destructive interference
In constructive interference between two identical waves, what is the resultant amplitude? | Double the amplitude of each individual wave (2A)
In complete destructive interference between two identical waves, what is the resultant amplitude? | Zero
Waves superimposing to produce a larger resultant amplitude describes what? | Constructive interference
Waves superimposing to produce zero resultant amplitude describes what? | Destructive interference
When two waves are neither fully in phase nor in antiphase, what happens to the resultant amplitude? | It is somewhere between the maximum (constructive) and zero (destructive)
How are individual wave displacements combined during superposition? | They are combined in the same way as other vector quantities (positive and negative displacements are added algebraically)
What is coherence? | When waves have the same frequency and a constant phase difference
What two conditions must waves satisfy to be coherent? | 1) Same frequency, 2) Constant phase difference
Why must waves be coherent to produce a stable interference pattern? | Without the same frequency and a constant phase difference, the interference pattern would constantly shift and not be observable
Give two examples of coherent light sources | Monochromatic laser light, and sound waves from two nearby speakers emitting the same frequency
What makes non-coherent waves unable to produce stable interference? | An abrupt or random change in phase creates an inconsistent phase difference
What is path difference? | The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to the point where they meet
How does path difference differ from phase difference? | Path difference compares the amount of progress made by waves along a path; phase difference compares the distance between the phases (peaks and troughs) of coherent waves at a point
The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to the point where they meet is called what? | Path difference
What determines whether constructive or destructive interference occurs where two coherent waves meet? | The path difference between the two waves
What is the condition for constructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = nλ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
What is the condition for destructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = (n + ½)λ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
A path difference of nλ produces what type of interference? | Constructive interference
A path difference of (n + ½)λ produces what type of interference? | Destructive interference
A path difference of λ/2 results in what? | Destructive interference (first order, n = 0)
A path difference of 2λ results in what? | Constructive interference (n = 2)
In path difference equations, what values can n take? | n = 0, 1, 2, 3... (any non-negative integer)
What is a wavefront diagram? | A diagram where curved lines represent each wavefront (peak or trough), used to show interference between waves
On a wavefront diagram, what happens where a peak meets a peak? | Constructive interference occurs
On a wavefront diagram, what happens where a trough meets a trough? | Constructive interference occurs
On a wavefront diagram, what happens where a peak meets a trough? | Destructive interference occurs
How can you determine the type of interference at a point on a wavefront diagram? | Count the number of wavelengths from each source to that point and calculate the path difference
Why are lasers ideal for analysing diffraction and interference patterns? | They produce light that is coherent (constant phase difference and frequency) and monochromatic (same wavelength)
What does coherent mean in the context of laser light? | The light has a constant phase difference and the same frequency
What does monochromatic mean? | Having a single wavelength (or frequency)
In a laser diffraction pattern, bright fringes correspond to what? | Areas of constructive interference
In a laser diffraction pattern, dark fringes correspond to what? | Areas of destructive interference
Other light sources such as filament bulbs and sodium lamps are described as what, compared to a laser? | Non-coherent
Why do filament bulbs produce white light rather than interference fringes? | They are non-coherent sources (they do not have a constant phase difference or a single frequency)
Name three laser safety precautions | Never look directly at a laser or its reflection; wear laser safety goggles; place a 'laser on' warning light outside the room
Why are lasers dangerous? | They produce a very high-energy beam of light that can cause permanent eye damage or blindness
Why should reflective surfaces be removed when using lasers? | To prevent laser light from reflecting into someone's eyes
How can two-source interference be demonstrated with sound? | Using two speakers emitting coherent sound of the same frequency
In sound wave interference, constructive interference produces what effect? | The sound appears louder (compressions and rarefactions from each wave line up)
In sound wave interference, destructive interference produces what effect? | Zero sound is heard (a compression from one wave lines up with a rarefaction from the other, cancelling out)
What technology uses destructive interference of sound waves? | Noise-cancelling headphones
How can two-source interference be detected with microwaves? | Using a moveable microwave detector that measures amplitude/intensity at different positions
What does the microwave detector measure in regions of constructive interference? | Maximum amplitude or intensity
What does the microwave detector measure in regions of destructive interference? | Minimum or zero amplitude (no signal)
What is the relationship between intensity and amplitude of a wave? | Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude (I ∝ A²)
I ∝ A² — what do I and A represent? | I = intensity of the wave (W m⁻²), A = amplitude of the wave (m)
If the amplitude of a wave doubles, by what factor does the intensity change? | It increases by a factor of 4 (since I ∝ A², doubling A gives 2² = 4 times I)
The intensity of a wave is proportional to the square of what quantity? | Amplitude
What does Young's double-slit experiment demonstrate? | That light is a wave, by producing both a diffraction and an interference pattern through constructive and destructive interference
Describe the setup of Young's double-slit experiment | A laser is placed behind a single slit (to diffract the beam), which then passes through a double slit. The resulting interference pattern is observed on a distant screen
Why is a single slit sometimes placed before the double slit in Young's experiment? | To diffract the laser beam so it illuminates both slits of the double slit, producing two coherent light sources
In Young's double-slit experiment, what are bright fringes also called? | Maxima (or interference fringes)
In Young's double-slit experiment, what are dark fringes also called? | Minima
What forms the bright fringes (maxima) in Young's experiment? | Constructive interference of light
What forms the dark fringes (minima) in Young's experiment? | Destructive interference of light
In an ideal Young's double-slit pattern, describe the appearance of the bright fringes | Each bright fringe is identical, with the same width and equal maximum intensity
In reality, why does the central maximum in a double-slit pattern appear brighter than outer fringes? | Each slit also acts as a single slit that diffracts light, so the double-slit interference pattern is modulated by the single-slit diffraction envelope
What is the fringe spacing equation for Young's double-slit experiment? | w = λD / s
In the equation w = λD / s, what does w represent? | The fringe width (distance between successive bright fringes) in metres
In the equation w = λD / s, what does D represent? | The distance between the double slit and the screen in metres
In the equation w = λD / s, what does s represent? | The separation between the centres of the two slits in metres
In the equation w = λD / s, what does λ represent? | The wavelength of the light source in metres
When measuring fringe spacing, which variable is typically the largest? | D (the slit-to-screen distance), normally several metres
When measuring fringe spacing, which variable is typically the smallest? | s (the slit separation), normally in millimetres
How does fringe spacing w change if the wavelength λ increases? | w increases (fringe spacing gets wider)
How does fringe spacing w change if the slit-to-screen distance D increases? | w increases (fringe spacing gets wider)
How does fringe spacing w change if the slit separation s decreases? | w increases (fringe spacing gets wider)
How is fringe width w best measured experimentally? | Measure the distance across several fringes and divide by the number of fringe widths
How does path difference relate to maxima and minima in Young's experiment? | Maxima occur where path difference = nλ (constructive); minima occur where path difference = (n + ½)λ (destructive)
What does n = 0 represent in a double-slit interference pattern? | The central maximum (zero path difference)
What does n = 1 represent in a double-slit interference pattern? | The first-order maximum on either side of the central maximum
Describe the white light double-slit interference pattern | A white central maximum, with spectra on either side where violet/blue appears nearest to the centre and red appears furthest away
Why is the central maximum white when white light passes through a double slit? | Every wavelength of light interferes constructively at the centre (path difference = 0 for all wavelengths)
In a white light double-slit pattern, which colour appears nearest to the central maximum? | Violet/blue (shortest wavelength, diffracted the least)
In a white light double-slit pattern, which colour appears furthest from the central maximum? | Red (longest wavelength, diffracted the most)
Why do the spectra in a white light double-slit pattern become blurred further from the centre? | The fringe spacing gets so small that spectra from adjacent orders eventually overlap and merge
How does a white light double-slit pattern differ from a monochromatic laser pattern? | White light produces a white central fringe with coloured spectra either side; a laser produces equally spaced bright fringes of a single colour
How does a white light double-slit pattern differ from a diffraction grating pattern? | The double-slit pattern has wider, less sharp maxima that blur and overlap; a diffraction grating produces sharp, well-separated spectra
What did Newton (1672) propose about the nature of light? | That visible light is a stream of microscopic particles called corpuscles
Why was Newton's corpuscular theory of light eventually replaced? | Corpuscles could not explain interference or diffraction effects
What did Huygens (1678) propose about light? | The Wave Theory of Light, describing light as a series of wavefronts where every point is a source of waves (Huygens' wavelets)
Huygens' theory describes light as a series of wavefronts where every point acts as a source of secondary waves. What are these secondary waves called? | Huygens' wavelets
What did Thomas Young's (1801) double-slit experiment prove? | That light is a wave, by demonstrating that light can undergo constructive and destructive interference
What did Maxwell (1862) show about light? | That electric and magnetic fields obey the wave equation, meaning light consists of perpendicular oscillating electric and magnetic fields (electromagnetic waves)
What did Einstein (1905) discover about the nature of light? | That light also behaves as a particle, as demonstrated by the photoelectric effect, describing light in terms of packets of energy called photons
What is wave-particle duality? | The understanding that light (and other quantum entities) can behave both as a wave and as a particle
What is the name for packets of light energy in Einstein's model? | Photons
In the Required Practical for Young's double-slit experiment, what is the independent variable? | The distance between the slits and the screen (D)
In the Required Practical for Young's double-slit experiment, what is the dependent variable? | The fringe width (w)
In the Required Practical for Young's double-slit experiment, name two control variables | Wavelength of laser light (λ) and slit separation (s)
When plotting results from the Young's double-slit practical, what graph is drawn? | w (fringe width) against D (slit-to-screen distance)
In the w vs D graph from Young's experiment, what does the gradient equal? | λ/s (wavelength divided by slit separation)
How is the wavelength of laser light calculated from the w vs D graph? | λ = gradient × s (gradient multiplied by the slit separation)
What is the diffraction grating equation? | nλ = d sin θ
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does d represent? | The distance between adjacent slits on the grating (m)
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does θ represent? | The angle between the normal to the grating and the direction of the nth order maximum
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does n represent? | The order of the diffraction maximum (0, 1, 2, 3...)
How is the slit spacing d related to the number of slits per metre N? | d = 1/N
If a diffraction grating has 300 slits per mm, what is the slit spacing d? | d = 1/(300 × 10³) = 3.33 × 10⁻⁶ m
How is the angle θ calculated in the diffraction grating practical? | tan θ = h/D, so θ = tan⁻¹(h/D), where h is the distance from the central maximum to the nth order maximum and D is the grating-to-screen distance
Why is tan θ = h/D used instead of the small angle approximation in the diffraction grating experiment? | The angles are not small enough for the small angle approximation to be valid
How does the fringe spacing equation w = λD/s differ from the grating equation nλ = d sin θ? | The fringe spacing equation applies to double-slit interference and uses a small-angle approximation; the grating equation applies to diffraction gratings and uses exact trigonometry (sin θ)
How does the pattern from a diffraction grating compare to a double-slit pattern? | The grating produces sharper, brighter, more widely spaced maxima because more slits contribute to the interference
Name one systematic error in the Young's double-slit or diffraction grating practical | Not using a set square to ensure the beam is perpendicular to the screen/grating, causing parallax error
Name one way to reduce random error when measuring fringe width | Measure across all visible fringes and divide by the number of fringes, or take multiple measurements and calculate the mean
Why should the experiment be conducted in a darkened room? | So the fringes are clearly visible against the background
Why does increasing the slit-to-screen distance D improve accuracy in the diffraction grating experiment? | It increases the fringe separation, making measurements of h more precise (lower percentage uncertainty)
What class of laser should be used in school experiments, and what is the maximum output? | Class 2, with a maximum output of no more than 1 mW
What is the accepted wavelength value for a standard school red laser? | 635 nm
How does constructive interference of sound waves differ from constructive interference of light waves? | In sound, compressions and rarefactions line up to produce louder sound; in light, peaks and peaks line up to produce bright fringes. Both involve superposition of in-phase waves, but sound is longitudinal and light is transverse
How does path difference differ from the distance between two paths? | Path difference is the difference in the lengths of two paths from source to a point; it is not the spatial separation between the two paths themselves
