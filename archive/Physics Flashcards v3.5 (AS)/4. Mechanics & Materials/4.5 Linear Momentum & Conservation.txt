What is linear momentum? | The product of mass and velocity of an object moving in one dimension
The product of an object's mass and velocity is called what? | Linear momentum
What is the equation for momentum? | p = mv
In the equation p = mv, what does p represent? | Momentum, measured in kg m s⁻¹
In the equation p = mv, what does m represent? | Mass, measured in kg
In the equation p = mv, what does v represent? | Velocity, measured in m s⁻¹
What are the SI units of momentum? | kg m s⁻¹
Is momentum a scalar or vector quantity? | Vector — it has both magnitude and direction
When does the linear momentum of an object remain constant? | When no external resultant force acts on the system
If an object reverses direction, what happens to the sign of its momentum? | It becomes negative (opposite sign to the initial direction)
Why does a ball travelling in the opposite direction to its initial motion have negative momentum? | Velocity is negative when opposite to the chosen positive direction, and since p = mv, the momentum is also negative
A 60 g ball travels at 75 m s⁻¹ and a 3 kg brick travels at 1.5 m s⁻¹. Which has greater momentum? | They have equal momentum (both 4.5 kg m s⁻¹), showing that a light fast object can have the same momentum as a heavy slow object
State the principle of conservation of linear momentum | The total momentum before a collision equals the total momentum after a collision, provided no external force acts
What condition must be met for momentum to be conserved in a collision? | No external resultant force must act on the system
What is the conservation of momentum equation for two objects A and B colliding? | p_Ai + p_Bi = p_Af + p_Bf
Explain why opposing momentum vectors can result in a net momentum of zero | Momentum is a vector, so equal momenta in opposite directions cancel out, giving a total of zero
What happens to the momentum of an object that rebounds after a collision? | Its velocity (and momentum) changes sign — positive before the collision and negative after (or vice versa)
What is the momentum of a stationary object? | Zero, because its velocity is zero (p = mv = m × 0 = 0)
What are external forces? | Forces that act on a system from outside, e.g. friction and weight
What are internal forces? | Forces exchanged by particles within the system, e.g. tension in a string
Forces that act on a system from outside (e.g. friction, weight) are called what? | External forces
Forces exchanged between particles within a system (e.g. tension in a string) are called what? | Internal forces
What does it mean for a system to be 'closed' or 'isolated'? | The system has no external forces acting on it
Why does a boat move backwards when a swimmer dives forward from it? | The initial total momentum is zero. To conserve momentum with no external horizontal forces, the boat must gain equal and opposite momentum to the diver
A 0.80 kg trolley at 3.0 m s⁻¹ collides with a stationary 1.60 kg trolley and they stick together. What is their common velocity? | v = (0.80 × 3.0) / (0.80 + 1.60) = 2.4 / 2.4 = 1.0 m s⁻¹
How can force be defined in terms of momentum? | Force is the rate of change of momentum: F = Δp/Δt
What is the equation relating force to rate of change of momentum? | F = Δp/Δt
In the equation F = Δp/Δt, what does Δp represent? | Change in momentum, measured in kg m s⁻¹
In the equation F = Δp/Δt, what does Δt represent? | Change in time over which the force acts, measured in seconds (s)
How is change in momentum calculated? | Δp = p_final − p_initial = mv − mu
The rate of change of momentum equals what? | The resultant force acting on the object (Newton's second law in terms of momentum)
If a force opposes an object's initial velocity, what sign does the force have? | Negative (taking the initial direction of motion as positive)
Explain how Newton's third law applies to the forces during a collision | The force exerted by object A on B is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the force exerted by B on A
What is impulse? | The product of force and the time for which it acts: Impulse = FΔt
The product of force and the time for which it acts is called what? | Impulse
What are the units of impulse? | Newton seconds (N s)
What is the equation for impulse? | Impulse = FΔt
How is impulse related to change in momentum? | Impulse equals the change in momentum: Impulse = Δp = mv − mu
Why is it difficult to directly measure force during a short-duration impact? | The force acts for a very short time, making it hard to measure both the magnitude and duration directly
How can impulse be measured indirectly? | By calculating the change in momentum (Δp = mv − mu), since impulse equals change in momentum
The impulse equations (FΔt = Δp) are only valid when what condition is met? | The force F must be constant
Is impulse a scalar or vector quantity? | Vector — it has both magnitude and direction
In what direction does the impulse always act? | In the direction of the resultant force
Explain why a small force over a long time can have the same effect as a large force over a short time | Both can produce the same impulse (FΔt), meaning the same change in momentum
Why does the base unit analysis show that FΔt = Δp is consistent? | 1 N = 1 kg m s⁻², so N × s = kg m s⁻² × s = kg m s⁻¹, which matches the units of momentum
A 58 g tennis ball at 30 m s⁻¹ is hit back at 20 m s⁻¹. What is the impulse? | Impulse = m(v − u) = 0.058 × (−20 − 30) = −2.9 N s (negative indicates direction opposite to initial motion)
Why is the impulse on a rebounding object larger than if the object simply stopped? | Because the velocity changes sign, giving a larger total change in velocity (v − u) and therefore a larger change in momentum
Explain why hailstones exert a greater impulse on an umbrella than raindrops | Hailstones have larger mass and bounce back (rebound), so their change in momentum is greater than raindrops which splatter and barely change direction
Why does more force need to be applied to hold an umbrella upright in hail compared to rain? | Hailstones produce a greater impulse (larger change in momentum due to rebounding), requiring a greater force from the umbrella over the same contact time
Explain why a cricket fielder moves their hands backwards when catching a ball | Moving hands back increases the time over which the ball's momentum changes to zero, reducing the force experienced by the fielder's hands (since F = Δp/Δt)
How does increasing the contact time during a collision reduce the force experienced? | For the same change in momentum (Δp), increasing Δt in F = Δp/Δt gives a smaller force
If the force on a body is not constant, how is the impulse found from a force-time graph? | Impulse equals the area under the force-time graph
What does the area under a force-time graph represent? | Impulse (change in momentum)
If a force-time graph is a curve, how do you find the impulse? | Count the squares under the curve and multiply by the value of each square
If a force-time graph is made of straight lines, how do you find the impulse? | Split the area into geometric shapes (triangles, rectangles, trapeziums) and sum their areas
On a force-time graph, explain why a wider curve with a lower peak can represent the same impulse as a narrow curve with a high peak | Both have the same area under the graph, meaning the same change in momentum, but the wider curve has a lower maximum force
How are impact forces reduced to prevent injury? | By increasing the contact time over which the change in momentum occurs, which reduces the force (F = Δp/Δt)
How do seat belts reduce injury in a collision? | They stretch slightly, increasing the contact time over which the passenger's momentum reaches zero, thereby reducing the force exerted on them
How do airbags reduce injury in a collision? | They act as a soft cushion that increases the contact time over which the passenger's momentum changes, reducing the force exerted on them
How do crumple zones reduce injury in a collision? | They crush in a controlled way, increasing the time over which the vehicle's momentum reaches zero, which reduces the force on the passengers
Where are crumple zones located on a vehicle? | At the front and back of the vehicle
Explain why vehicles appear heavily damaged even after relatively small collisions | The crumple zones are designed to deform on purpose to absorb energy and increase contact time, reducing force on passengers
How does the follow-through action in kicking a football increase the ball's velocity? | It increases the contact time between foot and ball, transferring more momentum (larger impulse) to the ball
How does packaging such as bubble wrap protect fragile items? | It increases the time over which impact forces act on the item, reducing the peak force and the risk of damage
On a force-time graph, what does a lower peak and wider base indicate compared to a higher peak and narrower base? | A longer contact time and lower maximum force for the same impulse — this is the effect of safety features
What is an elastic collision? | A collision in which kinetic energy is conserved
What is an inelastic collision? | A collision in which kinetic energy is not conserved
A collision where kinetic energy is conserved is called what? | An elastic collision
A collision where kinetic energy is not conserved is called what? | An inelastic collision
How does an elastic collision differ from an inelastic collision? | In an elastic collision, kinetic energy is conserved and objects typically bounce apart. In an inelastic collision, kinetic energy is not conserved and objects often stick together
Is momentum conserved in both elastic and inelastic collisions? | Yes — momentum is always conserved in all collisions and explosions (provided no external forces act)
How do you determine whether a collision is elastic or inelastic? | Compare the total kinetic energy before and after the collision. If they are equal, it is elastic. If kinetic energy is lost, it is inelastic
What is the equation for kinetic energy? | KE = ½mv²
In elastic collisions, what typically happens to the colliding objects after the collision? | They bounce apart and move in opposite directions
In inelastic collisions, what typically happens to the colliding objects after the collision? | They stick together and move as a single combined object
What is an explosion in the context of momentum? | A situation where objects move apart from rest, such as a gun recoiling after firing or a nucleus emitting a particle — total momentum is conserved
When two objects stick together after a collision, what mass is used to calculate their combined momentum? | The sum of the two individual masses (m_A + m_B)
A 0.80 kg trolley at 3.0 m s⁻¹ hits a stationary 1.60 kg trolley and they stick together at 1.0 m s⁻¹. Is this elastic or inelastic? | Inelastic. KE before = ½(0.80)(3.0²) = 3.6 J. KE after = ½(2.4)(1.0²) = 1.2 J. Kinetic energy is not conserved.
Name three vehicle safety features designed to reduce impact force on passengers | Seat belts, airbags, and crumple zones
Explain why all vehicle safety features work on the same physical principle | They all increase the contact time over which the passenger's momentum changes to zero, reducing the force (F = Δp/Δt) and therefore reducing injury risk
If mass is given in grams, how do you convert to SI units for momentum calculations? | Divide by 1000 to convert to kilograms
Why must you assign a positive direction and keep signs consistent throughout a momentum problem? | Momentum is a vector quantity, so direction matters. Inconsistent signs lead to incorrect results, especially when objects change direction
