What is density? | The mass per unit volume of an object
The mass per unit volume of an object is called what? | Density
What is the equation for density? | ρ = m / V
What does ρ represent in the density equation? | Density (kg m⁻³)
What does m represent in the density equation? | Mass (kg)
What does V represent in the density equation? | Volume (m³)
What are the SI units of density? | kg m⁻³
If mass is measured in grams and volume in cm³, what are the units of density? | g / cm³
Which state of matter is generally less dense, a gas or a solid? | A gas
Why does a balloon have a lower mass than a small bar of lead despite occupying more volume? | The balloon is made from a much less dense material, so its mass per unit volume is much lower
What is the volume of a sphere? | V = (4/3)πr³
What is the volume of a cube with side length d? | V = d³
What is the volume of a cylinder? | V = πr²l
When converting mm³ to m³, what conversion factor is used? | Divide by (1000)³ = 10⁹, so 1 mm³ = 1 × 10⁻⁹ m³
When converting cm³ to m³, what conversion factor is used? | Divide by (100)³ = 10⁶, so 1 cm³ = 1 × 10⁻⁶ m³
When converting a larger unit to a smaller unit, do you multiply or divide? | Multiply
When converting a smaller unit to a larger unit, do you multiply or divide? | Divide
When converting squared or cubic units, what must you do with the conversion factor? | Square or cube the conversion factor as well
What happens when a force is applied to each end of a spring or wire? | It stretches
What is elasticity? | The property of a material that allows it to return to its original shape after a deforming force is removed
What does Hooke's Law state? | The extension of a material is directly proportional to the applied force (load) up to the limit of proportionality
The extension of a material is directly proportional to the applied force up to the limit of proportionality — what law is this? | Hooke's Law
What is the equation for Hooke's Law? | F = kΔL
What does F represent in Hooke's Law? | Force applied (N)
What does k represent in Hooke's Law? | Spring constant (N m⁻¹)
What does ΔL represent in Hooke's Law? | Extension (m)
What does the spring constant measure? | The stiffness of a material
The stiffness of a material is measured by which quantity? | The spring constant, k
What are the units of the spring constant? | N m⁻¹
What does a larger spring constant indicate about a material? | The material is stiffer
Does Hooke's Law apply to compressions as well as extensions? | Yes, it applies to both
How is extension calculated? | Extension = stretched length − unstretched length
How does extension differ from compression? | Extension is an increase in length; compression is a decrease in length
On a force-extension graph, how is Hooke's Law region identified? | It is the straight line section passing through the origin
What is the gradient of a force-extension graph in the Hooke's Law region equal to? | The spring constant, k
What is the gradient of an extension-force graph (extension on y-axis, force on x-axis) equal to? | 1/k (the reciprocal of the spring constant)
What is the limit of proportionality? | The point beyond which extension is no longer directly proportional to the applied force
The point beyond which extension is no longer proportional to force — what is this called? | The limit of proportionality
What is the elastic limit? | The point beyond which the material will no longer return to its original length when the force is removed
The point beyond which a material is permanently deformed — what is this called? | The elastic limit
How does the limit of proportionality differ from the elastic limit? | The limit of proportionality is where Hooke's Law stops applying (F ∝ ΔL ends). The elastic limit is where permanent deformation begins. The elastic limit is at or slightly beyond the limit of proportionality
Why must you check the axes before finding the spring constant from a graph? | If the axes are swapped (extension on y, force on x), the gradient gives 1/k, not k
What is tensile stress? | The force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material
The force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material is called what? | Tensile stress
What is the equation for tensile stress? | σ = F / A
What does σ represent in the stress equation? | Tensile stress (Pa)
What does A represent in the stress equation? | Cross-sectional area (m²)
What are the units of tensile stress? | Pascals (Pa)
Why does tensile stress have the same units as pressure? | Both are defined as force divided by area (F/A)
What is the ultimate tensile stress? | The maximum force per original cross-sectional area a wire is able to support until it breaks
The maximum stress a material can withstand before breaking — what is this called? | Ultimate tensile stress (UTS)
What is tensile strain? | The extension per unit length (ΔL / L)
The extension per unit length of a material is called what? | Tensile strain
What is the equation for tensile strain? | Strain = ΔL / L
Why is strain a dimensionless quantity? | Because it is the ratio of two lengths (extension ÷ original length), so the units cancel
How can strain be expressed as a percentage? | Strain × 100, e.g. an extension of 0.005 m on a 0.1 m wire gives (0.005 / 0.1) × 100 = 5%
Since strain is a ratio, do the extension and original length need to be in metres? | No, they just need to be in the same units as each other
What do stress-strain curves describe? | The properties of materials, such as whether they are brittle or ductile, and the regions of elastic and plastic behaviour
What is the yield stress? | The stress at which the material extends plastically for no or a small increase in stress
The stress at which a material begins to deform plastically for little or no increase in stress — what is this? | Yield stress
What is breaking stress? | The maximum stress a material can stand before it fractures
What does the area under the straight-line (Hooke's Law) region of a stress-strain graph represent? | The elastic strain energy stored per unit volume
What is the elastic region on a stress-strain graph? | The region up to the elastic limit where the material returns to its original shape when force is removed
What is the plastic region on a stress-strain graph? | The region beyond the elastic limit where the material is permanently deformed
How does the elastic region differ from the plastic region on a stress-strain graph? | In the elastic region, the material returns to its original shape when unloaded. In the plastic region, permanent deformation occurs and the material does not return to its original shape
What is elastic strain energy? | The energy stored in a material when it is stretched within its elastic limit
What is the equation for elastic strain energy when Hooke's Law is obeyed? | E = ½FΔL
What does E represent in the elastic strain energy equation? | Elastic strain energy or work done (J)
What does the area under a force-extension graph represent? | The work done on the material (or elastic strain energy stored)
Is the equation E = ½FΔL valid beyond the limit of proportionality? | No, it only applies in the Hooke's Law region. Beyond it, you must find the full area under the curve
Using Hooke's Law, how can the elastic strain energy equation be rewritten in terms of k and ΔL? | E = ½k(ΔL)²
When a force-extension graph is not linear, how do you calculate the work done? | Find the total area under the curve, splitting it into segments (triangles, rectangles, trapeziums) and summing them
What is a ductile material? | A material that can undergo significant plastic deformation before fracturing, stretching into a new shape before breaking
A material that undergoes significant plastic deformation before breaking is called what? | Ductile
Give two examples of ductile materials | Copper and rubber
What is a brittle material? | A material that fractures with little or no plastic deformation
A material that fractures with little or no plastic deformation is called what? | Brittle
Give two examples of brittle materials | Glass and concrete
How does a brittle material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin with no or negligible curved region before fracture
How does a ductile material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin followed by a significant curved region before fracture
Why is copper a suitable material for making wires? | It is both a good electrical conductor and ductile, so it can be drawn into thin wires without breaking
What is elastic deformation? | Deformation where the material returns to its original shape when the load is removed
What is plastic deformation? | Permanent deformation where the material does not return to its original shape when the load is removed
How should plastic deformation be described in an exam? | As the material being permanently deformed, not simply as "does not obey Hooke's Law"
What happens when a metal wire is loaded beyond its limit of proportionality and then unloaded? | It undergoes plastic deformation; the unloading line is parallel to the loading line but does not pass through the origin, indicating permanent extension
Why is the unloading line parallel to the loading line for a plastically deformed metal wire? | Because the spring constant (k) has not changed — the intermolecular forces between bonds are identical
What does the area between the loading and unloading lines on a force-extension graph represent? | The work done to permanently deform the material
On a rubber band's force-extension graph, what does the area between the loading and unloading curves (area X) represent? | The energy transferred to thermal energy (work done heating the rubber)
On a rubber band's force-extension graph, what does area Y (under the unloading curve) represent? | The work done by the rubber when returning to its original shape
On a rubber band's force-extension graph, what does area X + Y represent? | The total work done in stretching the rubber band
Why does a rubber band warm up when repeatedly stretched and released? | Some elastic strain energy is converted to thermal energy during each loading-unloading cycle (shown by the area between the curves)
Is a rubber band elastic or plastic after being loaded and unloaded? | Elastic — it returns to its original length with no permanent extension
Why is the unloading curve for a rubber band below the loading curve? | The rubber stores more strain energy during loading than it returns during unloading; the difference is dissipated as thermal energy
What three types of energy does a mass on a vertical spring convert between? | Elastic potential energy (EPE), kinetic energy (KE), and gravitational potential energy (GPE)
For a mass on a vertical spring, at which position is GPE at a maximum? | At the highest point (maximum compression of the spring)
For a mass on a vertical spring, at which position is KE at a maximum? | At the equilibrium position, where the mass moves at its maximum speed
For a mass on a vertical spring, at which position is EPE at a maximum? | At the lowest point (maximum extension of the spring)
For a mass on a vertical spring, at which positions is KE equal to zero? | At the highest and lowest points, where the mass is momentarily stationary
For a horizontal mass-spring system, which two energy types does it convert between? | Kinetic energy and elastic potential energy (no GPE to consider)
What are the three main components of a vehicle suspension system? | Tyres, springs, and shock absorbers
What is the function of shock absorbers in a vehicle? | To absorb or dampen the compression and rebound of the springs, keeping tyres on the road and improving passenger comfort
How do shock absorbers convert energy? | They convert kinetic energy from the vehicle's movement into thermal energy, which is dissipated
What happens if a vehicle has no shock absorbers and hits a bump? | The wheel can lose contact with the road, slam back down causing large vibrations, potentially damaging the vehicle and making it uncomfortable and unsafe
What happens to the resistance provided by a shock absorber as the springs move faster? | The resistance increases — the faster the springs move, the more resistance the shock absorber provides
