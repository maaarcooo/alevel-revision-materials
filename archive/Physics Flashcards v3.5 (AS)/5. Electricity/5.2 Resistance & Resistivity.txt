What is resistivity? | A property that describes the extent to which a material opposes the flow of electric current through it
A material property describing how strongly it opposes electric current flow — what is this called? | Resistivity
What is the SI unit of resistivity? | Ohm metre (Ω m)
What is the equation relating resistance, resistivity, length and cross-sectional area? | R = ρL / A
In the equation R = ρL / A, what does ρ represent? | Resistivity (Ω m)
In the equation R = ρL / A, what does L represent? | Length of the wire (m)
In the equation R = ρL / A, what does A represent? | Cross-sectional area of the wire (m²)
When calculating the resistance of a wire using R = ρL / A, which equation is used to find the cross-sectional area? | A = πd² / 4 (where d is the diameter of the wire)
What happens to resistance when the length of a wire is doubled (all else constant)? | Resistance doubles (R is directly proportional to L)
What happens to resistance when the cross-sectional area of a wire is doubled (all else constant)? | Resistance halves (R is inversely proportional to A)
What happens to resistance when the diameter of a wire is doubled (all else constant)? | Resistance drops to one quarter (area is proportional to diameter squared, so doubling diameter quadruples area)
Explain why resistance increases with the length of a wire | A longer wire means free electrons must travel further, making more collisions with metal ions, so more energy is transferred and resistance is greater
Explain why resistance decreases with greater cross-sectional area | A wider wire provides more pathways for free electrons to flow through, reducing the number of collisions per unit length, so resistance decreases
Explain the microscopic origin of electrical resistance in a metal wire | Free electrons moving through the wire collide with metal ions in the lattice. These collisions transfer kinetic energy to the ions as thermal energy, impeding the flow of charge
What causes electrical heating in a metal wire? | Free electrons collide with metal ions and transfer kinetic energy to them, which increases the thermal energy of the wire
Is resistivity a property of the material or the component? | It is a property of the material itself
Does resistivity depend on temperature? | Yes, resistivity is dependent on temperature
The resistivity of copper at room temperature is approximately [...] Ω m | 1.7 × 10⁻⁸
Why is copper used for electrical wiring? | Copper has very low resistivity at room temperature, so current flows through it very easily with minimal energy loss
How does the resistivity of metals compare to that of insulators? | Metals have very low resistivity (order of 10⁻⁸ Ω m), while insulators have extremely high resistivity (order of 10¹² to 10¹⁵ Ω m)
Explain why virtually no current flows through an insulator | Insulators have extremely high resistivity, so even with a potential difference applied, the resistance is so large that negligible current can flow
How does the resistivity of semiconductors compare to metals and insulators? | Semiconductors have resistivity values between metals and insulators (e.g. germanium ≈ 0.6 Ω m, silicon ≈ 2.3 × 10³ Ω m)
Higher resistivity means [...] resistance for a given shape of component | higher
Explain why resistance increases with temperature in a metallic conductor | At higher temperatures, metal ions vibrate with greater amplitude. Free electrons collide more frequently with these vibrating ions, impeding the flow of charge, so resistance increases
For a metallic conductor obeying Ohm's law, what happens to resistance when temperature increases? | Resistance increases
For a metallic conductor obeying Ohm's law, what happens to resistance when temperature decreases? | Resistance decreases
What is a thermistor? | A non-ohmic conductor and sensory resistor whose resistance varies with temperature
A non-ohmic component whose resistance changes with temperature — what is this? | A thermistor
What does NTC stand for in the context of thermistors? | Negative temperature coefficient
What does "negative temperature coefficient" mean for a thermistor? | As temperature increases, the resistance of the thermistor decreases (and vice versa)
How does an NTC thermistor's response to temperature differ from that of a metal? | In a metal, resistance increases with temperature. In an NTC thermistor, resistance decreases with temperature
Explain why resistance decreases with temperature in an NTC thermistor | As temperature increases, more charge carriers are released from atoms in the semiconductor material, greatly increasing the number available for conduction, which more than compensates for increased lattice vibrations
What happens to the resistance of a thermistor when it gets hotter? | Its resistance decreases
What happens to the resistance of a thermistor when it gets cooler? | Its resistance increases
Name three applications of thermistors | Temperature sensors in ovens, fire alarms, and digital thermometers
Describe the shape of the temperature-resistance graph for an NTC thermistor | A curve that starts high at low temperatures and decreases steeply, then levels off at higher temperatures (exponential decay shape)
A thermistor is in series with resistor R and a battery. If the temperature of the thermistor decreases, what happens to the p.d. across the thermistor? | The p.d. across the thermistor increases (its resistance increases, so it takes a greater share of the total voltage)
A thermistor is in series with resistor R and a battery. If the temperature of the thermistor decreases, what happens to the current in the circuit? | The current decreases (the thermistor's resistance increases, increasing total resistance, so current falls)
What is a superconductor? | A material with zero resistance below a critical temperature
A material that has zero electrical resistance below a specific temperature — what is this called? | A superconductor
What is the critical temperature (in the context of superconductivity)? | The temperature at which a material becomes superconducting (i.e. its resistance drops to zero)
The temperature at which a material's resistance drops to zero — what is this called? | The critical temperature (also called the transition temperature)
What is an alternative name for the critical temperature of a superconductor? | The transition temperature
What is the critical temperature of mercury? | 4.2 K
Name a common example of a superconducting material and its critical temperature | Mercury, with a critical temperature of 4.2 K
What happens to the resistivity of a superconductor at its critical temperature? | The resistivity drops to exactly zero (not just very low — truly zero)
How does the resistivity-temperature graph of a superconductor differ from that of a normal metal? | A normal metal's resistivity decreases gradually towards zero as temperature falls. A superconductor's resistivity drops sharply to exactly zero at the critical temperature
Why are superconductors useful for applications requiring large electric currents? | Because they have zero resistance, no electrical energy is wasted as thermal energy, so very large currents can flow without power loss or heating
Name two general uses of superconductors | Production of strong magnetic fields and reduction of energy loss in the transmission of electric power
Name three specific applications of superconductors | Any three from: MRI scanners, particle accelerators, maglev trains, power cables, electromagnets, transformers, generators, motors, fusion reactors, microchips
Why are superconductors used in MRI scanners? | MRI scanners need extremely strong magnetic fields, which require very large currents flowing through electromagnets. Superconductors carry these currents with zero resistance and no energy loss
Why are superconductors used in particle accelerators? | Particle accelerators need very large magnetic fields to bend and accelerate particles. Superconducting electromagnets can carry the enormous currents required without energy loss
What is a maglev train? | A magnetic levitation train that uses powerful superconducting electromagnets on the train and rails to levitate the train above the track, eliminating friction
How does the resistivity of a material change as its temperature is lowered (before reaching any critical temperature)? | The resistivity decreases as temperature is lowered
What is the aim of the required practical on investigating resistivity? | To determine the resistivity of a constantan wire by measuring how resistance varies with length
In the resistivity practical, what is the independent variable? | The length L of the wire (m)
In the resistivity practical, what is the dependent variable? | The current I through the wire (A)
Name two control variables in the resistivity practical | The voltage across the wire and the material the wire is made from
Why is a micrometer screw gauge used rather than a ruler to measure the wire's diameter? | A micrometer has a much higher resolution (0.01 mm) than a ruler (1 mm), which is necessary because the wire diameter is very small
Why should the diameter of the wire be measured 5-10 times at random positions along the wire? | To reduce random error by calculating a mean diameter, and to check the wire has a uniform cross-section
In the resistivity practical, how is the ammeter connected? | In series with the wire
In the resistivity practical, how is the voltmeter connected? | In parallel with the wire
Why should the current be switched off between readings in the resistivity practical? | To prevent the wire from heating up, which would change its temperature, altering its resistivity and resistance, and introducing error
Why must only small currents flow through the wire in the resistivity practical? | Larger currents cause greater heating of the wire, which changes the wire's temperature and therefore its resistivity, introducing a systematic error
In the resistivity practical, what graph is plotted? | Length of wire (L) on the x-axis against average resistance (R) on the y-axis
In the resistivity practical, what does the gradient of the R vs L graph represent? | ρ / A (resistivity divided by cross-sectional area)
How is resistivity calculated from the R vs L graph in the practical? | ρ = gradient × A (multiply the gradient of the graph by the cross-sectional area of the wire)
What systematic error should be checked for in the resistivity practical? | The fixed end of the wire must start at 0 on the ruler, otherwise a zero error is introduced in length measurements
In the equation R = ρL / A, comparing to y = mx, which variable corresponds to y? | R (resistance)
In the equation R = ρL / A, comparing to y = mx, which variable corresponds to x? | L (length)
The equation for the cross-sectional area of a circular wire is A = [...] | πd² / 4
A wire has resistivity 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω m, length 2.0 m, and cross-sectional area 5.0 × 10⁻⁶ m². What is its resistance? | R = ρL / A = (1.7 × 10⁻⁸ × 2.0) / 5.0 × 10⁻⁶ = 6.8 × 10⁻³ Ω
How does the relationship between resistance and temperature differ for a metallic conductor versus an NTC thermistor? | In a metal, resistance increases with temperature (more ion vibrations impede electrons). In an NTC thermistor, resistance decreases with temperature (more charge carriers are released at higher temperatures)
What is the key distinction between resistance and resistivity? | Resistance depends on the material, its dimensions (length and area), and temperature. Resistivity depends only on the material and its temperature, not the component's shape or size
