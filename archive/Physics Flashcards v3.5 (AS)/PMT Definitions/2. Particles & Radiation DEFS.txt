What is alpha decay? | The process of an unstable nucleus emitting an alpha particle (two protons and two neutrons) to become more stable
An unstable nucleus emits a particle containing two protons and two neutrons to become more stable — what is this process called? | Alpha decay
What does an alpha particle consist of? | Two protons and two neutrons (a helium-4 nucleus)
What is annihilation? | The process of a particle and its antiparticle colliding and being converted into energy, released as two photons
A particle and its antiparticle collide and are converted into energy released as two photons — what is this process called? | Annihilation
Why are two photons produced during annihilation rather than one? | To conserve momentum
What is an antiparticle? | A particle with the same mass as its corresponding particle but with opposite charge and opposite quantum numbers (e.g. baryon number, lepton number)
A particle has the same mass as another particle but opposite charge and conservation numbers — what is it called? | An antiparticle
What is baryon number? | A quantum number conserved in all particle interactions. Baryons have baryon number +1, antibaryons have -1, and non-baryons have 0
What baryon number does a baryon have? | +1
What baryon number does a non-baryon (e.g. a lepton or meson) have? | 0
What is a baryon? | A class of hadron made up of three quarks
A hadron made up of three quarks is classified as a [...] | baryon
Which is the only stable baryon? | The proton
Why is the proton the only stable baryon? | All other baryons eventually decay into a proton (the lowest-energy baryon state)
What is beta-minus decay? | A neutron inside a nucleus turns into a proton, emitting an electron (beta-minus particle) and an antineutrino
In beta-minus decay, what particle changes and what does it become? | A neutron changes into a proton
What two particles are emitted during beta-minus decay? | An electron (beta-minus particle) and an antineutrino
What is beta-plus decay? | A proton inside a nucleus turns into a neutron, emitting a positron (beta-plus particle) and a neutrino
In beta-plus decay, what particle changes and what does it become? | A proton changes into a neutron
What two particles are emitted during beta-plus decay? | A positron (beta-plus particle) and a neutrino
How does beta-minus decay differ from beta-plus decay? | In beta-minus decay, a neutron becomes a proton and emits an electron and antineutrino. In beta-plus decay, a proton becomes a neutron and emits a positron and neutrino
What is electron diffraction? | The spreading of electrons as they pass through a gap similar in size to their de Broglie wavelength
Why is electron diffraction significant? | It provides evidence of the wave-like properties of particles (wave-particle duality)
The spreading of electrons as they pass through a gap comparable to their de Broglie wavelength — what is this called? | Electron diffraction
What condition must be met for electron diffraction to be observed? | The gap size must be similar in magnitude to the de Broglie wavelength of the electrons
What is an electron-volt (eV)? | The work done (energy transferred) when an electron is accelerated through a potential difference of 1 V
The energy transferred when an electron moves through a potential difference of 1 V — what unit is this? | One electron-volt (1 eV)
How is the energy in electron-volts related to charge and voltage? | E = qV, so 1 eV equals the charge of an electron multiplied by 1 V
What are energy levels? | Defined, discrete energies at which electrons can exist within an atom
Why can't an electron exist between energy levels? | Energy levels are discrete (quantised), so electrons can only occupy specific allowed energy values
Electrons in an atom can only exist at defined, discrete energies — what are these called? | Energy levels
What is excitation? | The process of an electron absorbing exactly the right amount of energy to move to a higher energy level
An electron absorbs a specific quantity of energy and moves to a higher energy level — what is this process called? | Excitation
Why must the energy absorbed during excitation be an exact amount? | Because energy levels are discrete, so only specific energy transitions are allowed
What is a gauge boson? | An exchange particle that mediates (transmits) one of the four fundamental interactions between particles
The exchange particles responsible for transmitting the four fundamental forces — what are these called? | Gauge bosons
What is the ground state? | The lowest energy level that an electron can occupy in an atom
The lowest energy level an electron can exist in — what is this called? | The ground state
What is a hadron? | A class of subatomic particle that experiences the strong nuclear interaction
A subatomic particle that experiences the strong nuclear interaction is classified as a [...] | hadron
What are the two subclasses of hadron? | Baryons (three quarks) and mesons (quark-antiquark pair)
What is ionisation? | The process of an atom losing an orbital electron and becoming charged (forming an ion)
An atom loses an orbital electron and becomes positively charged — what is this process called? | Ionisation
How does ionisation differ from excitation? | In excitation, the electron moves to a higher energy level but stays bound to the atom. In ionisation, the electron is completely removed from the atom
What is an isotope? | An atom with the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons (same element, different mass number)
Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons — what are these called? | Isotopes
What is isotopic data? | Data obtained from isotopes that can be used for a specific purpose, such as carbon dating
What is a kaon? | A type of meson that decays into pions
A meson that decays into pions is called a [...] | kaon
What is lepton number? | A quantum number conserved in all particle interactions. Both electron lepton number and muon lepton number must be individually conserved
Why must both electron lepton number and muon lepton number be conserved separately? | They are independent conservation laws — each lepton generation has its own conserved lepton number
What is a lepton? | A fundamental (elementary) subatomic particle. The group includes electrons, muons, and their associated neutrinos
A fundamental particle from the group that includes electrons, muons, and neutrinos — what class of particle is this? | A lepton
What is a meson? | A class of hadron made up of a quark-antiquark pair
A hadron composed of a quark and an antiquark is classified as a [...] | meson
How does a meson differ from a baryon in terms of quark composition? | A meson is made of a quark-antiquark pair, whereas a baryon is made of three quarks
What is a muon? | A type of lepton that is heavier than an electron and decays into electrons
A lepton that decays into electrons is called a [...] | muon
What is a neutrino? | A nearly massless, neutral subatomic lepton whose existence was hypothesised to conserve energy and momentum in beta decay
Why was the neutrino originally hypothesised to exist? | To account for the conservation of energy (and momentum) in beta decay, where the emitted electron did not carry the full expected energy
A subatomic particle proposed to explain the missing energy in beta decay — what is it? | The neutrino
What is nucleon number (A)? | The total number of protons and neutrons in a given nucleus
The sum of protons and neutrons in a nucleus is called the [...] | nucleon number (A)
What is a nucleon? | A proton or neutron (a particle found in the nucleus)
What is pair production? | The process of a sufficiently high-energy photon converting into a particle and its corresponding antiparticle
A high-energy photon converts into a particle-antiparticle pair — what is this process called? | Pair production
Why does pair production usually occur near a nucleus? | To conserve momentum — the nearby nucleus absorbs the recoil momentum
How does pair production differ from annihilation? | In pair production, a photon creates a particle-antiparticle pair (energy to mass). In annihilation, a particle-antiparticle pair produces photons (mass to energy)
What is a photon? | A discrete packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy
What is a pion? | A type of meson that acts as the exchange particle for the strong nuclear force
A meson that mediates the strong nuclear force between nucleons is called a [...] | pion
What is a positron? | A positively charged particle that is the antiparticle of an electron (same mass, opposite charge)
The antiparticle of the electron is called the [...] | positron
What is proton number (Z)? | The number of protons in the nucleus of a given atom, which defines the element
The number of protons in a nucleus, which determines the element — what is this quantity called? | Proton number (Z)
What is stopping potential? | The minimum potential difference required to stop the most energetic photoelectrons from leaving the metal surface in the photoelectric effect
The minimum p.d. needed to prevent the fastest photoelectrons from escaping the metal surface — what is this called? | Stopping potential
What are strange particles? | Particles that are produced via the strong interaction but decay via the weak interaction
Particles produced through the strong interaction but decaying through the weak interaction are called [...] | strange particles
What is strangeness? | A quantum number that is conserved in strong interactions but not conserved in weak interactions
In which type of interaction is strangeness conserved? | The strong interaction (it is not conserved in weak interactions)
Why are strange particles always produced in pairs? | So that overall strangeness is conserved in the strong interaction that produces them (associated production)
How does conservation of strangeness differ from conservation of baryon number? | Baryon number is conserved in all interactions. Strangeness is conserved only in strong interactions, not in weak interactions
What is the strong nuclear force? | A short-range force that acts between nucleons to hold the nucleus together
At what range is the strong nuclear force attractive? | At separations up to about 3 fm
At what range does the strong nuclear force become repulsive? | At separations less than about 0.5 fm
The strong nuclear force is attractive up to ~3 fm and repulsive below ~0.5 fm — why is the repulsive component important? | It prevents nucleons from being crushed into each other, maintaining a minimum separation
What is threshold frequency? | The minimum frequency of incident photons required for photoelectrons to be emitted from a metal surface in the photoelectric effect
The minimum photon frequency needed to cause photoemission from a metal — what is this called? | Threshold frequency
How is threshold frequency related to work function? | Threshold frequency = work function / Planck's constant (f₀ = φ / h)
What is work function? | The minimum energy required to release an electron from the surface of a metal
The minimum energy needed to free an electron from a metal's surface — what quantity is this? | Work function
How are work function, threshold frequency, and Planck's constant related? | φ = hf₀, where φ is work function, h is Planck's constant, and f₀ is threshold frequency
