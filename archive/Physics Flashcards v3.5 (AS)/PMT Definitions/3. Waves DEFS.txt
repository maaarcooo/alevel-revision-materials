What is amplitude? | A wave's maximum displacement from its equilibrium position
A wave's maximum displacement from its equilibrium position is called what? | Amplitude
What is an antinode? | A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave
A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave is called what? | An antinode
What is cladding on an optical fibre? | A protective layer that improves tensile strength, prevents scratching, and prevents signal transfer between adjacent fibres
A protective layer on an optical fibre that improves tensile strength, prevents scratching, and stops signal transfer between fibres is called what? | Cladding
State the three functions of cladding on an optical fibre | Improves tensile strength, prevents scratching, and prevents signal transfer between adjacent fibres
What does it mean for waves to be coherent? | They have the same wavelength and frequency, with a fixed phase difference between them
Waves with the same wavelength and frequency, and a fixed phase difference between them, are described as what? | Coherent
What three conditions are required for coherence? | Same wavelength, same frequency, and a fixed phase difference
What is a diffraction grating? | A grating with hundreds of slits per millimetre that produces sharper interference patterns
A grating with hundreds of slits per millimetre that produces sharper interference patterns is called what? | A diffraction grating
State two uses of diffraction gratings | Calculating atomic spacing and analysing elements
What is diffraction? | The spreading of waves as they pass through a gap of a similar magnitude to their wavelength
The spreading of waves as they pass through a gap of similar size to their wavelength is called what? | Diffraction
For significant diffraction to occur, how must the gap size compare to the wavelength? | The gap must be of a similar magnitude to the wavelength
What are electromagnetic waves? | Waves consisting of perpendicular electric and magnetic oscillations
Waves consisting of perpendicular electric and magnetic oscillations are called what? | Electromagnetic waves
What is frequency? | The number of waves that pass a point in a unit time period
The number of waves passing a point per unit time is called what? | Frequency
How is frequency related to the time period? | Frequency is the inverse of the time period (f = 1/T)
What is fringe spacing? | The distance between two adjacent bright fringes or two adjacent dark fringes
The distance between two adjacent bright (or dark) fringes is called what? | Fringe spacing
What is interference? | The superposition of waves that occurs when two waves meet
The superposition of waves when two waves meet is called what? | Interference
What type of interference occurs when two waves meet in phase? | Constructive interference
What type of interference occurs when two waves meet out of phase? | Destructive interference
What is a laser? | A light source that produces a collimated and coherent beam
A light source that produces a collimated and coherent beam is called what? | A laser
What is a longitudinal wave? | A wave with oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation
A wave with oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation is called what? | A longitudinal wave
Give an example of a longitudinal wave | Sound waves
What is material dispersion? | Waves of different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds through an optical fibre, reaching the end at slightly different times, causing pulse broadening
Explain why material dispersion causes pulse broadening | Different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds through the fibre, so they arrive at the end at slightly different times, stretching the pulse
How can material dispersion be reduced? | By using monochromatic light (single wavelength), so all light travels at the same speed through the fibre
What is modal dispersion? | Waves enter an optical fibre at slightly different angles, travelling slightly different distances, reaching the end at different times, causing pulse broadening
Explain why modal dispersion causes pulse broadening | Waves enter the fibre at slightly different angles, so each beam travels a slightly different distance, meaning they arrive at the end at different times
How does modal dispersion differ from material dispersion? | Modal dispersion is caused by waves entering at different angles (different path lengths), while material dispersion is caused by different wavelengths travelling at different speeds
What is a node? | A position of minimum displacement in a stationary wave
A position of minimum displacement in a stationary wave is called what? | A node
How does a node differ from an antinode? | A node is a position of minimum displacement in a stationary wave, while an antinode is a position of maximum displacement
What is an optical fibre? | A thin glass fibre through which signals are passed, usually surrounded by cladding
A thin glass fibre used to transmit signals, usually surrounded by cladding, is called what? | An optical fibre
What is path difference? | A measure of how far ahead one wave is compared to another, usually expressed in terms of wavelength
A measure of how far ahead one wave is compared to another, usually expressed in terms of wavelength, is called what? | Path difference
What is phase difference? | The difference in phase between two points on a wave, usually expressed in radians
The difference in phase between two points on a wave, usually expressed in radians, is called what? | Phase difference
What is phase? | A measure of how far through a wave's cycle a given point on the wave is
A measure of how far through a wave's cycle a given point is describes what quantity? | Phase
What is polarisation? | The restriction of a wave so that it can only oscillate in a single plane
The restriction of a wave so it oscillates in only a single plane is called what? | Polarisation
Why can only transverse waves be polarised? | Only transverse waves have oscillations perpendicular to the direction of propagation, meaning they can be restricted to a single plane. Longitudinal waves oscillate parallel to propagation and cannot be restricted in this way.
What is pulse broadening? | The elongation of a signal passed down an optical fibre, commonly due to modal or material dispersion
The elongation of a signal in an optical fibre, caused by modal or material dispersion, is called what? | Pulse broadening
What two types of dispersion commonly cause pulse broadening? | Modal dispersion and material dispersion
What is the refractive index of a material? | The ratio between the speed of light in a vacuum and the speed of light in that material (n = c/v)
The ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in a material defines what quantity? | The refractive index of the material
What is Snell's Law? | A law linking a wave's angle of incidence to its angle of refraction using the refractive indices of the media involved (n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2)
A law that relates the angle of incidence to the angle of refraction using the refractive indices of the two media is called what? | Snell's Law
What is the equation for wave speed? | v = f × λ (speed = frequency × wavelength)
The product of a wave's frequency and wavelength gives what quantity? | Wave speed (v = fλ)
What is a stationary wave? | A wave that stores energy but does not transfer it
A wave that stores energy but does not transfer it is called what? | A stationary wave
What is total internal reflection? | Full reflection occurring at the inside boundary of an optical fibre, meaning no radiation passes out
Full reflection at the inside boundary of a fibre so that no radiation passes out is called what? | Total internal reflection
What is a transverse wave? | A wave with oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation
A wave with oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation is called what? | A transverse wave
Give an example of a transverse wave | Electromagnetic waves
How does a transverse wave differ from a longitudinal wave? | In a transverse wave, oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation. In a longitudinal wave, oscillations are parallel to it.
What is wavelength? | The distance between two identical positions on two adjacent waves (e.g. peak to peak or trough to trough)
The distance between two identical positions on adjacent waves is called what? | Wavelength
What is Young's double-slit experiment? | An experiment demonstrating the diffraction of light by passing monochromatic light through two narrow slits and observing the resulting pattern of bright and dark fringes
An experiment using monochromatic light and two narrow slits to produce a pattern of bright and dark fringes demonstrates what? | Young's double-slit experiment
What type of light source is used in Young's double-slit experiment? | Monochromatic light (single wavelength)
In a stationary wave, where are the positions of zero displacement found? | At the nodes
In a stationary wave, where are the positions of maximum displacement found? | At the antinodes
In what units is phase difference usually expressed? | Radians
In what units is path difference usually expressed? | In terms of wavelength (e.g. nλ or fractions of λ)
What property of a laser makes it suitable for interference experiments? | It produces coherent light (same wavelength and frequency with a fixed phase difference)
