What is an ammeter? | A device that measures the current in the loop of the circuit it is connected in series with
A device connected in series to measure current in a circuit loop — what is this called? | An ammeter
What resistance does an ideal ammeter have? | Zero resistance
Why must an ammeter be connected in series? | So that all the current flowing through the circuit component also flows through the ammeter, giving an accurate reading
What is current? | The rate of flow of charge in a circuit
The rate of flow of charge in a circuit — what quantity is this? | Current
What is electromotive force (emf)? | The amount of energy transferred by a source to each unit of charge that passes through it
The energy transferred by a source per unit charge passing through it — what quantity is this? | Electromotive force (emf)
How does emf differ from potential difference? | Emf is the energy transferred per unit charge by a source, while potential difference is the energy transferred per unit charge by a component
What is internal resistance? | The resistance to the flow of charge within a source
The resistance to charge flow within a source — what term describes this? | Internal resistance
What is the consequence of internal resistance? | Energy is dissipated within the source (as heat), reducing the terminal potential difference
What is a light dependent resistor (LDR)? | A light sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases when light intensity decreases
A light sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases as light intensity decreases — what component is this? | A light dependent resistor (LDR)
How does light intensity affect the resistance of an LDR? | As light intensity decreases, resistance increases (and vice versa)
What is an ohmic conductor? | A conductor for which the current is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, under constant physical conditions
A conductor where current is directly proportional to potential difference under constant physical conditions — what is this called? | An ohmic conductor
What condition must be maintained for a conductor to behave ohmically? | Constant physical conditions (e.g. constant temperature)
State Ohm's law | The current through an ohmic conductor under constant physical conditions is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, with the constant of proportionality being resistance
In Ohm's law, what is the constant of proportionality between current and potential difference? | Resistance
State Ohm's law in equation form | V = IR
What are parallel circuits? | Circuits where components are connected across each other in separate loops
Components connected across each other in separate loops are said to be connected in [...] | parallel
What is a potential divider? | A method of splitting a potential difference by connecting two resistors in series, where the total p.d. is split in the ratio of their resistances
A method of splitting a potential difference using two resistors in series — what is this called? | A potential divider
In a potential divider, how is the total potential difference shared between the two resistors? | It is split in the ratio of their resistances
What is resistance? | A measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a material
A measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a material — what quantity is this? | Resistance
What is resistivity? | A quantity proportional to an object's resistance and cross-sectional area, and inversely proportional to its length
A quantity proportional to resistance and cross-sectional area, and inversely proportional to length — what is this? | Resistivity
State the equation for resistivity | ρ = RA / L (where R = resistance, A = cross-sectional area, L = length)
What happens to the potential difference across resistors connected in parallel? | The potential difference is identical across each resistor
What happens to the current in a parallel circuit? | The current is split between the resistors
How do you calculate total resistance for resistors in parallel? | 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ... (inverse of the sum of inverses)
What happens to the current through resistors connected in series? | The current is identical through each resistor
How is the potential difference shared across resistors in series? | It is split in the ratio of their resistances
How do you calculate total resistance for resistors in series? | R_total = R₁ + R₂ + ... (sum of individual resistances)
What are series circuits? | Circuits where components are connected end to end in one loop
Components connected end to end in one loop are said to be connected in [...] | series
What is a superconductor? | A material that has zero resistivity when its temperature is decreased to or below its critical temperature
A material with zero resistivity at or below its critical temperature — what is this called? | A superconductor
What is the critical temperature of a superconductor? | The temperature at or below which the material's resistivity drops to zero
State two applications of superconductors | Producing strong magnetic fields and reducing energy loss when transmitting electric power
Explain why superconductors reduce energy loss in power transmission | They have zero resistivity, so no energy is dissipated as heat in the conductor when current flows through it
What is terminal potential difference? | The potential difference across the terminals of a power source, equal to the source's emf minus the voltage drop across its internal resistance
The p.d. across a source's terminals, equal to emf minus the voltage lost to internal resistance — what is this called? | Terminal potential difference
State the equation relating emf, terminal p.d., and internal resistance | V = ε - Ir (terminal p.d. = emf minus current times internal resistance)
What is a thermistor? | A temperature sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases when temperature decreases
A temperature sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases as temperature decreases — what component is this? | A thermistor
How does temperature affect the resistance of a thermistor? | As temperature decreases, resistance increases (and vice versa)
What is a voltmeter? | A device used to measure the potential difference across components
A device used to measure potential difference across components — what is this called? | A voltmeter
What resistance does an ideal voltmeter have? | Infinite resistance
Why must a voltmeter have very high resistance? | So that negligible current flows through it, avoiding significantly altering the current in the circuit and ensuring an accurate p.d. reading
How does an LDR differ from a thermistor? | An LDR's resistance changes with light intensity, while a thermistor's resistance changes with temperature. Both are semiconductors with resistance that increases as the stimulus (light/temperature) decreases
How does an ammeter differ from a voltmeter in terms of connection and ideal resistance? | An ammeter is connected in series with ideal resistance of zero. A voltmeter is connected in parallel with ideal resistance of infinity
How do series and parallel circuits differ in terms of current and potential difference? | In series: current is the same through all components, p.d. is split in the ratio of resistances. In parallel: p.d. is the same across all components, current is split between branches
How does the total resistance formula differ for series and parallel combinations? | Series: R_total = R₁ + R₂ (resistances add). Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ (reciprocals add). Total parallel resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance
